## I. Eve of Battle

Batu beureum Candi Abang baseuh ku cihujan sarta getih nu tacan kaluar.

The red stone of Candi Abang was wet with rain and with blood not yet spilled. The fortress clung to the spine of Giri Mataram like a prayer carved into the ribs of the earth -- tiered ramparts of dark red andesite rising in concentric arcs from the valley floor to the summit, fitted without mortar in the manner of the ancestors, each block shaped by hands that understood that stone does not require binding when it is cut to fit the way bone fits bone in a living body. The andesite was the color of dried blood in some lights, of dark copper in others, and in the strange, pewter-grey illumination of the gathering storm it seemed to pulse with its own inner warmth, as though the mountain's fire had been captured in the mineral structure of the rock and held there, banked but not extinguished, waiting for the hour when it would be needed.

Tonight, that hour had come.

They had ridden hard from Giri Mataram -- three days of punishing progress through mountain passes slick with monsoon rain and clogged with the detritus of a kingdom in retreat. The roads were choked with refugees: farmers with their livestock, merchants with their carts piled high with cloth and rice, mothers carrying children whose eyes held the particular blankness of those too young to understand what they were fleeing but old enough to feel its weight. They streamed westward, away from the frontier, away from the dark columns of smoke that marked the Buta advance, and their faces as they watched Raden Wijaya's company ride east against the tide held a mixture of hope and pity that was difficult to distinguish from grief.

Raden Wijaya rode at the head of the column. The Keris Nagasasra hung at his hip, its dark metal warm against his thigh even through the scabbard, its pamor patterns pulsing with a faint luminescence that he could feel rather than see -- the blade's awareness of what lay ahead, its ancient iron tasting the approach of enemies it had been forged to oppose. His batik sarong, the *parang rusak* pattern now so stained and faded that only a trained eye could read the lineage it declared, was tucked tight for riding, and his hair, unbound in the manner of a man who had long since abandoned the courtly grooming of the *kadaton*, streamed behind him in the wet wind like a battle pennant.

Beside him rode Prabu Jayabaya. The Lion of Mataram was awake.

It was a thing to see -- this awakening, this resurrection of a man from the living death of Ki Surasenta's spell. Jayabaya rode his war-horse with the rigid posture of a king who has remembered what royalty costs and has decided to pay it in full. His face was still gaunt from the years of enchanted decline, the bones of his cheeks and jaw standing sharp beneath skin that had not known sunlight for too long, but his eyes -- his eyes were the eyes of the portraits in the Balairung Agung, the eyes of Hayam Wuruk's line, dark and fierce and lit from within by a *semangat* that Ki Surasenta's whispers had suppressed but could not extinguish. He wore armor now -- not the ceremonial gold of the *kadaton* but campaign steel, dented and practical, borrowed from the armory of Kediri because the fine armor of Mataram had been left to corrode in the neglected stores of Giri Mataram. The ancestral sword of Mataram hung across his back, its hilt wrapped in leather darkened by the grip of seven generations of kings.

"How far?" Jayabaya asked, his voice still carrying the rasp of a man whose throat had been accustomed to whispers and was relearning the register of command.

"Before nightfall, Gusti Prabu," Raden Wijaya said. He used the full honorific deliberately, publicly, in the hearing of the column -- an act of allegiance that was also an act of strategy, binding the soldiers' loyalty to the restored king. "The scouts report that the garrison still holds, but they are pressed. The outer villages have fallen."

"And the enemy?"

Raden Wijaya looked east, where the mountains dissolved into a grey haze of rain and smoke. "The Buta vanguard was seen two days ago in the Cibeulah valley. Ten thousand, perhaps more. Siege engines. Black powder. Led by Leak commanders." He paused. "They will reach Candi Abang by nightfall."

Jayabaya's hands tightened on the reins, and for a moment the gauntness of his face contorted into something that was not fear but its cousin -- the cold recognition of odds that even a recovered king could not pretend were favorable.

"Then we race them," Jayabaya said.

"Then we race them," Raden Wijaya agreed. "*Inggih.*"

Behind them, the column stretched back along the mountain road: three hundred soldiers of the Mataram household guard -- the *Bayangkara*, the sworn protectors of the throne, who had answered Jayabaya's call with a fervor that was part loyalty, part relief, and part the desperate gratitude of men who had spent years watching their kingdom rot and were finally permitted to fight. They were not enough. Three hundred men to hold a fortress against ten thousand was not a battle plan; it was arithmetic of a particularly grim variety. But they were what remained, and they rode with the fierce discipline of soldiers who understood that the fortress behind them contained not merely walls and towers but the last defensible position between the Buta horde and the unprotected heartlands of Mataram.

Behind the Bayangkara rode Tumenggung Batu, his massive war-horse laboring under the combined weight of a Kurcaci in full iron armor and a temper that added its own gravitational pull. The chieftain had been silent for most of the ride, a silence that those who knew him had learned to read as the Kurcaci equivalent of a volcano building pressure. He rode with his war-hammer across his knees, its iron head black and heavy as a thundercloud, and his stone-grey eyes moved constantly -- reading the terrain, assessing the rock, the slope, the angle of the mountain's spine with the instinctive cartography of a being whose people had lived inside mountains since before the surface world had learned to stack one stone upon another.

Dewi Anjani rode beside him, and the contrast between them was the contrast between earth and air, between the mineral patience of deep stone and the luminous restlessness of a creature born above the clouds. The Bidadari warrior-princess sat her horse with the effortless grace of a being for whom gravity was a suggestion rather than a law, her silver hair streaming in the rain, her bow and quiver secured across her back in the manner of one who expected to need them soon. Her golden eyes scanned the mountain ridges with a sight that pierced the rain and the gathering dusk, reading the landscape not merely for threats but for the deeper patterns -- the flow of *bayu*, the spiritual wind, which told her things about the enemy's disposition that scouts on horseback could never discern.

"The darkness thickens in the east," she said, her voice carrying to Raden Wijaya despite the wind and the rain, the High Language of the Bidadari lending her words a clarity that cut through noise as an arrow cuts through air. "The *bayu* is corrupted for three leagues in every direction from the Buta column. They carry their master's shadow with them like a cloak."

"How many?" Tumenggung Batu grunted.

"More than ten thousand. The shadow is too dense for fewer." Her eyes narrowed. "And something else. Something heavy. Engines of iron and fire."

---

They reached Candi Abang as the last light drained from the sky like blood from a wound.

The fortress revealed itself in stages, the way a wayang figure emerges from behind the screen -- first the highest tower, a finger of red stone pointing at the storm-dark sky; then the upper ramparts, their battlements notched with the distinctive *tumpang sari* pattern of Mataram military architecture; then the middle walls, thicker, lower, pierced with arrow slits that stared into the valley like narrow, watchful eyes; and finally the outer walls, massive and scarred, their surfaces pitted by centuries of weather and, more recently, by the impact of catapult stones that had left fresh wounds in the ancient andesite.

The fortress was built into the mountain itself -- not merely upon it but into it, as though the builders had understood that a fortification separate from its foundation is a thing that can be moved, and what can be moved can be toppled. The outer walls grew from the bedrock like geological formations, their foundations invisible, their roots reaching down into the mountain's core. The inner walls rose in terraces that followed the mountain's contour, each level higher and narrower than the one below, so that the fortress as a whole resembled a ziggurat -- or, in the language of the culture that had built it, a *candi*, a temple-tower, a place where the sacred and the martial were unified in stone.

At its heart stood the inner sanctuary -- the *pendopo agung*, the great hall, carved from a single outcropping of red andesite that the builders had shaped into a space of austere, terrible beauty. Its columns were carved in the likeness of guardian figures -- *dwarapala*, the gate-keepers -- whose stone faces held expressions of eternal vigilance, their hands gripping maces and swords that would never dull because they would never be drawn. The bronze doors of the sanctuary were three spans thick, their surfaces embossed with scenes from the *Bharatayudha* -- the great war of the *wayang* epics -- as though the builders had known, with the prescience of the truly wise, that the doors would one day need to hold against a real war and had given them stories of ancient battles to remind the metal of its purpose.

The garrison met them at the outer gate.

They were fewer than Raden Wijaya had hoped and in worse condition than he had feared. Two hundred soldiers, perhaps -- the remnants of the border garrison, reinforced by local militia armed with farming implements and whatever weapons could be salvaged from the armory. Many were wounded. Their bandages were dirty. Their eyes held the sunken, glassy look of men who had been fighting a losing action for weeks and had reached the state beyond exhaustion where the body functions on habit and the mind retreats into a small, defended space where it does not have to process the full scope of the disaster.

Their commander, a grizzled *lurah* named Senapati Pandu, knelt when he saw the royal banners.

"*Duh Gusti Prabu,*" the old soldier said, his voice cracking. "We had heard -- we had been told that Giri Mataram had fallen. That the King was dead. We held because we had nowhere else to go."

Jayabaya dismounted. He walked to the kneeling man and placed both hands on his shoulders, and the gesture was so simple, so human, so utterly devoid of the ceremonial distance that kingship normally demands, that the soldiers around them stiffened and then softened, and something shifted in the air -- a re-alignment, a realignment of loyalty, of hope, of the particular energy that an army generates when it discovers that the thing it fights for is not an abstraction but a person who stands in the rain and touches the shoulders of a bleeding soldier.

"Rise, Senapati," Jayabaya said. "The King lives. Mataram lives. And tonight, we hold these walls." He turned to face the garrison, raising his voice so that it carried across the courtyard, and the rasp in his throat became not a weakness but a quality -- the sound of a voice that had been silenced and had broken free, rougher and realer for the breaking. "I was lost. I will not lie to you. I was lost in a fog of my own making, listening to whispers that should have been met with steel. But I am here now. I am awake. And I swear by the *karuhun* of Mataram, by the bones of Hayam Wuruk, by the sacred fire that burns in the heart of this mountain -- I will not sleep again until the enemy is broken or I am dead."

A sound rose from the garrison -- not a cheer, which would have been too grand, but a murmur, a collective exhalation, the sound of men whose shoulders had been bowed by despair straightening under the weight of renewed purpose. It was not enough to win a battle. But it was enough to face one.

Raden Wijaya wasted no time. He walked the walls with Senapati Pandu, his eyes cataloguing every crack, every weakness, every section where the ancient stonework had been compromised by time or enemy action. His mind worked with the cold, precise efficiency of a man who had studied siege warfare not as an academic exercise but as a condition of survival, and what it produced was not optimism but a plan -- a distribution of forces that maximized what they had and minimized what they lacked.

"The outer wall is the longest and the weakest," he said, tracing the line of the rampart with his hand. "We cannot hold it with five hundred men against ten thousand. We will defend it for as long as we can -- to break their momentum, to cost them -- but when it falls, we fall back to the middle wall. This is where we hold. The middle wall is shorter, higher, and built on a natural ledge that gives us a killing ground below. Archers on the wall, spearmen at the gates, reserves in the courtyard."

"And if the middle wall falls?" Senapati Pandu asked. The question was not defeatist; it was the question of a professional soldier who has learned that plans without contingencies are merely hopes in armor.

"Then we retreat to the inner sanctuary," Raden Wijaya said. "The bronze doors. The *pendopo agung*. We hold there until dawn." He paused, and something in his pause communicated the fragility of the plan's final element -- the thing upon which everything depended and which he could not guarantee. "The Dwija Putih rides to bring the Horse Lords of Kediri. If they come at dawn--"

"If," Senapati Pandu said.

"If," Raden Wijaya agreed. He did not embellish the word with false confidence. He let it stand -- naked, honest, a single syllable that contained the entire margin between survival and annihilation. "But *if* is what we have. We fight for *if*."

---

The Dwija Putih stood at the highest point of the fortress, his white robes billowing in the storm wind, his staff planted in the stone like a standard. The golden light at its tip flickered in the wind but did not go out -- a small, persistent flame against the vast dark, the kind of flame that darkness cannot extinguish because extinguishing it would require darkness to acknowledge its existence, and the flame was too small, too stubborn, too indifferent to darkness's opinion to merit the attention.

Raden Wijaya climbed to him.

"You must go now, Eyang," the prince said. The *Krama Inggil* came naturally, the high register of respect that was not deference but recognition -- the younger warrior acknowledging the older sage, the sword acknowledging the shield. "The Horse Lords will need time to muster. If you ride through the night--"

"I will reach Kediri by dawn," the Dwija Putih said. His golden eyes were fixed on the eastern valley, where the first torches were appearing -- scattered points of fire in the rain-dark, multiplying as he watched, like sparks on a field of tinder. "Arya Kamandanu is ready. He has been gathering his *éored* since the fall of the border posts. Three thousand spears. But they are a day's ride south, and the road is not safe."

"Can you bring them?"

The White Sage turned to Raden Wijaya, and in his golden eyes was something that the prince had seen only once before -- in the caverns beneath the Jembatan Batu, in the moment before the old man had fallen into fire and been remade. It was certainty. Not the false certainty of dogma or the brittle certainty of desperation, but the mineral certainty of a being who has passed through death and knows, with the absolute conviction of direct experience, that some things cannot be stopped.

"Hold until dawn, Raden," the Dwija Putih said. "Hold until the light breaks in the east. Whatever comes -- whatever darkness, whatever iron, whatever fire the enemy hurls against these walls -- hold. Do not yield. Do not despair. The dawn will come, and I will ride with it."

He struck his staff against the stone, and the golden light flared -- a single, brilliant pulse that illuminated the entire summit of the fortress for the space of a heartbeat, so that the soldiers below looked up and saw, silhouetted against the flash, the figure of the White Sage standing tall against the storm, and the sight entered them like a draught of *jamu* -- warming, strengthening, burning away the cold residue of fear.

Then he was gone. Down the stairs, through the postern gate, mounting the white horse that had been held ready in the courtyard below. Raden Wijaya watched from the rampart as the Dwija Putih rode into the storm-dark, his white robes a diminishing streak of light against the black mountain road, his staff blazing gold in the rain. He rode west, toward Kediri, and behind him the fortress of red stone swallowed his light and was dark again.

Raden Wijaya stood alone on the rampart.

The torches in the valley below had multiplied. They were no longer scattered points but a solid field of fire, a carpet of flame that stretched from the valley floor to the lower slopes of the surrounding mountains, and above the flames rose the sound -- a sound that had no equivalent in the world of men, a sound that was part war-drum and part animal cry and part the grinding of tectonic plates, a sound that was the voice of ten thousand Buta Raksasa preparing for slaughter.

Night fell. The rain fell. The torches burned.

And the fortress of Candi Abang, red stone against black sky, waited for the war to begin.

---

## II. The Siege Begins

The first wave came at the deepest hour of the night, when the rain was a solid wall of water and the darkness was so complete that the Buta's torches were the only light in the world.

They came with war-drums.

The sound preceded the attack by a full quarter-hour -- a deep, rhythmic pounding that rose from the valley floor and reverberated off the mountain walls, a percussion that was not music but its opposite, the anti-gamelan, the sound of rhythm stripped of melody and meaning and reduced to its most primitive function: the communication of mass, of momentum, of ten thousand bodies moving as one toward a single point. The drums were the voice of the horde, and the horde was vast, and the vastness was the weapon. Not the spears, not the clubs, not the siege ladders. The vastness. The sheer, crushing, oceanic weight of numbers that said: *We are more. We are always more. You cannot kill us fast enough.*

The *kendang* drums of the gamelan answer to the *dalang's* hand -- they quicken and slow, they whisper and shout, they follow the story's breath. The Buta drums knew no *dalang*. They were rhythm without story, beat without meaning, the mechanical pulse of an army that functioned not by inspiration but by compulsion, driven forward by the will of a distant master whose shadow lay on them like a physical weight.

Raden Wijaya stood on the main rampart, the rain streaming from his hair, the Keris Nagasasra unsheathed in his right hand. The blade was alive. Its pamor patterns pulsed with a cold, steady luminescence -- the sacred iron responding to the proximity of the corruption it had been forged to oppose, a light that was not bright enough to see by but bright enough to remind those who stood near it that there were forces in the world older than darkness and harder than iron.

"Archers, ready!" His voice cut through the rain and the drums like a *suling* note through a storm. It was the voice of the *Krama Inggil* -- not the register of polite conversation but the register of absolute command, the register that does not ask and does not argue and does not repeat itself, the register that a prince of Mataram uses when the margin between life and death is measured in the speed with which an order is obeyed.

Along the outer wall, the archers nocked. They were a thin line -- barely a hundred, strung along a rampart that was designed for three times their number -- and their bowstrings were wet, and their arrows were finite, and the darkness beyond the wall was infinite and full of things that wanted them dead. But they nocked. They drew. They waited.

The first Buta appeared at the base of the wall.

It came out of the rain like a nightmare given mass -- three meters of grey-green muscle, its tusks gleaming wet, its eyes a dull, burning orange that reflected the torchlight without intelligence, without mercy, without anything that could be called human. It carried a siege ladder in one hand, an iron-shod club in the other, and it ran at the wall with the headlong, unthinking momentum of a boulder rolling downhill, and behind it came another, and another, and another, and the slope below the outer wall became a river of grey-green flesh flowing uphill against the force of gravity by the sheer pressure of numbers.

"*Lepas!*" Raden Wijaya shouted. *Loose!*

The arrows flew. A hundred shafts hissing through the rain, finding throats and eyes and the gaps between crude armor plates, and the first rank of Buta stumbled, fell, were trampled by the rank behind them, and the rank behind them was struck by the second volley, and they too fell, and the slope became a tangle of thrashing bodies and abandoned ladders and the black, reeking ichor that served the Buta in place of blood.

But the third rank reached the wall.

The siege ladders slammed against the andesite with a sound like the *saron* struck at full force -- metallic, percussive, final. The Buta swarmed up them with the mindless ferocity of ants on a sugar trail, their clawed hands finding purchase on the rough stone, their tusked mouths open in a continuous howl of rage that was indistinguishable from agony.

And then the wall defense began in earnest.

"*Merak simper!*" Senapati Pandu bellowed from the eastern section, and the soldiers fell into the ancient defensive formation -- the Peacock's Fan, a pattern as old as the fortress itself, in which the defenders spread along the wall in overlapping arcs, each man covering his neighbor's blind side, so that the defensive line presented no gap, no weakness, no seam through which a single attacker could penetrate without facing three blades. It was a formation designed for walls, by builders who understood walls, and it turned the rampart into a killing ground.

Spears thrust downward into climbing Buta. Boiling oil -- rendered from the fat of *kerbau* that the garrison had slaughtered in preparation -- poured from iron cauldrons onto the creatures below, and the screams that rose from the scalded Buta were a sound that would live in the defenders' nightmares for whatever remained of their lives. Stones, cut to fit the hands of the masons who had built the walls, were hurled down on the ladders, smashing through the rungs, sending Buta tumbling back into the press below.

Raden Wijaya fought on the main rampart.

The Keris Nagasasra was a terrible beauty in his hands -- the blade's sacred iron cutting through Buta flesh with a hunger that was not the prince's own but the weapon's, the ancient metal's response to the corruption it encountered. Where the keris struck, the wounds smoked. Where it drew blood, the blood sizzled and burned, as though the Buta's corrupted ichor could not endure contact with the pure iron of the blade's forging. Raden Wijaya fought with the economy of a master -- no wasted motion, no theatrical flourish, each cut and thrust and parry placed with the precision of a man who has studied the geometry of combat since childhood and for whom every fight is an equation, and the solution is always: survive.

A Buta crested the rampart to his left, its club raised for a killing blow, and before the blow could fall a blade flashed beside Raden Wijaya and opened the creature's throat from ear to ear. King Jayabaya stepped into the gap, the ancestral sword of Mataram red with ichor, and the Lion was no longer merely awake -- he was *hunting*.

"*Mulah lewat!*" the King roared, his voice a thunder that rivaled the drums. *They shall not pass!* "By the bones of my fathers, they shall not pass this wall while a man of Mataram stands upon it!"

There was something in the King's fighting that was different from the prince's -- less disciplined, perhaps, less geometrically precise, but compensating with a raw, ferocious energy that came not from technique but from outrage. Jayabaya fought as a man fights who has woken from a stupor to find his house burning and his family in danger -- with the boundless, almost unhinged fury of guilt transformed into action, of wasted years repaid in blood. Every blow he struck was a blow against the enchantment that had stolen his will, against the weakness that had permitted Ki Surasenta's poison, against the king he had failed to be. He fought to redeem himself, and the redemption flowed through the ancestral sword like current through a river, and the Buta who faced him died not merely from steel but from the concentrated *semangat* of a royal line that had ruled these mountains for seven generations and was not prepared to stop.

"*Inggih!*" the soldiers shouted, the word carrying down the wall like a wave, each man adding his voice to the king's, and the *inggih* was not merely an affirmation but a battle cry, a pledge, a declaration: *Yes. We hold. We are here.*

---

On the eastern wall, Dewi Anjani stood like a statue of silver and starlight amid the rain and blood.

She did not shout. She did not roar. She sang.

Her bow was an instrument of ancient making -- carved from the wood of a *cemara* tree that had grown on the slopes of Giri Agung, the Mountain of Light, where the Bidadari had dwelled before the world was young. Its string was woven from the hair of the *dewi* themselves, and when drawn it hummed a high, pure note that cut through the noise of battle the way starlight cuts through smoke -- not by overwhelming the darkness but by being of a fundamentally different nature, a frequency that darkness could not replicate or suppress.

She drew and released, drew and released, and each arrow was a prayer.

Not metaphorically. Literally. The Bidadari did not separate the act of killing from the act of worship; for them, the defense of the *dharma* was itself a sacred rite, and each arrow that left Dewi Anjani's bow carried with it a whispered invocation to the *karuhun* of the Silver Mountain, a syllable of the old language that charged the iron arrowhead with a brilliance that burned the corruption out of whatever flesh it pierced. Her arrows did not merely wound; they purified. They struck a Buta's eye and the creature convulsed and fell, and the ichor that leaked from the wound was not black but clear, as though in the instant of death the corruption had been burned away and what remained was merely meat, merely matter, returned to the cycle of decay and renewal that governed all mortal things.

She sang the *Sinom* -- the pupuh of youth and beauty, of the rising sun and the opening flower -- and her voice was a silver thread winding through the cacophony of battle, and the soldiers who heard it fought harder, stood straighter, because the song reminded them that beauty existed, that grace existed, that there were things in the world worth defending that could not be measured in walls and weapons.

Her quiver was deep. But it was not infinite. And the night was long.

---

On the western section of the outer wall, Tumenggung Batu had found his rhythm.

The Kurcaci fought the way stone fights -- not with speed, not with agility, but with the implacable, grinding, geological patience of a force that cannot be moved, cannot be worn down, cannot be convinced that yielding is an option. He stood at the point where two sections of wall met at an angle, creating a corner that the Buta attacked from both sides simultaneously, and he held that corner alone.

His war-hammer was an extension of his body, an expression of his will made manifest in iron and oak. It swung in arcs that were not graceful but were something better -- inevitable. Each arc described a circle that encompassed the full reach of the hammer's head, and anything within that circle -- Buta flesh, Buta iron, Buta bone -- was struck with a force that was not merely physical but carried the weight of the deep earth itself, the accumulated pressure of the stone halls and the forge-fires and the ancient, patient anger of a people who had been insulted by the surface world's wars for the last time.

"Fourteen!" he bellowed as a Buta's skull caved under the hammer's descending stroke. "Fifteen! Sixteen! Are you counting, Bidadari? Because I'm counting!"

From the eastern wall, carried impossibly through the rain and the battle-noise, Dewi Anjani's voice drifted back: "Eighteen, Tumenggung. I passed sixteen two volleys ago."

"Impossible!" The Kurcaci's indignation was genuine, or as genuine as anything could be in the mouth of a being who found in the arithmetic of combat a comfort that bordered on the aesthetic. He swung the hammer in a horizontal arc that took two Buta off the wall simultaneously, sending them spinning into the darkness below. "Eighteen! That was eighteen and nineteen! I'm ahead!"

"Seventeen," Dewi Anjani corrected, and through the song in her voice there was the faintest trace of what might have been amusement, and the amusement was itself a kind of courage -- the refusal to let the horror of the moment consume the capacity for something that was, in its small way, joy.

Tumenggung Batu planted his feet and swung the hammer down onto a siege ladder, shattering it at the midpoint, spilling a dozen Buta into the void below. "Does ladder count?" he shouted. "If ladder counts, I'm at thirty!"

"The ladder does not count, Tumenggung."

"Stone-born politics," the Kurcaci muttered, and turned to face the next wave.

---

The rain was relentless.

It fell in sheets, in curtains, in solid walls of water that reduced visibility to a few arm-lengths and turned the stone ramparts into rivers of running mud and blood. It drummed on armor and on flesh with an indiscriminate percussion that was itself a kind of assault, and the defenders, already soaked through, fought in a world of water -- their footing treacherous, their grip uncertain, their eyes stinging with the grit and ash that the rain carried from the volcanic clouds above.

But the rain was also a weapon. It slowed the Buta's ladders. It made the wall-face slick, so that the climbing creatures lost their grip and fell, clutching at the wet stone with claws that found no purchase. It doused the torches that the attackers carried, plunging sections of the assault into darkness where the Buta's dull orange eyes were the only light, and those eyes made excellent targets for archers who had learned to shoot by sound and instinct when sight failed.

The first wave lasted two hours. It broke against the outer wall the way the sea breaks against a cliff -- with tremendous force, with spray and foam and the roar of impact, but ultimately without success, because the cliff does not move and the sea, for all its power, is merely water, and water cannot hold a shape.

The Buta fell back. They retreated down the slope in a disordered mass, leaving behind a carpet of dead and dying that covered the ground below the wall to a depth of three bodies in some places. The war-drums stuttered and ceased, and in the sudden silence the defenders heard other sounds -- the groaning of the wounded, the hiss of rain on hot iron, the distant rumble of thunder that was not thunder but the movement of siege engines being positioned in the valley below.

"They will come again," Raden Wijaya said, standing on the rampart, his keris dripping ichor into the rain. He was breathing hard. A cut on his left forearm bled freely, a shallow wound from a Buta spear that had passed his guard during the last minutes of the assault. He did not notice it. "And when they come again, they will bring their engines."

"Then we rest while we can," Jayabaya said. He was leaning against the parapet, his chest heaving, the ancestral sword resting on his shoulder, and in the pale grey light of the storm his face was a mask of exhaustion and exhilaration -- the particular face of a man who has discovered, after years of doubt, that he can still fight, that the body remembers what the mind has forgotten, and the discovery is both wonderful and terrible. "Rotate the watch. Tend the wounded. *Makan* while there is food."

He paused, looking out over the field of Buta dead, and the exhilaration drained from his face, leaving only the exhaustion and something else -- something that looked, in the rain-light, very much like wisdom.

"They will come again," the King said quietly, echoing his nephew. "And they will keep coming. Ten thousand does not tire. Five hundred does."

---

## III. The Long Night

The respite lasted less than an hour.

The soldiers used it with the fierce economy of men who understand that time is the only resource that cannot be replaced. They ate standing, shoveling handfuls of cold rice from communal pots, washing it down with water from the fortress cisterns. They bound their wounds -- the field *dukun* moving among the injured with the grim efficiency of healers who have already triaged the hopeless and are working on the salvageable, their hands red to the wrists, their linen bandages soaked through before they could be tied. They slept where they stood, leaning against the parapet with their weapons in their hands, catching minutes of unconsciousness that were not rest but the body's last-resort survival mechanism, the involuntary shutdown of a system pushed beyond its design limits.

Raden Wijaya did not sleep. He walked the walls.

He walked them the way a *dalang* works the screen -- touching each section, testing each joint, reading the condition of the defense with his hands and his eyes and the deep tactical instinct that was his inheritance from seven generations of warrior-kings. He spoke to soldiers. Not speeches -- he had no time for speeches, and the soldiers had no patience for them. He spoke in questions, in acknowledgments, in the small, precise exchanges that a commander uses to take the pulse of his force.

"How many arrows remain?" he asked the *lurah* commanding the eastern archers.

"Sixty per man, Raden. Perhaps less."

"Conserve them for the breach. When the wall is stormed, every arrow must find a throat."

"*Inggih.*"

"How is your arm, Senapati?" to the grizzled garrison commander, whose left arm hung in a sling stained dark with blood.

"The arm is finished, Raden. The sword is not."

"Then you command from the inner wall. I need your eyes more than your arm."

He found Tumenggung Batu sitting on a fallen Buta, using the creature's iron-shod club as a backrest, eating a rice ball with the methodical concentration of a man refueling a machine.

"How are you, old friend?" Raden Wijaya asked.

The Kurcaci looked up, and his stone-grey face held an expression that in a human would have been called contentment but in a Kurcaci was something deeper -- the deep-earth satisfaction of a being who has found the seam in the stone, the right fracture line, the point where pressure will yield the result that patience demands.

"Twenty-nine," Tumenggung Batu said.

"I was not asking about your count."

"You were not. But I am telling you anyway. Twenty-nine. The Bidadari claims twenty-two, but I saw her miss twice, and I am generous in my counting, so I give her twenty. That puts me nine ahead. Nine." He took a bite of rice. "In the morning, I will be fifty ahead, and she will owe me an apology and a good ale."

Raden Wijaya almost smiled. It was the closest thing to a smile that the situation permitted -- a softening of the jaw, a brief relaxation of the lines around the eyes, gone almost before it appeared. "Hold the western corner, Tumenggung. If the wall breaks, it will break there. The stone is oldest at that section, and the Buta know it."

"The stone is old," Tumenggung Batu agreed. "But I am older. And more stubborn." He patted the andesite beneath him with a broad palm. "The stone and I understand each other. We have an arrangement."

---

Raden Wijaya found Jayabaya in the inner courtyard, standing before the *pendopo agung*, looking up at the carved guardian figures above the bronze doors.

The King was alone. His guards had been sent to the walls. His armor was streaked with ichor and rainwater, and his hair, grey before its time from the years of enchantment, hung in wet strands across his face. He looked, in that moment, not like a king but like a man -- tired, middle-aged, carrying a weight that no crown could represent and no title could alleviate.

"Uncle," Raden Wijaya said.

Jayabaya did not turn. "I was thinking of my father," he said. His voice was quiet, stripped of the battle-rasp, the register of a man speaking to the only person before whom he could afford to be unarmored. "Prabu Airlangga. He stood on these walls once, you know. Against the Rajasa rebellion. He held them for three days with fewer men than we have now."

"I have read the chronicles," Raden Wijaya said.

"The chronicles do not tell you what it cost." Jayabaya finally turned, and his eyes were bright with something that was not tears but their precursor -- the pressure behind the dam. "They tell you he held. They do not tell you about the nights. The waiting. The sound of the drums in the dark. The feeling of -- of standing between your people and the dark, and knowing that the dark is deeper than you are, and the night is longer than your strength, and the only thing between the dark and your people is your refusal to move."

He paused. "I moved, Wijaya. I moved. Ki Surasenta whispered, and I listened, and I moved. I stepped aside and let the dark in, and my people--" His voice broke. He controlled it with visible effort, the muscles of his jaw tightening like cables. "My people suffered for it."

"You are here now, Gusti Prabu," Raden Wijaya said. "You stand on your father's wall. That is not nothing."

"It is not enough."

"It will have to be." Raden Wijaya's voice was gentle but absolute -- the voice of a man who understands that some comforts are lies and some truths are cruel but necessary. "We do not have the luxury of enough, Uncle. We have what we have. Three hundred Bayangkara. Two hundred garrison. One Kurcaci who is counting his kills like a merchant counting coins. One Bidadari who sings while she fights. And one king who has remembered how to hold a sword." He met Jayabaya's eyes. "The Dwija Putih said to hold until dawn. We hold until dawn."

"And if dawn does not come?"

"Then we hold in the dark."

Jayabaya looked at him for a long moment -- a measuring look, the look of a king taking the full measure of his heir, weighing blood and bone and will, and finding, perhaps for the first time, that the weight was sufficient. He nodded.

"We hold in the dark," the King repeated. And drew his sword. And went back to the wall.

---

The second wave came with engines of war.

The drums began again -- deeper this time, slower, a funeral cadence rather than a charge-beat, and through the rain the defenders could hear other sounds: the creak of massive timber, the rattle of iron chains, the deep, grinding rumble of wheels on stone. The Buta were bringing up their siege engines, and the engines were things of dark craft -- built in the forges of Merapi by the hands of Empu Gandring's corrupted smiths, iron and teak married in configurations that had no precedent in the defensive architecture of the archipelago because they had been designed specifically to unmake it.

Catapults came first -- massive frameworks of iron-banded timber, their throwing arms loaded with stones wrapped in pitch-soaked cloth and set ablaze. They launched in volleys, the burning projectiles arcing through the rain in parabolas of fire that painted brief, lurid arcs against the black sky before smashing into the walls with an impact that shook the ramparts to their foundations. The red andesite cracked under the bombardment. Sections of the parapet shattered, spraying the defenders with razor shards of stone that cut as cleanly as any blade.

"Take cover!" Raden Wijaya shouted as a burning stone struck the rampart three paces to his left, exploding in a shower of sparks and shattered rock. A Bayangkara who had been standing where the stone hit was simply gone -- not fallen, not wounded, but obliterated, reduced to a dark stain on the smoking stone. Raden Wijaya did not pause. He could not. The mathematics of command did not allow for pauses. "Archers, target the catapult crews! Break their rhythm!"

The archers shifted their aim. Arrows flew into the dark, seeking the Buta who worked the catapults, and some found their mark -- the catapults were large and their crews were many, and a target that cannot hide is a target that can be hit. But the Buta crews were replaced as fast as they fell, and the bombardment continued, relentless, mechanical, a percussion of fire and stone that was the anti-gamelan made manifest -- rhythm without art, impact without music, destruction without creation.

Then the battering rams reached the gate.

There were three of them -- massive constructions of iron-shod teak, each carried by fifty Buta who ran beneath wooden shields held above their heads to deflect the arrows and oil. They moved with a terrible, lurching coordination, like centipedes of iron and flesh, and when they struck the outer gate the impact transmitted through the stone of the entire wall, a vibration that the defenders felt in their bones, in their teeth, in the deep places of the chest where breath originates and fear resides.

*Boom.*

The gate shuddered.

*Boom.*

Dust cascaded from the lintel. Cracks appeared in the ancient masonry, thin as spider's silk, branching outward from the point of impact with the organic inevitability of a living thing growing.

*Boom.*

"The gate will not hold," Senapati Pandu reported, his voice flat with the professional calm of a man stating a fact. "Three more strokes. Perhaps four."

"Then we give them four strokes of something else," Raden Wijaya said. "Oil on the rams. Fire arrows on the oil."

The cauldrons were tipped. Boiling oil cascaded down the wall and onto the wooden shields above the ram crews, and the Buta screamed -- a sound like metal being torn, inhuman and unbearable -- and the fire arrows followed, and the oil ignited, and the rams became funeral pyres, columns of flame that roared upward in the rain and illuminated the scene below with a hellish, flickering clarity that revealed the full scope of what they faced.

The valley was a sea of Buta. Not ten thousand -- more. They stretched back from the wall in a density that defied counting, a living carpet of grey-green flesh and black iron that filled the valley floor and climbed the lower slopes of the surrounding mountains. Torches and watch-fires burned among them, but the fires were unnecessary; the Buta themselves seemed to generate a dull, organic heat, a warmth that rose from the massed bodies in visible waves, so that the air above the army shimmered and distorted, and the rain that fell into it evaporated before reaching the ground.

And among the Buta, directing them, their spectral forms visible in the firelight like shadows given substance, moved the Leak commanders -- tall, robed figures whose faces were hidden behind masks of black iron, whose hands made gestures of command that the Buta obeyed with the reflexive obedience of creatures whose will had been replaced by compulsion. The Leak were the Shadow King's field officers, his *hulujurit*, his war commanders, and their presence meant that the attack was not a raid or a probe but a committed assault -- the full weight of the Shadow King's western army brought to bear on a single point.

"*Duh Gusti,*" Jayabaya breathed, looking down at the army. "There are too many."

"There were always too many," Raden Wijaya said. "We knew this. The plan has not changed."

"The plan requires us to survive until dawn."

"Then we survive."

The second wave struck.

It struck with the weight of an avalanche, a tidal wall of Buta flesh that crashed against the outer wall from three directions simultaneously -- a *babah buhaya* formation, the Crocodile's Jaw, designed to envelop and crush, to divide the defense and prevent the concentration of force at any single point. The Leak commanders had learned from the first wave. They had studied the wall's weaknesses -- the section where the old stone was cracked, the corner where Tumenggung Batu stood, the eastern stretch where Dewi Anjani's arrows had been thickest -- and they directed the second wave to avoid the strong points and exploit the gaps.

Ladders went up in sections of the wall that were thinly manned. Buta crested the rampart in places where no defenders stood, and the fighting became not a line defense but a series of desperate individual actions -- soldiers turning from the parapet to face enemies who had appeared behind them, small knots of men fighting back-to-back in the rain and the dark, the organized defense dissolving into the oldest form of combat: each man for himself, each blade seeking its own survival.

Raden Wijaya felt the line waver.

He felt it the way a *dalang* feels the screen shift -- not through sight or sound but through the deep, intuitive awareness of a man whose life is bound to the pattern he holds, who knows by instinct when the rhythm breaks, when the formation falters, when the collective will of the defense begins to fragment under the pressure of fear and exhaustion and the overwhelming evidence of the senses that says: *This cannot be held.*

He acted.

"*Sira!* Bayangkara! To me! *Dharma* calls -- hold the breach! HOLD!"

He gathered twenty men -- the nearest, the steadiest, the ones whose eyes still held focus rather than the glazed despair of the breaking -- and he led them in a sortie along the inner face of the wall, a counter-attack that hit the Buta who had crested the rampart from behind and drove them back over the parapet with a ferocity that was part training, part desperation, and part the pure, incandescent fury of a prince who refuses to watch his people die without answering.

The sortie broke the Buta's foothold. The line reformed. The outer wall held.

But the cost was real. Twenty soldiers went into the sortie. Fourteen came back.

---

The rain intensified. It was no longer rain but a monsoon deluge, a vertical ocean that turned the world to water and reduced visibility to nothing. The volcanic lightning that had been playing along the horizon closed in, and now it struck the mountain itself -- great, blinding bolts of white-blue fire that split the sky and illuminated the battle in frozen instants of terrible clarity.

In one flash: the outer wall, its parapet broken in three places, its defenders fighting in the gaps with spears and swords and their bare hands.

In another: the field below, carpeted with Buta dead, and among the dead the living, climbing, always climbing, an endless, regenerating wave of grey-green flesh.

In another: Dewi Anjani on the eastern wall, her bow drawn to its full extension, her face luminous with an inner light that the rain could not dim, and the arrow leaving the string with a *twang* that was audible even above the thunder, and the arrow finding its mark in a Leak commander's iron mask, piercing the metal, and the Leak convulsing and falling from its shadow-steed, and the Buta in its immediate command stumbling, disoriented, as the compulsion that drove them flickered and failed.

In another: Tumenggung Batu at his corner, knee-deep in Buta dead, his hammer rising and falling with the metronomic regularity of a pendulum, his stone-grey face set in an expression that was beyond anger, beyond battle-joy, that had reached a place of pure, mineral concentration where the only reality was the hammer and the arc and the target and the impact. "Thirty-seven!" His voice was hoarse. "Thirty-eight!"

In another: Jayabaya, the Lion of Mataram, standing in a breach where the outer wall had crumbled, his ancestral sword in both hands, his crown lost, his armor shattered on the left side where a catapult stone had grazed him, and his mouth open in a roar that the lightning's crack swallowed but that the soldiers near him heard as clearly as if he had spoken in the silence of a temple: "*MATARAM! MATARAM! MATARAM!*"

And then the outer wall cracked.

Not a breach -- a structural failure. The ancient andesite, pounded by catapult stones and undermined by the vibration of the battering rams, gave way along a fault line that ran diagonally through the western section -- a crack that propagated through the stone with a sound like the *gong agung* struck by a giant's mallet, a sound so deep and so final that every man on the wall felt it in his diaphragm and knew, without needing to be told, what it meant.

A section of wall fifteen paces wide began to lean outward. Slowly at first -- a subtle tilting, a shift in the vertical that was more felt than seen -- and then with accelerating momentum, the massive blocks separating along the fault, the lower courses grinding against each other with a sound that was simultaneously the death of architecture and the birth of rubble.

"THE WALL! THE WALL IS FALLING!"

Raden Wijaya was already moving. "Retreat to the middle wall! RETREAT! All units fall back to the second line! NOW!"

The retreat was controlled chaos -- soldiers disengaging from combat, pulling back through the courtyards that separated the outer and middle walls, carrying their wounded, covering each other's withdrawal with rearguard actions that were small, desperate, heroic, and mostly unwitnessed. Raden Wijaya held the gap as long as he could, his keris blazing, his body blocking the flow of Buta through the collapsed section until the last of the defenders had passed the middle gate.

Then he ran.

He ran through the rain and the rubble and the chaos, and behind him the outer wall collapsed entirely, tons of ancient andesite cascading into the courtyard with a roar that silenced even the Buta drums, and the dust that rose from the collapse mixed with the rain to form a thick, red mud that coated everything -- walls, soldiers, weapons, the dead and the living alike -- in the color of the stone, the color of dried blood, the color of Candi Abang itself.

---

## IV. The Breach

The middle wall held.

It held because it was shorter, higher, and built on a natural ledge that created a killing ground below -- a steep slope of bare rock that the Buta had to cross under the full fire of the archers above. It held because the defenders, compressed into a shorter line, achieved a density of force that the outer wall had never permitted. It held because the soldiers who now stood upon it had survived the fall of the outer wall and the retreat through the courtyards, and the survival had done something to them -- had burned away the last residue of doubt, of the hope that someone else might solve this, of the comfortable fiction that reinforcements would arrive in time. What remained was the stripped, irreducible core of military resolve: the decision to stand, not because standing was rational or wise or likely to succeed, but because standing was the only action that remained, and to act was to be alive, and to be alive was to refuse.

The fighting on the middle wall was harder, closer, more intimate than the fighting on the outer wall. The Buta came up the slope in waves that broke against the stone and reformed and broke again, and each wave was closer to the crest, each wave found a foothold that was slightly higher, slightly nearer to the parapet, and the defenders fought them back -- inch by bloody inch, step by stone step, the geometry of the defense contracting around them like a fist closing.

In the corridors behind the middle wall, individual combats played out in the close quarters that favored the defenders' training over the Buta's bulk. A young Bayangkara -- barely more than a boy, his name Arya, his face too young for the blood that covered it -- held a doorway alone for seventeen minutes while the wounded were evacuated through the corridor behind him. He fought with a spear in his right hand and a *golok* in his left, and the doorway was narrow enough that the Buta could only come at him one at a time, and he killed three of them before a fourth broke his spear, and he killed two more with the *golok* before a blow to his helmet knocked him to his knees, and he was dragged backward by hands he could not see and replaced by two fresh soldiers who took up the defense of the doorway as though it were a position on a map and not a place where a boy had become a man in seventeen minutes of blood.

Dewi Anjani ran out of arrows.

It happened without ceremony -- one moment her quiver yielded a shaft, the next it was empty, and the next arrow she reached for was not there, and the absence was a small, precise silence in the midst of the roar, a silence that only she could hear because only she knew what it meant. For three hundred years she had carried a bow. For three hundred years the *twang* of the released string had been the sound of her purpose, her art, her contribution to the *dharma* of the world. And now the quiver was empty, and the bow was merely a piece of wood.

She did not pause. She did not mourn. She slung the bow across her back, and in a single fluid motion she took up a spear from the hands of a fallen soldier -- a clumsy weapon, thick and heavy, designed for hands broader than hers, for a fighting style that was brutish where hers was precise. She adjusted. She adapted. She found the weapon's balance point with the instinct of a being who has studied combat for longer than most civilizations have existed, and she entered the melee on the middle wall with the spear held low and the *Sinom* still on her lips, and if her movements with the spear lacked the transcendent grace of her archery, they compensated with a ferocity that surprised even Tumenggung Batu, who had fought beside her for weeks and thought he had seen the full range of her capabilities.

"The Bidadari has claws!" the Kurcaci shouted, genuine admiration in his voice, as Dewi Anjani drove the spear through a Buta's breastplate with a thrust that lifted the creature off its feet. "Forty-one for me, Anjani! Where are you?"

"I have stopped counting, Tumenggung," she said, and her voice was the High Language at its most serene, and the serenity was itself a weapon -- the calm at the center of the storm, the eye of the hurricane, the point of absolute stillness around which the chaos revolves. "Counting is for those who need to remember. I will remember without numbers."

"That," said Tumenggung Batu, bringing his hammer down on a Buta's shield with a blow that shattered both shield and arm, "is the most Bidadari thing anyone has ever said."

They fought back-to-back.

It was not a formation they had planned. It was not a tactic they had discussed. It happened the way the best things happen in battle -- organically, instinctively, two warriors finding each other in the chaos and locking together like puzzle pieces, each covering the other's weakness, each amplifying the other's strength. Tumenggung Batu was the anchor -- immovable, indestructible, the stone foundation upon which the defense was built. Dewi Anjani was the blade -- quick, precise, striking from behind his shield into the gaps that his hammer opened.

They were earth and air, iron and silver, the mountain and the sky, and together they held the eastern section of the middle wall against everything the Buta could bring, and the Buta brought everything, and it was not enough, and the section held, and the *Sinom* sang, and the hammer fell, and the rain poured down on all of it, impartial and relentless and unconcerned with the distinction between the living and the dead.

---

Then came the black powder.

The explosion split the night.

It came not at the wall but at the postern gate -- the small, secondary entrance on the fortress's northern face, a gate designed for sorties and supply runs, reinforced with iron but smaller than the main gate, less watched, less defended, because the military doctrine of a thousand years said that postern gates were secondary targets and could be held by a small force while the main defense concentrated on the primary assault.

The doctrine did not account for *obat bedil*.

The Buta sappers had worked through the rain and the dark, tunneling under the northern approach, carrying barrels of the Shadow King's black powder -- a mixture of saltpeter, sulfur, and charcoal that Empu Gandring had perfected in his forges on Merapi, a corruption of the ancient Chinese formula modified by dark *sakti* to achieve a thunder that no wall of stone could endure. They placed the barrels against the postern gate's foundations. They lit the fuse. They ran.

The explosion was not merely loud -- it was geological. It displaced air. It displaced stone. It displaced the fundamental assumption upon which the defense had been built: that the walls would hold. The postern gate did not break; it evaporated. One moment it was there -- iron and timber and stone, solid, ancient, real -- and the next moment it was a cloud of debris expanding outward at a speed that killed the three soldiers standing nearest to it before the sound of the explosion reached their ears.

The wave of dark thunder rolled through the fortress like a living thing, a wall of force that knocked men off their feet, shattered pottery, extinguished torches, and left behind it a ringing silence in which the only sound was the patter of falling debris -- stone chips and iron fragments and pieces of things that had been, a moment before, part of the structure of a fortress that had stood for a thousand years.

"*Duh Gusti!*" Jayabaya's voice, raw with shock, cut through the silence. "The postern! They've breached the postern!"

Raden Wijaya was already running.

He ran through the inner courtyard, past the bronze doors of the sanctuary, down the narrow stairs that led to the northern face, and what he found at the bottom stopped him for a single, terrible heartbeat.

The postern gate was gone. In its place was a hole -- a ragged, smoking wound in the fortress wall, its edges charred and fractured, its dimensions wide enough for five Buta to enter abreast. And through the hole, pouring like water through a breached dam, came the Buta.

They came with a roar that was amplified by the corridor into a sound that hit the body like a physical blow -- a concentrated blast of hatred and hunger and the mindless, compulsive violence that was the only emotion the Shadow King's soldiers knew. They poured into the inner courtyard, their tusks bared, their stone clubs raised, their orange eyes blazing in the smoke-dark, and they met the defenders of Candi Abang on the temple stairs.

The fighting on the stairs was the worst.

It was close -- so close that the combatants could smell each other's breath, the Buta's stench of rotting vegetation and corrupted flesh mixing with the defenders' sweat and blood and the copper tang of exhaustion. It was vertical -- each step a position to be held or lost, each retreat upward a concession of ground that could not be taken back, each step slick with rain and blood and the ichor of the fallen. It was personal -- no room for formations, no space for tactics, only the intimate, exhausting, step-by-step rearguard action of men who are fighting not to win but to buy time, each second purchased with a blow given and a blow received, each moment of delay another moment in which the dawn might -- *might* -- arrive.

Raden Wijaya fought on the stairs with a fury that was the concentrated essence of everything he had been, everything he had lost, everything he still hoped to be. The Keris Nagasasra was a storm in his hands -- cutting, thrusting, parrying, each movement a calligraphy of violence that wrote itself on Buta flesh in lines of smoking ichor. He fought with the discipline of a prince and the desperation of a hunted animal, and the combination produced something that was neither grace nor brutality but a third thing -- a synthesis, a fusion, the particular fighting style of a man who has accepted that he may die and has decided to make the dying expensive.

Beside him, always beside him, Jayabaya fought. The King was wounded -- a gash across his ribs where a Buta's club had found the gap in his borrowed armor -- but the wound seemed to fuel rather than diminish him, as though the pain was a kind of clarity, a reminder that the body was real and the sword was real and the stakes were real and the duty was real, and nothing else mattered. "*MATARAM!*" he shouted with every blow, and the name was a prayer and a battle cry and a declaration of identity, and the soldiers on the stairs took it up, and the name rang through the corridors of the fortress like a bell, like a *gamelan gong* struck at the moment of climax, and the sound was the sound of a kingdom refusing to die.

In the corridor behind the stairs, Tumenggung Batu performed the act for which the songs would remember him.

A section of corridor -- narrow, arched, its walls carved with the images of guardian figures whose stone faces looked down on the chaos with expressions of eternal, unhelpful serenity -- had become the only passage between the inner courtyard and the sanctuary. Through this corridor the wounded were being carried, the last reserves were moving to reinforce the stairs, the final threads of the defense were being drawn into the tight, desperate knot of the last stand. And through this corridor the Buta were trying to come.

Tumenggung Batu filled the corridor.

He planted his iron-shod boots on the stone floor, spread his arms to touch both walls, and brought his hammer to the ready position -- head down, shaft angled, the stance of a Kurcaci berserker preparing to receive a charge. His stone-grey body, broad and dense and built to the proportions of a being designed to bear the weight of mountains, blocked the corridor as effectively as a wall of iron. The Buta could not get past him. They could not get around him. They could only come at him, one or two at a time through the narrow arch, and the hammer was waiting.

"*Mulah!*" the Kurcaci roared, and the word was not Javanese but something older, something that came from the deep places where the Kurcaci language still carried the weight of the first words spoken in the first caverns when the world was young. "None shall pass! My hammer says NO!"

They came. They came, and they came, and they came. And the hammer rose and fell, rose and fell, and each impact was a sentence, a statement, a declaration in the language of iron and bone that needed no translation: *This far. No further. The mountain does not move.*

He held the corridor alone for forty minutes. When they finally pulled him back -- Raden Wijaya and two Bayangkara physically hauling the Kurcaci away from his position, his boots scraping furrows in the stone floor because he would not lift his feet, would not yield even the smallest fraction of ground voluntarily -- the corridor was choked with Buta dead, stacked three deep, the walls painted with ichor to the height of a man's shoulder, and Tumenggung Batu's hammer was so caked with blood and bone and fragments of iron that it looked less like a weapon than a geological formation -- a stalagmite of violence, a mineral deposit laid down layer by layer by the patient, relentless application of force.

"Forty-nine," the Kurcaci said, his voice a rasp, his arms shaking. "Count them. Forty-nine. The Bidadari owes me a *barrel*."

---

The retreat to the sanctuary was the hardest thing Raden Wijaya had ever done.

Not because of the fighting -- though the fighting was terrible, a running battle through corridors and up staircases and across courtyards that were slippery with rain and blood and the debris of a fortress coming apart around them. Not because of the fear -- though the fear was a living thing, a pressure in the chest, a taste of copper on the tongue, a voice in the back of the mind that whispered *this is how it ends, this is how it ends*.

It was hard because retreat was a concession. Each step backward was a step away from a position that men had died to defend, a position whose loss meant that those deaths had purchased nothing, that the blood spent on the outer wall and the middle wall and the stairs had been currency expended for a return of zero. Retreat meant that the plan was failing. Retreat meant that the dawn was still hours away and the fortress was almost gone and the Horse Lords of Kediri were a hope, not a certainty, and hope is a fragile thing to build a defense on when the walls are falling and the enemy is in the corridor.

But Raden Wijaya retreated, because retreat was the plan, and the plan was all they had.

The bronze doors of the sanctuary closed with a sound that resonated through the stone of the mountain -- a deep, final, *gong agung* note that vibrated in the bones and the walls and the air itself, a sound that said: *This is the last door. There is nothing behind this.*

Seventy soldiers stood in the sanctuary. Seventy, from the five hundred who had manned the walls at sunset. The arithmetic was a wound that would not stop bleeding.

The bronze doors were three spans thick. They were embossed with scenes from the *Bharatayudha*. They had been designed to hold against siege engines and battering rams and the concentrated malice of any army that might breach the outer defenses. But they had not been designed to hold against *obat bedil*, and the question that hung in the thick, incense-heavy air of the sanctuary -- the question that no one asked because asking it would be the same as answering it -- was whether the Buta had enough black powder for a second breach.

The hammering began.

Not battering rams this time -- fists. Buta fists, stone clubs, iron-shod maces, beating against the bronze in a rhythm that was the dark parody of the *gamelan gong*, each impact a note in a composition whose only theme was: *We are here. We are coming. You cannot stop us.*

Jayabaya leaned against a pillar. His chest was heaving. The wound at his ribs had opened, and the blood soaked through his armor and dripped onto the stone floor in a slow, steady rhythm that matched the hammering on the doors. He looked at the carved guardian figures above him, and his face held the expression of a man who has come to the end of his resources and is looking for one more -- one more breath, one more reason, one more fragment of will to set against the overwhelming evidence that will is not enough.

"It is over," the King said. His voice was quiet -- not defeated, not despairing, but exhausted beyond the capacity for either defeat or despair, a voice that had traveled past emotion into the flat, grey territory of fact. "We are trapped. The dawn will find only our bones."

The soldiers in the sanctuary heard him, and they did not argue, and the silence that followed his words was a silence of agreement -- not vocal, not conscious, but the deep, involuntary assent of bodies and minds that had been pushed beyond their limits and were ready, not to surrender, but to stop. Just to stop. To lay down the weight and the weapon and the duty and the fear and simply, mercifully, stop.

Raden Wijaya walked to the narrow window that looked out over the eastern valley.

The rain was easing. Not stopping -- the monsoon would not stop for hours yet -- but easing, the solid curtain of water thinning to a heavy drizzle, the visibility improving from nothing to very little, the world beyond the window resolving from total blackness into shades of grey and dark grey and the dull, bruised purple of a sky that was not yet dawn but was no longer the deepest night.

*The sun rises,* he thought. *But who will see it?*

He pressed his hand against the cold stone of the window frame. The Keris Nagasasra hummed at his hip, its light dimmed but not extinguished, its sacred iron still warm, still alive, still refusing the darkness with the same stubborn, irreducible persistence that had characterized its forging a thousand years ago, when the first *empu* had taken iron from the mountain's heart and shaped it into a blade that would not bend, would not break, would not accept that the dark was stronger than the light.

He thought of Kian Santang. Somewhere in the east, beyond the mountains, beyond the strait, a small Tuyul was climbing a volcano with a cursed blade on his back, and the entire purpose of this battle -- this fortress, these walls, these dead -- was to buy time for that climb. To draw the Shadow King's eye westward while the real stroke fell in the east. To be loud while the keris-bearer was silent. To die, if necessary, so that the quest might live.

*Hold until dawn,* the Dwija Putih had said. *Hold until the light breaks in the east.*

Raden Wijaya looked at the sky. The purple was lightening. Not much. A shade. A suggestion. The faintest, most tenuous intimation that somewhere beyond the storm and the smoke and the volcanic haze, the sun existed, and the sun was rising, and the darkness could not prevent it -- could delay it, could obscure it, could make the world forget it, but could not prevent it, because the sun does not rise by permission, and dawn does not require darkness's consent.

He turned from the window.

"It is not over," he said.

His voice was quiet. It was not the voice of the *Krama Inggil*, not the battle-roar, not the commander's bark. It was the voice of a man speaking to seventy exhausted, bleeding, terrified people in a room that smelled of bronze and blood and the last fading traces of ancient incense, and the voice said what needed to be said with the only currency that remained -- the currency of absolute, unreasonable, unjustifiable conviction.

"It is not over. The dawn is coming. I can see it. Not the sun -- not yet -- but the shape of the light behind the clouds, the color of the sky above the mountains, the first faint line of grey along the eastern ridge. The dawn is coming, and the Dwija Putih rides with it, and the Horse Lords ride with him, and they are coming *here*, to *this* wall, to *this* door, to *us*."

He drew the Keris Nagasasra. Its light flared -- not the blinding pulse of the Dwija Putih's staff, not the transcendent brilliance of the Bidadari, but a warm, steady glow, the light of sacred iron remembering its purpose, the light of a blade that had been carried through darkness by men who refused to lay it down.

"We hold," Raden Wijaya said. "We hold until the dawn comes through those doors instead of the dark. And when the dawn comes, we ride out. We ride out and we meet it. And the enemy will learn -- will learn at last -- that the men of Mataram do not die quietly in the dark. We die in the light, with our swords drawn and our faces to the east and the name of our kingdom on our lips."

He raised the keris.

"Who holds with me?"

The silence lasted one heartbeat. Two.

Then Jayabaya pushed himself off the pillar, took up his sword, and stood beside his nephew. The blood from his wound dripped onto the stone, and he did not notice, and it did not matter.

"*Inggih,*" the King said. "Mataram holds."

Tumenggung Batu planted his hammer on the stone and leaned on it, and his stone-grey face held an expression that was purely Kurcaci -- the expression of a being who has heard a speech and found it acceptable, not because of its eloquence but because of its stubbornness, which is the only eloquence the Kurcaci recognize.

"Fifty," he said. "When those doors open, I start at fifty."

Dewi Anjani raised her borrowed spear, and the *Sinom* rose from her lips one final time, and the silver thread of her voice wound through the sanctuary like starlight through shadow, and the soldiers who heard it stood straighter, and the hammering on the doors became -- for a moment, just a moment -- less loud than the singing.

"*Inggih,*" the soldiers said. First one, then three, then all of them, the word spreading through the sanctuary like fire through dry grass, seventy voices joining in a single syllable that was affirmation and defiance and prayer and battle cry, and the word rang against the bronze doors and the stone walls and the carved guardian figures and the mountain itself, and the mountain heard it, and the mountain held.

"*Inggih. Inggih. INGGIH.*"

## V. The Dawn Charge

The sky cracked.

Not with lightning -- with light.

It began as a thin line along the eastern ridge, so faint that only Raden Wijaya, standing at the narrow window with his hand on the cold stone, could see it -- a seam of grey in the black fabric of the night, a fracture in the darkness that widened, imperceptibly at first and then with gathering momentum, as though the darkness were a shell and the light within it was hatching.

The grey became pewter. The pewter became silver. The silver became the particular, luminous, rain-washed gold that occurs only in the moments before sunrise when the light is traveling through the full depth of the atmosphere and arrives at the eye filtered through a thousand miles of air and cloud and the accumulated moisture of the monsoon, so that the gold is not pure but complex, layered, containing within it traces of amber and rose and the palest, most delicate blue, a gold that is not metallic but organic, the gold of mangoes and turmeric and the *batik cap* dyes that the women of Mataram press into cloth with wax and patience.

"The dawn," Raden Wijaya breathed.

And then he heard it.

Faintly at first -- so faintly that he thought it was the wind, or the rain, or the last echo of Dewi Anjani's singing reverberating off the stone walls of the sanctuary. But the sound grew, climbing through the registers of audibility the way a river climbs through the registers of a watershed -- from trickle to stream to torrent to flood -- and it was not the wind, and it was not the rain, and it was not the echo of a song.

It was a horn.

A single, clear note, winding through the valleys of Mataram like a river of liquid bronze, rising above the storm and the battle and the hammering on the bronze doors, rising above the Buta drums and the Leak commanders' shrieks and the groaning of the wounded and the silence of the dead. It was the sound of the *terompet* -- the great war-horn of Kediri, forged from the horn of a *banteng* bull and bound with rings of silver, carried by the first rider of the *éored*, the herald whose single function was to announce the coming of the Horse Lords to those who waited for salvation and those who should have feared it.

The note held. It sustained itself for a span of time that should not have been possible from a single breath in a single horn -- a note that seemed to draw its strength not from the lungs of the herald who blew it but from the land itself, from the mountains and the valleys and the ancient *bayu* that flowed through the stone and the soil, a note that was the voice of the land saying: *I have not forgotten. I am here. I am coming.*

"Look!" Raden Wijaya shouted, pointing through the narrow window, and his voice broke on the word, broke the way a dam breaks -- not in weakness but in release, the release of hours of pressure and fear and the terrible, disciplined refusal to hope, all of it breaking at once in a flood that was not despair but its opposite, the thing that despair had been holding back, the thing that the night had tried to kill and could not.

On the crest of the eastern hill, the sun broke through the storm clouds.

It did not rise -- it *erupted*. A shaft of golden light drove through the grey ceiling of cloud like a spear thrown by the hand of the divine, and the light struck the ridge with an impact that was almost physical, a detonation of color that turned the rain-dark stone to fire and the wet grass to gold and the drops of rain that fell through the shaft to tiny, tumbling jewels that flashed and sparkled and were gone.

And in that shaft of light, silhouetted against the gold, mounted on horses whose coats shone like hammered copper, their bronze armor blazing, their spears a forest of steel points catching the sun and throwing it back in a thousand burning fragments -- they appeared.

The Horse Lords of Kediri.

Three thousand strong.

They lined the ridge from end to end, a wall of horse and rider and iron and light that stretched across the full width of the valley's eastern boundary. Their horses were the *kuda turangga* of the eastern ranges -- tall, lean, bred for speed and endurance on the open savanna, their manes braided with bells that chimed in the wind, their hooves stamping the wet stone with a percussion that was the anti-drum, the answer to the Buta war-beat, a rhythm that said not *We are more* but *We are here, and we are enough.*

At their center, mounted on a white stallion whose mane and tail streamed in the wind like banners of silver, rode Kanjeng Tumenggung Arya Kamandanu. The Balapati of Kediri was armored in burnished bronze and silver filigree, his *kuluk* gleaming white against the golden sky, his *tombak* spear held vertically in his right hand, its point catching the first light of the sun and burning there, a single star of fire at the apex of the formation.

And beside him -- riding at his right hand, his white robes blazing with a light that was not the sun's light but his own, his staff held aloft and burning with the golden fire of the *geni suci* -- rode the Dwija Putih.

The White Sage had kept his word.

The horn sounded again -- two notes this time, the signal to form. And the Horse Lords moved.

They shifted on the ridge with the fluid precision of a single organism, three thousand riders recomposing themselves from a static line into the *Garudabihwa* -- the Eagle formation, the most devastating cavalry charge configuration in the military tradition of Nusantara. The formation was shaped like the outstretched wings of the garuda, its center point -- the beak -- narrow and sharp, composed of the heaviest cavalry, the most experienced riders, the hardest steel. The wings spread backward and outward, each rider positioned to follow the rider ahead, so that the formation would strike the enemy not as a line but as a wedge, penetrating deep into the mass and then expanding, spreading, tearing the formation apart from within like a blade opening a wound.

Arya Kamandanu raised his spear. The gesture was unhurried, deliberate, the gesture of a man who has fought a hundred engagements and understands that the moment before the charge is as important as the charge itself -- that the breath before the blow determines the blow's force, that the silence before the war-cry determines the war-cry's reach.

His voice, when it came, was not loud. It did not need to be. It was the voice of a general speaking to soldiers who knew what was required and needed only the word.

"*Garudabihwa... MAJU!*"

*Eagle formation... ADVANCE!*

The charge began.

It began slowly -- the walk, the trot, the controlled acceleration of three thousand horses moving from rest to motion with the coordinated power of a river breaking through a dam. The ridge trembled. The earth trembled. The rain that fell on the slope broke into patterns of spray beneath the hooves, and the spray caught the golden light and became a mist of fire that surrounded the advancing cavalry like a luminous cloak.

The trot became a canter. The canter became a gallop. And the gallop became something that had no name in the vocabulary of normal military operations -- a thundering, earth-shaking, geological-scale event, the full weight of three thousand horses and three thousand riders and three thousand spears and the accumulated *semangat* of a warrior culture that had bred and trained and lived for this moment for generations, all of it concentrated into a single point of maximum impact, aimed like a spear at the flank of the Buta army.

"*MATARAM! NUSANTARA! DHAARRMAAAA!*"

The battle cry rolled down the mountain like an avalanche. It struck the Buta army before the horses did, and the sound alone -- the sheer, overwhelming, primal force of three thousand human voices raised in a single shout of fury and purpose -- caused the nearest Buta to flinch, to hesitate, to turn their dull orange eyes toward the ridge and see, for the first time, what was coming at them.

What was coming at them was the mountain itself.

The impact was not a collision. It was a tectonic event. It was the meeting of two geological forces -- the irresistible momentum of the Kediri cavalry striking the immovable mass of the Buta horde -- and the result was what happens when irresistible meets immovable: something breaks. The Buta line did not bend. It did not flex. It *shattered*. The garuda's beak -- fifty riders abreast, the heaviest cavalry, their spears leveled, their horses running at full extension -- drove into the flank of the Buta army like a chisel into rotten wood, and the wood split, and the split propagated outward from the point of impact in fracture lines that ran through the Buta formation like cracks through ice, and the formation came apart, and the pieces milled and crashed and trampled each other in a chaos that was the opposite of the organized, drum-driven assault that had hammered Candi Abang through the night.

The Dwija Putih rode at the center of the charge, and his staff was a torch, and the torch was the sun brought down to earth. Where the golden light fell, the Buta screamed -- not the battle-scream, not the rage-scream, but the scream of corruption exposed to purity, the scream of shadow forced into light, the scream of creatures that existed by the sufferance of darkness discovering that the darkness could not protect them from this.

The Leak commanders tried to rally. They rode their shadow-steeds among the panicking Buta, their iron-masked faces turning toward the cavalry, their hands weaving gestures of compulsion and control. But the Horse Lords of Kediri were not Buta -- they could not be compelled, could not be controlled by gesture or shadow or the distant will of a dark master. They were free men on fast horses with sharp spears and the *semangat* of their ancestors burning in their blood, and they rode through the Leak's shadow-craft the way sunlight rides through fog -- illuminating it, dispersing it, revealing it for what it was: smoke, illusion, the absence of substance masquerading as force.

---

Inside the sanctuary, Raden Wijaya heard the charge.

He heard it through the stone -- the vibration of three thousand hooves transmitted through the bedrock of the mountain, a trembling that was felt in the floor and the walls and the pillars and the carved guardian figures whose stone faces seemed, in the trembling light, to shift from expressions of eternal vigilance to expressions of something else -- something that might have been, had stone been capable of emotion, satisfaction.

He heard the horn again. And again. The two-note signal, the three-note signal, the five-note fanfare that meant: *We are here. We are in the enemy's flank. We are winning.*

And the hammering on the bronze doors stopped.

The silence was so sudden, so absolute, so total that it hit the defenders with the force of a physical blow -- the cessation of a sound that had become so constant, so integrated into the texture of their reality, that its absence was itself a shock, a void, a hole in the world where the beating of ten thousand fists on bronze had been.

Raden Wijaya walked to the doors.

He placed both hands flat on the bronze surface, feeling the metal -- warm from the pounding, vibrating faintly with the echoes of the charge outside, alive beneath his palms with the ancient stories embossed into its surface, the *Bharatayudha* warriors who had been fighting their own war in relief for a thousand years and who now seemed to lean forward from the bronze, eager, intent, ready.

He looked back at the seventy soldiers behind him. At Jayabaya, who stood with his sword drawn and his blood dripping and his eyes blazing. At Tumenggung Batu, who had set his hammer to the ready position and was muttering "Fifty, fifty, let it be at least fifty" under his breath. At Dewi Anjani, who held her borrowed spear and whose golden eyes caught the thin light from the window and reflected it back as flame. At the Bayangkara and the garrison and the farmers and the wounded who stood beside them, seventy souls who had survived the night and were standing, standing, standing, because they had been asked to stand and they had said *inggih* and the word had meant what it said.

He flung open the bronze doors.

The morning poured in.

Golden light, rain-washed and storm-cleaned, flooding through the doorway and filling the sanctuary with a brilliance that made the torches dim and the bronze glow and the carved guardian figures cast long shadows across the stone floor. And with the light came the sound -- the horn, the hooves, the battle-cry, the oceanic roar of the Kediri cavalry sweeping through the Buta army like a fire through dry grass.

"RIDE OUT!" Raden Wijaya shouted, and his voice was the *Krama Inggil* in its highest, purest, most absolute register, the register that kings use when the moment demands not a command but a destiny. "Ride out and meet them! *Sira -- dharma -- semangat -- MAJU!* WITH ME!"

He went through the doors first. Always first. The prince of Mataram, the heir of Hayam Wuruk, running down the temple stairs that he had retreated up in the dark, running through the courtyard where the Buta dead lay in heaps, running toward the breach in the wall where the morning light streamed through the smoke and the rubble, and the Keris Nagasasra was a blazing torch in his hand, its sacred iron catching the dawn and burning with a light that was not the reflection of the sun but the blade's own fire, released at last, set free by the coming of the day it had been made to herald.

The defenders of Candi Abang poured from the fortress.

They came like water from a burst dam -- surging through the doors, down the stairs, across the courtyards, through the breaches in the walls, out into the morning with a fury that was the compressed energy of an entire night of fear and fighting and the bitter, grinding endurance of men who had been told to hold and had held and had been rewarded, at last, with the thing they had been holding for.

Jayabaya rode beside his nephew. Horses had been found -- abandoned Buta mounts, ugly creatures, but fast, and the King of Mataram did not discriminate between mounts when the moment called for speed. He rode with the ancestral sword raised above his head, and the rain streamed from the blade and from his hair and from his face, and from his face the streaks were not all rain, and the laughter that erupted from his chest was not the laughter of joy alone but of release -- the release of years of enchantment and months of guilt and hours of battle and the final, overwhelming relief of a man who has been tested and not found wanting.

"*MATARAM!*" the King roared, laughing, weeping, riding into the golden light. "*MATARAM! MATARAM JAYA! MATARAM RISES!*"

Tumenggung Batu charged through the breach in the outer wall, his war-hammer describing arcs of destruction that added numbers to his count at a rate that he could no longer track, which annoyed him intensely. "Fifty-one! Fifty-three! Did I miss fifty-two? I missed fifty-two!" He crashed into a knot of Buta who were trying to form a defensive line and scattered them like a *gong agung* scatters silence. "Fifty-seven! Or fifty-eight! The math has become unreliable!"

Dewi Anjani emerged from the fortress with the *Sinom* on her lips and a borrowed spear in her hands, and the morning light caught her silver hair and made it a banner, a flag, a signal of something that the Buta could not name and could not face -- the luminous, terrible beauty of a being who has fought through the night and is still standing, still singing, still bright.

The Buta army was caught between the hammer and the anvil.

Behind them -- the Kediri cavalry, sweeping down the slope in the *Garudabihwa*, their charge unspent, their momentum unstoppable, their spears reaping a harvest that turned the valley into a charnel field. Before them -- the defenders of Candi Abang, erupting from the fortress with the concentrated fury of an army that has been compressed beyond endurance and is now released, expanding, exploding outward with a force that is proportional to the compression.

They broke.

The Buta army broke. Not in the organized, tactical retreat of a force withdrawing to regroup, but in the total, catastrophic collapse of a formation that has lost its center -- the Leak commanders dead or fleeing, the drum-beat silenced, the compulsion that had held ten thousand Buta in a single fighting body severed by the Dwija Putih's light and the cavalry's charge and the pure, incandescent defiance of the men who had held the red stone through the night.

They fled. They fled into the valleys, into the mountain passes, into the rain and the smoke and the darkness from which they had come. They dropped their weapons. They abandoned their engines. They trampled each other in the narrow defiles, a stampede of grey-green flesh that the cavalry pursued and the light drove and the morning consumed, and the torches that had filled the valley like a sea of fire were extinguished, one by one, until the valley held only the light of the sun and the rain and the red stone of Candi Abang, standing, standing, still standing, against the golden sky.

## VI. Victory and Aftermath

The siege was lifted. The Red Temple stood.

The morning matured from gold to the pale, clean blue of a sky washed by monsoon rain and stripped of the volcanic haze that had hung over Giri Mataram for weeks. The storm had passed. The clouds were breaking, and through the gaps the sun fell in shafts of light that struck the red andesite of the fortress and made it glow -- not the dull, dark red of the night, but a warm, living red, the red of embers and garnets and the *batik* dyes of Mataram, the red of a thing that has been tested by fire and has not been consumed.

The valley below the walls was a horror that the morning light rendered in pitiless detail. The Buta dead lay in their thousands -- grey-green heaps of corrupted flesh that were already beginning to decompose in the tropical heat, their forms losing definition, melting back into the earth from which they had been shaped. The siege engines stood abandoned among the dead -- catapults with their arms cocked and loaded, battering rams wedged in the mud, ladders leaning drunkenly against the walls they had failed to conquer. The rain washed over all of it with a cold, indiscriminate cleansing, carrying the blood and the ichor downhill in thin red streams that joined and merged and flowed into the river at the valley's floor, turning its water dark.

On the slope below the eastern wall, the Kediri cavalry was reforming. The *Garudabihwa* had disintegrated during the pursuit -- individual riders and small groups had chased the fleeing Buta deep into the mountain passes, and now they were returning, their horses lathered and blowing, their spears dark with ichor, their faces holding the particular expression of warriors who have won a decisive engagement and are processing the transition from combat to its aftermath, from the simplicity of fighting to the complexity of what comes after.

Arya Kamandanu rode through the shattered outer gate of Candi Abang and dismounted in the inner courtyard. He was a large man, broad-shouldered and lean, with the sun-darkened skin and wind-carved features of a rider who has spent his life on the eastern plains. His bronze armor was dented and streaked with Buta ichor, and a cut above his left eye had been hastily bound with a strip of cloth that was already soaked through. He moved with the controlled economy of a professional soldier, his dark eyes taking in the damage -- the collapsed walls, the pitted stone, the blood on the stairs, the bodies being carried from the corridors -- and his expression was not triumph but assessment, the face of a man cataloguing costs.

"Kanjeng Tumenggung," Raden Wijaya greeted him, the *Krama Inggil* formal and precise, the register of allies meeting on a field of shared blood. "Mataram owes Kediri a debt that cannot be measured in rice or gold."

Arya Kamandanu clasped Raden Wijaya's forearm in the grip of the *éored* -- a warrior's greeting, hand to forearm, pressure matching pressure, the physical compact of men who have fought on the same side and will fight again.

"There is no debt between brothers, Raden," the Tumenggung said. His voice was deep, measured, the voice of a commander who speaks precisely because imprecision costs lives. "The Dwija Putih came to us at dawn, riding through the night, his light a beacon that the *éored* followed through passes that should have been impassable in the dark. He said: *Candi Abang holds, but not for long. Ride, or there will be nothing left to ride for.* We rode."

"Three thousand spears," Raden Wijaya said. "Through the night, through the storm. I will not forget it, Tumenggung."

"You will remember it by standing with us when the time comes," Arya Kamandanu said. "And the time will come. This--" he gestured at the valley, at the Buta dead, at the ruined walls "--this is not the end. This is a skirmish. The Shadow King has armies beyond counting. What we broke today was his vanguard. The main force is still at Merapi."

---

They counted the dead in the late morning, when the sun was high enough to illuminate the deepest corners of the fortress and the darkest hollows of the killing ground below the walls.

The count took hours. It was performed with the methodical, unsentimental efficiency of soldiers who understand that the dead must be enumerated not as a ritual of grief but as a function of command -- to know who remains, to know what is lost, to adjust the arithmetic of the next battle. Senapati Pandu supervised the count, his broken arm in its sling, his one functional hand making marks on a slate with the careful strokes of a man who understands that each mark represents a life ended and an absence created.

Three hundred and twelve defenders had died. Of the five hundred who had stood on the walls at sunset, one hundred and eighty-eight remained. The Bayangkara had lost one hundred and forty of their three hundred. The garrison had lost a hundred and twenty of their two hundred. The militia -- farmers and merchants and craftsmen who had taken up weapons because they lived in the fortress's shadow and had nowhere else to go -- had lost fifty-two of their seventy.

The Kediri cavalry had lost thirty-nine riders in the charge and the pursuit.

Tumenggung Batu sat on a fallen column in the outer courtyard and looked at the count on the slate, and his stone-grey face held no expression at all, which was the Kurcaci expression for grief so deep that it bypassed the surface entirely and went straight to the foundations.

"Three hundred and twelve," he said. "And seven thousand Buta." He looked at his hammer, which lay across his knees, its iron head scarred and pitted with the record of the night. "The ratio is adequate. The cost is not."

Dewi Anjani stood beside him, her hand resting on his broad shoulder, and the gesture was not comfort -- the Kurcaci did not accept comfort -- but acknowledgment, the touch of a being who understood that grief comes in different forms and that the form the Kurcaci expressed -- the deep-earth tremor, the foundation-shake, the invisible earthquake of the heart -- was as real and as valid as tears.

"They died in the *dharma*," she said. Her voice was the High Language at its most gentle, and the gentleness was not weakness but the particular strength of those who have seen enough death to know that gentleness is sometimes the bravest response. "They stood. They held. The *karuhun* will know their names."

"The *karuhun* had better," Tumenggung Batu said, his voice rough. "Because I intend to recite every one of them when I return to the stone halls, and if the ancestors are not listening, I will shout until the mountain shakes."

---

The reunion of the defenders and the Horse Lords took place in the inner courtyard, among the rubble and the bodies and the slow, persistent rain that would not entirely stop. It was not a celebration. It was too soon for celebration, too close to the killing, too near to the dead who lay in rows along the corridor walls with their faces covered by their cloaks. But it was something -- a coming together, a recognition, the physical fact of allies standing in the same space and knowing that they were alive and that the other was alive and that the combination of these two facts constituted, if not victory, then at least survival, and survival was the precondition for everything else.

The Dwija Putih stood at the center of the courtyard, his white robes unstained -- not because he had not fought but because the light he carried seemed to repel the mud and the blood and the debris, as though purity were a physical property that functioned like a shield. His golden eyes moved across the scene -- the soldiers, the wounded, the dead, the ruined walls, the shattered gate -- and what they held was not sorrow and not satisfaction but the deeper thing that encompassed both: the comprehension of cost, the understanding that every victory is purchased and the price is always specific, always personal, always paid by someone whose name will be remembered only by those who loved them.

He turned to Raden Wijaya and Jayabaya, who stood together in the rain, uncle and nephew, king and heir, two men whose relationship had been forged in a single night of iron and blood into something that no court intrigue could undo.

"You have held," the Dwija Putih said, and the two words were sufficient -- not praise, which would have been patronizing, not congratulation, which would have been premature, but acknowledgment, the simple recognition of a fact that had been in doubt and was now settled. "The walls have fallen, but the fortress stands. The stones are broken, but the *semangat* holds. This is the meaning of *gotong royong* -- not the help of the convenient or the alliance of the comfortable, but the unity that forms when everything else has failed, the cooperation that emerges from the last extremity, when Manusa and Bidadari and Kurcaci and the Horse Lords of the east stand in the same rain and refuse the same darkness."

He paused, and his golden eyes went distant -- seeing, perhaps, beyond the walls and the valley and the mountains, seeing the larger pattern, the wider war, the threads that connected this battle to a hundred other battles being fought and waiting to be fought across the breadth of Nusantara.

"But hear me," he said, and his voice sharpened, and the golden light at the tip of his staff intensified, a reminder that the Dwija Putih was not merely a sage but a force -- the embodiment of the sacred fire, the living will of the *geni suci*, a being who had passed through death and returned with the authority of one who has seen the architecture of the world and knows where the stress points lie. "This is but one battle. The Shadow King has lost a vanguard. He has not lost his war. His main armies are at Merapi, his forges are burning, his Buta breed in the darkness faster than the light can reach them. Candi Abang was a test. He has tested us, and we have answered, and the answer has bought us time -- but time only. The true war is at the mountain. The true stroke must fall at Krakatau, where the keris was forged and where it must be unmade."

A messenger arrived as the Dwija Putih spoke -- a Kediri scout, his horse lathered and trembling, his face tight with the expression of a man carrying news that cannot wait.

"Kanjeng Tumenggung!" the scout addressed Arya Kamandanu, saluting. "News from the southern border. The forest -- the Wanantara -- it is *moving*. The trees are marching. They march on Merapi. The Sanghyang Wana have gone to war."

A murmur passed through the assembled warriors -- surprise, wonder, the uncertain awe of men confronting something that belongs to legend rather than report. The Sanghyang Wana -- the forest spirits, the ancient guardians of the wood, who had slept for centuries while the world of men and shadow contended above their roots -- had awakened. The march that the Dwija Putih had prophesied, the march that Cepot and Dawala had witnessed in the deep forest, had begun.

"The *Sabha Wana* marches," Dewi Anjani said, and her golden eyes blazed. "Mbah Jangkung leads them. The forest reclaims what was taken."

Raden Wijaya looked east. Beyond the mountains, beyond the valleys, beyond the haze and the smoke and the distance, the land of the Shadow King waited -- dark, volcanic, burning with the fires that the keris had been forged in and must return to. Somewhere in that darkness, Kian Santang climbed. Somewhere in that darkness, Bagus walked beside him. Somewhere in that darkness, the smallest and the bravest carried the heaviest burden, and the fate of every soul standing in this rain-soaked courtyard depended on two Tuyul who were not warriors, not kings, not sages, but simply friends who had promised to see a thing through and were keeping the promise.

*Hold on, Kian,* Raden Wijaya thought. *Hold on. We are coming.*

He did not know if the thought could cross the distance. He did not know if the *bayu* carried more than wind. But he sent it anyway -- a pulse of *semangat*, of will, of the fierce, protective love that a prince feels for the smallest and the bravest of his people -- and the wind carried it east, and the rain carried it east, and perhaps it arrived, and perhaps it did not, but the sending was its own purpose.

Jayabaya stood beside him, looking at the same horizon, and the King's face held a new expression -- not the gaunt despair of the enchanted years, not the wild exhilaration of the night's fighting, but something quieter, steadier, more durable. It was the face of a king who has found his footing.

"The alliance holds," Jayabaya said. His voice was the voice of a proclamation, spoken not to Raden Wijaya alone but to the courtyard, to the soldiers, to the dead, to the mountains and the rain and the listening earth. "Mataram and Kediri. The Horse Lords and the Bayangkara. The Kurcaci and the Bidadari. The Sanghyang Wana in the forest. The Tuyul on the mountain." He drew the ancestral sword and held it before him, its blade catching the rain and the light. "The Shadow King sows division. He whispers that we are separate, that our differences are walls, that the distance between us is a gulf that cannot be crossed. He lies. *Gotong royong.* Mutual aid. Cooperation. The old principle. The first principle. The principle that built these walls and held them through the night."

He looked at Raden Wijaya. "Mataram rises, nephew. The alliance holds. And when the time comes to march east -- to Krakatau, to the mountain of fire, to the place where this war ends -- Mataram will march. *Inggih.*"

"*Inggih,*" Raden Wijaya said.

"*Inggih,*" Arya Kamandanu said, his deep voice joining the word.

"*Inggih,*" Tumenggung Batu said, his hammer rising from his knees.

"*Inggih,*" Dewi Anjani said, and her voice was the *Sinom* one last time, the pupuh of beauty and youth, sung not as a battle hymn but as a benediction, a blessing over the living and the dead.

"*INGGIH,*" the soldiers said -- all of them, Mataram and Kediri, Bayangkara and *éored*, the wounded and the whole, three hundred voices rising from the rain-soaked courtyard of a battered fortress on a mountain in the heart of Nusantara, and the word was a chord, a *gong agung*, a sound that struck the air and the stone and the water and the *bayu* and sent ripples outward in every direction, east and west and north and south, ripples that carried the word to the mountains and the forests and the rivers and the sea, ripples that said: *We are here. We hold. The dawn came, and we were still standing. And we will stand again.*

The Dwija Putih leaned upon his staff, and his golden eyes turned south -- toward Merapi, toward the smoke and the marching forest and the ironworks that the Sanghyang Wana had risen to destroy. "I must go to them," he said quietly, to Raden Wijaya alone. "The forest needs a witness. And there are wards upon Gandring's tower that only sacred fire can break." He clasped the prince's arm -- a brief, fierce grip, the grip of a sage who has learned that farewells are a luxury the war does not afford. Then he mounted the white horse and rode south into the morning, his staff blazing gold against the grey mountain road, and within minutes, the light of him was gone.

The rain eased. The sun strengthened. The red stone of Candi Abang, battered and broken and streaked with the marks of a night that had tried to destroy it, glowed in the morning light like a heart beating, like a fire banked but not extinguished, like the promise of a kingdom that had been given up for dead and was, against all evidence and all expectation, alive.
