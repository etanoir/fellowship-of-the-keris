# PERSEKUTUAN KERIS
## (The Fellowship of the Keris)

### A Novel

---

*In the age when the great temple of Barabudur rose stone by stone toward heaven,*
*when the kingdoms of Nusantara shone like scattered jewels across the emerald seas,*
*there existed a darkness older than memory—*
*and a blade forged to bind them all.*

---

## Prologue: Sang Dalang (The Puppeteer)

Listen, children of the archipelago. Sit close to the flickering flame of the *blencong* oil lamp, and let its light cast shadows upon the white screen of the world. For I am the *dalang*, the puppeteer, the keeper of stories that stretch back to the first dawn when Sang Hyang Widhi breathed life into the islands of Nusantara, scattering them across the warm seas like offerings of flowers upon sacred water.

This is no ordinary tale. It is not a story of princes and their courtly dances, though princes you shall meet. It is not merely a chronicle of kingdoms rising and falling like the tides of the Laut Jawa, though kingdoms shall shake before its end. No — this is the story of a keris. A single blade, slender as a serpent's tongue, beautiful as sin, terrible as the fire that sleeps beneath Gunung Krakatau.

Every keris, as the *empu* smiths know, possesses a soul. A *tangguh* — a spiritual character written in the folds of its metal, in the sinuous patterns of its *pamor*. A keris may protect its bearer or destroy him. A keris may bring fortune to a kingdom or ruin to an empire. But the Keris Murka — the Keris of Wrath — was forged with a purpose beyond all others. It was made to dominate. To corrupt. To enslave every throne from the highlands of Pasundan to the distant shores of Kutai.

It was forged by one who had no name that mortal tongues should speak, though the old stories call him Raja Iblis — the Shadow King — who ruled from a throne of obsidian in the molten heart of Krakatau, where the earth bleeds fire into the sea. He poured his malice into the blade, seven *pamor* patterns coiled into the metal, each one a chain meant to bind the will of kings. And he forged nine lesser keris, nine shadow blades, and gave them to nine great rulers whom he had seduced with promises of empire. Those nine became the Leak — the shape-shifting terrors of the night, neither living nor dead, bound forever to the Shadow King's will.

For an age, the darkness spread. The Buta — those monstrous children of spite and shadow — poured from the volcanic wastes in endless hordes. Kingdoms fell. The great forests burned. The seas ran red.

But there came a time, an age of alliance, when the free peoples of Nusantara rose together. The Bidadari of the mountain sanctuaries, immortal and luminous, lent their ancient wisdom. The Kurcaci of Gunung Sewu brought blades and armor forged in the deep fires of the earth. The Tuyul of the Pasundan highlands, small in stature but fierce in heart, stood upon the field of battle alongside the great warriors of Mataram and Sriwijaya.

And the Shadow King was thrown down. His body was shattered. His spirit retreated into the deepest caldera of Krakatau, a thing of smoke and rage and endless hunger.

But the Keris Murka was not destroyed.

It was lost — carried from the battlefield, hidden, forgotten. It passed from hand to hand across the centuries, leaving ruin in its wake, until at last it came to rest in darkness, deep in the limestone labyrinth of Gunung Sewu, where it waited. Patient as stone. Hungry as fire.

Until it was found.

But that, little ones, is where our story truly begins. Adjust your seats upon the tikar mat. Let me pour you *wedang jahe*, warm ginger brew, for the night is long and the tale longer still. The gamelan is tuning — can you hear it? The *bonang* chimes ring out, and the *kendang* drum sets the rhythm of fate.

I shall tell it in the old way, in the manner of the *kidung* — that ancient lyric form, older than the candi temples, older than the courts of kings, whose measured verses carry the weight of all the sorrows and triumphs the archipelago has ever known. For some stories are too vast for common speech. They must be sung, or they shatter in the telling.

*Om Awighnam Astu* — may there be no obstacles. Let the tale begin.

The *wayang* screen is lit.

The shadows are ready to dance.
