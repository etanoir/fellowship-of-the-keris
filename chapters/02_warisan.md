# Bab 2: Warisan (The Inheritance)

---

The morning came slowly to Lembur Padalarang, as though the sun itself had drunk too deeply of the tuak palm wine the night before and now rose with reluctance, bleary-eyed and amber, over the eastern ridgeline. Mist lay upon the sawah in long pale scarves, and the terraced fields, still heavy with the stubble of the harvest, glowed like beaten copper in the early light. Somewhere below the village, a water buffalo lowed. A rooster answered from a bamboo pen. Then another, and another, until the whole hillside rang with their competing declarations of dawn, each bird insisting with the absolute conviction of the ignorant that it alone had summoned the day.

Kian Santang woke with the taste of ketupat rice cakes still lingering on his tongue and the distant thrum of the previous night's gamelan echoing in his bones like the aftershock of a dream. The Seren Taun festival had been magnificent — the finest anyone could remember, the elders had agreed, nodding their heads over cups of *bandrek* warm ginger brew, the finest in forty years. The wayang kulit performance had stretched past midnight, the dalang's voice weaving stories of ancient Pajajaran until children slept curled at their mothers' feet and the oil in the blencong lamp burned low. There had been laughter and rice wine and the deep satisfaction of a good harvest gathered safely in.

But Kian Santang had not slept well.

He lay in his hammock for a long moment, staring at the woven bamboo ceiling of his modest rumah panggung, and tried to name the unease that sat upon his chest like a stone. It was the memory of Uncle Kian's face during the wayang performance — that expression he had caught in the shifting light of the oil lamp. Uncle Kian had been watching the shadow play with an intensity that went beyond enjoyment, his fingers curled around something beneath his selendang shawl, his lips moving in silent conversation with no one visible. And beside him, Eyang Guru Batara — that towering, ancient figure who appeared in Padalarang only when the winds of trouble blew — had leaned close, his white beard brushing Uncle Kian's shoulder, and whispered words that made the old Tuyul flinch as though stung.

Kian Santang rose, splashed water on his face from the clay jambangan beside his door, and set out for Uncle Kian's house.

---

Uncle Kian's rumah panggung stood at the upper edge of the lembur, where the village gave way to the wild slopes of the highland and the morning fog gathered thickest. It was a fine house — finer than any Tuyul of Padalarang had a right to own, the gossips said — raised on sturdy ironwood stilts, its roof of layered palm thatch sweeping upward in the elegant horn-shaped silhouette of Sundanese tradition. Uncle Kian had built it with wealth he'd brought back from his decades of wandering, and its beranda was wide enough to seat twenty, its carved wooden railings dark with age and the oil of countless hands.

Kian Santang found Eyang Guru Batara there, sitting alone on the beranda in the pearl-grey light.

The old sage looked as though he had not moved all night. He sat cross-legged on a tikar mat, his travelling staff — that gnarled length of waringin wood, polished by years of use — resting across his knees. In one hand he held a long bamboo pipe, and from its bowl rose the thin, fragrant thread of kretek smoke, clove and tobacco mingling in a scent that was at once sweet and bitter, familiar and strange. The smoke rose straight in the windless air, a pale column that seemed to hold up the low ceiling of cloud above. Eyang Guru's eyes, deep-set beneath brows like ledges of grey stone, were fixed on some distant point beyond the mist, beyond the terraced fields, beyond perhaps the visible world itself — gazing, it seemed, into the Triloka, the three realms of the Sundanese cosmos: the upper world of the gods, the middle world of the living, and the lower world where the ancestors waited.

He did not look at Kian Santang as the young Tuyul climbed the wooden steps to the beranda, but he spoke.

"You felt it too," he said. It was not a question. "Last night, during the wayang. You saw his face."

Kian Santang sat down on the edge of the beranda, his feet dangling above the packed earth below. "I saw enough to worry me. Eyang, what is wrong with Uncle Kian? He has been... different, these last months. Secretive. He talks to himself when he thinks no one is listening. He keeps touching something beneath his shawl, something wrapped in batik cloth. The other villagers have noticed. They whisper about him."

The old sage drew on his pipe, and the kretek crackled faintly, a sound like tiny bones breaking. The clove-scented smoke poured from his nostrils in twin streams, blue-grey against the white mist. He said nothing for a long time.

Then: "He will tell you himself. He has been preparing for this morning for some time, though I do not think he knew it until last night."

Before Kian Santang could ask what that meant, the inner door slid open and Uncle Kian appeared.

---

The change in him was visible at once, and it was a change that Kian Santang felt in his stomach before his mind could articulate it. Uncle Kian had always been the most vital of old Tuyul — small and wiry, as all their kind were, but possessed of a restless energy that belied his centuries of age. His eyes had always been bright, quick, shrewd, full of stories and mischief. He was the uncle who had sailed beyond the known seas, who had walked among the Kurcaci deep-dwellers and bartered with the Bidadari of the mountain sanctuaries, who had returned with treasures and tales that made the children of Padalarang gather at his feet like iron filings drawn to a magnet.

But this morning Uncle Kian looked hollowed. His cheeks were drawn, his skin papery and dull. His eyes darted from shadow to shadow as though he expected something to lunge at him from the corners of his own house. His hands would not be still — they plucked at his sarong, tugged at his ear, folded and refolded against his chest. He looked, Kian Santang thought with a chill, like a man who had been slowly devoured from the inside, as termites devour the heartwood of a teak pillar until only the painted shell remains.

"Sit, sit," Uncle Kian said, waving a hand toward the beranda. His voice was thinner than Kian Santang remembered. "I have something to tell you. Something I should have told you long ago, perhaps. But the words — the words were difficult. They did not want to be spoken. It would not let me speak them."

He paused, and his hand went involuntarily to his chest, pressing against the folds of his shirt. Eyang Guru's eyes followed the movement with the careful attention of a healer watching a wound.

"Uncle," Kian Santang said gently. "What is it?"

Uncle Kian lowered himself to the tikar mat with the slow, careful movements of the truly old. He sat facing his nephew, and for a moment his face contorted in a struggle that seemed almost physical — as though something inside him resisted the act of speech, fought against the surrender of what it possessed. Then his shoulders dropped, and he let out a breath that seemed to carry decades of weight.

"I am leaving, Kian. I am going away."

The words hung in the close air of the beranda. Somewhere in the valley, a coel bird sang its descending three-note call.

"Leaving?" Kian Santang repeated. "Leaving Padalarang? But Uncle, you — you came home. You said your travelling days were done. You said this lembur was where your bones would rest."

"I said many things." Uncle Kian's fingers worked at the knotted cord around his neck, from which hung a small brass key. "I said them because I believed them. Because I wanted to believe them. But I have been holding onto something, Kian. For decades now. Holding it close, keeping it secret, keeping it safe — or so I told myself. But safe for whom?" His laugh was brittle, a dry leaf crackling underfoot. "It has been eating at my shadow. *Menggerogoti bayangannya.* Do you understand? Not my body — my shadow. The part of me that makes me who I am. My *atma* — my true self, the soul within the soul. And now I must let it go, or there will be nothing left of me at all."

He reached into his shirt and withdrew a bundle wrapped in batik cloth. Kian Santang had seen it before — glimpsed it in Uncle Kian's hands late at night, caught the edge of its outline beneath his selendang — but he had never seen it so close, had never been invited to see it at all. The batik was old, its indigo and soga-brown patterns faded but still beautiful, the familiar parang motif of interlocking diagonal blades that Javanese women had stamped in wax and dye for centuries beyond counting.

Uncle Kian's hands trembled. He looked at Eyang Guru, who gave a single, heavy nod.

Then he unwrapped the keris.

---

Kian Santang would later try to describe that first unveiling to others and find that language failed him, the way language fails before the face of the sacred or the truly terrible. The batik cloth fell away fold by fold, and what lay within was a keris — but to call it merely a keris was to call the sea merely water, to call fire merely light.

It was slender, no longer than a man's forearm, and its blade carried seven sinuous curves — seven luk, the sacred number, each wave flowing into the next with the liquid grace of a river winding through lowland jungle. The metal itself was unlike any Kian Santang had seen in the forges of Padalarang's smiths. The pamor pattern — that marriage of iron and nickel folded and hammered and folded again by the hands of some impossibly skilled empu — shifted and writhed in the grey morning light, forming shapes that seemed to move when looked at directly: serpents coiling, flames licking, faces pressing outward from within the metal as though trapped there, mouths open in silent screams or silent songs, it was impossible to tell which. The patterns did not merely decorate the blade; they lived upon it, restless and hungry, rearranging themselves in configurations that the eye insisted were impossible but could not look away from.

The hilt was carved from ebony so dark it seemed to drink the light — a naga, the great serpent of the deep waters, its body coiled in tight spirals, its head rearing back with jaws slightly parted, tiny fangs of ivory protruding from the black wood. Its eyes were rubies. Small, perfectly cut, red as arterial blood, and they caught the faint morning light and held it, and for one vertiginous moment Kian Santang felt that they were not reflecting light but generating it — that they were not gems but eyes, actual eyes, and that something behind them was looking back at him with an intelligence as old and patient as stone. The naga and the garuda — serpent below, eagle above — the ancient duality of the Nusantara cosmos, water and fire, earth and sky. But here only the naga spoke, and it spoke of depths where no garuda dared to fly.

The warangka — the sheath — was carved from sandalwood so ancient its scent had deepened beyond sweetness into something richer, something almost animal, like the musk of a creature that had never known sunlight. Dark as old honey, carved with floral motifs so intricate they seemed to grow from the wood's surface like living vines, branching and curling in patterns that echoed the pamor of the blade itself, as though sheath and blade were two halves of a single living organism, separated but yearning to be joined.

And beneath all this beauty — woven into it, inseparable from it — there was something else. Something that Kian Santang felt not with his eyes but with the hairs on his forearms, with the base of his spine, with the ancient animal part of his brain that had kept his ancestors alive in the dark forests before the first rice was planted. It was a pull. A gravity. As though the keris occupied a deeper place in the world than the objects around it, as though it sat at the bottom of an invisible well and everything else — the beranda, the mist, the morning, Kian Santang himself — was slowly sliding toward it.

The air in the beranda thickened. The kretek smoke from Eyang Guru's pipe, which had been rising in a straight column, began to bend, curling toward the exposed blade in a slow, sinuous spiral, as though drawn by a breath.

And at the very edge of hearing — so faint it might have been imagination, might have been the blood singing in his own ears — Kian Santang heard a whisper. Not words, not quite. A sound like wind through temple ruins, like water running through deep caves, like a voice calling a name from very far away, in a language older than the hills.

"Cover it," Eyang Guru said sharply. His voice cut through the air like a blade of its own, and Uncle Kian, who had been staring at the keris with an expression of mingled love and horror, flinched and drew the batik cloth back over the blade with the quick, guilty movements of a man caught in an act of transgression.

The pull vanished. The air thinned. The smoke rose straight again.

Kian Santang realized he had been holding his breath, and released it.

"What," he said, and his voice came out cracked and dry, "is that?"

---

Uncle Kian told his story in the halting, circuitous manner of one who has kept a secret so long that the act of speaking it aloud feels like tearing a bandage from a wound that never healed.

Decades ago — he would not say how many, and his eyes skittered away when pressed — he had ventured into the limestone caves of Gunung Sewu, that vast karst labyrinth in the southern mountains where a thousand hills rise from the earth like the vertebrae of a buried dragon. He had been younger then, bold and foolish and hungry for the kind of wonders that could not be found in the ordered world above. He had gone deeper than any Tuyul should go, deeper than the river caves where the Kurcaci dwarves mined their iron, deeper into passages where the darkness was so absolute it had weight and texture, where the silence was so profound he could hear his own heartbeat echoing off walls he could not see.

And there, in a chamber deep enough that the stone sweated with the earth's own fever, he had found it.

The keris lay on a stone altar — a rough slab of limestone, uncarved but worn smooth by water or by hands. Around its base were the remnants of ancient *sesajen* — shattered clay bowls, the dust of flowers long returned to earth, a *lingga-yoni* of black stone half-buried in the calcified floor, its masculine pillar and feminine basin worn but unmistakable, a marker from an age when someone had worshipped here, or guarded here, or kept vigil over the blade in the manner of a priest performing an endless *pradaksina*, circling the sacred object in devotion and dread. There was nothing else in the chamber. No bones, no indication of who had placed it there or how long ago. Only the keris, resting on the stone as though it had been waiting. As though it had been waiting specifically for him.

"I should have left it," Uncle Kian said. His voice had dropped to a murmur, and his eyes had gone distant, seeing not the beranda and the mist but the memory of that black chamber, that altar, that terrible and beautiful blade. "I knew it even then. Something in me said *jangan* — don't. Turn around. Climb back to the light. The *karuhun* — the ancestor spirits — I could feel them pressing against me, cold hands on my shoulders, whispering *pamali, pamali* — forbidden, forbidden. But there was another voice, louder, closer, and it said *ambil* — take it. It is yours. It has always been yours."

He had taken it. He had carried it out of the caves and into the sunlight, and from that day forward his life had changed.

The keris had brought him luck — luck of the uncanny kind, the kind that made other men uneasy. He could move unseen when he wished it, slipping through crowded markets and guarded gates as though he were no more substantial than a shadow. He could sense danger before it arrived, a prickling at the nape of his neck that never failed. Doors that should have been locked opened at his touch. Enemies who pursued him lost his trail as though the forest itself had conspired to hide him.

But there was a price.

"It changes you," Uncle Kian said, and now he looked directly at Kian Santang, and in his eyes the young Tuyul saw something that frightened him more than the keris itself — a desperate, weary honesty, the confession of a man who has finally stopped lying to himself. "Slowly. So slowly you do not notice at first. You become possessive. Secretive. You begin to believe that the world wants to take it from you, that everyone around you is a potential thief. You clutch it close and you tell yourself you are keeping it safe, but the truth is that it is keeping you. You hear whispers when you sleep. You see shadows at the corners of your vision — shapes that should not be there, shapes that vanish when you turn your head. You begin to forget things. Not memories, not facts — pieces of yourself. Your laughter. Your trust. Your ability to sit in the sunlight without feeling that something is watching you from a place where sunlight has never reached."

He paused. His hands, empty now of the batik bundle, opened and closed on his knees as though grasping for something that was no longer there.

"It is no ordinary keris, Kian. I do not know what empu forged it, or when, or for what purpose. But it is ancient. It is powerful. And it is — " He searched for the word. "It is *murka*. Wrathful. It carries a wrath inside it that is not mine and not yours and not any mortal creature's. It is a wrath that wants something. That is reaching for something. And I cannot carry it any longer."

He picked up the batik-wrapped bundle and held it out toward Kian Santang. His arms shook with the effort — not the physical weight, which was slight, but some other resistance, as though an invisible hand gripped the bundle and fought to keep it close.

"Take it," Uncle Kian whispered. "I do not fully understand why, but something tells me you are meant to carry it. Perhaps Eyang Guru understands better than I do. But take it, before I change my mind. Before it changes my mind for me."

Kian Santang looked at Eyang Guru. The old sage's face was unreadable, carved in shadow, but he gave a nod so slight it might have been a trick of the mist.

Kian Santang took the keris.

It was lighter than he expected. And warmer.

---

Uncle Kian left within the hour.

He had packed already — a small rattan pack, a change of clothes, a bamboo water tube, a pouch of dried rice and salted fish. The efficiency of it struck Kian Santang with a pang: Uncle Kian had been preparing for this departure for days, perhaps weeks, gathering his courage the way a man standing on a cliff edge gathers his will before the leap. He was going to the Dieng Plateau, he said — that ancient highland where the Hindu temples stood like the fossilized prayers of a forgotten age, where the sulfurous mists rose from the volcanic earth and the air was thin and cold and clean. A place where the *bayu* — the breath of the divine — still moved through the stones. A place of peace for weary travellers. A place where shadows had no purchase.

At the edge of the lembur, as the morning light touched the peaks of the distant mountains, Uncle Kian paused and turned. He pressed his palms together and bowed low. '*Nembah, Eyang*,' he said — *I bow to you, Grandfather* — switching to the refined register of respect, the *basa lemes* that the Tuyul reserved for moments of deep gratitude. '*Ingwang seja nyuhunkeun widi*' — *I respectfully request your permission to go*.

Eyang Guru placed his broad hand atop the old Tuyul's head in the gesture of a *Sinom* blessing — that gesture of the wise to the departing — and murmured: '*Om Namo Narayana. Pinareng lumaku rahayu, dinarmaan ku Sang Hyang Widhi.*' *May your journey be blessed, gifted grace by the Divine.*

Kian Santang walked with him to the edge of the lembur, where the footpath wound down through the bamboo groves toward the river valley below. They stood together in the morning light, and Kian Santang was struck by how small Uncle Kian looked — how fragile, how ordinary. Without the keris, the old Tuyul seemed diminished, as though some inner architecture had been removed and the remaining structure was settling, finding a new and lesser equilibrium. But his eyes, for the first time in months, were clear. The frantic, darting quality was gone, replaced by a tired calm that was, in its way, more heartbreaking than the agitation had been.

"Do not think of me with pity, boy," Uncle Kian said, as though reading the thought. He managed a smile — thin, but real. "I have had a long life and an interesting one. More interesting than any Tuyul of Padalarang has a right to expect. I have seen the coral palaces of the sea folk. I have drunk starlight wine with the Bidadari. I have held in my hands a thing of terrible power, and I have had the sense, at last, to let it go." He adjusted the straps of his pack. "That is more than most creatures can say."

He turned and walked away down the path. Kian Santang watched him go — watched the small figure grow smaller as it descended, watched it pass through bands of mist and shadow and sunlight, until at last it rounded a bend in the bamboo grove and was gone. The morning swallowed him up as though he had never been.

Kian Santang stood there for a long time, listening to the silence that Uncle Kian left behind.

When he returned to the beranda, Eyang Guru was still sitting where he had been, though his pipe had gone out. The old sage's face was grave — graver than Kian Santang had ever seen it, and he had known Eyang Guru Batara since childhood, had sat at the ancient empu's feet and listened to stories of the old wars and the old alliances, had seen the sage angry and amused and sorrowful but never this. This expression was something else entirely. This was the face of a man who has looked upon a map and found that the territory it describes is far more dangerous than he had feared.

"Eyang," Kian Santang said. "Tell me what it is. Tell me everything."

"I cannot tell you everything," the old sage replied. "Because I do not yet know everything. But I will tell you what you must know now, and the rest will come when I return."

He rose to his feet with a slowness that spoke of great age and great weariness, and he crossed to where the batik-wrapped keris lay on the tikar mat. He looked down at it, and Kian Santang saw something flicker across the sage's ancient features — not fear, exactly, but a recognition, the way a man might look at a serpent he has encountered before and knows to be venomous.

"Do not unsheathe it," Eyang Guru said. His voice was quiet but carried the weight of an absolute command. "Do not gaze upon the pamor by moonlight — the patterns are alive in ways that mortal eyes were not meant to follow, and moonlight feeds them. And above all, do not let it touch your bare skin for too long. A moment, perhaps, you can endure. But prolonged contact will begin to... erode the boundaries between you and it. The keris has a will of its own, and that will is not benign."

"But what is it?" Kian Santang pressed. "Uncle Kian called it Keris Murka. Is it — is it the one from the old stories? The one the dalang speaks of? The blade forged in the fires of Krakatau?"

Eyang Guru did not answer directly. He picked up the batik bundle with both hands, handling it with a deliberate care that suggested not reverence but caution, the way one might carry a vessel of poison. He wrapped the cloth more tightly around the blade, tucking the edges with precise, practiced movements.

"I must go away," he said. "There are old texts — lontar manuscripts in places that few remember. I must consult them. I must speak with those who remember what I have begun to suspect." He placed the wrapped keris in Kian Santang's hands. "Keep it hidden. Tell no one — not your friends, not the village elders, no one. Put it in a chest of jati wood, if you have one. Teak resists corruption; it is one of the few woods that does. And wait for me. I will return."

Together they placed the keris in a chest of old teak that sat in the corner of Uncle Kian's main room — a chest that Uncle Kian had brought back from his travels, carved with simple geometric patterns, its brass lock green with age. The batik-wrapped blade settled into the chest's interior as though into a bed prepared for it, and when Eyang Guru closed the lid and turned the brass key, Kian Santang felt something ease in the air — not vanish, but diminish, the way a sound diminishes when a door is closed between the listener and its source.

Eyang Guru handed him the key. "Carry this with you. Do not leave it where others might find it."

Then the old sage took up his staff, slung his worn leather satchel over his shoulder, and descended the beranda steps. He paused at the bottom and looked back up at Kian Santang, and for a moment his expression softened — the grave strategist giving way, briefly, to the old teacher who had once shown a small Tuyul boy how to read the stars.

Eyang Guru began a *Sinom* — the pupuh of wisdom, its melody measured and contemplative like water finding its level:

*Welas asih nu sajati / moal laas ku jaman / nu leungit tinangtu kapanggih / lumaku ma rahayu / ...*
(*True compassion / will not fade with the ages / what is lost is surely destined to be found / the journey shall be blessed / ...*)

His voice was rough with age but steady with conviction, and the ancient verse hung in the morning air like smoke from a *homa* fire, its meaning settling into Kian Santang's bones.

"Be careful, Kian Santang," he said. "Be watchful. And be brave, though you do not yet know what you must be brave against."

Then he, too, was gone — striding down the path with that deceptive speed of his, his staff striking the packed earth in a steady rhythm, his tall figure dissolving into the mist as though the world were a wayang screen and he a shadow being drawn behind it.

---

Kian Santang carried the teak chest back to his own house.

He set it in the corner of his sleeping room, beneath the window where the breeze carried the scent of jasmine from the garden, and he tried to go about his day. He swept his beranda. He fed his chickens. He walked down to the river to wash his clothes, and he exchanged greetings with his neighbors, who were still warm and sleepy from the festival, still trading stories about the wayang performance, still savoring the afterglow of celebration. Everything was normal. Everything was as it had always been.

But the keris was in his house.

He could feel it the way you feel the presence of another person in a dark room — not through any specific sense, but through the absence of solitude, the subtle pressure of being observed. It was like a second heartbeat in the house — faint, persistent, slightly wrong in its tempo. It was like a voice calling from deep underwater — no words he could distinguish, no meaning he could parse, but a presence, an intention, a beckoning that tugged at the edges of his attention the way the moon tugs at the tide.

He went about his day, and he tried not to think about it, and he failed.

That night, as the lembur settled into its customary quiet — the last cooking fires banked, the last murmured conversations fading, the dogs curling beneath the houses, the frogs beginning their evening chorus in the sawah — Kian Santang lay in his hammock and stared at the teak chest and could not sleep.

The chest sat in the corner, solid and still, its brass fittings faintly visible in the moonlight that filtered through the bamboo shutters. It looked ordinary. It looked like what it was — an old teak chest, handsome but unremarkable, the kind of thing one might find in any prosperous household in the highlands. There was nothing about it to suggest that it contained anything more significant than folded sarongs or bags of dried spice.

But Kian Santang could feel it breathing.

Not literally. Not a sound he could have recorded or a movement he could have measured. But a rhythm, a tide, a slow expansion and contraction that seemed to radiate from the chest in waves, like the ripples from a stone dropped into still water. It pressed against his awareness in a way that was not quite uncomfortable but was deeply, fundamentally wrong — the sensation of something that should be inanimate behaving as though it were alive.

He closed his eyes, and the keris followed him into sleep.

---

The dream came like a wave — sudden, total, and inescapable.

He was standing on black sand. Beneath his bare feet the sand was warm, almost hot, and it was not sand at all but ash — volcanic ash, fine as powder, stretching in every direction to a horizon that flickered with distant red light. The sky above was the color of a bruise, purple-black and heavy, choked with smoke that moved in vast slow spirals as though stirred by an unseen hand. There were no stars. There was no moon. The only light came from below the horizon — a sullen, pulsing glow, the color of embers, the color of blood held up to firelight.

He walked. He did not choose to walk; his feet carried him forward across the ash plain as though pulled by a cord tied to his ribs. The air smelled of sulfur and heated metal, and with each step the glow on the horizon grew brighter, and the ground beneath him grew warmer, and the ash became cinders became shards of black glass that cut his feet without pain, as though pain had been removed from the dream like a color drained from a painting.

And then he saw it.

Rising from the ash plain like a fist thrust upward from the earth — an island, or a mountain, or both. Krakatau. He knew it without being told, the way one knows things in dreams, with a certainty that precedes evidence. The great volcano rose in a column of black stone, its slopes scarred and fissured, rivers of molten rock crawling down its flanks like luminous veins in the body of a dying god. At its summit, a crown of fire and smoke churned against the poisoned sky, and from its caldera rose a tower — no, a throne — no, a structure that was both and neither, a thing of obsidian and shadow that seemed to grow from the mountain's heart as naturally as a stalagmite grows from a cave floor, as though the earth itself had decided to build a seat for something terrible.

Armies moved on the plains below. Buta — the monstrous children of shadow, broad as oxen, their skin the grey-green of swamp water, their tusks curving upward from jutting jaws — marched in columns so vast they seemed to flow across the landscape like a tide of flesh and iron. They carried spears of black wood and shields of beaten bronze, and their feet churned the ash into clouds that rose and mingled with the smoke above until earth and sky became one choking darkness. Among them moved other shapes — taller, thinner, wraithlike, mounted on creatures that were not horses but something else, something that moved with the jerking, wrong-jointed gait of insects scaled to the size of cattle. The Leak. The nine shadow kings, their forms shifting and uncertain, their faces hidden beneath hoods of black silk, and from within those hoods, nine pairs of eyes burned with a cold fire that was not light but the opposite of light, a radiance that devoured rather than illuminated.

And above them all — above the armies, above the Leak, above the mountain and its throne of obsidian and its rivers of molten stone — there were the eyes.

Two eyes. Red as the heart of a forge. Ancient as the basalt bones of the earth. They hung in the churning sky like twin suns in a universe where light itself had curdled into malice, and they looked down upon the marching armies with a satisfaction that was worse than hunger, worse than rage — the satisfaction of a will that had learned to be patient across millennia, that had endured defeat and dissolution and the slow crawl of centuries in the dark, and now felt the tide turning, felt the scattered pieces of its power beginning to converge, felt the keris — *his* keris, the keris in the teak chest in the corner of a small house in a highland lembur impossibly far from this place of ash and fire — felt it, and reached for it, and smiled.

The eyes found him.

They turned — those vast, burning, ancient eyes — and they looked directly at Kian Santang, and in that look was a recognition that stopped his heart. *I see you*, the eyes said, without words, without sound, with nothing but the pure, unmediated pressure of a will so vast it bent reality around it like a stone bends a river. *I see you, and I know what you carry, and I am coming.*

Kian Santang screamed, but the ash plain swallowed his voice, and the eyes burned, and the armies marched, and the mountain of fire churned against the bruised sky, and the dream held him, held him, held him —

He woke.

---

He was drenched in sweat. His hammock swung gently, set in motion by the violence of his waking, and for a long moment he lay there, gasping, staring at the ceiling of his room, waiting for the walls to reassert their reality, waiting for the dream to release its grip on his senses.

The teak chest sat in the corner of the room. Moonlight touched its brass fittings, and they gleamed dully, and Kian Santang would have sworn — would have staked his life upon it, though no one would have believed him — that the chest was warmer than it should have been. Not to the touch — he did not touch it, would not touch it, not now, not in the small dark hours with the sweat still cooling on his skin and the ember-red afterimage of those eyes still floating in his vision. But warm in the way that a presence is warm, the way that a living body radiates heat that can be felt across a room.

The keris was awake. Or rather, the keris had never been asleep. It had been waiting, patient as stone, patient as fire, and now it had spoken to him in the only language it knew — the language of visions, of dread, of a future written in ash and flame.

Kian Santang did not sleep again that night.

---

Dawn came to the Pasundan highlands the way it always did — gently, generously, a slow suffusion of gold and rose that began at the eastern ridgeline and spread across the sky like dye bleeding through wet silk. The mist rose from the sawah in long, luminous columns, caught by the first light and transformed into pillars of pale fire. Roosters sang. Somewhere in the lembur, a woman began pounding rice in a wooden mortar, the steady thud-thud-thud as rhythmic and reliable as a heartbeat. A child laughed. A dog barked. The bamboo wind chimes on a neighbor's beranda rang in a passing breeze, a sound like small bells, like scattered coins, like the laughter of spirits in the brightening air.

Kian Santang sat on his beranda with a cup of *wedang jahe* that had grown cold in his hands, and watched the world wake up around him. The sawah spread below the village in green and gold terraces, the young rice shoots standing in their flooded paddies like rows of tiny soldiers, impossibly green against the dark water. A white heron picked its way along a bund between fields, moving with the slow deliberation of a priest performing a ritual. Beyond the terraces, the forested slopes of the highlands rose in wave after blue-green wave toward the volcanic peaks that crowned the horizon, their summits hidden in cloud, as they always were, as they had been since before the first Tuyul settled this valley and planted the first rice.

It was the same view he had woken to every morning of his life. The same sounds, the same smells — woodsmoke and jasmine and wet earth and the faint, sweet rot of fallen fruit in the orchards. The same golden, peaceful, eternal morning, repeated without variation since the beginning of memory, the heartbeat of a world that did not know it was about to change.

But Kian Santang knew.

He knew it in his bones, in the cold, settled certainty that had taken up residence somewhere beneath his ribs and would not be dislodged. He knew it the way the animals know before an earthquake, the way the birds fall silent before a storm — not through reason, not through evidence, but through some faculty older and more reliable than either, the body's ancient ability to feel the tremor before the ground begins to shake. The *karma* of the keris had begun its slow, terrible unwinding, and he could feel the threads of consequence pulling taut around his life like the strands of a web around a fly that has only just realized it is caught.

He turned and looked through the open door at the teak chest sitting in the corner of his room. It sat there, solid and dark and still, its brass lock glinting faintly in the morning light. Ordinary. Unremarkable. Containing within it a slender blade wrapped in faded batik cloth that was, he now understood with a certainty that tasted like iron on his tongue, the most dangerous object in all the islands of Nusantara.

Kian Santang looked at the chest, and the chest — or what lay within it — looked back.

And he was afraid.
