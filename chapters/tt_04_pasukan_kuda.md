# Bab 4: Pasukan Kuda Kediri

## (The Horsemen of Kediri)

---

*Gunungan ditancepkeun dina tengah layar, miring ka katuhu.*
*Wanda anyar geus mitembeyan: Wanda Sembada.*
*Hawa panas hampos, lebu ngebul di tegalan lega.*
*Angin taneuh ngelun ti tegalan, mawa bau jukut garing jeung kuda.*
*Di langit, manuk elang ngapung nguriling, neangan mangsa.*
*Tapi di handap, nu leumpang aya tilu: nu gagah, nu suci, jeung nu pondok.*
*Maranehna leumpang ka wetan, ka tanah nu geus poho ka rajana.*

*The Gunungan is set at the center of the screen, tilted to the right.*
*A new act begins: The Act of Readiness.*
*The air is thin and hot, dust rises over the vast plains.*
*An earth-wind blows from the savanna, carrying the smell of dry grass and horses.*
*In the sky, a hawk circles, searching for prey.*
*But below, there walk three: the valiant, the sacred, and the stout.*
*They walk eastward, toward a land that has forgotten its king.*

## A. The Savanna and the Hunt

Tegalan alang-alang di wates Kediri warnana siga emas nu geus kabeuleum ku poe.

The savanna of the Kediri borders was the color of gold tempered by an unforgiving sun. Here, at the eastern rim of the world they had known, the dense, emerald canopy of the Wanantara forest frayed and then surrendered -- thinning first into scattered groves of stunted *damar* and flame-tree, then into isolated sentinels of bleached teak standing alone like wayward monks who had wandered too far from their monastery, and finally into nothing but the vast, undulating expanse of *alang-alang* grass that stretched from the foothills of the central volcanoes to the pale, shimmering blur where earth and sky dissolved into a single trembling line. It was a sea -- not of water but of silver-tipped stalks, each blade as tall as a man's waist, each crowned with a feathery seed-head that caught the wind and released its cargo in slow, drifting clouds of white that resembled, from a distance, the souls of the dead rising from a battlefield no one remembered.

The grass rippled and hissed in the dry wind like a thousand escaping serpents, and the sound was ceaseless, layered, hypnotic -- the base note of the savanna's gamelan, over which the cicadas laid their grinding drone, a metallic tremolo that vibrated in the teeth and behind the eyes, a sound so persistent and so total that the mind eventually ceased to hear it as noise and began to process it as silence, as the fundamental frequency of this burned and beautiful place. Above, the sky was a hard, brilliant turquoise, stripped of clouds, vibrating with heat-shimmer that made the distant volcanic peaks -- Kelud to the south, Arjuno to the east, and the dark, perpetual smear of Merapi's plume on the western horizon -- seem to float above the earth, untethered, as though even the mountains sought to escape the land's slow dying.

The air was thick with the scent of parched earth, wild jasmine, and the sharp, ozone tang of distant volcanic lightning -- that peculiar smell that lived at the intersection of fire and atmosphere, where the breath of the deep earth met the indifference of the sky. Here and there, a patch of wild jasmine clung to a termite mound or a dry streambed, its white blossoms incongruously fragrant in the dust, exhaling their sweetness as if in defiance of the desolation, as if beauty were a form of resistance against the encroaching dark. Butterflies -- iridescent blue, the color of *batik* dye before it sets -- gathered at the jasmine in trembling clusters, and their wings caught the light and threw it back as fragments of sapphire that flickered and vanished like the memories of a kingdom that once was.

Raden Wijaya moved through the tall grass with the rhythmic, effortless stride of a man who had forgotten how to tire. His batik sarong, the *parang rusak* pattern now stained with the grey ash of the strait and the dark loam of the forest, whipped against his legs as he parted the stalks with his forearms, leaving a wake of bent and broken grass behind him like the trail of a ship through a becalmed ocean. He wore no crown, no regalia, no visible sign of the blood that ran through his veins like a river of fire and obligation. His chest was bare above the sarong, the lean muscles of his torso dark with sun and scarred from the weeks of fighting in the strait -- a long, pale line across his ribs where a Buta's crude axe had found its mark, a constellation of small burns on his left shoulder from the volcanic sparks at Krakatau, a deeper, older scar across his collarbone that dated from his first duel at Dieng, when Eyang Guru had let him fight a practice bout with real blades to teach him that steel does not care about bloodlines. His hair, unbound and unwashed, hung past his shoulders in a black curtain that the wind lifted and tangled. Around his neck, a cord of braided palm fiber held a small bronze amulet -- the *jimat* of Hayam Wuruk, the last physical relic of his father's reign, warm against his skin as though the old king's spirit still resided within the metal, still watching, still judging.

He walked not as an exile, but as a predator in his own domain, his hand never straying far from the hilt of the Keris Nagasasra. His eyes -- dark, intelligent, perpetually scanning the terrain with the automatic vigilance of a man who had learned that the world's beauty and its violence were often separated by no more than a single heartbeat -- read the savanna like a sacred text written in the language of bent fern, crushed grass, and scattered soil. Here, a cluster of *alang-alang* stalks had been snapped at the base, not by wind but by the passage of something heavy, something that dragged its feet -- the artless gait of a Buta Raksasa, those grey-green monstrosities bred from volcanic clay and dark *sakti* in the forges of Empu Gandring. There, a patch of ferns had been flattened in a pattern that spoke not of walking but of crouching, of waiting, of ambush aborted -- the scouts had been here, perhaps a day ago, and then moved on when no quarry appeared. The prints told him everything. The depth of the impressions revealed weight; the spacing revealed stride; the direction revealed intent.

But it was another set of tracks that held his attention -- that had held it, in truth, for the better part of the morning. These were not the crude, heavy prints of the Buta. They were something else entirely. Long, narrow depressions in the earth, each one tipped with four claw-marks that pressed deep into the soil, too deep for any natural cat, even the great tiger of the highland forests. And where the tracks crossed patches of bare stone -- flat slabs of volcanic basalt exposed by erosion -- they left marks of a different kind: faint, luminous scratches that glowed a pale, spectral blue in the shadow of the tall grass, as though the creature that made them walked with fire in its claws.

*Leak tracks,* Raden Wijaya thought, and the recognition was a cold stone dropped into the warm pool of his concentration. *The spectral tiger. Ki Surasenta's eyes in the wild.* The Leak were shapeshifters, sorcerers who had surrendered their humanity to the Shadow King's service in exchange for the power to become beasts -- to walk the night as tigers, as boars, as great carrion birds with wingspans wider than a *prahu* sail. In their transformed state, they were neither animal nor human but something between, something that retained the cunning of a man and the savagery of a predator and the malice of a spirit that had been twisted beyond recognition. Their tracks glowed because the *bayu* -- the life-force of the land -- recoiled from their passage, leaving behind a residue of violated energy that lingered for hours, sometimes days, a ghostly signature that proclaimed: *something wrong walked here.*

He knelt beside a particularly clear set of prints, pressing his fingers into the blue-glowing claw-marks, feeling the residual chill that no natural earth should carry beneath a sun this fierce. The tracks were fresh -- hours old, not days. The tiger-Leak had passed this way during the night, moving southeast, following the same route that the three hunters themselves were taking. Parallel. Pacing them.

*It knows we are here,* Raden Wijaya thought. *It has been watching us since we left the forest. Reporting our position, our speed, our direction. Ki Surasenta sits in the Balairung of Giri Mataram and sees through its eyes, and the Shadow King sees through his, and all the darkness of the world is aware that the heir of Mataram walks the plains with a sacred keris and two companions who should have been dead long ago.*

*Am I leading them to salvation or to a grave?*

The thought was a constant, heavy stone in his gut, worn smooth by three days of ceaseless carrying. He had lost the Tuyul. He had lost the *jimat* bearer. He had lost the wise old man who had started it all -- though the Dwija Putih had returned, changed and terrible and radiant, the loss of those first days still lived in him like a bruise that no amount of healing could erase. All that remained were the Warrior and the Hunter, and a kingdom that might already be dead. The oath he had sworn on the burning island -- *I claim my birthright* -- echoed in his mind with the insistence of a gamelan gong struck too hard, its reverberations refusing to fade, growing louder in the silence rather than softer, demanding that he act, that he prove the words were more than sound, more than a prince's vanity spoken into the volcanic dark.

Three days they had walked since the parting at the forest's edge, since the Dwija Putih had sent them eastward with words that burned like prophecy: *The Horse Lords are ready, but the King is not yet awake.* Three days through the transitional country where forest gave way to savanna, through terrain that shifted beneath their feet like the moods of a sleeping giant -- from the dense, moss-carpeted undergrowth of the Wanantara, where every tree seemed to watch with ancient eyes, to the bamboo thickets that rattled and whispered in the mountain wind like a thousand hollow bones, to the papyrus swamps of the lowland where the water was black and the mosquitoes rose in clouds so thick they darkened the air and Tumenggung Batu had cursed continuously for six hours in a dialect of Kurcaci so old and so profane that even Dewi Anjani, who spoke seventeen languages and had witnessed the fall of civilizations, had raised an eyebrow.

Through swamp and forest and the burned margins where the Buta Raksasa had set fire to the tree-line to deny cover to the defenders of Kediri -- a tactic of mindless destruction that left behind landscapes of charred trunks standing like the columns of a ruined temple, their branches gone, their bark flaking in ashen sheets, the ground beneath them a grey waste where nothing grew except a tenacious variety of fire-fern that sent up small, defiant green fronds through the cinders. Through rain that came suddenly and without warning, a tropical downpour that turned the trail to mud and the dry riverbeds to brown torrents, and then departed as abruptly as it had arrived, leaving behind a steaming, dripping world that smelled of rebirth and rot in equal measure. Through nights where they slept in shifts, two hours each, their backs against whatever shelter the terrain provided -- a rock outcropping, a fallen tree, the hollow beneath a tangled banyan whose aerial roots formed a cage of living wood that provided concealment if not comfort.

The journey's toll was written on all three of them. Raden Wijaya's stride, though it appeared effortless, was maintained through an act of disciplined will that had become so habitual it resembled instinct. His body wanted rest -- his muscles sang with a low, persistent ache that no amount of stretching could silence, and his feet, wrapped in the remains of sandals that had been disintegrating since the strait, were blistered and raw on the soles, each step a small negotiation between determination and pain. But he did not slow.

"The land is wounded," Dewi Anjani whispered, her voice carrying over the wind like a flute note played in a minor key, a sound that was beautiful and sorrowful at once, as though beauty and sorrow were, for the Bidadari, not opposites but harmonics of the same fundamental tone. The Bidadari moved behind him, her feet barely depressing the dry soil. Her silver-shod sandals, gifts from the court of the Kahyangan, left no prints in the earth, as though the ground chose not to record her passage, preserving her anonymity out of reverence or fear. She was tall -- taller than Raden Wijaya by the width of a hand, though her slenderness and the ethereal quality of her bearing made her seem both larger and smaller than her physical dimensions, as though she existed at a slightly different scale than the mortal world around her, occupying a space that was adjacent to reality rather than fully within it. Her skin, usually luminous with the inner light of the Bidadari -- that soft, pearl-white radiance that was not reflected but generated, a biological phenomenon unique to her kind, the visible manifestation of *bayu* flowing through an immortal vessel -- was dimmed by the harsh sun, muted to a soft ivory that could almost have been mistaken for mortal flesh if one did not look closely. But her eyes were sharp as diamond -- golden irises in which the pupil was not round but elongated, almost feline, an anatomical inheritance from the First Age when the Bidadari were not yet fully separated from the great spiritual beasts of the Kahyangan.

"*Bayu* is thin here, *Manusa*," she continued, using the Bidadari term for the mortal races -- not as an insult but as a designation, the way a mapmaker might label a continent. "I can hear the grass weeping for the water that has been turned to steam by the Shadow King's breath. The roots reach deep, seeking the underground rivers, but the rivers are retreating. Something is drawing the life from this land -- not taking it, but *poisoning* it, corrupting the flow the way a single drop of venom corrupts a well. It is the work of the *Empu*, the forger of dark iron. His foundries beneath Merapi drink the earth's blood, and the savanna withers."

She paused, her golden eyes half-closing, her head tilting in a gesture that Raden Wijaya had come to recognize as the Bidadari equivalent of listening -- not with her ears but with the totality of her being, her skin, her bones, her breath all attuned to frequencies that mortal senses could not access. The wind moved through her unbound hair -- black as a moonless night, but threaded with strands of silver that caught the light like veins of precious metal in dark stone -- and she trembled, very slightly, the way a *saron* bar trembles after it is struck.

"There is something else," she said. "The land remembers what walked upon it. I can feel the passage of the Leak -- that wrongness, that tearing -- but beneath it, older and deeper... there is memory of horses. Many horses. Their hooves upon the earth left a pattern that the soil has not yet forgotten. The horse-lords have ridden these paths recently. Within days. The patrol routes are well-worn, beaten into the *bayu*-fabric of the terrain like the pathways of ants through a colony."

"Which means they are still fighting," Raden Wijaya said, and the knowledge was a small, fierce warmth in his chest -- a coal of hope in the grey ash of the journey. If Kediri's cavalry still rode the borders, then the eastern plains had not yet fallen. If the horse-lords still patrolled, then there was something left worth defending, and something left to build an alliance upon.

"It is more than the wind," Raden Wijaya said, stopping at the crest of a low rise where the *alang-alang* thinned over a spine of exposed volcanic rock. The rise was perhaps twenty feet higher than the surrounding plain -- enough to see over the grass-tops to a wider horizon, where the heat-shimmer painted the world in wavering, impressionist strokes. He knelt, pressing his palm to the sun-baked earth. He closed his eyes, feeling the vibrations of the world -- not with his ears but with the bones of his hand, with the flat of his palm where it met the stone, with the deep, ancestral awareness that the Mataram bloodline carried like a second sight, a gift from the ancient kings who had built their throne at the foot of volcanoes and learned to read the earth's moods as other men read faces.

"The ground trembles. Not with the pulse of the mountain, but with the cadence of iron on stone. *Kuda*."

Horses. The word was a key turning in a lock that had been waiting for it. He could feel them through the rock -- not individual hooves but a collective vibration, a rolling, rhythmic thunder that propagated through the basalt substratum of the savanna like the echo of a drumbeat through the body of a drum. They were distant still -- several leagues, perhaps, moving at a canter rather than a gallop, which suggested patrol rather than charge, discipline rather than flight. The vibration pattern was complex: not a single column but a spread formation, riders moving in a coordinated sweep across a wide front, the way fishermen drag a net through shallows, covering ground, searching for something.

*Or someone,* he thought. *They are looking for something. And we are the only things out here worth finding.*

Tumenggung Batu grunted, hoisting his war hammer onto his shoulder with the practiced, economical motion of a warrior who had carried his weapon for so long that it had become an extension of his body, an additional limb that happened to be made of iron and capable of shattering stone. The Kurcaci was a moving fortress of iron and basalt, sweat streaming down his broad face into his beard -- a magnificent construction of russet and grey hair, braided in the Kurcaci style with small iron rings that clinked softly as he walked, each ring inscribed with a rune of his clan's lineage, a portable genealogy that extended back twenty-seven generations to the first Kurcaci who had emerged from the stone of Gunung Tangkuban Perahu and declared it a fine place to build a forge.

"Horses? Good." His voice was the sound of gravel being crushed in a mortar -- deep, rough, with an undertone of bass that resonated in the chest of anyone standing within a meter of him. "My legs are shorter than my patience right now. If they are enemies, I will take their legs. If they are friends, I will take a ride."

He stood perhaps four feet tall in his iron-shod boots -- a height that, among the Kurcaci, was considered above average and among the human races was considered comical, a disparity that Tumenggung Batu had spent the better part of a century addressing through the simple expedient of hitting anyone who laughed hard enough to reconsider the nature of humor. His frame was extraordinary -- not fat but dense, built wide and low like a watchtower designed to survive earthquakes, his chest a barrel of muscle enclosed in a breastplate of riveted iron that he had forged himself in the volcanic furnaces of the Kurcaci citadel, its surface decorated with a pattern of interlocking diamonds that turned what might have been a killing strike into a bruise, a bruise into an annoyance, and an annoyance into a reason to hit back harder.

He was suffering in this heat, and he did not trouble himself to conceal it. The Kurcaci were creatures of stone and mountain, bred for the cool depths of caverns and the volcanic warmth of forges, not for the relentless, exposure-based assault of a tropical sun on an open plain. His face was the color of a brick kiln, sweat flowing in rivers that pooled in the channels of his wrinkles and dripped from his braided beard in a steady percussion that he found personally insulting. His iron armor, which in the cool of the mountains was a comfort and a companion, was now a portable oven that was slowly cooking him from the outside in, and every few minutes he adjusted the straps with the irritable, muttering energy of a man who is too proud to remove his armor and too sensible to ignore the fact that it is actively trying to kill him.

"Three days," he growled, as much to himself as to the others. "Three days on this flat, featureless, furnace-like excuse for a landscape. Not a cave in sight. Not a decent boulder to sit on. Not a single mountain worthy of the name. This land is an insult to geology."

"The land serves its own purposes," Dewi Anjani said mildly.

"Then its purposes are misguided." Batu wiped his face with a forearm that left a smear of grime and iron filings across his brow. "A land without mountains is a land without bones. No wonder the people here ride horses. Their legs are the only things preventing them from falling straight through the earth."

Raden Wijaya might have smiled, under other circumstances. The Kurcaci's complaints were as constant as the cicada-drone and approximately as subtle, but they served a function that went beyond the mere ventilation of discomfort: they were the rhythm section of the trio, the steady, grounding pulse that prevented the silence from becoming too heavy, too weighted with the thoughts that all three of them carried but only Batu had the constitution to keep at bay through the sheer volume of his objections to the physical world.

He rose from the rock, brushing the volcanic dust from his palm. The vibrations were strengthening, resolving from a vague, distributed tremor into a directional pulse that approached from the northeast, from the open plains beyond the rise where the grass ran unbroken for leagues. He scanned the horizon, his hand shading his eyes against the brutal turquoise glare, and saw nothing -- not yet -- but the air in that direction had a quality he recognized, a faint, golden thickening, as though the atmosphere itself were gathering density in anticipation of what was coming.

"We will know soon enough whether they are friend or foe," he said. "But if the ground speaks true, they are many. Thirty riders, perhaps more. A full patrol. If they are Kediri's, then the border guard holds. If they are not..."

He did not finish the sentence. He did not need to. If they were not Kediri's men -- if the horse-lords had been defeated and their steeds taken by the Buta Raksasa or the iron-men of Empu Gandring -- then the three of them were standing on the crest of a low rise with no cover, no retreat, and no hope of outrunning mounted cavalry in open grassland, and the only question would be how many they could take with them before the mathematics of the situation asserted itself.

He touched the hilt of the Keris Nagasasra and felt it answer -- a faint, warm pulse, like a heartbeat, like a word spoken in a language that predated speech. The keris was alive, in the way that all sacred blades were alive in the old understanding, imbued with the *bayu* of its makers and the accumulated will of the kings who had carried it. It could not speak. It could not advise. But it could *respond* -- a warmth for safety, a chill for danger -- and at this moment it was warm, or at least not cold, and Raden Wijaya took what comfort he could from the ambiguity.

"Hold your weapons low," he said. "We wait. We watch. And we remember that in the old days, the horse-lords of Kediri and the kings of Mataram were blood-brothers, sworn beneath the same moon, bound by the same *dharma*. If any memory of that oath survives in the rider at their head, we may yet have allies."

"And if it doesn't?" Batu asked, hefting his hammer.

"Then we have a fight," Raden Wijaya said simply. "And we have had worse."

## B. The Horse Lords Appear

Across the plain, a cloud of dust rose against the blue.

It began as a smudge -- a golden-brown discoloration at the horizon line, no larger than a man's thumb held at arm's length, easily mistaken for the heat-shimmer that danced perpetually above the savanna like the ghost of water that the land remembered but could no longer produce. But the shimmer was random, directionless, a trick of heated air rising and bending light. This was purposeful. It moved with a lateral sweep that no natural phenomenon could replicate -- a curved advance from northeast to east, the deliberate arc of a military formation adjusting its approach angle, and it moved with a speed that no Buta horde could match. It was a golden-brown veil, illuminated from within by the flash of bronze and the glint of spear-tips that caught the sun and threw it back as brief, fierce stabs of white light, like the signals of a heliograph, like a code being transmitted across the empty miles in a language that only warriors understood.

Raden Wijaya watched the cloud approach and felt, despite himself, a stirring in his blood that was older than thought -- the primordial recognition that occurs when a creature of the earth encounters a force that is, simultaneously, beautiful and potentially lethal, the way a man standing on a cliff watches an approaching storm, his fear and his admiration braided together so tightly that he cannot separate them.

"*Pasukan Kuda Kediri*," he said, rising from the rock where he had knelt to read the earth's vibrations, his voice carrying the controlled, measured cadence of a man who is narrating a situation as much for his own benefit as for his companions'. "The Horse Lords of the East. I had heard they still held the plains, but I expected to find them dead or driven to the mountains."

The dust cloud grew. It was no longer a smudge but a wall -- a rolling, golden curtain that consumed the horizon, blotting out the lower third of the sky as effectively as a monsoon front, and from within that curtain came sound: the thunder of hooves on hard-packed earth, not the disordered cacophony of a stampede but the rhythmic, synchronized percussion of trained cavalry moving in formation, each horse's stride matched to its neighbor's, each set of hooves striking the ground at the same instant so that the collective impact produced not chaos but a single, enormous heartbeat, a pulse that shook the savanna like the footfall of a god.

Over the thunder, other sounds resolved -- the jingle of harness bells, high and bright, a metallic birdsong that wove through the bass of the hooves like the *suling* flute through the lower registers of a gamelan; the creak of saddle leather being stressed by the weight of armored riders leaning into their horses' necks; the snap of pennants, small triangular flags of red and gold that flew from the tips of upraised spears and were visible now through the thinning dust as brief, vivid splashes of color in the golden murk, like tropical birds glimpsed through monsoon rain. And beneath it all, felt more than heard, a deep, sustained vibration in the earth itself, a tremor that climbed through the soles of Raden Wijaya's damaged sandals and through the bones of his feet and up his legs and into his chest, where it met his own heartbeat and synchronized with it, as though the approaching cavalry and the heir of Mataram were being tuned to the same frequency by a hand that understood that war is, at its core, a form of music.

The riders emerged from the dust like figures carved from the savanna itself -- like wayang puppets stepping through the leather screen into the three-dimensional world, trailing the golden light of the *blencong* lamp behind them as they materialized from abstraction into terrifying specificity.

They were mounted on *kuda turangga* -- the swift, lean horses of the eastern ranges, a breed apart from the heavier, broader mounts of the western kingdoms. These were creatures of the grassland, born to speed and endurance, their bodies refined by generations of selective breeding into instruments of perfect economy: long legs with clean, narrow cannon bones; deep chests that could process the thin, hot air of the savanna into the fuel required for sustained gallop; short, powerful backs designed to carry a rider's weight without sacrificing the flexibility needed for the violent, twisting maneuvers of mounted combat. Their coats shone like polished copper in the afternoon light -- every shade of the metal's spectrum represented, from the pale, pinkish gold of new-smelted ore to the deep, almost burgundy bronze of a temple bell aged by centuries of incense smoke. Their manes were braided in the Kediri fashion -- tight, intricate plaits woven close to the neck and decorated with small brass bells that chimed with each stride and with protective amulets of carved bone and black stone that were said to ward against the Leak's influence, to keep the horses sane and manageable in the presence of dark *sakti* that might otherwise drive them to bolt.

And the riders -- *Duh Gusti*, the riders.

They wore breastplates of hardened leather and bronze, the leather boiled and shaped to follow the contours of the torso, the bronze plates riveted at the shoulders, the sternum, the spine -- not the heavy, full-body armor of the Mataram court guard but the lean, functional protection of men who fought from horseback and needed mobility more than they needed invulnerability. The leather was dark with oil and sweat and the accumulated patina of hard service, and it bore the scars of its use: slashes across the chest-piece where a Buta's crude blade had failed to penetrate, a jagged puncture near the left shoulder where something had gotten through and been repaired with a patch of different-colored leather, the stitching visible, rough, functional -- a warrior's repair, not an artisan's. Their heads were crowned with the *kuluk* of Kediri warriors -- tall, conical helmets of lacquered leather with a bronze peak and side-guards that covered the cheeks and the back of the neck, leaving only the eyes and the bridge of the nose exposed. Below the *kuluk*, their faces were masked by the dust of the long patrol, coated in a fine, golden powder that filled every crease and crevice and turned their features into the anonymized, almost ritualistic visages of war itself -- not individuals but a collective, a single organism with many bodies and one will.

They carried *tombak* spears -- long, slender shafts of iron-hard teak tipped with leaf-shaped blades of forged steel, the edges honed to a brightness that the dust could not dim. The spears were held upright, their butt-ends socketed into saddle-rests, their blades catching the sun in a forest of silver points that swayed with the motion of the horses like the masts of a fleet approaching harbor. At their hips, short swords in plain leather scabbards; across their backs, compact recurve bows of laminated bamboo and horn, unstrung for travel but ready, the quivers riding low on the right hip where a mounted archer could reach them without shifting his weight.

There were thirty-two of them. Raden Wijaya counted them as they emerged from the dust-cloud, his tactical mind performing its automatic inventory: thirty-two riders, thirty-two horses, thirty-two *tombak*, suggesting a standard Kediri border patrol, the *sanggariaksa* -- a unit large enough to engage a Buta scouting party and fast enough to disengage from a larger force, the basic operational unit of the eastern cavalry's doctrine of mobile defense. Thirty-two was a number that Raden Wijaya's tutors at Dieng would have called significant -- a full *penggel*, commanded by a *tumenggung* of the border, answerable directly to the *Balapati* of Kediri and, through him, to the throne of Mataram itself, though that chain of command had been corroded by treachery and broken by war and was now, perhaps, no more than a memory of discipline preserved in the habits of men who continued to follow protocols whose authority no longer existed.

At their head rode a man of imposing presence.

He was tall in the saddle -- not merely seated on his horse but merged with it, his body rising from the animal's back with the organic authority of a tree growing from the earth, his spine straight, his shoulders square, his weight settled deep into his hips in the way that only a man who has spent more of his life mounted than afoot can achieve. His armor was different from his men's: the same leather base, but overlaid with plates of silver-inlaid bronze that covered his chest and shoulders in overlapping scales, each scale engraved with a tiny, intricate pattern -- the repeating motif of the *singa barong*, the heraldic lion of Kediri, whose stylized mane and bared fangs decorated every surface in a mesh of silver filigree that caught the light and scattered it in a halo of brilliance around the rider's torso, so that he appeared, in the dust-diffused sunlight, to be wearing a breastplate made of captured daylight.

His *kuluk* was taller than the others, its peak surmounted by a crest of red horsehair that swept backward in the wind like a flame, and beneath it his face was visible -- not masked by dust like his men's, but exposed, clean-shaven, the face of a man in his late thirties with skin the color of teak heartwood and features that were simultaneously aristocratic and brutal, the face of a man who had been born to command and had spent twenty years in the field proving that the birth was not a lie. His eyes were dark, deep-set beneath heavy brows, and they carried an expression that Raden Wijaya recognized instantly because he saw it every time he looked at his own reflection in still water: the focused, calculating, perpetually evaluating gaze of a man who has been fighting a war he is slowly losing and has not yet decided whether to despair.

In his right hand, a long *tombak* spear held vertically -- not in the saddle-rest but upraised, gripped at the balance point with a hand that was gloved in leather so worn that the original color had been replaced by a uniform, permanent brown, the hand of a man who had not released that spear for anything less than sleep since the war began. The spear's blade was wider than his men's -- a commander's weapon, designed not only for combat but for signaling, its broad flat surface capable of catching the sun and sending flashes of command across the formation, a semaphore that his riders could read at full gallop.

He led his troop in a wide, sweeping arc, a *Singhabihwa* formation -- the lion formation, the enveloping crescent that was the Kediri cavalry's signature maneuver, a tactical move perfected over generations of border warfare against the raiding parties of the eastern isles and now adapted to the grimmer business of fighting the Shadow King's forces. The riders split into two horns of the crescent, each horn curving around the flanks of the three travelers with the practiced, effortless coordination of wolves circling prey, their horses maintaining a perfect, equidistant spacing that spoke of years of drilling, years of fighting together, years of a shared language written in the angle of a spear and the pressure of a knee against a horse's flank. The crescent closed behind the rise where the three companions stood, hemming them against the elevated ground with a wall of horses and dust and steel that was, simultaneously, a military maneuver and a statement of absolute territorial authority.

*We own this land,* the formation said, more eloquently than any words. *And you are on it uninvited.*

The horses slowed from canter to trot, from trot to walk, from walk to a halt that rippled through the formation like a wave reaching shore, each rider stopping in sequence from the center outward, each horse planted and still, each spear vertical, each rider's eyes fixed on the three figures who stood, impossibly small and impossibly exposed, at the center of the iron crescent.

The silence was sudden and total. The thunder of hooves ceased, and in its absence the savanna's own sounds rushed back -- the cicada drone, the wind in the grass, the chiming of harness bells as the horses settled, the creak of leather as riders shifted their weight. The dust hung in the air like a golden curtain slowly falling, and through it the sun blazed, and in that light the formation of the *Pasukan Kuda* was not merely military but mythological, a scene from a wayang tale brought to life -- the moment when the hero meets the force that will determine whether his story ends in the next scene or continues for another act.

"*Mulah gerak!*" the leader thundered, his voice a command that brooked no argument, a voice trained to carry across the din of battle and the width of a formation at full gallop, a voice that contained within its frequencies the assumption that obedience was not a choice but a physical law, as inevitable as gravity. "*Kula Kanjeng Tumenggung Arya Kamandanu, balapati wates Kediri. Sira, sapa wani lumaku ing lemah ingsun mawa hawa pati?*"

*Do not move! I am Kanjeng Tumenggung Arya Kamandanu, general of the Kediri borders. You there, who dares to walk upon my land carrying the scent of death?*

The words rolled across the savanna in the *Krama Madya* -- the middle register of formal Javanese, a register that sat between the lofty heights of court speech and the plain ground of common address, a register chosen with precision because it acknowledged the possibility that the strangers might be worthy of respect while reserving the full measure of that respect until evidence was provided. It was the language of a man who had been tricked before and had learned to keep his sword in hand during introductions.

The spear tilted -- not a threat, not yet, but a statement of readiness, the way a cat's ears flatten not to signal attack but to signal the capacity for it. Behind him, thirty-one riders watched, their hands on their weapons, their horses still, their discipline a palpable force that surrounded the three travelers like the walls of a room from which there was no exit.

## C. The Confrontation in Krama Inggil

Raden Wijaya stood slowly, his hands held open and away from his blades. He rose from the rock with a deliberateness that was itself a form of communication -- not the hasty, defensive scramble of a man caught off-guard, but the measured, unhurried ascent of a man who understood that his body's language would be read by thirty-two pairs of trained eyes, and who chose to speak, with that body, the dialect of sovereignty: *I rise because I choose to rise. Your spears do not concern me. I have faced worse than spears.*

He looked up at the rider, meeting his gaze with the calm authority of a man who has looked into the eyes of dragons. The sun was behind Kamandanu, turning the mounted commander into a silhouette framed in gold, a figure from a *wayang* screen backlit by the *blencong's* fire -- and Raden Wijaya understood, with the part of his mind that was always composing the narrative of his own actions, that this was a *lakon*, a scene, a performance in which the roles were not yet assigned and the outcome depended entirely on whether two men of pride and power could recognize each other through the dust and blood and bitter history that separated them.

He spoke in the *Krama Inggil*, the highest register of the Javanese court, the language of kings that recognized neither dust nor distance. The words came from a place deeper than memory -- from the bloodline itself, from twelve generations of Mataram kings who had spoken this language as their native tongue, who had breathed it and dreamed in it and sworn their oaths in it and died with its syllables on their lips. To speak *Krama Inggil* was not merely to use a linguistic register; it was to claim a position in the cosmic hierarchy, to assert that the speaker stood at the summit of the mortal world and addressed the divine, the ancestral, and the temporal powers as an equal.

"*Sembah bakti, Kanjeng Tumenggung. Kula Raden Wijaya, putranipun Prabu Hayam Wuruk, ahli waris dhampar Mataram. Kula mawa wartos malapetaka saking Selat Sunda, sarta mawa harepan kanggo Nusantara.*"

*Respectful greetings, Kanjeng Tumenggung. I am Raden Wijaya, son of King Hayam Wuruk, heir to the throne of Mataram. I bring news of disaster from the Sunda Strait, and hope for Nusantara.*

The words fell into the silence like stones into still water, and the ripples spread outward through the formation of riders with visible effect. The riders went silent -- a deeper silence than the tactical quiet they had maintained since halting, a silence that was not the absence of speech but the presence of listening, the collective intake of breath that occurs when a word is spoken that changes the shape of the world. The horses shifted nervously beneath their riders, their bells chiming softly in the heat, their sensitive ears swiveling toward the sound of the voice as though they too understood the weight of what had been said. The name *Mataram* hung in the dry air like a prayer, heavy with the weight of lost glory -- a name that had once been synonymous with power and order and the divine mandate of just kingship, and that had become, in the months since the Shadow King's treachery, a byword for ruin, for betrayal, for the catastrophic failure of the mortal world to protect itself against the darkness that had always waited at its borders.

Arya Kamandanu did not move. For a long, stretching moment -- five heartbeats, ten, an eternity measured in the slow drip of sweat and the distant cry of a hawk -- the Tumenggung of the Kediri border sat his horse and stared at the man who had just claimed to be the heir of the most powerful kingdom in Nusantara, and his face revealed nothing. It was the face of a man who had trained himself, through years of border warfare and the daily negotiation with death that such warfare entails, to receive information without reacting to it, to process claims without validating them, to hold his judgment in abeyance the way a *dalang* holds a puppet -- suspended, waiting, ready to animate it in whatever direction the story demanded.

His dark eyes moved over Raden Wijaya with the systematic, unhurried thoroughness of a man appraising a horse before purchase -- not with contempt but with the clinical detachment of someone who has learned that appearances are the first and least reliable layer of truth. He saw the filth -- the volcanic ash ground into the grain of the skin, the forest loam caked in the hair, the sweat-salt crusting at the temples and the collarbones. He saw the fatigue -- the shadows beneath the eyes, the slight tremor in the hands that spoke of muscles pushed beyond their natural limits and sustained by will alone. He saw the bloodstains on the batik, some brown with age, some darker and more recent, a palimpsest of violence that told a story of sustained combat across many days and many terrains.

But he also saw the bearing. He saw the way the man stood as if the earth belonged to him by right of blood -- not with arrogance, but with the unconscious, ingrained certainty of someone who had been raised in the knowledge that he would one day be responsible for everything his feet touched. He saw the way the man's weight was distributed -- equally on both feet, centered, rooted, the stance of a silat master who has moved beyond the need for guard positions because his entire body is a guard position, because readiness has become indistinguishable from rest. He saw the keris at the man's hip -- and more than the keris, he saw the way the man's hand related to it, not gripping but hovering, maintaining a proximity that was as natural and as automatic as breathing, the relationship between a king and his regalia, between a musician and his instrument, between a dancer and the rhythm.

And he saw the keris itself. The Nagasasra. Even through the dust, even in its sheath, the blade emitted a presence that a man attuned to *sakti* could feel like a change in air pressure, like the moment before a storm breaks when the atmosphere goes still and dense and charged with unreleased energy. Kamandanu's eyes lingered on the weapon, and for the first time his controlled expression shifted -- a narrowing of the eyes, a tightening of the jaw, recognition passing across his features like a shadow crossing stone.

"The Keris Nagasasra has not been seen in the eastern lands since Prabu Hayam Wuruk rode to the Selat Sunda to treat with the lords of Pajajaran," Kamandanu said slowly, his voice dropping from the parade-ground volume of his initial challenge to a quieter, more dangerous register. "The throne of Mataram showed that blade only on occasions of state -- coronations, treaties, declarations of war. That *sira* carries it means one of three things: *sira* is indeed the heir, *sira* has killed the heir and taken his weapon, or *sira* is a fool who found a keris in a ruin and does not understand what he holds."

He tilted his spear, just slightly, the blade catching the sun and throwing a bar of reflected light across Raden Wijaya's face. "Which is it, traveler?"

Raden Wijaya did not flinch from the light. He stood in its glare the way he had learned to stand in the glare of Krakatau's fire -- without blinking, without turning away, letting the light do what it would to his eyes because closing them would be a surrender he could not afford.

"The first," he said. "I am the heir. And I carry the blade not for display but for purpose. Mataram is in chains. Its king is a puppet. Its halls are haunted by the Leak of Ki Surasenta. And its heir -- the last of the line of Hayam Wuruk -- stands before you covered in the dirt of three kingdoms and the blood of the enemies who tried to stop him from reaching this plain." He paused, and when he continued, his voice carried the Krama Inggil like a banner carried into battle -- not waved for show but held aloft because it was the standard around which men rallied. "*Kula mboten badhe njaluk pitulung. Kula badhe nawekaken persekutuan. Dharma ingkang sami, mungsuh ingkang sami, nasib ingkang sami. Menawi Mataram rubuh, Kediri namung dados dhaharan kangge Naga Laut.*"

*I do not come to beg for help. I come to offer alliance. The same dharma, the same enemy, the same fate. If Mataram falls, Kediri is but a snack for the Naga Laut.*

The translation was not needed for the riders -- they were men of the eastern plains, raised in the Javanese tongue, fluent in every register from the courtly heights to the battlefield crude -- but it hung in the air nonetheless, translated by the tone and the bearing and the sheer, stubborn refusal to plead, and the riders heard it and sat straighter in their saddles, and their horses, sensing the shift in their riders' attention, pricked their ears and stood more still, as though the animals too understood that something of consequence was being negotiated in the language of the kings.

Kamandanu studied the prince. He studied the way the claim sat on the man -- whether it was an ill-fitting garment donned for the occasion or a second skin that had been worn so long it could not be removed. He had met impostors before. The war had produced them the way a plague produces fever-dreams -- desperate men who claimed royal blood because royal blood was the only currency that might still purchase allegiance, the only story compelling enough to convince broken, exhausted warriors to fight one more battle. He had met three such pretenders in the past six months alone, and he had exposed them all, not through interrogation but through the simple test of bearing: a true heir moved differently than a false one, the way a kuda turangga moved differently than a farm horse. The breeding was in the bones, in the gait, in the way the weight was carried, and it could not be imitated by anyone who had not been born to it.

This man had the bearing. This man had the blade. But Kamandanu was not yet satisfied.

"And your companions?" he asked, his eyes moving to the luminous figure who stood behind the prince. "The Bidadari walks the mortal world in a time of war. That is unusual."

"I am Dewi Anjani," Anjani said, stepping forward. She spoke not in Krama Inggil -- the Bidadari did not use the registers of mortal languages, for their own tongue predated all mortal speech -- but in the High Language of the Kahyangan, the celestial court, her words falling into the space between the riders like drops of silver rain, each syllable carrying a weight of beauty and authority that made several of the younger riders lower their eyes involuntarily, the way men lower their eyes before a light that is too pure to look upon directly. "*Kula pancen kautus dening Kahyangan kangge njaga ahli waris Mataram. Cahya kula mboten kenging dipun goroh. Deleng-en.*"

*I was indeed sent by the Kahyangan to guard the heir of Mataram. My light cannot be deceived. Watch.*

She raised her right hand, palm upward, and the *bayu* within her responded -- not summoned but released, a dam opened, a restraint removed -- and the Bidadari light bloomed from her skin like dawn breaking over the ocean, a luminescence that was not external illumination but internal truth made visible, the physical manifestation of a soul so old and so aligned with the fundamental harmonics of creation that it could not help but glow, the way heated metal cannot help but radiate, the way the deep ocean cannot help but phosphoresce. The light was white at its core, but its edges shimmered with colors that the human eye could not quite categorize -- not-quite-gold, not-quite-silver, the color that sunlight becomes after it has passed through the heart of a diamond and emerged carrying the gemstone's memory.

The light fell on Raden Wijaya, and where it touched him, the filth and the blood and the dust became transparent, and what was beneath became visible -- not his body but his *bayu*, his life-force, the spiritual architecture of a man born to the bloodline of kings, and it shone, faintly but unmistakably, with the deep amber radiance that was the signature of the Mataram line, the hereditary luminescence that no imposter could replicate because it was not a skill or a possession but an inheritance, woven into the fabric of his being at the moment of his conception, as inseparable from him as his bones.

Several of the riders inhaled sharply. One -- a young man on the left horn of the crescent, his face still boyish beneath the dust -- whispered "*Duh Gusti*" and touched his forehead in the reflexive gesture of devotion.

Kamandanu's face did not change. But his spear, which had been tilted toward the strangers throughout the exchange, rose slowly to the vertical -- the *tombak* standing straight as a pillar beside him, its blade pointing at the turquoise sky. It was, in the lexicon of cavalry signals, the sign for *stand down* -- not disarmament but a suspension of hostility, a transition from confrontation to parley.

"And the small one?" Kamandanu asked, a faint -- very faint -- thread of amusement entering his voice for the first time.

Tumenggung Batu planted the haft of his war hammer on the ground and leaned on it with the air of a man who has been standing in the sun for an unconscionable amount of time and intends to make his displeasure known regardless of the diplomatic consequences.

"Tumenggung Batu," he said, in *basa loma* -- the casual register, the everyday language that stood at the opposite end of the formality spectrum from the Krama Inggil like a peasant's hut standing opposite a palace. He did not use the high language because the Kurcaci did not believe in linguistic hierarchy. Words were words. They meant what they meant. Dressing them in silk did not make them truer. "Kurcaci. Mountain-born. Forge-trained. Currently sun-baked, saddle-deprived, and in severe need of a drink. If your horses are as fast as they look, I would greatly appreciate not having to walk any further on this flat, miserable, bone-dry excuse for terrain."

He paused, squinting up at the mounted commander with eyes that reflected the light like chips of polished obsidian.

"Also, the prince is telling the truth. I was there when he swore the oath. I was there when he drew the Nagasasra. I was there for all of it -- the strait, the fire, the bridge, the Naga. If you want proof, I'll give you the name of every Buta Raksasa I killed on the way here, but it would take a while. I lost count after forty."

"Forty-three," Dewi Anjani corrected, her luminescence fading to its usual subdued glow.

"Forty-three," Batu amended. "The Bidadari counts. It's a Bidadari thing."

A sound passed through the formation of riders -- not laughter, exactly, for these were men at war and laughter was a luxury they could not easily afford, but a vibration of recognition, a loosening of tension, the sound that occurs when men who have been braced for violence are given permission to consider the possibility that violence may not, on this occasion, be required. Horses shifted. Leather creaked. A few spears settled more deeply into their rests.

Kamandanu looked at the three of them -- the prince, the Bidadari, the Kurcaci -- and the calculation behind his eyes shifted, not from suspicion to trust (trust, in these times, was a bridge that could not be built in a single conversation) but from active assessment to provisional acceptance, a willingness to proceed to the next stage of engagement without the exchange of violence that the moment had, until now, carried as a dormant but real possibility.

"The Bidadari light does not shine for liars," he said, repeating the old proverb as though weighing it on a scale. "And the Nagasasra's *bayu* answers only to the blood of Mataram. These things I know." He looked south, toward the dark plume of Merapi that dominated the horizon like a bruise on the sky, the volcano's perpetual smoke-column visible from every point on the plain, an inescapable reminder of the darkness that festered at the heart of the island. "But knowing a man's blood does not tell you his purpose. Mataram has fallen before, and its heirs have crawled to Kediri before, not for alliance but for shelter -- for a place to hide while the world burned."

He turned back to Raden Wijaya, and his eyes were not hostile but testing -- the eyes of a man who would not accept the easy answer because the easy answer was the one that impostors and cowards offered first.

"Tell me, Raden: do you come to hide behind my men, or to ride beside them?"

Raden Wijaya met the Tumenggung's gaze, and in that meeting of eyes -- the heir of the fallen kingdom and the general of the dwindling border, two men standing in the wreckage of the world they had been born to defend -- something passed between them that was not verbal, not physical, but essential: the recognition that occurs when two men of *dharma* find each other in the dark.

"I do not seek a throne," Raden Wijaya said, his voice echoing with the iron certainty of the oath he had sworn on the burning island, each word placed with the care and the finality of a mason setting stones in an arch, each one dependent on all the others, each one bearing a portion of the structure's weight. "I seek the people. I seek the alliance. If Mataram falls, Kediri is but a snack for the Naga Laut. If we do not stand together now, there will be no one left to tell our stories. *Dharma* demands it."

He paused. The wind moved through the *alang-alang*, and the grass whispered, and somewhere a hawk cried, and the sound was thin and piercing and lonely in the vast, bright emptiness of the sky.

"I do not come to hide behind your men, Tumenggung. I come to lead beside them. And if the road leads to a grave, then I will be the first into it, because that is what the heir of Mataram does -- not shelter behind the shield, but *be* the shield."

Arya Kamandanu studied him for a long, silent moment. The wind rustled the dry grass, whispering secrets of the dead who lay beneath the soil -- the bones of warriors from earlier wars, earlier empires, men who had fought for Kediri and Mataram and kingdoms whose names had been erased by time, all of them now part of the earth that the *alang-alang* roots gripped with their tenacious, enduring fingers.

The Tumenggung's jaw tightened. Not in anger but in decision -- the visible, physical manifestation of a mind that had completed its assessment and arrived at a conclusion.

"The King of Mataram would not walk the plains as a ranger," the Tumenggung said. "But the man who stands before me has the eyes of a king. And the Bidadari light does not shine for liars."

He straightened in the saddle, and the movement rippled through the formation as thirty-one riders read their commander's body language and adjusted their own posture accordingly, the unconscious, synchronized response of men who had served together long enough to function as a single organism with thirty-two bodies.

"We camp tonight in the Kali Kering, two leagues east," Kamandanu said, his voice returning to the command register. "You will eat with us, and you will tell us everything -- the strait, the fire, the Tuyul and the keris, the Dwija Putih. Everything. And then, perhaps, we will talk about alliance."

He turned his horse -- a single, effortless rotation that brought the animal around in its own length, the horse responding to the weight-shift of its rider's hips without the need for rein or voice, the partnership of horse and man so complete that they moved as one creature -- and raised his spear. The blade caught the sun, and for a moment Arya Kamandanu was silhouetted against the turquoise sky with a bar of silver fire above him, and the image burned itself into Raden Wijaya's mind with the permanence of a brand: the Horse Lord of Kediri, tall in the saddle, lit by the sun, the first potential ally in a war that the prince had, until this moment, been fighting with only three.

"Bring them horses," Kamandanu said to his nearest rider. "And water. The Kurcaci looks like he's about to melt."

"I do not melt," Batu growled, but he accepted the waterskin that was offered to him with a speed that somewhat undermined the dignity of his protest.

## D. Intelligence Exchange and Camp

The Kali Kering was the color of old bone.

It had been a river, once -- a wide, generous watercourse that wound through the savanna in lazy, looping meanders, its banks shaded by stands of *waru* and *trembesi* trees, its waters home to freshwater fish and the small, grey herons that hunted them with the patient, one-legged stillness of monks in meditation. That was before the war. That was before the Shadow King's forges beneath Merapi began to drink the groundwater, drawing it down through fissures in the basalt to feed the steam-engines that drove the bellows, the quenching-troughs that cooled the dark iron, the reservoirs that kept ten thousand Buta Raksasa alive in the volcanic caverns where no natural water flowed. Now the Kali Kering was what its name proclaimed -- a dry river, its bed exposed, a winding depression of sun-bleached stones and cracked clay that cut through the savanna like a scar on the land's body, its banks still lined with trees that were slowly dying, their leaves yellowing, their roots reaching deeper and deeper into the receding water table like the fingers of drowning men.

It was, despite its desolation, an excellent camp. The riverbed sat three meters below the level of the plain, which meant that fires could be lit without their light being visible from a distance -- a consideration that had once been unnecessary in the Kediri heartland and was now, in these darkened times, the first and most essential requirement of any resting place. The banks provided natural walls against wind and against the approach of enemies on foot, and the scattered boulders of river-smoothed basalt that studded the dry bed offered cover and seating and the raw materials for field fortifications that a Kurcaci's trained eye could have improved upon but that served their purpose well enough.

Kamandanu's patrol had made this place their own. The signs of repeated habitation were everywhere -- fire-blackened circles of stone where cooking fires had been built and rebuilt many times, hitching lines strung between the bankside trees where the horses could be tethered in the shade, a crude but functional latrine dug at the downstream end of the camp where the prevailing wind would carry its smell away from the sleeping area, a weapons rack of lashed bamboo where spare *tombak* stood in a bristling row like a miniature palisade. It was the camp of men who had been living in the field for months, men who had transformed the discipline of military life into a domestic routine, finding in the repetition of chores and watches and shared meals a structure that kept despair at bay -- the warrior's answer to existential crisis, which is to clean your weapon, feed your horse, sharpen your blade, and trust that purpose will reveal itself in the doing.

The horses were tended first, as they always were in a cavalry camp -- each rider unsaddling his mount with the careful, almost reverential attention of a priest caring for a temple, checking hooves for stones, inspecting legs for heat or swelling, rubbing down coats dark with sweat using handfuls of dry grass, offering water from leather buckets filled at a seep-spring that Kamandanu's men had dug at the upstream end of the dry bed, where a trickle of groundwater still emerged from the bank, thin and reluctant but enough to keep thirty-two horses and thirty-two men alive if no one was extravagant. The horses drank, and their riders watched them drink, and in the way they watched there was a tenderness that the warriors would have denied if asked about it but could not suppress: these animals were not tools but partners, the other half of a bond that was, in many ways, closer than marriage, for a man may survive the loss of a wife but a cavalryman without his horse is not merely unmounted but unmade, reduced from a force of nature to a man with sore feet and no purpose.

Raden Wijaya sat on a flat river-stone near the fire that Kamandanu's orderly had built -- a small, efficient fire of dried *alang-alang* and deadwood, its smoke thin and pale, rising vertically in the still, warm air of the riverbed and dispersing above the banks before it could form a visible column. He had been given water, a clean cloth to wipe the worst of the grime from his face and hands, and a place to sit that was marginally more comfortable than the volcanic rock he had occupied for the past three days, and these small mercies had the paradoxical effect of making him feel the accumulated exhaustion more acutely, not less, as though his body, sensing that the immediate crisis had passed, was now presenting him with the bill for services rendered and demanding payment in the currency of rest.

He did not rest. A king does not rest while his kingdom burns. He sat, and he waited, and he watched the camp come to life around him with the observant, cataloguing attention of a man who was evaluating not just the strengths of potential allies but the vulnerabilities -- the fraying morale, the thin horses, the weapons that had been repaired too many times, the eyes of men who were tired in a way that sleep could not remedy.

The meal was simple, prepared with the efficient, unshowy competence of men who had been cooking in the field long enough to have eliminated every unnecessary step. Roasted game -- two jungle fowl and a monitor lizard, caught that morning by a pair of riders who served as the patrol's designated hunters, their quarry gutted and spitted and turned over the coals with the practiced indifference of men for whom the procurement of food had become as routine and as joyless as any other military operation. Rice, brought from the patrol's diminishing stores -- plain white rice, cooked in a clay pot blackened by a hundred previous fires, served without accompaniment or seasoning because the condiments had run out weeks ago and the supply lines from the Kediri heartland were increasingly unreliable. And *wedang jahe* -- ginger tea, the national comfort, brewed from wild ginger root dug from the riverbank and boiled with a chip of palm sugar that one of the riders -- a lean, hawk-nosed man named Wiradanu who seemed to serve as the patrol's unofficial quartermaster -- produced from a pouch at his belt with the gravity of a man presenting a treasure.

"The last of it," Wiradanu said, dropping the sugar chip into the pot with the precise, economical gesture of a man who had learned to measure luxuries in fractions. "After this, it's plain ginger. Which is still better than plain water, which is itself better than no water, which is what we'll have if the seep-spring dries up."

"You have a Kurcaci's gift for cheerfulness," Batu observed, wrapping his broad hands around the clay cup he had been given. The ginger tea steamed in the cooling air of early evening, its fragrance rising -- sharp, warm, peppery, the smell of every roadside *warung* and every grandmother's kitchen in every village across Nusantara -- and the Kurcaci inhaled it with an expression that came as close to bliss as his craggy features could accommodate. "This is the first civilized thing that has happened to me in a month."

"You have a low threshold for civilization," Kamandanu said, settling across the fire from Raden Wijaya with the controlled, minimal movements of a man conserving energy. He had removed his silver-inlaid breastplate, and beneath it his cotton under-shirt was dark with sweat and bore the imprints of the bronze scales, pressed into the fabric by the weight of the armor over the long day's ride. Without the regalia, he looked older -- the lines around his eyes deeper, the set of his mouth harder, the fatigue that the armor's bearing had concealed now visible in the way he held his shoulders, slightly forward, slightly hunched, as though the weight he carried was not metal but responsibility. "Now. Tell me everything."

Raden Wijaya told him.

He told it as a commander tells a subordinate the tactical situation -- not as a storyteller but as a strategist, stripping the narrative of ornament and emotion, presenting the facts in the order that would produce the clearest picture of the current reality. He told of the Selat Sunda: the desperate battle on the burning islands, the fellowship broken by the overwhelming force of the Buta Raksasa hordes, the companions scattered like seeds in a storm. He told of Kian Santang and Bagus, climbing toward Krakatau with the Keris Murka and the weight of the world's hope -- the *jimat* bearer and his guardian, walking into the fire because someone had to, because the Keris Murka must be returned to the volcano's heart if the Shadow King's power was to be broken. He told of the Tuyul -- Cepot and Dawala, taken by the Leak but found again, alive and fighting, guarded now by Mbah Jangkung and the tree-spirits of the Sanghyang Wana, marching with the ancient forest toward Merapi's flanks. He told of Eyang Guru Batara, fallen into fire beneath the Jembatan Batu and returned as the Dwija Putih, the White Sage, carrying the power of the *geni suci* within him like a lantern carrying a flame.

And he told of his own path: the oath, the blade, the march eastward to find the alliance that the old prophecies claimed could still be forged, if there were hands strong enough to work the metal and hearts brave enough to withstand the heat.

Kamandanu listened. He listened the way a general listens to intelligence -- with his entire being, his body still, his eyes fixed on the speaker, his mind constructing a map of the situation from the verbal terrain that was being laid before him. He did not interrupt. He did not ask questions. He let the prince speak, and he weighed each word on the scales of his own experience, and when the telling was done, the silence that followed was not the silence of confusion or disbelief but the silence of a mind processing information that confirmed its worst fears and, simultaneously, offered the first thread of possibility it had encountered in months.

"So," Kamandanu said, after a pause in which the fire popped and the horses shifted in their sleep and the stars, emerging one by one in the darkening sky above the riverbed, began their slow, ancient wheel. "The world is even worse than I thought. And I thought it was very bad."

He stared into the fire, and the flames painted his face in shifting planes of orange and shadow, and in that light he looked not like a general but like a man standing at the edge of a cliff, looking down at the drop, calculating whether the distance was survivable.

"Let me tell you what we know from this side."

He spoke with the clipped, matter-of-fact delivery of a field report. The news from Mataram was grim, grimmer than rumor, grimmer than fear.

"Prabu Jayabaya sits the throne," Kamandanu said, his voice flat, deliberately stripped of the contempt that sat behind every word. "He was crowned three months ago, when it became clear that Prabu Hayam Wuruk's line was -- we thought -- extinguished. He is the closest surviving relative. A cousin. Seventeenth in the line of succession. A man who was born to administer a rice estate, not to rule a kingdom, and who was elevated to the *dhampar* not because of any quality he possessed but because there was, quite simply, no one else."

He paused, poking the fire with a stick, sending a spiral of sparks upward into the darkening sky.

"He is not a bad man. He is a weak one. And weak men in positions of power do not remain neutral -- they become instruments of whoever is strongest, the way a leaf in a river does not choose its direction but follows the current. The current in Giri Mataram is Ki Surasenta."

The name fell into the fire-lit silence like a drop of poison into clear water.

"Ki Surasenta," Kamandanu repeated, as though the name required emphasis to convey the full measure of its threat. "The Shadow King's voice in the mortal courts. The whisperer. The poisoner of minds. He arrived in Giri Mataram six weeks after the fall of the strait, dressed as a *pendeta*, a holy man, claiming to bring wisdom from the eastern monasteries. He brought gifts -- gold, *sakti*-imbued talismans, medicines that actually worked, at first. He healed the sick. He counseled the grieving. He made himself indispensable."

The Tumenggung's lip curled. "And then the whispers began. Slowly. Like rain that starts as mist and becomes a flood. *The war cannot be won. The old alliances have failed. The Bidadari have abandoned us. The Kurcaci have sealed their mountains. The only path to survival is accommodation. The Shadow King does not want destruction -- he wants order. His order. Submit, and the rains will return. Submit, and the forges will fall silent. Submit, and your children will grow old.*"

He spat into the fire. The saliva hissed and vanished.

"Jayabaya listens. Jayabaya believes. The court is divided -- some see through the lie, but they are fewer every day, and those who speak against Surasenta tend to disappear. Not killed -- that would be too obvious. Reassigned. Sent to distant posts. Retired to estates that happen to be in territories already overrun by Buta scouts. The Leak ride the halls of Giri Mataram at night, and the guards who should stop them stand aside, because the guards answer to Surasenta now, and Surasenta answers to the dark."

A young rider seated to Kamandanu's left -- a compact, broad-shouldered man with a scar that ran from his right ear to the corner of his mouth, pulling his lip into a permanent, lopsided grimace that made him look as though he were perpetually about to deliver bad news -- leaned forward and spoke for the first time.

"The rice fields are burning," he said. His name was Jayanegara, and he served as Kamandanu's second, his *wadana*, the officer who carried the commander's orders to the flanks and who would assume command if Kamandanu fell. His voice was quieter than his commander's but carried a different kind of weight -- the weight of a man who dealt in specifics rather than strategy, in the granular, ground-level reality of suffering. "Not all of them. Not yet. But the Buta Raksasa have been burning the eastern terraces for three weeks, moving systematically from the lowland paddies upward toward the highland gardens. It is not random destruction -- it is a strategy. Starve the population. Make them dependent on Surasenta's 'gifts' of grain that come from somewhere no one asks about. The refugees are moving east, toward our lines. Hundreds. Thousands. Women, children, old men who can barely walk. We feed them when we can, but we cannot feed them and fight at the same time, and Surasenta knows this, because Surasenta knows everything, because Surasenta has eyes in the halls and ears in the walls and the patience of a spider who knows the fly is already on the web."

Another rider spoke -- a woman, Raden Wijaya noticed with interest, one of perhaps four women in the patrol, distinguished from the men only by a slightly different cut to her *kuluk* and a voice that was pitched higher but carried equal authority. Her name was Ratnaningsih, and she rode the right flank, commanding the patrol's six-person archery detachment.

"Empu Gandring's forces are massing at Merapi," she said, her tone the clipped, professional delivery of a scout reporting observations. "We have watched them from the eastern ridges through the *teropong* -- the far-seeing glass. The Buta Raksasa are thick on the mountain's western slope, building something. Fortifications, perhaps. Or staging areas. The smoke from their forges is constant -- day and night, the bellows never stop. They are making weapons. War machines. Siege towers of dark iron that stand as tall as the *candi* temples. And the iron-men."

She said the last two words with a particular emphasis that conveyed, more effectively than any elaboration, the depth of her concern.

"Iron-men?" Raden Wijaya asked.

"Human mercenaries," Kamandanu clarified, his voice hardening. "Men -- and women -- who have sold their service to Empu Gandring. Not slaves. Not prisoners. Volunteers. Warriors from the outer islands, from the pirate fleets of the eastern seas, from the disbanded armies of kingdoms that have already fallen. They fight for gold and for the dark *sakti* that Gandring gives them -- talismans that make them stronger, faster, harder to kill. They are better trained than the Buta. More disciplined. They think. They plan. They adapt. And there are many of them. We estimate three thousand, possibly more. Enough to form the core of an army that, combined with the Buta hordes, could overwhelm any single kingdom in Nusantara."

The fire crackled. In the darkness beyond the camp, a nightjar called -- three sharp notes followed by a descending trill, the sound of the savanna at dusk, the music of a world that continued its ancient rhythms regardless of the wars fought upon its surface.

"We have lost a third of our patrol strength since the war began," Kamandanu continued, and now the fatigue in his voice was no longer concealed but admitted, offered as evidence in the case he was building -- the case for the desperation that made alliance not merely desirable but necessary. "Not all to battle. Some to desertion. Some to the Leak -- scouts who ride out on patrol and simply do not return, and we find their horses days later, wandering riderless, their saddles slashed, their amulets torn away. Some to the iron-men's ambushes in the tall grass, where a cavalry formation becomes a trap rather than an advantage, where the spear-length advantage of a mounted warrior is negated by the close quarters, and the horses panic and throw their riders and the iron-men are waiting with their Gandring-forged blades."

He looked at Raden Wijaya with eyes that were no longer testing but confiding -- the eyes of a man who has decided to trust and is now showing the full extent of what that trust encompasses, the ugly truth beneath the armor, the fear beneath the courage.

"My border is shrinking, Raden. When the war began, we patrolled a front of thirty leagues. Now it is twelve. In another month, if nothing changes, it will be six. And then Kediri itself -- the city, the *kraton*, the people who shelter behind our spears -- will be within reach of the Buta scouts. And after that..."

He did not finish the sentence. He did not need to.

The silence that followed was heavy with the accumulated weight of every piece of intelligence that had been shared -- a silence that was not absence but presence, the tangible, almost physical weight of a situation that was, by any rational assessment, hopeless.

It was into this silence that Raden Wijaya spoke, and his voice, when it came, was quiet -- not the quiet of defeat but the quiet of focus, the quiet of a blade being drawn not for show but for use.

"Then we must act quickly," he said. "Before the border shrinks further. Before Surasenta's whispers become law. Before Gandring's army marches."

He leaned forward, and the firelight caught his face, and in that light the exhaustion was still visible but the despair was not -- because Raden Wijaya had processed the intelligence, had absorbed it, had fitted each piece of bad news into the strategic map he was constructing in his mind, and had found within the catastrophe not hope, exactly, but *opportunity* -- the narrow, perilous, demanding kind of opportunity that only a mind trained for war could identify, the crack in the wall that a lesser man would mistake for just another crack and that a king would recognize as a door.

"Surasenta's strength is also his weakness," Raden Wijaya said. "He rules through whispers, not through force. His power in Giri Mataram depends on the appearance of legitimacy -- on Jayabaya's cooperation, on the court's acquiescence, on the fiction that the Shadow King offers peace rather than subjugation. If that fiction is shattered -- if the court sees with their own eyes that the heir of Mataram lives, that the Bidadari stand with him, that the Kurcaci have not sealed their mountains, that the horse-lords of Kediri ride at his side -- then Surasenta's web of lies collapses. Because a web of lies can survive any number of new lies, but it cannot survive a single, undeniable truth."

Kamandanu's eyes narrowed. "You propose to walk into Giri Mataram. Into the heart of Surasenta's power. With what -- three companions and a keris?"

"With three companions, a keris, and the cavalry of Kediri," Raden Wijaya said. "If Kediri will ride."

"You ask me to commit my men to an assault on the capital," Kamandanu said, and his voice carried not refusal but the weight of a man measuring a proposition against the lives it would cost. "A capital defended by the Leak, by Surasenta's corrupted guard, by whatever Buta forces are garrisoned in the surrounding hills. I have thirty-two riders, Raden. Thirty-two. Against what you describe, we are a raindrop against a fire."

"A raindrop that falls at the right moment can crack stone," Raden Wijaya said. "I do not propose a siege. I propose a *ruse* -- a gambit. We do not need to conquer Giri Mataram. We need to *reach the Balairung*. If I can stand in the throne room and show them the Nagasasra -- if Anjani's light can reveal Surasenta's Leak for what they are -- if the court sees with their own eyes that the line of Hayam Wuruk lives..." He paused, and when he continued, his voice carried the harmonic of the Krama Inggil, the register that made words into oaths. "Then the court must choose. The generals must choose. The *punggawa* and the *priyayi* and every man who swore an oath to Mataram must choose: the true heir or the puppet. *Dharma* or shadow. And I believe -- I must believe -- that enough of them will choose *dharma*."

"You believe," Kamandanu repeated, and the word hung between them, neither acceptance nor rejection but a question -- the essential question, the question that all alliances finally reduce to: *can I trust this man's judgment with my men's lives?*

"I believe," Raden Wijaya said, "because I have seen what *dharma* does when it is given a choice. I saw it in the Tuyul, who chose to carry the keris into the fire. I saw it in the Kurcaci, who chose to follow a prince instead of sealing his mountains. I saw it in the Bidadari, who chose the mortal world over the eternal Kahyangan. *Dharma* is not hope. It is not optimism. It is the refusal to accept that darkness is the only option. And that refusal is the only weapon that the Shadow King cannot forge a defense against."

Arya Kamandanu looked at him across the fire, and the moment stretched, and the stars turned above them, and the night insects sang their ancient, indifferent song, and the horses breathed in their sleep, and the world waited.

"My father served the throne of Mataram," Kamandanu said, and his voice was different now -- lower, rougher, carrying the grain of personal history beneath the smooth surface of command. "He was Kanjeng Tumenggung Ariawangsa, balapati of the Kediri cavalry under Prabu Hayam Wuruk. He rode with the king to the Selat Sunda for the parley with Pajajaran. He was one of twelve riders who escorted the king home when the treaty was signed." He paused. "He told me, when I was old enough to understand, that a king is not the man who sits on the throne. A king is the man whom the throne would choose, if thrones could choose. He said that Prabu Hayam Wuruk had that quality -- the quality that made the earth itself seem to rise beneath his feet, not in worship but in support, the way the earth supports a tree."

He studied Raden Wijaya's face in the firelight.

"Your father had that quality. Do you?"

"That is not for me to say," Raden Wijaya answered. "It is for the earth to say. And for the men who ride upon it."

Another silence. Longer than the last. The fire had burned low, its flames replaced by a bed of coals that glowed deep red and orange, pulsing with a gentle, living rhythm that mimicked the heartbeat of the land. Wiradanu added a stick, and sparks rose, and in their brief, ascending life they were like the stars in miniature -- born in fire, climbing toward the dark, extinguished by the vastness of the sky.

Jayanegara leaned toward his commander and spoke in a voice pitched for Kamandanu's ears alone, but in the quiet of the riverbed the words carried: "Tumenggung. The men are watching. They will follow your decision, whatever it is. But they need a decision. Not tomorrow. Tonight."

Kamandanu nodded, slowly. He looked around the camp -- at his men, scattered in small groups along the riverbed, some sleeping, some cleaning weapons, some sitting in the quiet, companionable silence of warriors who have said everything there is to say and find comfort in the presence of people who understand without words. They were good men. Hard men. Men who had given up the comforts of home and family to ride the border and fight a war that had no visible end. They deserved better than the slow, grinding attrition that was consuming them. They deserved a purpose.

"Tell me," Kamandanu said, turning back to Raden Wijaya, "about the Tuyul. The *jimat* bearer. You said he climbs toward Krakatau."

"Kian Santang," Raden Wijaya said, and the name carried a tenderness that surprised him, an ache that he had not permitted himself to feel during the march. "A boy. Barely more than a child. He carries the Keris Murka toward the volcano's heart, because the keris must be returned to the fire that forged it, and the Shadow King's power must be broken at its source. He walks with Bagus -- a *Orang Rimba*, silent and fierce and loyal beyond measure. And they walk alone, into a land of fire and darkness, while we -- the warriors, the generals, the heirs of kingdoms -- fight our battles out here in the sunlight and tell ourselves that our wars are the important ones."

He paused, and the honesty of the next words cost him something visible -- a tightening of the jaw, a brief closing of the eyes, as though he were swallowing something bitter.

"The truth is that everything we do -- every alliance we forge, every battle we fight, every kingdom we try to reclaim -- is a distraction. A necessary distraction, but a distraction nonetheless. We are the noise. Kian Santang is the signal. Our job is to make enough noise that the Shadow King looks at us instead of at the small figure climbing his mountain. If we fail at that -- if the Shadow King's eye finds the keris-bearer before he reaches the summit -- then nothing else matters. Not Mataram. Not Kediri. Not alliances. Not *dharma*. Nothing."

The camp was very quiet. Even the horses had stopped shifting. Even the insects seemed to have paused their song, as though the night itself were listening.

Ratnaningsih spoke, from the outer ring of the firelight, her voice carrying with the clarity of an archer's focus: "Then the noise must be loud enough to shake the mountain."

Raden Wijaya looked at her -- this woman warrior of the eastern plains, her face half-lit by fire, her eyes reflecting the coals like twin signals in the dark -- and nodded.

"Yes," he said. "Exactly that."

Arya Kamandanu exhaled. It was a long, slow breath, the kind that carries the weight of a decision being made, the last air breathed by the man who existed before the choice and the first air breathed by the man who would live with its consequences. He reached for his *tombak*, which lay across his knees, and gripped it at the balance point, and stood.

The camp stirred. Men who had been sleeping came awake. Men who had been sitting rose. The thirty-two riders of the Kediri border patrol oriented toward their commander with the automatic, magnetic precision of iron filings aligning to a lodestone, each man finding his place in the invisible structure of the unit, each man reading the Tumenggung's posture and adjusting his own accordingly.

Kamandanu looked at Raden Wijaya, and the look contained everything that the negotiation had produced -- the testing, the doubt, the gradual, grudging recognition, the final, hard-won acceptance.

"My father taught me one other thing," Kamandanu said. "He taught me that when the world is burning, you do not stand in the ashes and calculate the odds. You find the man who is running toward the fire, and you run beside him, because that man has already made the decision that matters, and the rest is just details."

He raised his spear.

## E. Alliance Formed, Ride to Mataram

The *tombak* rose into the night air, its blade catching the firelight and the starlight and transmuting them into a single, fierce point of brilliance that was visible to every rider in the camp, a beacon that needed no words to interpret because its meaning was written in the oldest language of command -- the language of steel raised above the heads of men.

"*Pasukan Kuda Kediri!*" Kamandanu's voice carried the length of the riverbed, bouncing off the stone banks, amplified by the natural acoustics of the channel into a sound that was more than volume, that was authority given acoustic form, the voice of a commander addressing not individual men but the collective spirit that they composed. "*Kula wis mutusake. Raden Wijaya putranipun Hayam Wuruk gesang. Ahli waris Mataram ana ing kene. Persekutuan lawas antarane Kediri lan Mataram diwangun maneh ing wengi iki. Kita badhe ngiring panjenengane menyang Giri Mataram, lan kita badhe nggawa perang menyang lawang Ki Surasenta!*"

*Horse Lords of Kediri! I have decided. Raden Wijaya, son of Hayam Wuruk, lives. The heir of Mataram stands among us. The ancient alliance between Kediri and Mataram is reforged this night. We will ride with him to Giri Mataram, and we will bring war to Ki Surasenta's door!*

The silence that followed was the silence of a held breath -- thirty-two riders processing the declaration, each man running the words through the filter of his own exhaustion, his own fear, his own assessment of what the commitment would cost. And then the sound came, not from one throat but from all of them, a single syllable that rose from the riverbed like a physical force, like the shout of the earth itself:

"*Inggih!*"

The word -- that old, weighted syllable of affirmation that meant not merely *yes* but *I hear, I acknowledge, I obey, I am with you* -- rang across the camp and out across the savanna, and the horses startled at the volume and stamped and tossed their braided manes, and the bells chimed in a sudden, chaotic jangle that resolved into a kind of music, and the *tombak* spears rose throughout the camp, thirty-one blades joining Kamandanu's in the sky, a forest of steel that caught the starlight and held it.

"*Inggih!*" they shouted again, and the spears clashed against shields -- the small, round cavalry shields that were strapped to the left arm during charges -- and the percussion of steel on leather-covered wood was like the beating of a great heart, and the night received the sound and amplified it and sent it rolling out across the *alang-alang* in diminishing echoes that may have reached, in their faintest and most distant iteration, the slopes of Merapi itself, where the Shadow King's forges burned and the darkness that heard everything heard this too and wondered.

Raden Wijaya stood at the center of the sound, and for the first time since the burning island -- for the first time since he had raised the Nagasasra and sworn his oath to a sky of fire -- he felt the oath begin to take on weight, to accrete substance, to transform from words into the beginnings of reality. Three companions had been the oath's witnesses. Thirty-two riders were its first soldiers. The number was small -- absurdly small, strategically insignificant -- but numbers were the language of accountants, not kings. Kings spoke in commitments, in loyalties, in the unquantifiable arithmetic of men who chose to ride toward danger instead of away from it. And the Horse Lords of Kediri had chosen.

What followed was ceremony -- brief, functional, stripped of the elaborate ritual that a peacetime alliance would have demanded but invested with a gravity that no amount of ritual could have improved upon.

Kamandanu descended from the bank to the fire where Raden Wijaya stood, and without preamble he drew his short sword and held it horizontally between them, the blade flat, an offering and a barrier at once. Raden Wijaya drew the Nagasasra and laid it across the sword, the two blades crossing, and where the Mataram keris touched the Kediri steel the contact produced a sound -- a faint, clear tone, like the highest note of a *saron*, a sound that had no physical explanation and needed none, because it was not physics but *sakti*, the meeting of two weapons that carried the accumulated *bayu* of their lineages, and the lineages recognized each other and sang.

"*Kula Arya Kamandanu, sumpahing pertama karo ahli waris Mataram*," Kamandanu said, his voice carrying the Krama Inggil now -- the highest register, used for the first time, a linguistic escalation that signaled the solemnity of what was being formalized. "*Pedang kula dadi pedangipun. Jaran kula dadi jaranipun. Getih kula dadi getihipun. Sabdhane Kediri, sabdhane Mataram.*"

*I, Arya Kamandanu, swear the first oath to the heir of Mataram. My sword becomes his sword. My horse becomes his horse. My blood becomes his blood. The word of Kediri is the word of Mataram.*

"*Kula Raden Wijaya, nampi sumpah menika*," Raden Wijaya responded, and the words were both acceptance and reciprocation, the ancient, bilateral structure of the Nusantara alliance-oath that bound both parties equally, that could not be given without being returned. "*Pedang kula dadi pedangipun. Jaran kula dadi jaranipun. Getih kula dadi getihipun. Sabdhane Mataram, sabdhane Kediri.*"

*I, Raden Wijaya, accept this oath. My sword becomes his sword. My horse becomes his horse. My blood becomes his blood. The word of Mataram is the word of Kediri.*

The blades were separated, sheathed, and the oath was complete -- not with a flourish but with a nod, a meeting of eyes, a mutual recognition that what had been spoken could not be unspoken, that the words were now part of the architecture of the world, as real and as binding as the stone beneath their feet.

Then came the horses.

Kamandanu gestured to Wiradanu, who led three animals from the hitching line -- not the spent, trail-worn mounts of the day's patrol but three horses that had been kept in reserve, their strength preserved for exactly this kind of necessity. They were *kuda turangga* of exceptional quality: deep-chested, clean-limbed, their copper coats brushed to a dull shine despite the field conditions, their eyes bright with the intelligent, alert expression of animals bred for war and trained for partnership.

"*Paringana kuda!*" Kamandanu said -- *Give them horses!* -- and the phrase carried within it a significance that transcended the practical. In the culture of the eastern cavalry, the horse was not a possession but a relationship -- a bond that, once established, carried moral and spiritual weight equivalent to a blood-tie. To give a horse was to give a piece of oneself, to extend one's mobility, one's speed, one's power to another, to say: *my reach is your reach, my speed is your speed, where my horse can carry you, I am already there.* The gift of a *kuda turangga* from a Kediri warrior to an outsider was an act of trust so profound that it was, in the old traditions, considered binding even in the absence of spoken oaths, a contract written not in words but in the warm breath and the beating heart of the animal that carried it.

The first horse was brought to Raden Wijaya -- a stallion the color of burnished bronze, his mane braided tight with red thread and small brass bells, his flanks marked with the white scars of old battles, his eyes regarding the prince with the frank, evaluating intelligence of an animal that had seen enough of men to have opinions about them. Raden Wijaya approached him with the slow, respectful deliberation of a man who understood that the horse's acceptance was not guaranteed -- that these animals, these *turangga*, chose their riders as much as riders chose them.

He extended his hand, palm down, and the stallion lowered his great head and breathed upon it -- a warm, grassy exhalation that was, in the equine vocabulary, both greeting and assessment. The nostrils flared, reading the prince's scent the way Raden Wijaya read tracks: the volcanic ash of the strait, the loam of the forest, the sweat of three days' march, and beneath all of it, the deeper, subtler scent of the man himself, the chemical signature of his blood and his *bayu*, the essence that no amount of grime could conceal.

The horse nickered. Softly. An acceptance.

Raden Wijaya swung into the saddle, and the motion -- though he had ridden the heavier horses of the western kingdoms, the stout, placid animals of the highland estates -- was unfamiliar in the way that a new language is unfamiliar to a man who already speaks several. The eastern saddle was different: lighter, built close to the horse's back with minimal padding, designed to transmit the animal's movements directly to the rider's body, to eliminate the intermediary of cushioning and create instead a direct, physical conversation between man and beast. He felt the horse's muscles shift beneath him, felt the warmth of the animal's body against his inner thighs, felt the subtle, constant adjustments of balance that the horse made to accommodate his weight, and he understood -- with the part of his mind that was always learning, always adapting, the ranger's mind that treated every new skill as a weapon to be acquired -- that riding a *kuda turangga* was not a matter of controlling the horse but of *becoming* part of it, of surrendering the rigid, top-down authority of the human will and entering instead into a negotiation, a partnership, a dialogue conducted in the language of weight and pressure and the almost imperceptible shifts of intention that the animal could read in the rider's body before the rider himself was fully conscious of having made a decision.

"He is called Surya," Kamandanu said, watching the prince settle into the saddle with an expression that might have been approval. "He was my second horse. He has carried me through three engagements with the Buta. He is fast, and he is brave, and he does not panic in the dark."

"Surya," Raden Wijaya repeated, and laid his hand on the stallion's neck, and felt the animal lean into the touch, and knew that the gift was more than generous -- it was sacrificial, the Tumenggung giving the prince not merely a mount but one of his own, a horse he trusted with his life.

"I will honor the gift," Raden Wijaya said. "And the giver."

Dewi Anjani received a mare -- a lighter, faster animal with a coat the color of pale gold and a temperament that was, by horse standards, serene. The Bidadari required no instruction. She mounted the horse in a single, fluid movement that seemed to involve no effort, no adjustment, no accommodation between the celestial body and the animal beneath it. She simply rose from the earth and was seated, and the mare stood perfectly still, as though enchanted, which she was -- Anjani's *bayu* flowing through the contact points between her body and the horse's back, communicating in the pre-verbal language of the Kahyangan that preceded all mortal speech, a language that animals understood because they had never lost the connection to it that mortals had surrendered in exchange for words.

She rode with a grace that made the act seem not athletic but aesthetic -- the natural posture of a being for whom the boundary between body and environment was more permeable than it was for mortal creatures, who existed not *on* the horse but *with* it, the way a river exists with its banks, the way a flame exists with its fuel.

"Show-off," Batu muttered.

The Kurcaci's mount presented a different challenge. The horse assigned to him was the smallest of the three -- a compact, sturdy gelding with the patience of a saint and the ground-clearance of a watering trough -- and even so, the distance from the ground to the saddle was greater than Tumenggung Batu was prepared to acknowledge as a reasonable vertical journey. He regarded the animal with the expression of a Kurcaci confronting a geological formation that was technically within his professional competence but practically offensive to his principles.

"I am a creature of stone," he announced to no one in particular. "Stone does not ride. Stone is ridden upon."

"Nevertheless," Kamandanu said, and the faintest ghost of a smile crossed the Tumenggung's face for the first time that evening.

Batu approached the horse. The horse regarded Batu. A negotiation took place, conducted entirely in the language of mutual skepticism. Then, with a grunt that expressed every opinion he held about the fundamental wrongness of the situation, Tumenggung Batu seized the pommel of the saddle, planted his iron-shod boot in the stirrup, and hauled himself upward with the graceless, determined strength of a man lifting a boulder -- which was, in terms of mass and leverage, approximately what was happening. He arrived in the saddle with the air of a man who has conquered a mountain and finds the summit disappointing.

"This," he said, gripping the reins with hands more accustomed to hammers and forge-tongs, "is undignified."

"Everything is undignified from a sufficient height," Kamandanu observed.

"I am not at a sufficient height. I am at an *insufficient* height. I am at a height that a Kurcaci should never be required to occupy. My people were not designed for this distance from the ground. The ground is where the real work happens. The ground is where the stones are. Up here, there is nothing but air and the very real possibility of falling."

"You will not fall," Anjani said, from the effortless, luminous stability of her own seat. "I will not permit it."

"That is marginally reassuring," Batu conceded. "Marginally."

The camp was broken with the efficient, well-drilled speed of men who had done it a hundred times before. Fires were buried. Hitching lines were coiled. Weapons were checked, secured, made ready. The horses, sensing the change in energy that preceded a ride, shifted and stamped and blew through their nostrils with the eager, anticipatory restlessness of animals bred for motion and constrained, always uncomfortably, by the requirements of rest.

They rode out of the Kali Kering as the last light of day drained from the western sky and the stars emerged in their millions -- not the scattered, light-polluted stars of the coastal settlements but the deep, dense, overwhelming star-field of the inland savanna, where the sky was a dome of black silk scattered with diamonds so thick that the gaps between them seemed the anomaly and the light the norm. The moon had not yet risen, but the stars alone provided enough light to see by -- a pale, silver illumination that turned the *alang-alang* into a sea of mercury, each grass-blade tipped with a point of reflected starlight, the entire savanna shimmering with a cold, ethereal luminescence that was, in its own way, as beautiful as the sun-fired gold of the day.

The formation was different now. No longer the enveloping crescent of the *Singhabihwa* but the column of march -- the *Garudabihwa*, the eagle formation, narrow and deep, designed for speed and for the ability to deploy rapidly into combat formation at the first sign of contact. Kamandanu rode at the head, his silhouette a dark figure against the star-field, his red horsehair crest invisible in the darkness, his *tombak* held vertical, its blade a sliver of starlight. Behind him, the riders fell into their places in the column with the automatic, unconscious precision of men who had ridden this formation so many times that their bodies found the correct spacing without the intervention of their minds.

Raden Wijaya rode fourth in the column -- behind Kamandanu, Jayanegara, and a scout whose name he had not yet learned -- and the sensation of riding at speed through the night-lit savanna was like nothing he had experienced before. The stallion Surya moved beneath him with a power and a fluidity that was qualitatively different from the western horses he had ridden -- not the heavy, rocking gait of a beast carrying weight but the long, reaching stride of an animal designed to cover distance, each extension of the forelegs propelling horse and rider forward with an economy of motion that felt less like riding and more like flying, the earth passing beneath them in a blur of silver grass and dark stone, the wind of their passage pulling at his hair and his sarong and singing in his ears with a high, sustained note that was the voice of speed itself.

The savanna opened before them -- vast, silver, seemingly infinite, stretching to horizons that were defined not by mountains or trees but by the curvature of the earth itself, the gentle downward bending of the world's surface into the distance where the star-field met the grass-line in a seamless, shimmering junction of light. It was a landscape that demanded the long view, that forced the eye to the horizon and the mind beyond it, that made the concerns of individual men -- their fears, their doubts, their private calculations of survival -- seem simultaneously trivial and essential, like the notes of a single *suling* flute in the midst of a full gamelan: small, easily lost, but carrying the melody that gave the whole its meaning.

To his left, a league distant, the dark mass of the Wanantara forest rose like a wall of shadow against the stars -- the ancient trees visible only as a ragged edge where their canopy blocked the star-field, a boundary between the open world of the savanna and the enclosed, watchful world of the wood. Somewhere in that forest, the Sanghyang Wana stirred, the tree-spirits rousing from their centuries of sleep to march on Merapi, to reclaim what the darkness had taken. Somewhere in there, two small Tuyul moved through the undergrowth, guided by Mbah Jangkung and protected by forces older than memory. The thought was a comfort and a pain at once -- comfort because they were alive, pain because they were far away and the distance between them was filled with danger.

To his right, the savanna ran unbroken to the southern horizon, where the dark plume of Merapi dominated the sky even at night, visible not as a shape but as an absence -- a void where stars should have been but were not, blotted out by the volcano's perpetual column of smoke and ash. The plume was underlit from below by the glow of the mountain's fires -- a dull, sullen orange that pulsed with the rhythm of the eruptions, painting the base of the smoke-column with a lurid, infernal radiance that made the volcano look not like a feature of the landscape but like a wound in the world, a place where the skin of reality had been burned away and the raw, burning flesh beneath was visible.

*That is where they are going,* Raden Wijaya thought, his eyes on the distant glow. *Kian Santang and Bagus. Into that fire. Into that wound. While I ride in the opposite direction, toward politics and deception and the subtle, bloodless war of courts.* The thought carried guilt, as it always did -- the persistent, gnawing guilt of a man who knows that someone else is bearing the greater burden, that the real hero of the story is not the king on horseback but the small, frightened, impossibly brave figure climbing into the dark with a blade that might save or destroy the world.

*But I cannot climb for him. He cannot negotiate for me. We each have our mountain. The keris and the throne. The fire and the court. Two paths to the same end.*

They crossed the remains of a village as the moon rose -- a pale, white disc emerging from behind the eastern hills with the slow, inevitable gravity of a *dalang* revealing a new scene, its light transforming the savanna from silver to a deeper, more luminous white that washed the colors from the world and replaced them with a thousand variations of pale. The village was abandoned -- not destroyed, which would have been easier to process, but simply emptied, left behind with the casual, heartbreaking incompleteness of a life interrupted in mid-sentence. Thatched roofs still covered most of the houses, though some had begun to sag under the weight of neglect. A wooden gate stood open on its hinges, swinging gently in the night wind, creaking with a rhythm that was almost musical. A *lesung* -- a wooden mortar for pounding rice -- sat in the central yard, still containing the husks of the last batch of rice that had been processed before the inhabitants fled. A child's toy -- a carved wooden horse, small and crude and heartbreaking in its artlessness -- lay in the dust of the main path, and as the column passed, Surya stepped around it with the delicate, precise foot-placement of an animal that understood, in whatever way animals understand such things, that some objects are too important to be trampled.

"The eastern villages emptied first," Jayanegara said, his voice carrying back along the column, low and flat. "The Buta scouts came at night. They did not attack -- not at first. They simply appeared. At the edge of the fields. At the village gate. Standing, watching. Making themselves known. And the next morning, the families would begin to pack. No one commanded them to leave. No one needed to. The presence was enough. The *hawa pati* -- the scent of death -- clung to the ground where the Buta had stood, and the children would not stop crying, and the animals refused to eat, and the wells tasted of iron. So they left. One family, then ten, then the whole village. East, toward Kediri. Away from the shadow."

"And when the villages are empty," Ratnaningsih added, her voice carrying from further back in the column, "the Buta burn the fields. Not to destroy the food -- though that is a consequence -- but to destroy the *connection*. The bond between the people and their land. A burned field can be replanted. A broken bond cannot. When the refugees reach Kediri, they are no longer farmers. They are dependents. Mouths to feed. A burden. And that is what Surasenta wants. A kingdom overwhelmed by its own displaced people, too busy surviving to fight."

The column rode on, through the ghost village and out the other side, into the open savanna again, and the abandoned houses fell behind them, and the swinging gate creaked in the darkness, and the carved wooden horse lay in the dust, and no one came to pick it up.

They crossed two more dry riverbeds before midnight -- tributaries of the Kali Kering, equally desiccated, their stones gleaming in the moonlight like the bones of a world that was slowly starving. At each crossing, the horses' hooves clattered on the exposed rock with a sound that carried far in the night air, and the scouts who rode ahead of the column signaled back with quick, precise gestures of their *tombak* blades -- *clear, clear, continue* -- and the column flowed across the riverbeds like water finding its way through a landscape of obstacles, each rider trusting the rider ahead, each horse following the horse before it, the collective intelligence of the formation navigating terrain that would have stymied an individual rider and barely slowing the group.

Raden Wijaya felt something changing within him as they rode -- a shift in the internal landscape that paralleled the shift in the external one, a realignment of the emotional tectonic plates that had been grinding against each other since the burning island. The weight was still there -- the weight of the oath, the weight of the fallen, the weight of the future that pressed against the present like the sea presses against a dike. But the weight was no longer unsupported. The weight now sat upon a foundation, however narrow, however fragile -- the foundation of thirty-two riders who had chosen to carry it with him.

He looked at the column stretching behind him, and in the moonlight the riders were not individuals but a single, sinuous entity, a serpent of horse and man and steel that moved through the grass with a purpose that transcended the concerns of any single constituent. This was the beginning. This was the first thread of the alliance that the Dwija Putih had spoken of, the first fiber spun, the first stone set in the foundation of the resistance that would either save Nusantara or perish in the attempt. It was not an army. It was not a force capable of besieging a capital or meeting the Buta Raksasa in open battle. But it was a *commitment* -- a declaration, spoken not in words but in the act of riding, of moving toward danger instead of away from it, of choosing to be part of the story rather than a footnote to its ending.

Dewi Anjani rode beside him now, having moved up from her position in the column with the silent, effortless ease of a Bidadari navigating the mortal world. Her mare moved in perfect synchrony with Surya, the two horses matching stride for stride as though choreographed, and the Bidadari sat her mount with the supernatural grace that made watching her ride feel less like observing a physical action and more like witnessing a spiritual one -- a meditation conducted at speed, a prayer expressed through motion.

"You are thinking of Kian Santang," she said. Not a question.

"I am always thinking of Kian Santang," Raden Wijaya admitted. "Of the boy climbing the mountain. Of the weight he carries. Of the fact that I am here, on horseback, with allies and a plan, and he is there, alone, in the fire."

"He is not alone," Anjani said. "Bagus is with him. And the *karuhun* watch."

"The *karuhun* watch," Raden Wijaya repeated, and the word -- ancestors -- carried a weight that was both comfort and accusation, the weight of all the dead kings and dead warriors and dead ordinary people who had built Nusantara over millennia and whose legacy was now threatened by a darkness that sought to unmake everything they had created. "But watching is not carrying. The boy carries the keris. The boy carries the hope. And we ride in the opposite direction."

"You ride toward the other battle," Anjani said, and her golden eyes caught the moonlight and held it, and in that light her face was the face of something ancient and patient and profoundly certain, the face of a being who had watched the patterns of mortal history long enough to see the shapes that mortals themselves could not perceive. "There are two towers of fire in this war, *Manusa*. Two *menara berapi*. Krakatau and Merapi. The keris and the throne. Kian Santang climbs toward one. You ride toward the other. Both must fall for the darkness to break. Neither alone is sufficient."

She turned her face toward the distant glow of Merapi, and the Bidadari light stirred within her, responding to the proximity of the mountain's dark *sakti* the way a compass needle responds to iron -- not seeking it but acknowledging it, marking its direction, measuring its strength.

"The old prophecies speak of this night," she said. "Not specifically -- prophecy does not deal in specifics, because the future is not a road but a river, and rivers change course. But they speak of a night when the horse-lords ride again under the sign of the heir, and the two towers tremble, and the darkness, which has been certain of its victory, begins for the first time to doubt."

"Prophecies," Batu grunted, from somewhere behind them, his voice carrying the particular inflection of a Kurcaci who has been on horseback for too long and has developed opinions about the experience. "Prophecies are what mystics produce when they're too lazy to pick up a hammer. Give me iron over prophecy any day. Iron you can trust. Prophecy is just iron that hasn't been forged yet."

"An observation of unexpected philosophical depth," Anjani said.

"I'm a deep person," Batu said. "Currently deep in discomfort. When do we stop riding? My spine is developing structural concerns."

"At dawn," Kamandanu called back from the head of the column, and his voice carried a thread of something that had not been there before -- amusement, perhaps, or the relaxation that comes when a decision has been made and the mind is freed from the burden of choosing. "There is a fortified watchtower at the edge of the Mataram forest. We will rest there, water the horses, and plan the approach to Giri Mataram. The prince will need to look less like a vagrant and more like an heir if we are to achieve anything at the court."

"I can clean the batik," Anjani offered.

"The batik is beyond cleaning," Batu said. "The batik is beyond batik. The batik has evolved into a new substance. If we presented it to the court, they would think we were declaring war through the medium of textile."

Raden Wijaya did smile, then. A small, quiet smile that the moonlight caught and the darkness absorbed, a smile that was not joy but something adjacent to it -- the recognition that even in the midst of war, even riding through a wounded land toward a battle whose outcome was anything but certain, the human capacity for humor persisted, stubborn and irreducible, a fire that no darkness could fully extinguish because it burned on a fuel that the darkness could not reach.

The column rode on. The moon climbed. The stars wheeled above them in their ancient, indifferent procession, the constellation of Waluku -- the Plough, the star-pattern that Nusantara's farmers had navigated by for a thousand years -- standing bright and clear above the eastern horizon, pointing the way as it had always pointed the way, toward the dawn, toward the future, toward whatever waited there with open arms or bared teeth.

Behind them, the Kali Kering and the ghost village and the abandoned toy receded into the distance, swallowed by the vast, silver darkness of the savanna. Ahead, the dark mass of the Mataram forest grew larger against the sky, its boundary visible now as a wall of shadow that stretched from north to south across their path like the rampart of a fortress, and above it -- always above it, always present, always burning -- the plume of Merapi, the dark tower of fire, the volcanic heart of the island where the Shadow King's power festered and grew.

Raden Wijaya rode toward it, and the stallion Surya carried him, and beside him rode a Bidadari whose light answered the truth and a Kurcaci whose hammer answered everything else, and behind him rode thirty-two warriors of the Kediri plains who had chosen, in the face of overwhelming odds and the reasonable counsel of despair, to ride toward the fire instead of away from it.

It was not an army. Not yet.

But it was the first thread, spun in dust and starlight and the ancient, stubborn *dharma* of men who refused to let the darkness have the final word. And threads, given time and will and the weaver's patient hand, become rope. And rope becomes net. And net becomes the thing that catches the falling world and holds it, trembling, above the void.

The Horse Lords of Kediri rode east through the moonlit savanna, and the grass whispered behind them, and the stars burned above them, and the distant volcano pulsed with its sullen, patient fire, and the night was large and dark and full of the sound of hooves.
