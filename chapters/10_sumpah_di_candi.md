# Bab 10: Sumpah di Candi
## (The Oath at the Temple)

---

They left the Dieng Plateau on a morning of rain and mist, nine figures descending the southern trail in single file, and the ancient temples at their backs dissolved into cloud as though the stones themselves were reclaiming their solitude, withdrawing behind veils of grey to wait, as they had always waited, for the next age of the world.

Eyang Guru Batara led them. His staff struck the wet stone of the path with a rhythm as steady as a heartbeat, and where the iron tip touched the earth it left small sparks that the rain extinguished before they could catch -- or perhaps that was only a trick of the mist. He walked with the long, unhurried stride of a man who had spent the greater part of his life walking and who understood, as few did, that the pace of a journey mattered more than its speed. Behind him came Raden Wijaya, the prince of Mataram, who moved with a silence that seemed to offend the very concept of sound -- his feet finding the driest stones, the firmest earth, without apparent effort, his body flowing through the mist like something poured rather than walking. He had volunteered to scout, and for the first hour he ranged ahead, disappearing into the grey and reappearing minutes later with a slight shake of his head -- *clear* -- before falling back to let Eyang Guru resume the lead.

Dewi Anjani walked behind them, and the rain did not touch her.

This was not entirely true. The rain fell upon her as it fell upon them all, but it seemed reluctant, as though the drops hesitated at the last instant before striking her skin, and when they did land -- on the silver-white fabric of her traveling cloak, on the dark fall of her hair -- they rolled away without leaving any trace of wetness. She walked with a grace that made the others feel clumsy by proximity, her bare feet silent on the stone, her golden eyes scanning the landscape with a penetrating attention that had nothing to do with the physical world. She was listening -- not with her ears but with some deeper faculty, some Bidadari sense attuned to the spiritual currents that flowed through the earth like underground rivers, the invisible channels of *bayu* that connected the sacred places of the archipelago the way roots connect the trees of a wana. Twice in the first hour she paused, her head tilted, her lips parted, and the others stopped with her, holding their breath. But both times she shook her head and walked on, and the tension eased, and the rain continued to fall.

Tumenggung Batu came next, and the Kurcaci chieftain was miserable.

He was a creature of stone and shadow, born in the deep galleries of Gunung Sewu, raised in the amber glow of forge-light and the warm dark of limestone chambers. The open sky above him was an insult -- vast, empty, uncontained. He had no ceiling. No walls. Nothing between his iron helm and the infinite grey expanse of cloud but air, endless and offensive air, and the rain that fell from it struck his armor with a sound like gravel on tin. He walked with his shoulders hunched and his broad jaw set, his war-axe strapped to his back, his stone-grey hands balled into fists inside his leather gauntlets.

"Too much sky," he muttered, for the fourth time that morning. "Too much nothing. A dwarf needs rock over his head. Rock and root and the honest dark."

"You will have your rock soon enough, Tumenggung," Eyang Guru said from the front, without turning. "More than you wish, perhaps, before this road is done."

Gajahmada walked at the column's rear -- by choice, not by assignment. The Majapahit general had the restless energy of a man built for decisive action who found himself bound to the plodding pace of a mixed company. His stride was long and martial, his posture erect even in the rain, his hand resting on the hilt of his keris -- not the Keris Murka, no, but his own blade, the weapon of a tumenggung of the Gajah Mada regiment, its pamor patterns forged in the royal foundries of Trowulan. He watched the trail behind them with a soldier's vigilance, but his eyes kept drifting forward, toward the small figure at the center of the column, toward the wrapped bundle on Kian Santang's back that seemed to bend the light around it, pulling at attention the way a lodestone pulls iron filings.

And between the warriors and the wise, between the vanguard and the rear guard, the four Tuyul walked together, as they had always walked together, since boyhood, since the sawah paths of Padalarang -- but now they walked through a world that had grown immeasurably larger and immeasurably more dangerous, and the paths they followed led not to familiar fields but toward the molten heart of Krakatau.

Bagus walked at Kian Santang's right shoulder, steady as a second shadow, his golok at his belt, his pack neatly balanced, his broad brown face calm. He carried enough provisions for the Tuyul contingent -- nasi timbel, dried fish, sambal, a clay pot of jamu, dried cassava chips wrapped in pandanus leaf -- and he had distributed the weight with the careful efficiency of a man who understood that comfort on the road was not luxury but survival. He said little. He watched much. When the path narrowed or the stones grew slick, his hand appeared at Kian Santang's elbow, guiding without grasping, supporting without diminishing.

Cepot walked at Kian Santang's left, and he talked.

He talked about the food they had eaten at the council in Dieng -- "The gudeg was acceptable, I suppose, if you enjoy the taste of jackfruit cooked by people who have never met a jackfruit." He talked about Tumenggung Batu's armor -- "How does he not sink? If we cross a river, do we fish him out or leave him as a monument?" He talked about the rain, the path, the quality of the air in the highlands compared to the lowlands, the relative merits of rambutan versus mangosteen, and a complicated theory he had developed about the migratory patterns of the Javan hawk-eagle that was almost certainly wrong but was delivered with such conviction that Dawala nodded along as though receiving sacred wisdom.

Dawala walked behind them all, eating.

He had begun the morning with a banana. Then a portion of dried cassava. Then, somewhere around the second hour, he had produced from the depths of his pack a parcel of klepon -- small rice-flour balls filled with melted palm sugar, rolled in grated coconut -- that he ate one by one, his round face bright with simple pleasure, green crumbs of coconut freckling his chin. Where he had acquired klepon for the journey was a mystery. That he would eat them while descending a mountain in a rainstorm was not.

Kian Santang carried the keris.

He no longer strapped it to his back. Eyang Guru had fashioned a new wrapping -- layers of sacred batik, the patterns drawn by the sage's own hand in a dye made from indigo and turmeric and something else, something that smelled of temple smoke and old mantras. The keris rested in a bamboo case hung from Kian Santang's shoulder, and even through the wrapping and the case and the strap, he could feel it. Not the burning heat of those first terrible days after Padalarang -- the batik and Eyang Guru's blessings held that at bay -- but a weight. A presence. A low, constant murmuring at the edge of his awareness, like the hum of a gamelan heard through walls, like a conversation in a room he could not enter but could not stop overhearing. The Keris Murka did not sleep. It waited. It listened. And sometimes, in moments of silence -- when the rain eased, when Cepot paused for breath, when the forest sounds fell away -- it whispered to him in a language that was not language, in images that rose unbidden behind his eyes: a throne of black stone, a crown of fire, a world bent in submission.

He shook his head and walked on.

---

They descended through the volcanic highlands of central Jawa for three days, and the landscape changed around them like the movements of a wayang performance -- scene by scene, each one more vivid than the last, as though the island itself were telling a story.

On the first day, the path led through fields of volcanic ash and scoria, grey and lifeless, where the skeleton of Gunung Prahu loomed to the west, its caldera open like a wound in the earth. Steam rose from vents in the grey terrain, smelling of sulfur and hot metal, and Eyang Guru paused at each one, pressing his palm to the warm stone, his eyes closed, reading the mountain's mood the way a healer reads a pulse. "The fire beneath is restless," he murmured. "It has been restless since the Keris Murka was found. The mountains feel it. They remember."

He knew everything, the old empu. Every stone had a story. Every plant had a purpose. He pointed to a spray of edelweiss growing impossibly in a crack of volcanic rock -- "Bunga abadi, the eternal flower. *Tumuwuh* where nothing else will. Take heart from it." He identified a tangle of roots at the trailside as akar bahar, black coral root, dried and carried as protection against malign spirits -- "*jimat* of the earth itself, blessed by the hyang of the mountain." He stopped the company once to kneel beside a small spring bubbling from the base of a boulder, and when he dipped his fingers in and tasted the water, he smiled -- "Clean. Blessed. The mountain gives what it can."

On the second day, the grey ash gave way to green. They descended into the lush valleys between the volcanic peaks, where the soil was black and rich and the vegetation exploded with tropical exuberance -- stands of wild banana with leaves as broad as shields, groves of bamboo so tall and dense they formed green tunnels through which the sunlight filtered in shifting patterns of gold and emerald, thickets of wild ginger and galangal whose crushed leaves released a fragrance that made Dawala close his eyes and sigh. The terraced sawah appeared on the lower slopes, cascading down the hillsides in steps of reflected sky, and the sound of water returned -- water in the irrigation channels, water in the paddies, water tumbling down the mountainside in white threads of waterfall.

They passed through villages -- small highland lembur where the people looked up from their work with curiosity and caution. A company of nine was unusual: four small Tuyul, a towering old sage, a Javanese prince in dark traveling clothes, a woman of unearthly beauty, a grey-skinned dwarf in iron armor, and a warrior whose bearing announced his rank with every step. The villagers offered them food and shelter with the customary hospitality of the Javanese highlands -- rice and sayur asem and sweet wedang jahe -- and asked no questions, because the highlands had their own code: a traveler's business was his own, and a host's duty was to feed and shelter, not to pry.

On the third morning, Raden Wijaya returned from scouting with a different expression on his face.

"Candi," he said. "An old temple complex. Half a day's walk south. And beyond it -- Merapi."

Eyang Guru's hand tightened on his staff.

---

The candi rose from the jungle like the bones of a forgotten god.

There were seven temples, or the remains of seven -- dark stone structures, square at the base and tapering to broken spires, arranged in a pattern that was unmistakably a mandala -- the sacred geometry of the cosmos rendered in stone, each temple corresponding to a cardinal direction, a divine principle, a layer of existence. The central candi had been the axis mundi, the pillar connecting Bhur-loka below to Swarga-loka above, and even in ruin its proportions spoke of a precision that was not merely architectural but cosmological. Roots of banyan trees had insinuated themselves into the stonework, prying apart the fitted blocks with the patient strength of centuries, so that in places the temples seemed to be growing from the trees, or the trees from the temples, and the boundary between built and grown had dissolved into something new, something that was neither architecture nor forest but a collaboration between them. Moss covered the lower stones in a velvet of green so deep it was nearly black. Ferns sprouted from every crevice. The air smelled of wet stone and old incense and the slow decay of fallen leaves.

The carvings were still visible.

They covered the temple walls in dense panels -- relief sculptures depicting scenes that Kian Santang recognized from the lontar manuscripts he had read by candlelight in Padalarang, a lifetime ago. Here were the armies of the First Alliance: the Bidadari descending from their mountain sanctuaries, luminous even in stone; the Kurcaci emerging from the earth in ranked formations, axes and hammers raised; the warriors of Mataram and Sriwijaya in their battle array; and among them, smaller figures -- Tuyul, armed with spears and knives, standing firm. And facing them all, carved with a ferocity that the sculptor's chisel had not diminished across the centuries: the Buta Raksasa, the demon giants, and behind them, enthroned in fire, a figure with no face and a crown of serpents.

Raja Iblis.

"This place," Eyang Guru said quietly, running his fingers along the carved stone, "was built to remember. To warn." He turned to face the company. The filtered jungle light fell across his features, casting his deep-set eyes into shadow, and in that moment he looked as ancient and weathered as the temple itself. "It is fitting that we stop here. There is something that must be done."

He led them through a passage choked with vines into the inner sanctum of the central temple. The chamber was small, perhaps ten paces across, roofed in darkness where the upper structure had held against the jungle's advance. The walls were carved with the same relief panels, but here the scenes were different -- not battle but ceremony. Figures standing in a circle, hands placed upon a central pillar, their faces lifted, their mouths open in what might have been song or oath. Above the relief, inscribed in a band of elegant Pallawa script -- the ancient lettering of the sacred texts -- ran a line of kakawin verse, the old Javanese poetic form that wove Sanskrit meter with the rhythms of the archipelago's own tongue.

At the center of the chamber stood a lingga.

The sacred stone pillar rose from the floor to the height of a man's chest, carved from a single block of black andesite, its surface worn smooth by the touch of uncounted hands across uncounted years. It rose from a yoni -- the carved stone basin that was its complement, the feminine principle cradling the masculine, the receptive earth embracing the generative sky. Together they were the symbol of cosmic unity, of creation itself, the same force that drove the seed through the soil and the magma through the mountain and the thought through the mind. It was old -- older, perhaps, than the temple that housed it, older than the carvings on the walls, a thing from the deepest stratum of belief, from the time before temples, when the sacred was not housed in stone but was the stone itself. Despite the jungle's dominion over the rest of the complex, the lingga-yoni was untouched. No moss grew on it. No vine had dared to climb it. It stood in the dim chamber like a dark flame, silent and absolute.

"A Sumpah Suci," Eyang Guru said. "A sacred oath. *Mulah* we go further -- into the dark, into danger, into whatever awaits us on the road to Krakatau -- without first binding ourselves. Not merely by word. By *atma*. By the ancient *bayu* that still sleeps in this stone."

He looked at each of them in turn. His gaze was gentle but unyielding, and it carried the weight of knowledge that none of them fully shared -- knowledge of what lay ahead, of what the oath would mean, of what it would cost.

"This is no small thing," he continued. "An oath *ginawé* upon a lingga, in a mandala of ancient *bayu*, is a chain stronger than iron. *Mulah* it be broken without breaking the one who swore it. If any among you has doubt -- if any among you would turn back -- now is the moment. There is no shame in it. The road to Krakatau is not for the unwilling."

Silence. The jungle sounds -- birds, insects, the drip of water -- seemed distant, muffled, as though the temple had drawn a curtain between its inner world and the outer.

Gajahmada stepped forward first. "I swore the Sumpah Palapa before the court of Majapahit," he said, his voice deep and resonant in the stone chamber. Then he spoke in *krama inggil* -- the highest register of Javanese, the language of sacred vows and royal pronouncement -- and the ancient syllables rang against the temple walls like bronze bells: "*Kula sampun ngucapaken sumpah wonten ing ngarsanipun para Dewata lan para leluhur. Kula badhe mboten kendel ngantos Nusantara manunggal. Sumpah punika kula ucapaken malih.*" *I have spoken my oath before the Dewata and the ancestors. I shall not rest until Nusantara is united. This oath I speak again.* "This keris threatens that unity. My oath is already made. I will swear it again."

Raden Wijaya inclined his head. "The Aliran Mataram does not break its word. I will swear."

Dewi Anjani spoke without moving, her voice the sound of wind through silver chimes, each word shaped in the *basa luhur* that was her birthright: "*Bidadari parantos sumpah nalika fajar dunya. Eta sumpah ngalir dina getih ingwang. Ingwang bade sumpah.*" *The Bidadari swore the first oath at the dawn of the world. I carry that oath in my blood. I shall swear.*

Tumenggung Batu struck his fist against his breastplate, and the iron rang. "The deep roads of the Kurcaci have never bowed to darkness. I will swear."

Bagus said simply: "Where Kian Santang goes, I go."

Cepot looked around the circle with an expression of theatrical resignation. "I had plans, you know. I was going to sell sangu and lalab by the Citarum. Best sangu bakar in Pasundan. But fine. Destiny, fate, sacred oaths, probable death. *Nya kumaha atuh,*" he sighed in easy Sundanese. *Well, what can you do.* "I will swear."

Dawala, who had been quietly chewing a piece of dried cassava, swallowed and said: "I will swear too. Can we eat after?"

Eight voices. Eight oaths, offered freely. Eyang Guru nodded, and something in his eyes -- gratitude, perhaps, or grief for what he was asking of them -- gleamed in the dim light before he blinked it away.

Before the oath could be spoken, Eyang Guru knelt at the base of the lingga-yoni and prepared the homa -- the sacred fire ceremony that must consecrate any oath of binding. He cleared a small space on the flagstone floor and built a fire-pit of nine stones, each one gathered from the temple grounds. Into the center he placed dried wood shavings and a twist of kemenyan incense. He struck flint to steel, and the spark caught, and a small, steady flame rose in the dimness of the sanctum -- not the golden blaze of his staff's power but an earthly fire, a mortal flame, the kind that the first priests had kindled in the first temples when the world was new.

Into the fire he fed offerings, speaking mantras from the Weda as he did: clarified ghee that made the flame leap and brighten, grains of rice that popped and scattered like tiny stars, dried petals of frangipani and cempaka that curled and blackened and released their sweetness into the sacred smoke. A Balinese *pandita* -- a priest of the old rites -- would have recognized the ceremony, for it was of the same lineage as the homa fires still tended in the temples of Bali, where the Hindu-Buddhist traditions of the ancient kingdoms survived in unbroken succession. The smoke rose in a column through the chamber, straight and unwavering despite the absence of any chimney, as though the fire knew its purpose and directed its breath toward the sky.

"Stand in a circle," he said. "Place your weapon hand upon the lingga."

They arranged themselves around the sacred pillar. Nine hands -- large and small, calloused and smooth, brown and grey and pale -- reached out and pressed against the black stone. The lingga was warm. Not the feverish heat of the Keris Murka, but a deep, slow warmth, like sun-heated earth, like the memory of fire that had burned long ago and left its warmth embedded in the stone itself. Beneath their palms, the yoni's basin seemed to hum -- a vibration too low for hearing but felt in the bones, the resonance of the earth-mother holding the sky-father, creation cradled in the oldest embrace.

Eyang Guru spoke the words.

He spoke first in Sanskrit -- the language of the Weda, the tongue of the mantras that had consecrated every temple and every oath since the rishis first taught the sacred syllables to the peoples of the archipelago. Then he rendered the oath in the common tongue, so that each of them could carry its meaning in their heart:

"Demi api yang menempa kami, dan bumi yang menopang kami -- aku mengikat takdirku pada pencarian ini dan pada priya-priyaku, hingga Keris Murka dimusnahkan atau maut menjemputku."

*By the fire that forged us and the earth that holds us -- I bind my fate to this quest and to my priya, until the Keris Murka is unmade or death takes me.*

Each of them repeated the words. Gajahmada, his voice a rumble of iron conviction, speaking in the *krama inggil* that transformed the oath into a royal decree. Raden Wijaya, quiet and precise, his Javanese inflected with the archaic formality of the Mataram court. Dewi Anjani, her voice carrying harmonics that no human throat could produce, the words ascending into the *basa luhur* of the Bidadari, each syllable luminous with spiritual authority. Tumenggung Batu, the syllables shaped by a tongue accustomed to the guttural cadences of Kurcaci speech. Bagus, steady. Cepot, for once entirely without irony. Dawala, stumbling slightly over the formal Javanese but meaning every word.

As each voice spoke, the homa fire leaped -- responding to each oath as though the flame were a living witness, its light brightening with Gajahmada's thunder, steadying with Bagus's quiet resolve, flickering with playful warmth at Cepot's reluctant sincerity.

The letters carved into the stone above them -- the kakawin verse, the Pallawa script -- seemed to glow faintly in the firelight, and Kian Santang thought of the tale of Ajisaka, who first brought the hanacaraka script to the people of Java -- each character a covenant between knowledge and responsibility, each letter a promise that the power of the written word would serve wisdom and not tyranny. The oath they swore now was of that same lineage -- words given weight, made binding, *tinulis* not into stone but into *atma*.

Kian Santang swore last.

He opened his mouth and spoke, and the words came out clear and steady, and they were his own voice and his own will, and the Keris Murka *screamed*.

Not aloud. Not in any sound the ears could hear. But through the bamboo case and the sacred batik wrapping, the blade convulsed with a pulse of dark energy that struck Kian Santang's palm like a physical blow. He staggered. The temple stones beneath their feet thrummed, vibrating at a frequency that rattled teeth and blurred vision. The keris fought the oath -- fought the sacred energy of the lingga, the ancient power bound into the stone -- fought it with the blind fury of a thing that recognized its antithesis, its enemy, the kind of power that could not be corrupted because it was built on sacrifice rather than domination.

The carvings on the walls blazed.

A golden light, warm and steady, flowed through the incised lines of the relief panels as though the stone had become transparent and a great fire burned behind it. The figures of the First Alliance -- Bidadari, Kurcaci, Tuyul, human warriors -- seemed to move, to breathe, to turn their carved faces toward the nine who stood in their echo, swearing their echo of that ancient oath. The lingga pulsed beneath their hands, and the warmth intensified, and the homa fire blazed high and bright, and Kian Santang felt something pass through him -- not the keris's dark whisper but something older, cleaner, fiercer: a current of *bayu* that was not his alone but belonged to everyone who had ever stood in a mandala and said *Mulah the darkness prevail*.

Then the light faded. The carvings were stone again. The lingga cooled beneath their palms. The homa fire settled to embers, its sacred smoke still rising in a thin, unwavering column.

*Tinangtu.* It was done. They were bound.

Kian Santang withdrew his hand. His palm was marked -- a faint imprint, like a burn that had healed to a scar, in the shape of the lingga's worn surface. He looked around the circle and saw the same mark on every hand. Nine palms, nine marks. The oath made visible.

Cepot examined his palm with raised eyebrows. "Well," he said. "That's going to be difficult to explain at the pasar."

No one laughed, but no one needed to. Something had settled over the company -- a quiet cohesion, a sense of interlocking purpose, like the notes of a gamelan finding their shared rhythm. They were no longer nine individuals traveling in the same direction. They were a pasukan. A fellowship. Bound by word and stone and the ancient fire that still burned, somewhere deep in the bones of Nusantara, waiting to be called.

---

They saw Gunung Merapi on the morning of the fifth day, and the sight stopped them where they stood.

Eyang Guru had intended to lead them through the pass between Gunung Merbabu to the north and Gunung Merapi to the south -- a wide highland saddle that would bring them down to the lowlands beyond, where they could turn east toward Gunung Sewu and the caves. It was the direct route. The obvious route. The route that, under ordinary circumstances, would have been nothing more than a strenuous walk through volcanic country.

But Gunung Merapi was no longer an ordinary mountain.

Merapi had always smoked. Every child in central Jawa knew the mountain's temper -- the periodic eruptions, the rivers of lahar, the ashfall that dusted the rice paddies in grey. The mountain was alive, restless, dangerous in the way that all volcanoes are dangerous: a reminder that the earth is not solid ground but a thin shell over an ocean of fire. The people of the surrounding villages had lived with Merapi's moods for centuries, reading the mountain's smoke the way sailors read clouds, knowing when to stay and when to flee.

But this smoke was wrong.

It rose from the summit in a column of black so dense it seemed solid -- not the grey-white plume of volcanic gas and ash that was Merapi's natural breath, but something oily, something that moved with intention, coiling and uncoiling against the sky like a living thing. Where ordinary volcanic smoke dispersed on the wind, this smoke defied the wind, holding its shape, spreading outward in tendrils that reached across the sky like the fingers of a vast and grasping hand. It was black in a way that had nothing to do with the absence of light and everything to do with the presence of something else -- a darkness that was not merely visual but spiritual, a corruption of the air itself, as though the smoke were not rising from the mountain but leaking into the world from somewhere outside of it.

The corruption reminded the elders of Calon Arang, the widow-witch of Girah whose rage had transformed her into a vessel of black magic -- a warning from the old tales that grief perverted becomes destruction. Empu Gandring's darkness bore the same signature: not the natural malice of demons born to shadow, but the twisted fury of knowledge turned against itself, of sacred power inverted, of a soul that had once served the light and now fed on its corruption.

Beneath the smoke, the mountain had changed.

The upper slopes, which should have been clothed in the dark green of montane forest, were bare. Not burned -- burning leaves a particular quality of desolation, a recognizable aftermath. This was different. The trees were still standing, but they were dead -- grey, skeletal, stripped of leaf and bark and life, their branches twisted into shapes that no natural death would produce, like a *wanantara* corrupted from within. They looked wrenched. Tortured. As though the life had been pulled from them by force, sucked out through their roots into the earth, leaving only husks that stood like monuments to what they had been.

The earth around the mountain's base was black. Not the grey-black of volcanic soil but a wet, gleaming black, like spilled ink, like the skin of something that had crawled up from a depth where light had never reached. And in the black earth, things moved. At this distance -- they stood on a ridge perhaps five leagues from the mountain's base -- the shapes were indistinct, but Raden Wijaya, whose eyes were the sharpest among them, described what he saw in a low, tight voice.

"Buta Raksasa. Dozens of them. Patrolling the pass in formations. And forges -- I can see forge-fires on the lower slopes. They are making weapons."

"Empu Gandring's stronghold," Eyang Guru said. His voice was flat, drained of its usual warmth. He leaned on his staff and looked at the corrupted mountain with an expression that Kian Santang had seen only once before -- on the night of Seren Taun, when the old sage had spoken with Uncle Kian beneath the eaves of the meeting hall. Fear. Deep, knowing, ancient fear. "He has made Merapi his fortress. His eyes are on the mountain passes. He watches for us."

"Then we fight through," Gajahmada said. He stepped forward, his hand on his keris, his jaw set with the hard certainty of a man who had won battles against worse odds and saw no reason to stop now. "*Kula siap perang,*" he added in terse Javanese *ngoko* -- the blunt register of the battlefield. *I am ready to fight.* "We have warriors enough. The Buta are strong but stupid. A forced march through the pass at night, with your sorcery to cover our approach -- we could be through before dawn."

"No." Raden Wijaya's voice was quiet but it cut through Gajahmada's certainty like a blade through silk. "*Aja grusa-grusu, Tumenggung,*" he said, matching Gajahmada's *ngoko* with his own -- the direct speech of one warrior to another, stripped of courtly pretense. *Do not be reckless, General.* "Stealth, not strength. We are nine. They are hundreds. And it is not the Buta I fear -- it is Gandring himself. If we are detected, he will know what we carry. He will send word to his master. Every Leak in Nusantara will converge on our position." The prince's dark eyes met Gajahmada's. "We cannot alert Gandring. We cannot fight this battle and win the war."

Gajahmada's face tightened. For a moment the two warriors held each other's gaze -- the general and the prince, two men accustomed to command, neither willing to yield -- and the air between them crackled with the tension of incompatible certainties.

"There is another way."

Tumenggung Batu's gravelled voice broke the standoff. The Kurcaci chieftain had been staring not at Merapi but south, toward the distant line of pale limestone hills that rose above the forest canopy like the spine of a buried beast.

"Gunung Sewu," he said. "The Thousand Giri. Beneath them -- the Gua Seribu Ruang. The Cave of a Thousand Chambers." He turned to face the company, and his stone-grey face held an expression that was part pride and part something else -- something cautious, something that knew the weight of what he was proposing. "My people know the deep roads. The Kurcaci have walked them since before the first candi was raised. I can lead us through -- beneath the mountains, beneath Gandring's gaze, emerging on the far side of the highlands, within reach of the southern coast."

Eyang Guru was silent for a long time. He stood on the ridge with the wind pulling at his indigo cloak and his white beard streaming, and his eyes moved between the corrupted bulk of Merapi to the north and the pale karst hills of Gunung Sewu to the south, and Kian Santang could see the calculation in his face -- the weighing of dangers, the measuring of evils.

"The deep caves of Gunung Sewu are ancient," the old empu said at last. "Older than the Kurcaci. Older than the kingdoms. Things dwell there that were old when the first Bidadari descended from the sky." He looked at Tumenggung Batu. "You know this."

"I know it," the chieftain said. "But I know the roads. The upper galleries -- the paths my people use -- are safe. Or were safe, when last I walked them."

"And when was that?"

A pause. "Sixty years."

Eyang Guru closed his eyes. The wind blew. The black smoke of Merapi coiled against the sky.

"The caves, then," he said. "But I warn you all -- the deep places hold their own perils. Stay together. Stay alert. And if we must go deep -- deeper than the Kurcaci roads -- be prepared for what we may find."

---

They made camp that night in the lee of a limestone outcropping, sheltered from the wind that blew down from Merapi carrying the faint stench of sulfur and something worse -- something chemical, something rotten, the smell of nature being unmade. Raden Wijaya built the fire small and smokeless, the way the Mataram scouts were trained, feeding it with dry wood that he had carried in his pack for this purpose. The flames gave little light but good warmth, and the nine companions gathered around them in a loose circle, eating their evening meal in a silence that was not uncomfortable but heavy with the weight of the decision made.

Kian Santang sat apart from the fire, his back against the limestone, the bamboo case containing the keris resting on the ground beside him. He could feel it pulsing -- faintly, rhythmically, like a second heartbeat -- and the whispers were louder tonight. The proximity to Merapi, to Gandring's stronghold, had stirred the blade. It sensed its maker's servant, perhaps. Or it sensed the dark forges on the mountain's slopes, the kindred fires, and it yearned toward them the way a river yearns toward the sea.

He was tired. Not the honest fatigue of a day's walking -- he was accustomed to that now, after weeks on the road -- but a deeper weariness, a tiredness of the spirit, a slow erosion caused by the constant effort of carrying something that did not want to be carried, that fought him with every step, that whispered and promised and threatened in equal measure. The oath at the temple had helped -- the mark on his palm pulsed with its own faint warmth, a counterweight to the keris's cold pull -- but the burden was still his. Would always be his. Until Krakatau.

A shadow fell across him. He looked up.

Gajahmada stood there, firelight behind him, his broad frame silhouetted against the glow. He was carrying two cups of wedang jahe, and he offered one to Kian Santang with a gesture that was, for a man of his martial bearing, surprisingly gentle.

"You should eat more," the general said, settling himself on the ground beside Kian Santang with the careful movements of a large man in a small space. "You've eaten less every day since Dieng. The others notice. Bagus watches you as a mother watches a sick child."

Kian Santang took the cup. The wedang jahe was hot, the ginger sharp on his tongue, the palm sugar dissolving in slow sweetness. "I eat enough."

"You don't." Gajahmada was quiet for a moment, sipping his own wedang, his eyes on the fire. When he spoke again, his voice was lower, and there was something in it -- a roughness, a depth -- that had not been there before. "I have led armies, Kian Santang. I have watched men carry burdens that broke them -- not the weight on their backs but the weight on their spirits. The ones who fell were always the ones who carried alone." He turned to look at the young Tuyul, and his dark eyes were earnest, unguarded, and in them Kian Santang saw genuine concern -- the concern of a commander for a soldier in his charge, the concern of a strong man for one who was, by any measure, not strong. "You carry too heavy a burden for one so small. Let me help you bear it."

His gaze dropped -- just for an instant, just a flicker -- to the bamboo case at Kian Santang's side. And the keris responded.

The whispers surged. Not directed at Kian Santang now but outward, toward Gajahmada, reaching for the general with tendrils of dark persuasion that Kian Santang could feel like heat radiating from a forge. The keris sensed something in Gajahmada -- ambition, perhaps. Will. The immense, unyielding force of purpose that had driven a man from a minor court appointment to the leadership of the greatest military force in Nusantara. It was the same quality that made Gajahmada magnificent, and it was the same quality that made him vulnerable, because the Keris Murka did not seduce the weak. It seduced the strong. It whispered to those who already believed they could change the world and offered them the means to do it.

*Give me to him,* the keris breathed. *He is worthy. He is strong. He could wield me as I was meant to be wielded. Give me to him, and see what empires he could build.*

"The burden is mine, Tumenggung Gajahmada," Kian Santang said. His voice was quiet but firm, firmer than he felt, and he laid his marked palm flat against the bamboo case, and the oath's warmth flared, and the whispers dimmed. "It was given to me."

Gajahmada looked at him. Something moved behind his eyes -- a flash of emotion, there and gone, too quick to name with certainty. Frustration. Desire. The sharp, involuntary hunger of a man who has glimpsed something he cannot have and knows, in his deepest self, that wanting it is the first step toward ruin. He blinked, and the expression was gone, replaced by the composed mask of the general, the statesman, the hero of a dozen campaigns.

"Of course," he said. He smiled, and the smile was almost convincing. "Forgive me. I only meant -- you are not alone in this." He rose, nodded once, and walked back to the fire.

Kian Santang watched him go. His hand was still on the bamboo case, and beneath his palm the keris pulsed, slow and satisfied, like a predator that has tested the bars of its cage and found one that, in time, might bend.

From the far side of the fire, half-hidden in shadow, Eyang Guru Batara watched. The old empu's face was unreadable, but his hand, resting on his staff, was white-knuckled with a tension that his expression did not betray.

---

The night deepened. The fire burned low. The companions settled into their cloaks and blankets -- Pasundan weave for the Tuyul, fine Mataram cotton for Raden Wijaya, no blanket at all for Tumenggung Batu, who slept sitting upright in his armor with the contentment of a being for whom cold stone was the softest bed. Raden Wijaya took the first watch, melting into the darkness beyond the firelight with a silence that bordered on the supernatural.

Then Dewi Anjani began to sing.

She sat on a flat stone at the edge of the camp, her legs folded beneath her, her cloak drawn around her shoulders, her face tilted toward the sky where the stars burned through gaps in the cloud. She did not announce her intention to sing. She did not clear her throat or draw attention. The song simply began -- emerging from the silence the way a spring emerges from rock, as though it had always been there, flowing beneath the surface, and had only now found its way into the air.

It was a *Dangdanggula* -- the most exalted of the pupuh forms, a *kawih lalanguan* of ceremony and grandeur and sacred occasion. Its structure was precise and ancient: ten syllables, then ten, then eight, then seven, then twelve, then seven, then eight, then eight, then twelve -- the numerical architecture of beauty itself, each line building upon the last like the tiers of a candi rising toward the sky. The melody moved in the Sundanese pelog scale, its intervals wide and luminous, each phrase lifting slightly higher than the last before descending again in a shower of ornamental notes, never quite returning to where it had begun, always moving, always searching, like a river that remembers the mountain it came from and the sea it has not yet reached.

The words were in the old tongue -- pre-Hindu Sundanese, the language of the mountain spirits and the first farmers, a language of vowels as open as valleys and consonants as soft as rain on leaves. But interwoven with the Sundanese came fragments of kakawin verse -- the Sanskrit-inflected poetry of the ancient courts -- as though the Bidadari's song drew from both traditions at once, weaving the earthly and the celestial into a single thread of sound. Kian Santang could not understand them all, but the fragments he caught were images, not arguments: the stars scattered across the dark water of the sky like white flowers on a black pond. The islands of Nusantara rising from the sea at the world's beginning, each one placed by a divine hand, each one a gift and a responsibility. The first dawn, when light touched the volcanic peaks and turned them gold, and the Bidadari wept to see the beauty of the new-made world.

Dewi Anjani's voice was not human.

It was beautiful in the way that starlight is beautiful -- remote, ancient, belonging to a scale of time and distance that the mortal mind could apprehend but not contain. It carried harmonics that no single throat should have been able to produce -- a fundamental note rich and warm as dark honey, and above it, winding like silver wire, an overtone that seemed to come from somewhere beyond the singer, from the air itself, from the stones, from the deep structure of the night. The two voices -- if they were two -- intertwined in patterns that shifted and recombined with each phrase, creating chords that were at once entirely strange and entirely familiar, as though the listener were remembering music heard before birth, before thought, in the formless dark before the world began.

The effect was immediate and absolute.

Cepot, who had been composing a complaint about the hardness of the ground, fell silent mid-syllable. Dawala's hand, carrying a piece of dried cassava to his mouth, stopped in midair and did not move. Tumenggung Batu, asleep in his armor, opened his eyes and stared at the Bidadari with an expression of stunned reverence, his iron face softened by a wonder that his waking self would never have permitted. Gajahmada, lying in his cloak by the fire, turned his head toward the sound, and the tension in his jaw -- the residual tightness of his conversation with Kian Santang -- eased, dissolved, vanished, as though the music were a solvent that could dissolve ambition and leave only the clean, bare human beneath.

And the keris went quiet.

For the first time since Kian Santang had taken up the burden -- since that terrible night in Padalarang when Eyang Guru had placed the wrapped blade in his hands and the weight of the world had descended on his shoulders -- the whispers stopped. Completely. Utterly. The Keris Murka, that restless, malevolent intelligence, that ceaseless dark murmur, fell silent in the presence of Dewi Anjani's Dangdanggula as though it had been struck dumb. The pulsing ceased. The heat faded. For the duration of the pupuh, the keris was nothing more than what it appeared to be: a slender blade of folded metal, wrapped in cloth, lying in a bamboo case. Inert. Harmless. Just a thing.

Kian Santang exhaled. He had not realized he had been holding his breath. Tears stood in his eyes -- not from sorrow but from relief, the overwhelming relief of a man who has carried a screaming weight for so long that he has forgotten what silence sounds like, and who hears it again and understands, with a shock that is almost physical, what has been taken from him.

The song continued. The stars turned overhead. The fire settled into coals that glowed like the eyes of small, benevolent spirits. The nine companions lay in their cloaks and blankets, wrapped in music that was older than sorrow and stronger than fear, and for those few minutes -- those brief, shining, impossible minutes -- the darkness drew back. The corrupted smoke of Merapi, visible as a darker stain against the northern sky, seemed to thin. The wind softened. The earth beneath them was warm and solid and good, and the world was only this: music and starlight and the warmth of fellowship, the simple, staggering miracle of being alive, together, on the skin of a beautiful and imperiled world.

Dewi Anjani's voice faded. The last note of the Dangdanggula hung in the air like a held breath, trembling at the edge of audibility, and then it was gone.

Silence. But a different silence now -- not the absence of sound but the presence of peace, a silence that had been cleaned and made new by the music that had filled it.

Bagus, who was not a man given to poetry, said quietly: "Thank you."

The Bidadari inclined her head. Her golden eyes were bright with something that might have been tears, though whether Bidadari wept as mortals wept, Kian Santang did not know. "*Lagu-lagu heubeul emut naon anu kami hilap,*" she said, in the highest Sundanese, her voice carrying the authority of ages. *The old songs remember what we forget.* "They remember that the world was made in beauty. That beauty is the first truth, and the last."

One by one, the companions slept. The fire died to ash. The stars wheeled in their ancient courses, indifferent and magnificent, and the night held the small camp in its palm like a treasure it was afraid to drop.

Kian Santang slept last. Before he closed his eyes, he looked at his marked palm -- the faint scar from the lingga, the oath made flesh -- and pressed it against his heart.

The keris was quiet. For now, it was enough.

---

Dawn came grey and cold. The wind had shifted in the night, blowing from the south now, carrying the scent of limestone and deep water -- the breath of caves.

They broke camp in near silence, packing with the efficiency of travelers who have learned each other's rhythms. Eyang Guru spoke briefly, his voice carrying a gravity that the morning light did nothing to soften: "We go east and south. To Gunung Sewu. To the caves."

Tumenggung Batu stepped to the front of the column. It was the first time the Kurcaci chieftain had led, and the change was visible in his bearing -- the hunched shoulders straightened, the grumbling jaw set firm, the small dark eyes lit with a purpose and a knowledge that the open sky had stolen from him. He was going home. Not to his own halls -- those lay far to the south -- but to the stone, the dark, the deep roads. His element.

They walked east through the morning, descending from the volcanic highlands into a landscape unlike anything the Tuyul had seen. The green mountains gave way to pale hills of limestone, rounded and bare, rising from the earth in close-packed ranks like the waves of a petrified sea. Between them, the valleys were narrow and deep, choked with vegetation that grew with a desperate lushness in the thin soil between the rocks. The rivers here did not run on the surface. They sank into the limestone through cracks and sinkholes and vanished, flowing through hidden channels beneath the hills, emerging miles away as springs that burst from cliff faces in curtains of white water.

By midday, they reached the entrance.

The mouth of the cave system opened in the face of a limestone cliff a hundred feet high, a vast arch of pale stone draped in ferns and trailing moss that hung in green curtains across the opening, swaying gently in the air that flowed outward from the darkness within. That air was cold -- shockingly cold after the tropical warmth of the surface, cold and damp and carrying a mineral tang that spoke of deep water and ancient stone and places where the sun had never reached and never would. It moved steadily outward, a slow, rhythmic exhalation, as though the cave system were breathing -- as though the entire karst landscape of Gunung Sewu were a single vast organism, and this was its mouth, and it was alive.

The companions stood before the entrance in a loose semicircle, looking in.

The darkness beyond the arch was absolute. Not the darkness of night, which is softened by starlight and the memory of day, but the darkness of the earth's interior -- a darkness that had substance, that pressed against the eyes, that seemed to absorb not just light but the very concept of illumination. From somewhere deep within, far beyond the reach of daylight, came the sound of water -- a distant, echoing drip that reverberated through unseen chambers and galleries, patient and ceaseless, the metronome of a world without time.

Tumenggung Batu turned to face them. In the grey light of the overcast morning, his stone-colored skin seemed to blend with the limestone around him, as though he were becoming part of the cliff itself, a carving come to life, or a living thing returning to stone.

"Stay close," he said. His gravelled voice carried an authority that it had not held on the open road -- here, at the threshold of the deep places, he was no longer the grumbling tagalong uncomfortable under too much sky. He was a chieftain of the Kurcaci, and the stone was his domain. "Touch nothing that glows. The deep fungi are beautiful, but some are poison, and others -- worse than poison. They attract things." He paused. His dark eyes moved across each face in the company, lingering, measuring, warning. "And if you hear singing that isn't the Bidadari -- run."

He turned and walked into the darkness.

Eyang Guru followed. Then Raden Wijaya. Then Dewi Anjani, whose golden eyes gave off the faintest luminescence in the gloom, twin points of warm light, like candles carried into a cathedral. Then Gajahmada, ducking his head beneath the hanging ferns. Then Bagus, then Cepot, then Dawala, who paused at the threshold to take one last bite of dried cassava and brush the crumbs from his shirt with a solemnity that suggested he understood, in some wordless way, that this was a farewell to daylight.

Kian Santang stood alone before the cave mouth.

He looked back. Behind him, the world lay open and green -- the limestone hills, the distant volcanic peaks, the sky of grey cloud through which, even now, a single shaft of sunlight broke and illuminated a patch of hillside in vivid, painful gold. The wind stirred the ferns at the cave's entrance. A bird sang, somewhere in the scrub -- a perkutut dove, its call a low, liquid trill, the most ordinary and the most beautiful sound in the world.

He looked at the sky. He looked at the light.

Then he turned and followed his companions into the dark.

The ferns closed behind him like a curtain, and the mouth of the cave swallowed the last of the daylight, and the Pasukan Sembilan passed from the world of sun and sky and open air into the deep places of the earth, where old things dwelt in silence, and the dark had teeth.
