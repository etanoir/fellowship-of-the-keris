# Bab 4: Pelarian (The Flight)

---

The rain came like a curtain of needles.

Kian Santang ran through it with the Keris Murka strapped to his back, wrapped in three layers of batik cloth that Eyang Guru had blessed with whispered prayers. The keris was warm against his spine, warm in a way that had nothing to do with his body heat — a slow, sick warmth, like fever, like the breath of something alive pressed against his skin. He tried not to think about it. He tried to think only of the path beneath his feet, the slick clay of the trail that wound down from Eyang Guru's cottage toward the lower terraces of Lembur Padalarang, toward the small house where Bagus lived alone at the edge of the sawah.

The sky was black. The storm had swallowed the moon. Lightning cracked somewhere to the west, and for one frozen instant the entire valley was lit in white — the sawah terraces cascading down like silver stairways, the dark line of the forest beyond, the mountains rising into cloud. Then darkness again, absolute, and the thunder came rolling over the highlands like the voice of a god turning in troubled sleep.

He reached Bagus's cottage and pounded on the door.

It opened before his fist fell a third time. Bagus stood there in his sleeping sarung, hair wild, a small oil lamp in one hand. His broad face showed no surprise. He looked at Kian Santang — soaked, trembling, wide-eyed — and simply stepped aside to let him in.

"I knew," Bagus said, already moving to his storage corner where he kept provisions. "I knew the moment Eyang Guru arrived at Seren Taun. The way he looked at you, Kang. The way he watched the crowd, as if he expected something to come crawling out of the torchlight."

"Bagus, we have to go. Now. Tonight."

"I know." He was already packing. His hands moved with the calm, deliberate speed of a man who had spent his life doing practical things — planting, harvesting, mending, building. While Kian Santang stood dripping and shaking by the door, Bagus assembled provisions with the efficiency of a quartermaster. Nasi timbel, wrapped tight in banana leaves, six parcels of them, firm and fragrant. A clay pot of jamu, stoppered with wax. Dried ikan asin folded in layers of pandanus leaf. A bamboo tube of sambal that he sealed with a twist of rattan. Two rolled blankets of woven cotton. A length of rope. A fire flint.

And last, from the hook by the door, his golok — not a warrior's weapon but a farmer's tool, a broad Sundanese machete with a wooden handle worn smooth by years of use. The blade was thick, the edge well-honed. Bagus had cleared fields with it, split coconuts, trimmed bamboo. He tucked it into his belt with the ease of long habit.

"*Hayu kami indit*," Bagus said quietly — *Let's go* — in the plain, informal Sundanese of one friend speaking to another, as though they were heading to the sawah for a morning's work rather than fleeing into darkness.

"Eyang Guru," Kian Santang said. "He's waiting outside. He says he cannot come with us — he must ride ahead to Dieng, to warn the wise ones."

Bagus paused, just for a moment. "Dieng," he repeated. The name carried weight — the plateau of the ancient temples, days and days to the east. He shouldered his pack and nodded. "Then we go to Dieng."

Outside, in the hammering rain, Eyang Guru sat upon his horse beneath the shadow of a jambu tree. The old sage looked diminished in the darkness, his white robes grey with rain, but his eyes burned with an intensity that belied his age. He leaned down from the saddle and gripped Kian Santang's shoulder with a hand that was all sinew and bone.

"Follow the Citarum north," he said, his voice cutting through the storm. "Then east through the highland forests. Stay beneath the canopy. Do not use the main roads — the Leak will watch them. The batik wrapping will conceal the keris from their senses, but only for a time. Move quickly. Do not stop until you must."

"Eyang —"

"I will meet you at Dieng. At the temple compound. You must trust me in this." The old man's grip tightened. "And you must not draw the keris, Kian Santang. No matter what happens. No matter what you feel. The blade will call to you. It will whisper. It will promise. You must not listen."

Then he released him, and with a word to his horse, Eyang Guru was gone — a grey shape swallowed by the rain and the dark, hooves drumming up the northern trail until the sound was lost beneath the thunder.

Kian Santang and Bagus stood alone in the storm.

"Come," Bagus said. And they ran.

---

They fled through the landscape of their childhood, and it was no longer theirs.

The sawah that had blazed gold at Seren Taun were now flooded mirrors reflecting a sky of ink, and the rice stubble rose from the water like the fingers of drowned hands. The paths between the paddies, where they had walked a thousand times in sunlight, were now treacherous ribbons of mud. Kian Santang slipped and fell twice, and each time the keris flared with heat against his back — a pulse of dark warmth that seemed to respond to his fear, feeding on it, and he thought: *It knows. It knows we are running.*

Bagus pulled him up without a word. Bagus always pulled him up.

They crossed the lower terraces and entered the bamboo groves that bordered the Citarum. Here the rain was fractured by the tall culms into a thousand smaller streams, and the sound changed — from the open roar of rain on water to a closer, more intimate drumming, drops on leaves, on bark, on the hollows of cut bamboo that sang low notes like distant suling flutes. The river itself was invisible in the dark but audible — a deep, muscular rush of water swollen by the storm, running north through the gorge.

Kian Santang strained to listen for pursuit. Every sound became the padding of enormous paws, the creak of shadow-robes, the wet hiss of breath through a wooden mask. But there was only the rain, the river, the bamboo.

Then — distant, muffled by hills but unmistakable — a roar.

Not a tiger's roar. Kian Santang had heard tigers before, the Javan tigers that still haunted the deep forests of Pasundan. This was something else. This was a sound that had no business existing in the natural world, a sound that was to a tiger's roar what a scream is to a whisper — louder, longer, vibrating at a frequency that made his teeth ache and his vision blur. The Harimau Siluman. The spirit-tiger. Behind them and to the south, but not far enough behind.

"Faster," Bagus breathed, and they ran faster.

---

They almost missed the crossroads entirely.

The path forked at a point where the Citarum bent sharply east, and a smaller trail ran down to a bamboo bridge over the gorge. In the darkness and rain, Kian Santang registered the fork only as a moment of confusion — which way? — and then he registered the two figures huddled beneath a rambutan tree at the junction, and his hand went to the keris on his back before his mind caught up with his fear.

"Kang Kian?"

The voice was incredulous. Familiar. Entirely too loud for the circumstances.

"Cepot?" Kian Santang hissed.

Cepot stepped out from beneath the rambutan tree with the insouciant confidence of someone who had never once in his life felt appropriately afraid. He was small, even for a Tuyul — wiry, sharp-featured, with quick dark eyes that missed nothing and a mouth that never stopped. He was carrying a sack that, from the round shapes bulging within it, was clearly full of stolen rambutan. Behind him, a larger and softer shape emerged: Dawala, Cepot's younger companion, round-faced, perpetually bewildered, clutching his own sack of pilfered fruit.

"What in the name of Semar's belly are you doing out here?" Cepot demanded, as if Kian Santang and Bagus were the ones doing something strange by running through a thunderstorm at midnight. "And why do you look like you've seen a jenglot?"

"Cepot, be quiet —"

"You're soaking wet. You're both carrying packs. Bagus has his golok." Cepot's sharp eyes catalogued everything in the space of a breath. His voice dropped, and the wit fell away from it like a mask. "You're running from something."

"Yes," Kian Santang said, because there was no time to lie and Cepot would have seen through it anyway. "And you need to get off this road. Now. Go home. Both of you."

"We can't go home," Dawala said miserably. "Pak Rahmat saw us in his orchard. He'll tell our parents."

"There are worse things than Pak Rahmat on this road tonight," Bagus said, and something in his voice — low, steady, utterly serious — made both Cepot and Dawala fall silent.

The roar came again. Closer now. The rambutan tree shivered, and fruit fell from its branches like small dark hearts.

Cepot looked south, toward the sound. Then he looked at Kian Santang. Whatever he saw in his cousin's face — the fear, the exhaustion, the weight of something terrible — it was enough.

"Where are you going?"

"East. Dieng."

"Then we're coming with you."

"No." Kian Santang grabbed Cepot's arm. "You don't understand what's happening. I can't — I won't put you in danger."

"We're already in danger, bodoh. Whatever made that sound is between us and home." Cepot shook free of his grip. "And you look like you're about to collapse. You need help. *Mulah boroning!*" — *Don't be stubborn!* — he added, his voice sharp with the rough, informal register that Tuyul peers used when courtesy was a waste of time. "Don't argue with me — you know you never win."

Dawala, who had been looking back and forth between them with increasingly wide eyes, let his sack of rambutan drop to the ground. "I'm not leaving Kang Kian," he said simply. His voice trembled, but his jaw was set.

Kian Santang looked at Bagus. Bagus said nothing, but his eyes said: *We don't have time for this argument.*

"Fine," Kian Santang said. "Stay close. Stay quiet. And Cepot — for the love of everything sacred — do not talk."

"That," Cepot said, already falling into step beside them, "is a promise I will almost certainly break."

---

They reached the bamboo bridge over the Citarum gorge, and the world ended.

The bridge was old — a construction of lashed bamboo and rattan rope, spanning a gap of thirty meters above the river. In daylight it swayed pleasantly, a familiar crossing for farmers and herb-gatherers. In the darkness, with the storm-swollen river roaring below and the rain turning everything to a chaos of water and shadow, it was a thing of nightmare.

But the nightmare was not the bridge.

It was what stood at the far end of it.

A shape. Tall — impossibly tall, taller than any man of Nusantara or any other land. Robed in something that was not cloth but darkness given texture, a flowing weight of shadow that bled into the rain as if the figure were dissolving at its edges, or as if the night itself had gathered into a roughly human form and decided to stand there, waiting. Beneath the robes, nothing was visible — no hands, no feet, no body. Only the suggestion of substance. Only the certainty of malice.

It sat upon a Harimau Siluman.

The tiger was enormous — twice the size of any Javan tiger, its shoulders as high as a man's chest. Its fur was wrong. In the fitful lightning it seemed striped, black on orange, almost natural, but between the flashes it was simply darkness, a tiger-shaped absence of light. Its eyes were green fire — not the reflective glow of an animal caught in lamplight but an active, burning radiance, twin flames set in a skull of shadow. It stood at the bridge's far end with the patience of a predator that has never once failed to catch its prey.

And the Leak wore a mask.

A topeng — wooden, carved, painted in red and gold that the rain had not faded and the years had not touched. The face on the mask was frozen in an expression of absolute rage, the features exaggerated into something beyond human: bulging eyes, flared nostrils, a mouth wrenched open in a silent scream that showed teeth filed to points. It was the kind of mask that belonged in old ceremonies, in stories told to frighten children. But this was no story. The mask turned slowly, left, right, and from behind it came a sound.

Not a voice. A vibration.

It moved through the air like a physical thing, pressing against Kian Santang's chest, squeezing his lungs, making his vision narrow to a tunnel. It was the sound of something immeasurably old and immeasurably patient searching for what it had been sent to find. It was sniffing. Not with a nose, not with any organ of sense that belonged to the living world, but with something else — a hunger, a resonance, an awareness that reached out through the rain and the dark and tested the air for the signature of the Keris Murka.

And the keris answered.

The heat against Kian Santang's back exploded into a blaze. He gasped, and the sound was too loud, far too loud, and Bagus's hand clamped over his mouth with a grip like iron. They were crouched in the undergrowth at the near end of the bridge — when had they stopped? When had Bagus pulled him down? — and the ground was mud and the rain was in his eyes and the keris was burning, burning, pulling toward the Leak like a compass needle swinging toward some terrible north.

"Under the bridge," Bagus breathed. The words were barely a movement of lips. "Now."

They scrambled down the riverbank, all four of them, sliding in the mud, grabbing at roots and rocks. Beneath the bridge was a narrow shelf of clay above the waterline, slick and foul, littered with debris from the flood. They pressed themselves against the earthen wall, into the shadow of the bamboo structure above, and Kian Santang bit down on his own fist to keep from crying out because the keris was alive against his back, writhing with dark heat, and it wanted — it *wanted* — it wanted to be drawn, to be held, to be lifted into the air and shown to the thing above like a child raising its arms to a parent.

*Draw me,* something whispered. Not in words. In sensation. In the crawling certainty that all of this would stop if he simply reached back and pulled the blade free. The Leak would see him. The Leak would know him. And that would be — good? Right? Inevitable? The logic unspooled in his mind like silk, beautiful and terrible, and his hand moved toward the wrapping on his back.

Bagus caught his wrist. Held it. Said nothing. Just held it, and with his other hand pressed against his own lips began to murmur, so quietly that the sound was less than the rain, less than the river: "Om Swasti Astu, Sang Hyang Widhi, nu Maha Suci, nu Maha Agung..." — the old Sundanese prayer to the Divine, the invocation to the karuhun and the gods of the Triloka that their grandmothers had whispered over their cradles. In his voice it became a *Kinanti* — the pupuh of hope and longing, the melody that is sung when the heart wavers between fear and faith:

*Mugi kersa ngaping ingwang / dina peuting nu poék ieu / sinindungan ti nu jahat / ...*
(*Watch over your servant / in this darkest night / be shielded from evil / ...*)

Above them, the bridge groaned.

The Harimau Siluman was crossing.

Each step was a detonation. The bamboo flexed and creaked, and showers of grit and debris fell onto the four Tuyul pressed into the mud below. The tiger's paws were directly overhead — vast, heavy, deliberate. Through the gaps in the bamboo lashing, Kian Santang could see a sliver of the creature's underside: dark fur, the suggestion of massive ribs expanding and contracting, and a smell — a smell that was not animal but mineral, sulfurous, the breath of a volcano vent, of Krakatau itself.

The Leak paused.

The vibration intensified. It was no longer a sound but a pressure, a weight on the skull, a squeezing of the inner ear. Dawala was shaking so violently that Cepot had to wrap both arms around him and hold him still, and Cepot's own face was a mask of terror that Kian Santang had never seen on him before and never wanted to see again. The Leak was directly above them. It was testing the air. It was reaching out with whatever sense it possessed, probing the darkness beneath the bridge, and the keris flared and pulsed and screamed in silence against Kian Santang's back.

*Here,* the keris seemed to cry. *Here I am. Here.*

Bagus held his wrist. The prayer continued, barely audible, a thread of faith against an ocean of darkness: "...nu kagungan langit sareng bumi, ingwang pasrahkeun diri ka Sang Hyang Widhi, ka karuhun nu linuhung..."

A moment. An eternity.

The Leak moved on.

The steps continued across the bridge — one, two, three, four — each one a little farther, a little softer, until the bamboo settled and the vibration faded and there was only the rain and the river and the four of them pressed into the mud like *sesajen* offerings buried at a foundation stone.

No one moved. No one spoke. They lay there for what might have been minutes or hours, until Cepot, with a sound that was half-laugh and half-sob, whispered: "That was worse than Pak Rahmat."

---

They crawled out from beneath the bridge like creatures being born — mud-slicked, trembling, newborn into a world that would never again feel safe. The Leak had gone south, following the main road, fooled by the batik wrapping's concealment. Eyang Guru's blessing had held. But for how long?

They ran like Timun Mas fleeing the giant — throwing distance behind them, trusting that cleverness would outrun brute strength. The old folktale lived in every Tuyul child's bones: the girl escaping the raksasa who had raised her, casting her magic seeds behind her — salt that became a sea, chili that became a field of fire, shrimp paste that became a swamp of mud. They had no magic seeds, but they had Bagus's knowledge of the forest paths and the batik wrapping's fading concealment, and they ran as Timun Mas had run, not with the speed of warriors but with the desperate, stubborn swiftness of the small and the hunted.

They crossed the bridge in silence, one at a time, each step a prayer. The bamboo swayed. The river raged below. Kian Santang did not look south.

On the far side, the forest rose like a wall.

The highland forests of Pasundan were ancient — old-growth dipterocarp forest, the kind that had stood since before the first kingdoms, before the first temples, before the first human hand had struck flint to steel in these mountains. The trees were enormous: pohon meranti with trunks as wide as houses, their crowns lost in the darkness above; pohon beringin, the great banyan trees, with their curtains of aerial roots descending from branch to earth like the pillars of a living cathedral. The undergrowth was dense — ferns as tall as a man, rattans with thorns like fishhooks, carpets of moss that muffled footsteps to silence.

Bagus took the lead. He knew these forests. Since boyhood he had walked them, gathering firewood, collecting herbs and roots for the village healer, learning the paths that were not paths — the gaps between trees, the dry ridges, the game trails that wound through the undergrowth like threads through a loom. He moved with a sureness that even the darkness could not diminish, and the others followed: Kian Santang behind him, then Dawala, then Cepot guarding the rear.

The keris had gone quiet. The terrible heat had faded to a faint warmth, a sleeping ember. Kian Santang was grateful for the silence, but he did not trust it. The blade was not asleep. It was listening. It was always listening.

The forest closed around them, and the rain, filtered through the immense canopy above, became a gentle patter. The sounds changed. The roar of the storm was replaced by the intimate, intricate music of the forest at night: the high metallic whine of cicadas, pulsing in waves; the distant cry of a koel bird, mournful and strange; the rustle and chatter of leaf monkeys disturbed in their sleep; the drip, drip, drip of water descending through layers of leaves and branches and moss, arriving at the forest floor minutes after it had fallen from the sky.

They walked for hours. Kian Santang's legs burned. His clothes were heavy with rain and mud. The pack on his back — and the thing beneath it — seemed to grow heavier with every step. He stumbled over roots. He walked into spider webs that clung to his face like the fingers of ghosts. He said nothing. He put one foot in front of the other and followed the shape of Bagus's back through the dark.

Behind him, Cepot muttered: "I want it noted, for the historical record, that this is the worst rambutan theft of my entire life."

"Cepot," Kian Santang said wearily.

"I'm just saying. In terms of consequences per rambutan stolen, this is a catastrophic ratio. We didn't even get to eat them."

"You can eat rambutan later," Bagus said from the front, not turning.

"If there is a later."

"There will be a later. Keep walking."

Dawala, who had been silent since the bridge, spoke up in a small voice. "Can we stop to eat? I'm hungry."

"You're always hungry," Cepot said. "It's your defining characteristic. Historians will write: 'Dawala — he was hungry.'"

"I packed nasi timbel," Bagus said. "When we find shelter."

They kept walking. The forest breathed around them. Somewhere far to the south, so faint it might have been imagination, the spirit-tiger roared.

---

Dawn came to the Pasundan highlands like a benediction.

The rain had stopped sometime in the last hour of walking, and the clouds had thinned and torn, and now the first light of morning came filtering through the canopy in long, golden shafts that turned the wet leaves to stained glass — emerald, jade, amber. The mist rose from the forest floor in slow, curling tendrils, and the birds woke, one by one, until the air was filled with their singing: the bright cascade of a cucak hijau, the rhythmic hammering of a woodpecker, the long fluting call of a perkutut dove. The *bayu* of the forest stirred with the dawn — a current of living energy rising through root and trunk and leaf, visible to no eye but felt by every creature that breathed, the earth's own *sakti* greeting the sun.

Bagus found them shelter beneath a beringin tree so ancient and vast that it seemed less like a tree and more like a building — a temple, perhaps, or a *candi* built by something that thought in centuries rather than years. Its aerial roots descended from branches high above in thick, rope-like curtains, creating a natural enclosure, a room within the forest. The ground beneath it was dry, padded with generations of fallen leaves. The roots formed walls. The canopy formed a roof. It was the kind of place the Tuyul called *kabuyutan* — a sacred grove, a dwelling of the *karuhun*, where the old spirits of the land gathered and the air hummed with a quiet, watchful holiness.

"Here," Bagus said, and set down his pack.

They collapsed. Dawala simply lay down on the leaf litter and did not move. Cepot leaned against a root the thickness of his own body and closed his eyes. Kian Santang lowered himself carefully, mindful of the keris, and sat with his back against the great trunk of the beringin, feeling the ancient bark against his shoulders, rough and real, an anchor to the world.

Bagus, because he was Bagus, did not rest. He unpacked the food — the parcels of nasi timbel, the dried fish, the sambal — and laid them out on a broad banana leaf with the quiet ceremony of a man who understood that meals mattered, that the act of eating together was an act of civilization, a small defiance against the darkness that pursued them. He poured jamu from the clay pot into four small cups he had brought — warm, bitter, fragrant with turmeric and ginger.

"Eat," he said. "Then we talk."

They ate. The nasi timbel was cold but perfect — the rice compressed inside its banana leaf wrapping, fragrant with the leaf's faint sweetness, the grains firm and distinct. The dried fish was salty and good. The sambal burned their tongues and brought tears to their eyes and made them feel, for a few minutes, like themselves again.

Cepot finished first and looked at Kian Santang with eyes that were sharp and steady and entirely without humor.

"So," he said. "Tell us."

Kian Santang looked at his three companions — Bagus, solid and patient; Cepot, keen and waiting; Dawala, mouth full of rice, eyes wide. His friends. His family. Cousins by blood, brothers by the thousand small bonds of a shared childhood in the lembur. He did not deserve their company on this road. He did not want their lives tangled with his doom.

But they were here. And they would not leave.

He told them what he could. Not everything — not the full history of the Keris Murka, not the details of Raja Iblis and the shadow-soul bound in the blade. That was too much, too heavy, too terrifying for a dawn beneath a banyan tree. But he told them enough. He was carrying something dangerous. Something that dark forces — the Leak, the things they had hidden from beneath the bridge — were hunting. It had to be taken east, far east, to a place where it could be dealt with. Eyang Guru Batara was involved. It was as serious as anything had ever been.

Silence, when he finished. The forest sounds seemed louder in the quiet between them — birds, insects, the slow drip of water from leaf to leaf.

Cepot looked at him for a long time. Then he said: "Then we go to Dieng."

No argument. No demand for more information. No dramatic declaration of loyalty. Just the statement, plain and practical, delivered with the same tone he might have used to say *then we cross the river* or *then we plant the rice*. It was, in its way, the bravest thing Kian Santang had ever heard.

Dawala swallowed his mouthful of nasi timbel, wiped his lips with the back of his hand, and nodded. "We go to Dieng," he agreed. His voice was steady. The boy who had shaken like a leaf beneath the bridge, who had needed Cepot's arms to hold him still, looked at Kian Santang with a certainty that had no business being there and yet was, unshakeable as the roots of the beringin tree above them.

Kian Santang could not speak. His throat was tight with something he could not name — gratitude, grief, love, fear, all of it wound together like the strands of a rope. He nodded.

Bagus handed him a cup of jamu. "Drink," he said. "We rest until midday. Then we walk."

Kian Santang drank. The jamu was bitter and warm, and it spread through his body like a small fire, pushing back the cold and the exhaustion and the memory of the thing on the bridge. He leaned his head back against the bark of the ancient tree and closed his eyes.

Around them, the Pasundan forest came fully awake. Shafts of golden light pierced the canopy and fell in bright columns through the mist, illuminating drifting motes of pollen and tiny winged insects that spun in the warmth. A family of leaf monkeys moved through the branches above, their soft calls drifting down like conversation. Somewhere nearby, water trickled over stone — a spring, a stream, a small gift of the mountain. The air smelled of wet earth and green things growing and the faint sweetness of forest flowers whose names only Bagus would know.

Four Tuyul, huddled beneath an ancient beringin tree, wrapped in borrowed blankets and the remains of a meal. Small figures in a vast and darkening world, with the shadow of Krakatau reaching toward them from the west and the long road to Dieng stretching out before them into the unknown east.

But for now — for this one morning — they were together. And that was enough.

Cepot's voice broke the silence, drowsy and half-asleep: "Next time, I'm stealing durian. If I'm going to flee for my life, I want better fruit."

Dawala laughed. Bagus shook his head. And Kian Santang, despite everything — despite the keris on his back and the Leak in the darkness and the long impossible road ahead — smiled.

The beringin tree stood over them like a guardian, its roots a fortress, its canopy a shield. The forest sang. The light grew warm.

They rested.
