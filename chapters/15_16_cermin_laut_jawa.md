# Bab 15: Cermin Laut Selatan
## (Mirror of the Southern Sea)

---

The river carried them out of enchantment and into the world.

For three days the leaf-boats of Ngarai Sianok had borne the fellowship down the winding gorge -- vessels of impossible craft, each one shaped from a single *daun jati* leaf the size of a fishing prau, curled at the edges by Bidadari song, hardened by some art that made the green membrane stronger than planked teak. They sat low in the water, responsive to the current the way a horse is responsive to its rider's knees, turning and adjusting without oar or rudder, guided by whatever intelligence the Bidadari had breathed into them at the moment of their making.

The Sianok gorge was behind them now. Its sheer basalt walls, its hanging gardens of fern and moss, its waterfalls that fell from cliffs so high the water dissolved into mist before it reached the river -- all of it receding like a dream relinquished at the moment of waking. Kian Santang sat in the prow of the lead boat and watched the gorge narrow to a green slit and then vanish behind a bend of the river, and he felt its loss the way one feels the loss of a beautiful thing that was never truly yours to keep.

Eyang Guru sat behind him, his teak staff laid across the boat's curved sides like a crossbeam, his white hair damp with river spray. The old sage had said little since they departed the sanctuary. His eyes were fixed ahead, reading the current, the sky, the changing character of the banks with the quiet intensity of a man translating a text written in a language only he could fully comprehend.

They traveled in two boats. In the lead vessel rode Kian Santang, Eyang Guru, Bagus, and Raden Wijaya -- the prince seated at the stern with the stillness of a carved figurehead, his hand resting on the hilt of the Keris Nagasasra, his dark eyes scanning the tree line on both banks with the habitual vigilance of a man who had spent his life expecting ambush. In the second boat came Dewi Anjani, Tumenggung Batu, Gajahmada, and the two younger Tuyul -- Cepot perched on the gunwale with the precarious confidence of a monkey on a branch, Dawala wedged between the Kurcaci chieftain and the Bidadari archer in what was perhaps the most uncomfortable seating arrangement in the history of river travel.

"I can feel his elbow in my kidney," Dawala said.

"That is my hammer," Tumenggung Batu said.

"It is in my kidney."

"Then move your kidney."

The river widened. The jungle walls drew back. And slowly, by degrees so gradual that Kian Santang did not notice the transition until it was complete, the world changed around them.

---

The Minangkabau highlands unfolded like a scroll.

The dense, primordial jungle of the gorge gave way to a landscape shaped by centuries of human labor -- terraced sawah that climbed the hillsides in steps of luminous green, each level held by walls of fitted stone, each paddy a mirror reflecting the pewter sky. The rice was young, shin-high, dense and vivid, swaying in the breeze that funneled down the valley with a susurrus that sounded like the breathing of the earth itself. Irrigation channels threaded between the terraces, feeding water from terrace to terrace in a system of such elegant complexity that it seemed less like agriculture and more like music made physical -- a liquid fugue, each voice descending from the one above, each contributing to the whole.

And above the terraces, on the ridges and the hilltops, stood the *rumah gadang*.

Kian Santang had heard of them but never seen them. The great houses of the Minangkabau were extraordinary -- enormous communal dwellings built of dark hardwood, their walls adorned with carved panels painted in red and black and gold, their multi-tiered roofs sweeping upward at both ends in great curving horns that reached toward the sky like the horns of a water buffalo or the prow and stern of a ship sailing through cloud. Each horn-tip was sharp enough to cut the light that fell upon it, and the thatch beneath was layered so thickly that it seemed less a roof than a living thing, a dark mass that breathed and settled in the wind. The houses were vast -- thirty, forty paces long, raised upon stilts of teak as thick as a man's waist, their underbellies dark and cool, the space beneath used for storage and shade and the gathering of families too numerous to count.

They were matrilineal, the Minangkabau. Eyang Guru had told him this during the long nights of their journey from Dieng. The houses belonged to the women. The land descended through the mothers. The men -- the warriors, the traders, the wanderers who *merantau*, who left home to seek fortune in distant lands -- they were guests in the houses of their wives and mothers, honored and loved but never owners. It was a system as old as the terraces themselves, rooted in the same volcanic soil, sustained by the same principle that governed the irrigation channels: that which flows must be shared, and that which endures must be tended by those who stay.

"Beautiful," Bagus said, watching a rumah gadang pass on the left bank, its horned roof golden in the late afternoon light. "My mother would love those kitchens."

"Your mother would reorganize the entire house within half an hour," Kian Santang said, slipping into the easy *basa loma* of old friends. "*Indung dika mah, da sok kitu*" -- *Your mother, she's always like that.*

"Within half an hour. She is efficient."

But even here, in this landscape of ancient beauty and patient cultivation, the shadow had reached.

The villages they passed were tense. People stood on the riverbank and watched the leaf-boats glide past with expressions that held not curiosity but fear. Men carried golok machetes at their belts, which was unusual for farmers in peacetime. Women gathered children from the water's edge and hurried them behind the stilted houses. At one village, a row of bamboo stakes had been driven into the mud at the river's margin -- a defensive palisade, crude but recent, the bamboo still green.

"They have heard," Raden Wijaya said quietly. His eyes had not left the banks.

"More than heard," Eyang Guru replied. "Look at the sawah. Third terrace from the top."

Kian Santang looked. It took him a moment to see it. The third terrace was not green. Its rice was grey. Not the grey of drought or the brown of blight, but a cold, ash-colored grey that did not belong in any palette of the living world. It looked as though the color had been drained from the paddy -- not killed, but leeched, emptied, the vitality sucked from the grain the way a spider empties a fly. The paddy stood upright, still swaying in the breeze, but it was a corpse of rice, a ghost of what it should have been.

"The bayu is weakening," Eyang Guru said. "The spiritual energy that sustains the land. As Raja Iblis's power grows in Krakatau, it draws upon the bayu of the surrounding islands -- feeds on it, corrupts it, turns fertility to ash. The effect spreads outward from the Sunda Strait like poison in a well."

"How far does it reach?" Kian Santang asked.

The old sage did not answer. He did not need to. The grey paddy spoke for him, and the frightened villages, and the palisade stakes driven into the mud of a river that had been peaceful for centuries.

---

On the evening of the third day upon the river, Raden Wijaya spoke.

They had made camp on a sandbar where the river broadened and slowed, a flat tongue of grey sand sheltered by overhanging rattan palms whose fronds whispered in the night wind like old women sharing secrets. The fire was small -- Eyang Guru had insisted on caution, and even the firelight was dimmed by a ward the sage had set around the camp, a murmured boundary of old Sanskrit that bent the light inward, making the flames visible to those within the circle but invisible to watchers beyond. The two leaf-boats were drawn up on the sand, their green hulls gleaming faintly in the firelight.

They had eaten Bagus's provisions in silence -- cold rice wrapped in banana leaf, dendeng jerky, a sambal of crushed chili and shrimp paste that Tumenggung Batu consumed in quantities that would have blistered the mouth of a lesser being. The Kurcaci had a theory, frequently and loudly stated, that chilies improved circulation, sharpened the senses, and contributed to the legendary Kurcaci temperament. No one argued. No one dared.

The silence after the meal was the heavy, satisfied silence of tired travelers who have eaten enough but not too much, and who are beginning to feel the weight of the day settle into their muscles and their thoughts. Dawala was already asleep, curled on his mat like a cat, his mouth open, his breathing deep and even. Cepot was whittling something from a stick -- a puppet figure, by the look of it, its angular limbs taking shape under his quick knife.

It was into this silence that Raden Wijaya spoke, and the words he chose were not the words anyone expected.

"I owe you a story," he said.

His voice was quiet. He did not look at any of them directly. He sat at the edge of the firelight, his long legs drawn up, his forearms resting on his knees, the Keris Nagasasra laid across his thighs. His face was in half-shadow, and in the flickering light the scar that ran from his left temple to his jaw -- an old wound, long healed, the souvenir of some violence he had never discussed -- stood out like a line of pale script on dark parchment.

"You follow me into danger," he continued. "You trust me at your back. You have the right to know who walks beside you."

Eyang Guru inclined his head but said nothing. The old sage's eyes were gentle. He knew this story already, Kian Santang realized -- had known it, perhaps, before the prince himself had decided to tell it.

Raden Wijaya looked into the fire. When he spoke again, his voice had changed -- it was lower, rougher, as though the words were being extracted from a depth where they had been buried for a long time, compacted under the weight of years and silence.

"My name is Raden Wijaya Kartaningrat. I am the last of the line of Mataram."

He paused. The fire cracked. Somewhere on the river, a night bird called -- a low, mournful sound that carried across the water like a question without answer.

"The kingdom of Mataram was the greatest power in central Jawa for three hundred years. Its boundaries stretched from the slopes of Merapi to the southern sea. Its courts were centers of learning, of art, of the pencak silat traditions that shaped every warrior school on the island. The Keris Nagasasra -- " he touched the blade on his thighs " -- was the symbol of Mataram's kings. Its pamor held the naga, the serpent-dragon, guardian of the realm. To carry the Nagasasra was to carry Mataram itself."

His jaw tightened. The scar whitened.

"But the Nagasasra was not the only keris my ancestors carried. My forefather, Raden Kartawijaya -- the one who struck the Keris Murka from the Shadow King's hand at the Battle of the Straits -- he kept both blades. The Nagasasra in his right hand, the Murka in his left. Light and shadow, side by side. He believed he could master both."

Gajahmada stirred but said nothing. His dark eyes were fixed on Raden Wijaya with an expression that Kian Santang could not read -- it might have been empathy, or recognition, or the discomfort of a man hearing his own argument dismantled by its consequences.

"He could not." Raden Wijaya's voice was flat, stripped of ornament. "Within a generation, the Keris Murka had begun its work. Kartawijaya's son was ambitious. His grandson was tyrannical. His great-grandson was mad. Each generation the corruption deepened, the throne darkened, the kingdom sickened from the head down. The harvests failed. The provinces rebelled. The allies fell away. And the Leak -- the nine shadow riders who served the Keris Murka's maker -- they came at night to the palace, and they whispered to the kings, and the kings listened, because the keris had opened a door in their hearts that could not be closed."

He stopped. The silence was not comfortable, but it was necessary.

"Mataram fell in a single night. I was seven years old."

The words were delivered without inflection, the way a man describes a wound that has long since scarred over -- not because it no longer hurts, but because the pain has become so constant that it has lost the ability to surprise.

"They came in darkness. Buta, sent by Empu Gandring -- he who was already turning to the Shadow King's service, though we did not know it then. They breached the palace walls. They killed my father in his chambers. They killed my mother as she tried to reach me. My uncle -- my father's brother, a silat master named Jayabrata -- he took me from my bed and carried me through the burning corridors, through a passage in the foundations that only the royal family knew. He carried me for three days through the jungle, wounded, bleeding, until we reached the village of a man he trusted. A wandering silat guru. A man of no kingdom and no allegiance, who lived in the forests and taught the old arts to anyone willing to learn."

Raden Wijaya's hand closed around the hilt of the Nagasasra. Not a grip of aggression. A grip of anchoring. A man holding onto the one thing that connected him to everything he had lost.

"My uncle died of his wounds on the fourth day. The guru raised me. He taught me Aliran Mataram -- the silat of my ancestors, the forms and the philosophy, the stances that carry the weight of three centuries of warrior-kings. He taught me to fight, to survive, to move through the world without leaving a trail. And when I was old enough to understand, he told me what I was: the last of the Mataram blood. The final ember of a fire that had burned for three hundred years."

He fell silent. The fire had burned low. Bagus reached for a branch and fed the coals, and the flames rose, and in their light Raden Wijaya's face was a landscape of controlled grief.

"I have spent my life since then as a ranger. Wandering the borders of the free kingdoms, watching for shadows, fighting Buta where I found them, protecting the edges of a world that does not know I exist. I did not seek the throne. I do not want it. The throne was poisoned by my ancestors' pride, and I carry the shame of it." He looked down at the Nagasasra. "Eyang Guru told me, years ago, that the line of Mataram must endure. That Nusantara needs the symbol, even if the kingdom is gone. I did not believe him then. I am not certain I believe him now."

He raised his head, and for the first time he looked at them -- looked at each of them, one by one, as though measuring whether the truth he had given them would be received or rejected.

"But I am here. I carry the reforged Nagasasra, and I walk beside the bearer of the Murka, and the irony of that is not lost on me. My ancestor's greatest failure -- the keris he should have destroyed -- now requires his last descendant to help carry it to its destruction." A ghost of a smile, thin and bitter as quinine. "The gods have a sense of humor."

Kian Santang felt the Keris Murka stir against his back. It recognized the Nagasasra's proximity. It always did. The two blades were entangled in some deep metallurgical sympathy -- one forged in the fires of Krakatau, the other in the clean forges of Mataram -- and when they were close, the Murka whispered louder, as though the presence of its ancient rival excited some hunger in the metal.

"I know the shame you carry," Kian Santang said quietly. "Not the same shame. But the same weight. The keris chose me, and I do not know why, and every day I am afraid that I will fail the way your ancestor failed -- that I will keep it, or use it, or listen to it just once too many times."

Raden Wijaya looked at him. The prince's dark eyes held something new -- a recognition, a kinship of burden that transcended the vast difference between a prince of fallen kingdoms and a farm boy from the Pasundan highlands.

"You will not fail," Raden Wijaya said. "You do not have enough pride to fail that way."

It was, Kian Santang realized, the kindest thing anyone had ever said about his humility.

Tumenggung Batu cleared his throat. "Mataram made fine steel," he said. The bluntness of the Kurcaci, offering respect in the only currency he traded. "The old forges of Mataram understood carbon content. Your ancestors may have been fools with magic blades, but they knew their metallurgy."

Dewi Anjani spoke, and her voice held the layered resonance of the Bidadari -- not merely words, but the echo of ages behind them. "*Mataram tinalar ku cahaya na sorangan*," she said -- *Mataram was abandoned by its own light* -- in the elevated *basa luhur* of celestial proclamation, each syllable carrying the weight of ages. "The Bidadari remember Mataram at its height. A kingdom of light and learning. What your ancestor broke, you may yet mend. Not the kingdom -- that is stone, and stone crumbles. But the *legacy*. The meaning of the name. That is yours to rewrite."

Gajahmada said nothing for a long time. Then: "I spoke in the council of wielding the keris. Of using the Shadow King's weapon against him." He paused. His voice was rough, the voice of a man swallowing something that tasted of ash. "I heard myself in your story, Raden. I heard Kartawijaya. The same argument. The same pride." He met the prince's eyes. "I am glad you told it."

The fire burned down. The river murmured. Above them, the Minangkabau stars turned in their ancient circuits, and the night wind carried the scent of wet earth and young rice and something fainter, something carried from far to the east -- a sulfurous edge, a mineral sharpness, the first whisper of volcanic breath.

They slept. And the keris whispered. And the river carried them on.

---

The coast arrived on the morning of the fifth day.

The river broadened into an estuary, its banks dissolving into mangrove forest -- dense, tangled, root-networks rising from the brackish water like the fingers of drowned giants clawing toward the air. The water changed color: from the brown of upland silt to the grey-green of tidal influence, and the smell changed with it -- the fresh, earthy scent of the river giving way to the salt and iodine and vast, impersonal cleanness of the sea.

And then the mangroves fell away, and the Samudera Hindia -- the great *jaladri*, the boundless sagara -- opened before them.

The Indian Ocean.

Kian Santang had never seen the open ocean. He had grown up in the highlands of Pasundan, where the horizon was always bounded by mountains, where the farthest vista was a valley glimpsed through a notch in the ridgeline. The ocean was a different order of vastness -- a plane of grey-green water that extended in every direction to the curved edge of the world, meeting the sky at a line so far away that the eye could not fix upon it without a kind of vertigo, a sense of scale that the highland mind was not built to process. The waves rolled in from the south -- long, slow, powerful swells that had traveled thousands of leagues across the open Pacific, building energy over distances that beggared imagination, arriving at the coast of Sumatra with a patience and a force that made the mightiest river seem like a child's toy.

They were entering the domain of Nyai Roro Kidul now -- the Queen of the Southern Sea, whose sovereignty over these waters was not a matter of politics or territory but of spiritual fact, as immutable as the tides themselves. Kian Santang felt it in the way the waves received their leaf-boats: not with the indifference of open ocean but with a kind of recognition, a testing, as though the sea were examining the vessels and their passengers and deciding whether to grant passage or refuse it. The Triloka -- the three worlds of Hindu-Buddhist cosmology, the upper realm of the gods, the middle realm of mortals, and the lower realm of spirits and the dead -- met at the ocean's surface, and the Southern Sea was the threshold between them, the place where the boundaries grew thin and a traveler might pass from one world to another without knowing the moment of crossing.

She had once been Dewi Kadita, a princess of such beauty that her stepmother's jealousy manifested as a curse -- scales and sores that drove her from the palace, that transformed her luminous skin into a map of suffering, each blemish a word in the cruel vocabulary of envy. She walked into the Southern Sea to die. She walked into the waves with the deliberate calm of one who has exhausted all other options and found the sea's embrace preferable to the world's cruelty. And the sea, recognizing divinity in suffering, transformed her instead. The scales became armor of pearl, each one catching the light with the iridescence of abalone shell. The sores became stars -- points of cold light embedded in her skin, a constellation of endured pain transmuted into beauty that surpassed the beauty she had lost. She rose as Nyai Roro Kidul, Queen of the Southern Sea, and the waves themselves bent to her will, and the suffering that had driven her beneath the surface became the source of a power that no mortal and no god could challenge.

Her domain had rules, old as the tides and absolute as the deep. No one in her waters wore green -- the color of her mortal suffering, of the scales and sores that had covered Dewi Kadita's skin in the days before her transformation. Even now, centuries beyond the curse, the color green could call her attention, her memory, her wrath. Fishermen along the southern coast knew this the way they knew the currents and the phases of the moon: a fact of survival, taught from father to son, mother to daughter, never questioned, never tested. Kian Santang had noticed that even the leaf-boats, as they crossed from river to sea, had shifted their hue -- the bright green of fresh leaves darkening to the blue-green of deep water, as though the vessels themselves understood the prohibition and adjusted accordingly.

There were other laws. Offerings of flowers cast upon the waves -- frangipani and jasmine and the small yellow blooms of the *bunga kenanga* -- were not gestures of supplication but acts of acknowledgment, the way a guest removes their shoes before entering a host's home. The prohibition of whistling at night near the shore, lest the sound mimic the voices of the drowned and draw the Queen's attention to the boundary between her realm and the world above. The understanding that the sea's gifts -- its fish, its pearls, its coral, its safe passage -- were not rights but permissions, granted and revocable, and that gratitude was not optional but essential.

Eyang Guru, who understood these things with the clarity of a man who had walked between the Triloka for longer than any mortal lifetime, had murmured the proper invocations as they crossed from fresh water to salt -- old Sanskrit prayers modified by centuries of Nusantara practice, acknowledging Nyai Roro Kidul's sovereignty, requesting safe passage, offering in return the fellowship's respect and the promise that they carried no green cloth upon their persons. The sea had accepted. The waves smoothed. The leaf-boats steadied.

But the acceptance was conditional, and the condition was awareness: they sailed through a sacred space, a domain where the ordinary rules of the mortal world bent to accommodate the will of a queen who had been born from suffering and who ruled through a power that suffering had made absolute.

Her gift of foresight -- the *cermin laut*, the sea-mirror that showed what was and what might be -- was said to manifest on still days when the ocean's surface became glass, and in that glass the future could be read by those who knew how to look. But the mirror's prophecies were treacherous, not because they lied but because they showed too much: every possibility, every branching path, every future that might unfold from a single choice, and the burden of seeing was often worse than the burden of not knowing. Nyai Roro Kidul herself was said to consult the sea-mirror rarely, and to weep when she did, for the futures she saw were as numerous as the waves and as impossible to hold.

"Vast," Bagus said, beside him. The word was inadequate. Bagus knew it was inadequate. He said it anyway, because some things require acknowledgment even when language fails.

"Vast," Kian Santang agreed.

The leaf-boats changed.

It happened as they crossed from river to sea, from fresh water to salt. The enchanted vessels shuddered -- Kian Santang felt it through the hull, a vibration that was not mechanical but organic, the tremor of a living thing responding to a new environment. The leaf-membrane thickened. The curled edges rose higher, forming deeper gunwales. The flat bottom curved into a keel-shape that bit into the ocean swell with new authority. And from each side of each boat, lateral extensions grew -- outrigger arms of stiffened leaf-rib, reaching out and down to touch the water's surface, stabilizing the vessels against the roll of the open sea.

"Jukung," Tumenggung Batu said, with grudging approval. "Outrigger boats. The Bidadari have taught the leaves to become proper sea craft."

Dewi Anjani, in the second boat, smiled the faintest of smiles. "The leaves remember what they need to be," she said. "As all living things do."

They turned south.

The coast of western Sumatra rose to their left -- a wall of green jungle climbing steeply from the narrow coastal plain to the volcanic peaks of the Bukit Barisan, the great mountain spine that ran the length of the island. The peaks were hidden in cloud, but their presence was felt -- a weight, a density, a sense of immense geological forces held in temporary stillness. Below them, the coast was a ragged edge of black volcanic sand, pounded by surf, interrupted by headlands of dark basalt that jutted into the sea like the clenched fists of a buried titan.

As they rounded the first headland, Cepot spotted sails on the southern horizon -- not the leaf-sails of their enchanted vessels but canvas, real canvas, brown and weathered, stretched taut on twin masts. "Ships," he called from his perch. "Two -- no, three. Different shape. Low hulls, double-ended."

Eyang Guru shaded his eyes and studied the distant vessels. "Prahu phinisi," he said, and there was recognition in his voice, even warmth. "From Sulawesi."

The phinisi drew closer as the day wore on, and Kian Santang marveled at their craft. They were unlike any vessel he had seen -- their distinctive double-ended hulls cutting through the swells with a grace that seemed to defy their heavy timber construction, their twin masts carrying the distinctive rectangular sails of the Bugis seafarers, each sail patched and repatched in a patchwork of brown and tan that spoke of years at sea. They were crewed by Bugis sailors whose navigational knowledge was written not in books but in the patterns of stars and currents passed from father to son across generations stretching back to the age when the first Austronesian sailors had ventured beyond the sight of land and found, in the open ocean, not emptiness but a highway.

One of the phinisi passed close enough to hail, and the Bugis captain -- a lean man with skin the dark mahogany of the eastern coasts, his face etched by decades of salt and sun -- called across the water in the trade-pidgin of the sea lanes. He warned of rough water to the south, of strange currents near the strait, of a reddening in the sea that his crew had never seen before. His eyes lingered on the leaf-boats with curiosity but not surprise; the Bugis had seen everything the sea had to offer and were impressed by none of it. They sailed on, their phinisi heeling in the wind, bound for Makassar and the spice ports beyond, carrying trade goods from a dozen kingdoms in their deep holds -- pepper from Sumatra, gold dust from Kalimantan, textiles from Jawa, and the accumulated knowledge of the greatest seafaring people Nusantara had ever produced.

They sailed south for two days, following the coast, riding the swells. The leaf-boats proved themselves: they cut through the waves with a smoothness that no wooden hull could have matched, their living membranes flexing with each swell, absorbing the ocean's energy rather than fighting it. The outriggers held them steady. The current carried them.

But as they sailed, Kian Santang felt the keris change.

It was subtle at first. A warmth against his back that was not the warmth of his body or the sun. A heaviness that seemed to increase with each league they traveled eastward, as though the blade were gaining weight, drawing mass from the air, from the sea, from some source that Kian Santang could not identify. And the whispers -- always the whispers -- grew louder. Not in volume but in clarity. The formless murmur that had been his constant companion since Padalarang began to resolve into shapes, into almost-words, into fragments of meaning that flickered at the edge of comprehension like fish glimpsed in dark water.

On the second night at sea, anchored in the lee of a headland, he saw Krakatau for the first time.

Not with his eyes. With the keris.

He was sleeping -- or trying to sleep, wrapped in his highland cloak on the curved floor of the leaf-boat, the ocean rocking him with a rhythm that should have been soothing but was not. The keris lay beside him, wrapped in its batik, and in that space between waking and sleep it reached for him, and the visions came.

Fire. A mountain of fire, rising from the sea, its slopes black and barren and streaming with rivers of molten stone. A caldera at its summit, open like a wound, and within the caldera a darkness so dense it seemed solid -- a pillar of shadow rising from the molten rock, reaching upward, spreading outward, staining the sky the color of old blood. And within the shadow, two red eyes, ancient and patient and aware, turning slowly toward him like searchlights sweeping a dark sea.

*Closer*, the keris whispered. *You come closer. I feel you. The metal remembers the fire. The fire remembers the metal. Soon, little bearer. Soon you will bring me home.*

Kian Santang woke with a gasp. The stars wheeled above him, cold and indifferent. The ocean hissed against the hull. Beside him, Bagus slept on, undisturbed.

He pressed his hand against the batik wrapping and felt the keris pulse beneath the cloth -- warm, rhythmic, synchronized to a heartbeat that was not his own.

They were sailing toward the source. Every league brought the blade closer to its origin, and with proximity came power, and with power came clarity, and with clarity came the terrible, intimate certainty that the thing he carried was not merely an object but a presence -- a will, old and patient and hungry, that had been waiting for exactly this journey, this approach, this return.

Kian Santang lay in the rocking boat and stared at the stars and did not sleep again. He murmured to himself in the darkness -- "*Gusti, paparin ingwang kakiatan*" -- *Lord, grant me strength* -- the Sundanese prayer of the basa lemes, the language of supplication, of a small soul addressing the vast and the sacred.

---

On the third morning, the coast curved eastward. The Bukit Barisan fell away. The southernmost tip of Sumatra slid past them -- a low, jungle-covered cape, unremarkable, the kind of geography that maps record and travelers forget. But beyond it, to the east, the sea narrowed.

The Selat Sunda. The Sunda Strait. The passage between Sumatra and Jawa, where the two great islands reached toward each other across a channel of deep water, and where -- in the center of that channel, rising from the sea like a monument to the earth's rage -- stood Krakatau.

They could not see it yet. The strait was still a day's sailing ahead. But they could smell it.

The air changed. The clean salt of the open ocean acquired a new note -- sulfurous, acrid, the smell of hot stone and deep chemistry, of gases rising from vents in the ocean floor. It was faint but unmistakable, and it grew stronger as they sailed east, layering itself over the sea breeze like a warning written in chemistry rather than words.

Eyang Guru stood in the prow of the lead boat, his staff planted between his feet, his white robes snapping in the wind. He turned to face the fellowship, and his ancient face was grave.

"We enter the Java Sea tomorrow," he said. "Beyond the strait. The waters there are no longer safe. The Shadow King's influence extends through the sea itself -- the currents are corrupted, the creatures are bent to his will. We must be swift, and we must be silent."

He looked at each of them, and his eyes held a weight that was not merely concern but knowledge -- the knowledge of a man who had lived long enough to see the patterns of history repeat, and who understood that the sea ahead was not merely water but a threshold, a crossing point between the world they knew and the world they feared.

"Rest tonight," he said. "Tomorrow, everything changes."

That evening, as the sun melted into the Indian Ocean behind them, Dewi Anjani stood at the prow of the second boat and sang an *asmarandana* -- the pupuh of beauty and love, its lilting stanzas perfectly suited to the sight of the dying light upon the waves. Her voice carried across the water with the layered resonance of the Bidadari, each note doubling and tripling as it traveled, until the sea itself seemed to vibrate in sympathy:

*Asmarandana di sagara,*
*cahaya panonpoe surup,*
*jaladri nu jembar teu weleh,*
*nyimpen rusiah nu laluhur...*

*Asmarandana on the sagara, / the sunset's fading light, / the wide jaladri never ceases / to keep the secrets of the ancestors...*

The asmarandana's gentle intervals -- shorter than the kinanti, sweeter than the pangkur -- painted the ocean in sound, transforming the vast indifference of the waves into something intimate, something held and witnessed. The beauty of the form was not in its grandeur but in its tenderness: the asmarandana acknowledged that the world was beautiful even in the shadow of its ending, and that acknowledgment itself was an act of defiance against the darkness.

The leaf-boats sailed on, their enchanted hulls cutting the darkening water, their outriggers steady against the swell. Behind them, the sun set over the Indian Ocean in a blaze of crimson and gold. Ahead, to the east, the sky was darker -- not with the darkness of night, but with something else, a thickening of the air, a heaviness that had nothing to do with cloud and everything to do with the thing that waited beyond the strait, in its mountain of fire, patient as it had always been patient.

The keris burned.

Kian Santang pressed his hand against it and said nothing, and the fellowship sailed on into the gathering dark.

# Bab 16: Laut Jawa
## (The Java Sea)

---

The Java Sea was wrong.

Kian Santang knew it the moment they cleared the narrows of the Sunda Strait and the open water spread before them -- that great shallow basin between Jawa to the south, Sumatra to the west, and the dark mass of Kalimantan far to the north, where the horizon was nothing but water and sky and the faint suggestion of distances too vast to comprehend. He had never sailed these waters before. He had never seen them in their natural state -- the warm, turquoise shallows that the Sriwijayan traders called *Laut Susu*, the Milk Sea, for its pale, luminous color in the midday sun. But he knew, with the certainty of a farmer who can smell a bad harvest in the wind, that what he was looking at was not right.

The water was red.

Not the red of sunset or the ruddy hue of river silt carried to sea by monsoon floods. This was a deeper color, a darker stain -- the rust-red of iron oxide, of volcanic mineral, of water that had been in contact with something hot and corrosive far below the surface. It spread from the east in great tendrils, like ink diffusing through clear water, the leading edges pale and translucent, the deeper channels behind them opaque and thick, carrying with them a smell that hit the back of the throat and stayed there: sulfur, brimstone, the acrid chemistry of subterranean fire meeting cold seawater.

"Volcanic discharge," Eyang Guru said, from his position in the prow. His voice was calm, but his knuckles were white on his staff. "The vents on the ocean floor around Krakatau are active. The bayu of the jaladri is being corrupted from below -- poisoned at its source, the way a well is poisoned by fouling the aquifer."

The sky matched the water. To the east, where the horizon should have been a clean line of blue meeting blue, a bank of cloud sat low and heavy, bruise-colored, shot through with veins of dull orange that were not lightning but something else -- the reflected glow of fire, of magma light bouncing off the underside of volcanic cloud, giving the entire eastern sky the look of a furnace seen through dirty glass. It was midday. The sun was directly overhead. But the light that fell upon the Java Sea was filtered and dimmed, as though the sky itself had developed a cataract.

"This is his domain," Raden Wijaya said. He stood at the stern of the lead boat, his hand on the tiller -- the leaf-boats had grown tillers overnight, another adaptation of their living hulls to the demands of the open sea. His face was hard, set in the expression of a man who has seen the thing he most feared and has decided to face it anyway. "The Shadow King. His power extends through the water, through the air, through the fire beneath the sea floor. We sail through his blood."

Gajahmada, in the second boat, said nothing. But his hand was on the hilt of his keris, and his eyes scanned the discolored water with the tactical alertness of a general assessing a battlefield. He was counting. Measuring distances. Noting wind direction, current speed, the position of the few islands visible through the haze. Even now, even here, the soldier's mind was working.

"How far to Krakatau?" Kian Santang asked.

"Two days' sailing," Eyang Guru said. "If the wind holds and nothing bars our path." He paused. The qualification hung in the air like the smell of sulfur. "But the strait is narrow here, and the sea is shallow, and the things that live in these waters are no longer the things they were."

---

They had been sailing for half a day when the warships appeared.

Cepot saw them first -- he had climbed to the top of the mast (the leaf-boats had grown masts, too, with sails of stretched leaf-membrane that caught the wind with eerie efficiency) and was perched there with the easy balance of a creature accustomed to high places, his sharp eyes scanning the haze ahead.

"Ships," he called down. "Eight -- no, twelve. Big ones. Coming from the north."

Raden Wijaya was on his feet instantly, one hand shading his eyes, the other on the Nagasasra. "What kind?"

"Outriggers. Double outriggers. War pennants." Cepot paused, squinting through the murky air. "Red and gold banners. A serpent emblem."

"Sriwijaya," Eyang Guru said.

They came out of the haze like ghosts materializing from smoke -- twelve great warships of the Sriwijayan navy, each one a *jong* of massive construction, forty paces long, their double outriggers spreading wide as the wings of a garuda in flight. They were built for war and for the open sea: high-prowed, with fighting platforms fore and aft, their hulls of dark teak planked and pegged in the Sriwijayan style, caulked with damar resin until they gleamed like polished stone. Each ship carried a crew of fifty or more, and the crews were armed -- spearmen lining the gunwales, archers on the fighting platforms, their bows strung, their arrows nocked. The lead ship flew the banner of the Sriwijayan empire: a great serpent of gold on a field of crimson, its body coiling through stylized waves.

The fleet moved with the coordinated precision of long practice -- not the clumsy maneuvering of river boats but the disciplined geometry of a naval force that had controlled these sea lanes for centuries. They fanned out in a crescent formation, closing the gap between the islands on either side, and the fellowship's two leaf-boats found themselves in the center of the crescent's embrace, tiny and green and impossibly fragile against the dark mass of the war fleet.

"Hold course," Eyang Guru said. "Do not deviate. Do not draw weapons."

Tumenggung Batu's hand had gone to his hammer. "If they --"

"They are not the enemy," Eyang Guru said, with a sharpness that brooked no argument. "Not yet. Hold."

The lead Sriwijayan jong closed to within hailing distance. A figure stood on the fighting platform at its prow -- a tall man, bare-chested, his skin the dark mahogany of the Sumatran coast, his torso covered in tattoos that Kian Santang recognized from the Sriwijayan envoy at the Council of Dieng: waves and serpents and wind-patterns, the cartography of a life lived on the sea. He wore a sarong of deep indigo and a headcloth of crimson silk, and at his belt hung a cutlass of curved steel and a keris in a sheath of lacquered wood. His face was hard, angular, shaped by decades of salt wind and sun and the particular authority of a man accustomed to absolute command on the open water.

"Identify yourselves," the captain called across the water. His voice was the voice of the sea -- deep, carrying, accustomed to being heard over wind and wave. "These waters are under Sriwijayan jurisdiction. State your purpose, your origin, and your destination. You have until my archers lose patience."

Raden Wijaya stepped to the prow of the lead boat. He moved with the unhurried authority that was his nature -- the manner of a man who had never needed to hurry because the world, sooner or later, came to him. He drew himself to his full height, and in the filtered light of the volcanic sky his bearing was unmistakably royal -- the set of the shoulders, the lift of the chin, the quality of stillness that marked the blood of kings even when the kingdom was gone.

"I am Raden Wijaya Kartaningrat," he said. His voice carried across the water without strain. "Last of the line of Mataram. I travel with the Persekutuan Keris -- the Fellowship of the Keris -- by order of the Council of Dieng, under the blessing of Prabu Siliwangi of Pasundan. We carry a burden to Krakatau that concerns the survival of all Nusantara."

The Sriwijayan captain studied him. The tattooed man's eyes were the black of deep water, and they missed nothing -- the Keris Nagasasra at Raden Wijaya's hip, the quality of the highland cloaks, the impossible green of the leaf-boats, the Bidadari in the second vessel whose beauty marked her as something other than human.

"Mataram's line is dead," the captain said. "So say the records of the Sriwijayan court."

"The records are mistaken," Raden Wijaya said. "As the court will learn, in time."

A pause. The sea moved between them, red-stained and restless.

"I know your face," the captain said at last. "Or a face like it. I served under Admiral Jayasena at the Battle of the Brantas, and the admiral had a portrait in his quarters -- an old painting, very old, of a Mataram king. You wear that face." He paused. "I am Kapten Laksamana Surya of the Sriwijayan Western Fleet. My orders are to patrol these waters and prevent passage to unauthorized vessels. Krakatau and the strait around it are closed. Nothing goes in. Nothing comes out."

"On whose authority?" Gajahmada's voice cut across the water from the second boat, sharp and commanding. The Majapahit general had risen to his feet, and even on the small leaf-boat he carried the bearing of a man accustomed to commanding fleets, not riding in them.

The Sriwijayan captain looked at him. Something shifted in the tattooed man's expression -- recognition, perhaps, or the wariness of a sailor who knows the smell of a storm.

"On the authority of reality, General," the captain said. His title for Gajahmada was delivered without deference and without insult -- the professional courtesy of one military man to another. "The strait is death. I have lost four ships in the past month. Four good ships, with good crews, swallowed by the sea. Naga Laut swarm the deep channels. The water itself burns -- volcanic vents open without warning, boiling the surface. The currents are unpredictable, the winds corrupted. Even the stars are wrong above Krakatau. My navigators cannot hold a course."

He looked back at Raden Wijaya. "I do not question your purpose, son of Mataram. I question your survival. Turn back. Find another way. The sea route to Krakatau is closed."

"There is no other way," Eyang Guru said. His voice was quiet, but it carried across the water with a clarity that had nothing to do with volume and everything to do with the weight of centuries. The Sriwijayan captain looked at the old sage, and something in his expression changed -- a recognition deeper than rank or nationality, the instinctive deference of a man in the presence of something ancient and numinous.

"We go forward," Eyang Guru said. "As we must."

Kapten Laksamana Surya held the sage's gaze for a long moment. Then he nodded -- not in agreement, but in acceptance of the kind of decision that is beyond argument.

"Then may the sea carry you," he said. "And may you find it kinder than it has been to my fleet." He turned to his crew. "Open the formation. Let them through."

The Sriwijayan warships parted. The crescent widened, and the leaf-boats passed between the dark hulls, small and green and fragile, like two leaves blown between the trunks of ancient trees. As they passed the flagship, the captain called once more across the narrowing gap.

"Raden Wijaya."

The prince looked up.

"The strait is death now. Naga Laut swarm the waters. Ships disappear." A pause. "If you reach Krakatau, do what you must. If the darkness in that mountain can be ended, then every sailor on this sea owes you a debt that cannot be repaid."

Raden Wijaya inclined his head -- a gesture of respect from one warrior to another -- and the leaf-boats sailed on, and the Sriwijayan fleet closed its formation behind them, and the warships vanished into the haze like a memory of better times.

---

The attack came at the narrows.

They had been sailing for hours since the Sriwijayan patrol, threading between small islands that rose from the reddened sea like the backs of sleeping animals -- volcanic cones, most of them, barren or thinly forested, their shores black with basalt. The channel between two of the larger islands narrowed to a strait barely a hundred paces wide, and Eyang Guru had guided them toward it because the deep-water channels on either side were too exposed -- too visible, too open to whatever hunted in the volcanic depths.

The water in the narrows was dark. Not the red of the open sea but a deeper color -- the oily black of water saturated with volcanic mineral, water that had lost its transparency entirely, that reflected nothing, absorbed everything, lay between the islands like a pool of liquid obsidian.

"I do not like this," Tumenggung Batu said. His voice was low, his hand on his hammer. The Kurcaci chieftain's instincts were tuned to stone and metal, to the deep vibrations of the earth, and something in the vibrations of this water had set him on edge. "The sea floor is wrong. I can feel it through the hull. Something moves below us. Something large."

Dewi Anjani had unslung her bow. She stood in the second boat's prow, her feet planted wide against the roll, an arrow nocked, her amber eyes scanning the black water with the intensity of a hawk searching for prey -- or for the shadow of a predator.

"Naga Laut," she said. The words were quiet and certain.

The water erupted.

It came from below and to the right -- a detonation of black water that hit the boats like a monsoon wave, drenching them, blinding them, filling Kian Santang's mouth with the taste of salt and sulfur and something else, something organic and rotten, the stench of corruption made liquid. He was thrown against the gunwale, gasping, and through the spray he saw it.

The Naga Laut.

It was vast. Its body rose from the water in a column of scales and muscle, thick as the trunk of a waringin tree, its surface not the iridescent blue-green of the friendly sea serpent that had escorted them to Sianok but a sick, lightless black -- the black of oil, of tar, of organic matter in an advanced state of decay. Its scales were crusted with barnacles and volcanic mineral, and where the scales had been torn or damaged, the flesh beneath glowed a dull, sullen red, as though the creature's blood were magma rather than ichor. Its eyes -- two great discs set in a skull the size of a fisherman's hut -- were the red of forge-coals, pupilless, burning with a light that was not intelligence but something worse: compulsion. The blank, furious obedience of a living thing whose will had been overridden by a force it could not resist.

The serpent had been corrupted. Kian Santang understood this in the instant before terror took over -- understood it the way the keris understood it, the blade flaring with sudden heat against his back, recognizing the signature of its maker in the creature's ruin. This Naga Laut had once been a guardian of the deep, one of the ancient serpents that the Sriwijayan sailors had revered and feared in equal measure. Now it was a weapon. A thing of the Shadow King, turned against the sea it had once protected, driven from the volcanic depths to patrol and destroy.

"BRACE!" Raden Wijaya shouted, and the word was swallowed by the serpent's roar -- a sound that was not a sound but a vibration, a deep-frequency pulse that hit the chest like a fist, that shook the water into standing waves and set the leaf-boats pitching violently in the chop.

The Naga Laut struck.

Its head came down like a battering ram, aimed at the second boat. Gajahmada threw himself to one side as the massive skull crashed into the water a hand's breadth from the hull, sending up a wall of black spray. The serpent's body followed, coiling, and the coils were everywhere -- loops of black-scaled muscle rising from the water on both sides, encircling the boats, contracting. The creature was not trying to eat them. It was trying to crush them. To wrap them in its body and squeeze until the hulls shattered and the passengers were spilled into the oily water where the sea itself would finish the work.

Raden Wijaya was on the prow of the lead boat before Kian Santang had regained his feet. The prince's Keris Nagasasra was drawn -- the blade catching no light in this dark place, but the naga pamor in its steel seemed to writhe with a life of its own, responding to the presence of its reptilian namesake. Raden Wijaya planted his feet on the bucking hull and drove the blade into the nearest coil as it rose past the gunwale. "*Tempuken!*" he roared -- the Javanese Ngoko battle-cry, raw and unadorned, the word a warrior shouts not for his enemy's ears but for his own, to drive the blade deeper, to burn away fear with the heat of action.

The keris bit deep. Black ichor sprayed from the wound -- not blood, not truly, but a thick, viscous fluid that hissed where it struck the leaf-hull and smoked where it struck human skin. Raden Wijaya did not flinch. He twisted the blade, widened the wound, and the serpent screamed -- that sub-sonic roar again, louder, closer, vibrating the bones of every person in both boats.

The coil recoiled, pulling back, but another rose to replace it. The serpent's body was a labyrinth of muscle, loop crossing loop, and the boats were caught in it like insects in a web.

Tumenggung Batu did not wait for the coil to reach him. He leaped.

The Kurcaci chieftain launched himself from the gunwale of the second boat with the explosive power of a creature born from volcanic stone, his war hammer swinging overhead in a two-handed arc. He landed on the serpent's body -- on the slick, oil-black scales -- and his iron-shod boots found purchase where no human foot could have held, the metal studs biting into the gaps between scales with the instinctive precision of a creature who understood stone and scale and the geometry of grip. The hammer came down.

The impact was enormous. The serpent's scales, which had turned the point of Raden Wijaya's keris only at the thickest junctions, shattered under the hammer like pottery. Fragments of black scale flew in all directions, revealing the red-glowing flesh beneath, and the Naga Laut thrashed -- a violent convulsion that sent waves crashing over both boats and nearly threw Tumenggung Batu into the sea. He held. He held because Kurcaci do not fall from things they have chosen to stand on, as a point of geological principle, and he swung again, and again, each blow cracking the corrupted armor, each impact driving deeper into the creature's flank.

Dewi Anjani fired.

Her bow sang -- a sound like a harp string breaking -- and the arrow flew flat and true across the twenty paces between the second boat and the serpent's head. The head had risen again, rearing above the boats, the red eyes scanning for the source of its pain, the jaws opening to reveal teeth like curved daggers, each one stained black with the residue of whatever the creature had been devouring in the volcanic depths. The arrow struck below the jaw, in the soft tissue of the throat, and the shaft sank to its feathers.

The serpent flinched. Its head jerked sideways, and its roar became a hiss -- a terrible, pressurized exhalation of gas and fury that carried the stench of the deep vents. But the wound was not mortal. The creature's corruption had thickened its tissues, hardened its flesh, made it something more than a natural serpent and far harder to kill.

Gajahmada was fighting on the second boat. The general had drawn his keris and his long spear, and he fought with both in the cramped, pitching chaos of the deck with a skill that was breathtaking and terrifying in equal measure. A coil rose over the stern; he drove his spear into it, using the serpent's own weight to pin the coil against the gunwale. Another coil swept across the bow; he ducked under it, slashing upward with his keris, opening a gash that wept black fluid. His movements were precise, economical, brutally effective -- the movements of a man who had spent his life learning to kill and had never found a target he could not eventually bring down.

"WATER!" Bagus shouted. "We are taking water!"

Kian Santang turned. The lead boat was damaged. A coil had struck the hull amidships, and the enchanted leaf-membrane had torn -- not shattered, as wood would have, but split along a seam, and through the split the oily seawater was pouring in, black and foul-smelling, pooling in the bottom of the boat with a speed that meant they had minutes, not hours.

Bagus was already bailing. His thick arms worked with the mechanical efficiency of a man performing a task he understood -- water out, boat up, companions alive. He had found a cooking pot and was using it as a bailer, scooping and throwing, scooping and throwing, the rhythm as steady as a heartbeat. Beside him, Dawala had torn a section of his cloak free and was stuffing it into the tear, trying to slow the flood, his round face set in an expression of determination that was almost comically fierce.

"Cepot!" Bagus yelled. "Stop gawking and BAIL!"

Cepot, who had been clinging to the mast with the wide-eyed paralysis of a Tuyul confronting something far larger than anything in his considerable experience, shook himself free of his terror and dropped to the deck. He grabbed a waterskin, emptied it, and began scooping water with a speed and energy that matched Bagus's -- because whatever else Cepot was, he was not a coward, and when the crisis was real, the jokes stopped and the work began.

The keris screamed.

Not whispered. Not murmured. Screamed. The sound was not in Kian Santang's ears but in his bones, in his blood, in the marrow of his spine -- a sound like metal under impossible stress, like a blade being forced past its limit, and with the sound came the pull. The keris wanted to be drawn. It wanted it with a hunger that was physical, that tugged at Kian Santang's hands like ropes tied to his wrists, that filled his mind with images of power -- the serpent dissolving, the sea parting, the shadow fleeing before the blade's authority. *Draw me*, it said, and for the first time the whisper was not a suggestion but a command. *Draw me and I will end this. Draw me and you will be safe. Draw me and nothing in this sea or any sea will touch you again.*

Kian Santang's hand moved toward the wrapping.

He stopped it. He stopped it with an effort that was not physical but spiritual -- the effort of a man who stands at the edge of a cliff and wills his feet to root, who looks into the fire and wills his hand to close rather than reach. He pressed both hands flat against the deck and held them there, trembling, while the keris raged against its bindings and the serpent raged above him and the boat filled with black water.

"No," he said. The word was quiet, meant for the blade alone. "Not for you. Not for this."

The keris subsided. Not in acceptance -- in contempt. The silence it left was the silence of something that could afford to wait.

The battle turned on Dewi Anjani's arrows.

She had three arrows left -- three of the meteorite-tipped shafts that had been forged in the celestial fires of the Bidadari homeland, their points hammered from stones that had fallen from the sky, carrying with them the cold fire of the upper atmosphere, a light that the dark things feared. She had been saving them. Through the entire voyage, through every danger, she had kept these three arrows in a separate quiver, wrapped in silk, waiting for the moment when nothing less would serve.

The moment had arrived.

The first arrow struck the serpent's body where Tumenggung Batu's hammer had shattered the scales, driving into the exposed flesh with a flash of white light that made the oily water steam. The Naga Laut convulsed. Its coils loosened fractionally, and the second boat lurched free of one encircling loop, rocking wildly.

The second arrow struck the creature's flank, lower, closer to the waterline, and the flash was brighter -- a searing, actinic light that burned through the murk like a star born in the belly of the sea. The serpent's roar became something else -- a sound of pain, genuine and terrible, the cry of a creature that had been beyond pain for so long that the sensation was almost novel.

The third arrow.

Dewi Anjani stood in the prow of the second boat, her feet braced against the roll, her bow drawn to its full extent, the meteorite-tipped arrow nocked and steady. The serpent's head was above them -- twenty paces up, swaying, the red eyes blazing, the jaws agape, a rope of black saliva swinging from its teeth. It was looking at her. It was looking directly at her, and in its corrupted eyes there was a flicker of something that might have been recognition -- the ancient enmity between the forces of corruption and the Bidadari who had fought them since the dawn of the world.

Dewi Anjani loosed.

The arrow flew upward in a line so straight it seemed to divide the sky. It struck the serpent's left eye -- that great disc of burning red -- and passed through it, and the meteorite point carried its celestial fire deep into the creature's skull, and the light that erupted from the wound was not the white flash of the previous arrows but something brighter, something absolute, a radiance that for one instant turned the dark narrows into noonday and cast shadows so sharp they seemed carved from stone.

The Naga Laut screamed. Its head snapped backward, the arrow buried to its feathers in the shattered eye, and its body went rigid -- every coil locking, every muscle seizing -- and in that instant of paralysis Raden Wijaya moved.

He had been waiting. Through the entire battle, through the chaos and the spray and the pitching of the deck, some part of the prince's mind had been calculating -- watching the serpent's patterns, reading its movements, waiting for the moment when its guard would drop and its skull would come within reach. He leaped from the prow of the lead boat onto the nearest coil, ran along its curved surface with the sure-footed grace of a silat master whose art treated every surface as a floor, and reached the head.

The Keris Nagasasra went in below the jaw.

Raden Wijaya drove the ancestral blade of Mataram upward through the soft tissue of the throat and into the skull, and the naga pamor in the steel blazed -- a cold blue light that answered the celestial fire of Dewi Anjani's arrow, two ancient powers meeting in the ruin of a corrupted creature. The prince held the blade with both hands, bracing himself against the serpent's death-throes, and he spoke -- one word, in old Javanese, the language of the Mataram kings, the Ngoko of battle, a word that was not a spell but an authority:

"*Mati.*"

Die.

The Naga Laut died.

It died in a convulsion that threw black water fifty feet into the air, that sent waves crashing against the island shores on both sides, that rocked the leaf-boats so violently that Dawala was thrown overboard and had to be hauled back by Bagus and Cepot, each grabbing an arm, hauling him over the gunwale like a landed fish. It died with its coils unraveling, its massive body going slack in the water, its scales losing their oily black sheen and fading to the grey of ash, the corruption draining from the flesh like ink from a wet cloth. It died, and the red light in its remaining eye guttered and went dark, and the great head sank slowly beneath the surface, carrying Dewi Anjani's arrow and the memory of its enslavement into the deep.

Raden Wijaya pulled the Nagasasra free. He stood on the sinking skull, knee-deep in black water, the blade dripping ichor, his face streaked with the creature's fluids, and he looked up at the sky with an expression that held no triumph -- only the exhausted relief of a man who has survived something that should have killed him and knows that worse is coming.

He leaped back to the lead boat as the serpent's body rolled and sank.

---

The cost was one boat.

The lead vessel was dying. The tear in its hull had widened during the battle, and the enchantment that had sustained the leaf-membrane was failing -- the green was fading to brown, the resilience going out of the material, the living boat returning to the dead leaf it had once been. Water poured in from three separate breaches. The outrigger on the port side had been snapped off by a coil-strike, and the boat listed badly, taking on more water with every roll.

"We have minutes," Eyang Guru said. His voice was calm. It was always calm. But his ancient hands moved quickly as he transferred his staff and his satchel and his maps to the second boat, and his calmness was the calmness of a man who knows that panic helps nothing and composure helps everything.

They transferred. It was not elegant. Nine people, plus weapons, plus supplies, plus the packs that Bagus had prepared with such methodical care -- all of it had to be moved from one pitching, sinking vessel to another pitching, intact vessel in the time it took for the lead boat to lose its battle with the sea. They formed a chain: Raden Wijaya on the sinking boat, passing bundles; Gajahmada straddling the gap between the two hulls, his long arms reaching; Tumenggung Batu receiving, stacking, organizing with the efficiency of a creature accustomed to managing tight spaces underground. Bagus counted supplies as they came across, his lips moving, his face tight with the particular anguish of a quartermaster watching inventory diminish.

They lost half the water. They lost a quarter of the food. They lost Dawala's spare clothes, Cepot's whittling collection, one of Tumenggung Batu's armor repair kits, and -- most painfully -- three of Eyang Guru's lontar maps, which sank into the black water before anyone could reach them.

"My maps," the sage said, watching them go with an expression that, for one unguarded moment, held genuine grief.

"We will find the way without them," Raden Wijaya said. "We have found the way this far."

The lead boat sank. It went down slowly, with a kind of dignity -- the leaf curling inward as the enchantment left it, the green membrane folding upon itself, returning to the shape it had held before the Bidadari had sung it into something more. In its last moments, it looked like what it was: a leaf on the water, carried by the current, temporary and beautiful and gone.

They crowded into the remaining vessel. Nine companions in a boat built for five, with the cramped discomfort of too many bodies in too small a space. Tumenggung Batu sat amidships, his broad frame occupying the space of two, his hammer across his knees. Dewi Anjani stood in the prow, her bow across her back, her quiver lighter by three arrows that could never be replaced. Gajahmada and Raden Wijaya shared the stern, an arrangement that put two proud men within arm's reach of each other and required both to exercise a restraint that neither found natural. The four Tuyul wedged themselves into the spaces between -- Bagus against the mast, protecting the remaining supplies with his body; Cepot and Dawala port and starboard, their legs tangled; Kian Santang in the center, the keris burning against his back.

Eyang Guru sat at the tiller. He looked very old. The battle had cost him something -- not a wound, not a visible injury, but a depletion, a dimming, as though the energy he had spent maintaining the ward around their camp and guiding the boats through corrupted waters had been drawn from a reserve that was not infinite. His white hair hung lank and wet. His hands trembled faintly on the tiller.

But his eyes were clear. And when he turned them eastward, toward the horizon, those eyes held a steadiness that anchored them all.

"Look," he said.

They looked.

---

Krakatau.

It rose from the sea at the center of the Sunda Strait -- not a single mountain but a complex of volcanic peaks, a jagged crown of black rock and grey ash rising from the water with the brutal geometry of something that had been built rather than grown. The main cone was enormous, its slopes steep and barren, striped with rivers of hardened lava that ran from the summit to the waterline in dark channels, like the veins of a diseased heart made visible. Smaller cones flanked it -- parasitic vents, subsidiary craters, the geological children of the primary eruption. Together they formed an archipelago of fire and stone, an island group that looked less like land than like the earth's skeleton exposed, the flesh of soil and vegetation stripped away to reveal the raw, molten truth beneath.

The summit was shrouded in cloud. But this was not the white cloud of the highland peaks, not the gentle mist of Dieng or the sea fog of the Sriwijayan coast. This cloud was dark -- a pillar of black and grey and dirty brown that rose from the caldera in a column so vast it seemed to support the sky itself, spreading at its apex into an anvil-shaped mass that blocked the sun and cast a shadow on the sea for leagues in every direction. The underside of the cloud glowed dull orange, lit from beneath by the fires of the caldera, and occasional flashes of volcanic lightning flickered within the column, brief and silent at this distance, illuminating the internal structure of the smoke in instantaneous snapshots of turbulence.

The smell was thick now. No longer the faint sulfurous hint they had detected at the coast of Sumatra, but a wall of chemical stench -- hydrogen sulfide, sulfur dioxide, the acrid bite of volcanic gases that burned the nose and watered the eyes and laid a taste on the tongue like copper pennies and rotting eggs. The air itself felt different -- heavier, denser, saturated with particulates that dimmed the light and turned the sun, where it could be glimpsed through the volcanic cloud, into a disc of dull orange, a dying ember in a poisoned sky.

And the sea around the mountain was alive with wrongness. Patches of water boiled -- actual boiling, steam rising from the surface in white plumes where submarine vents discharged their heat. Dead fish floated in mats of grey foam, their scales stripped, their bodies cooked from below. The water's color shifted through the spectrum of volcanic chemistry: red, orange, sickly yellow, the deep black of water so saturated with mineral that it had ceased to be water in any meaningful sense and had become something else -- a medium, a carrier, a liquid extension of the mountain's toxic blood.

No one spoke.

The sight of Krakatau silenced them the way the sight of death silences -- not with shock, which passes, but with understanding, which does not. This was the Shadow King's domain. This was where the Keris Murka had been forged, where Raja Iblis had poured his malice into metal, where the nine lesser blades had been hammered on an anvil of obsidian above a forge of magma. This was the place they had been traveling toward since Dieng, since Padalarang, since the moment Kian Santang had lifted the keris from a limestone shelf in the caves of Gunung Sewu and felt it grip his soul.

This was where they had to go.

The keris burned. Not whispered -- burned. The heat of it seared through the batik wrapping, through the fabric of Kian Santang's shirt, into the skin of his back, and he gasped and arched away from it but there was nowhere to go in the cramped boat. The blade's reaction to Krakatau was not a whisper but a cry -- a sound of recognition, of longing, of a thing separated from its source for centuries now sensing that source across the diminishing miles of corrupted water. The pamor patterns moved beneath the cloth -- Kian Santang could feel them shifting, coiling, reaching toward the mountain like iron filings drawn to a magnet.

*Home*, the keris said. The word was clear. Unmistakable. Not whispered but spoken, projected into his mind with a clarity that left no room for doubt or interpretation. *Home. I am coming home.*

Kian Santang pressed his hand against the wrapping. His palm blistered. He did not remove it.

"That is where we must go," he said.

His voice was quiet. He did not raise it. He did not need to. The words carried across the small, crowded boat with the weight of the obvious -- a statement of fact so fundamental that ornamentation would have been an insult to its truth.

No one answered.

Raden Wijaya looked at the mountain and saw the end of his bloodline's shame. Dewi Anjani looked and saw the culmination of ages of Bidadari vigilance. Tumenggung Batu looked and saw stone that had been corrupted, and his craftsman's soul burned with the desire to see it cleansed. Gajahmada looked and saw a battlefield -- the final battlefield, the one that would vindicate his oath or render it meaningless. Eyang Guru looked and saw the pattern of history arriving at its crisis point, the moment toward which every thread of every age had been converging. Bagus looked and saw the place where his friend would face the worst, and set his jaw, and said nothing, and did not look away. Cepot looked and, for once in his life, found no joke equal to the occasion. Dawala looked and trembled, but he looked.

As they stared at the mountain, Kian Santang heard a sound from behind them -- faint, carried across the darkened water. Singing. A *kinanti*, rising from the direction of the open sea, its melody threading through the sulfurous air like a silver wire through smoke. It was not Dewi Anjani's voice, nor any voice he recognized -- it was the sea itself, or perhaps the memory of the sea, or perhaps the Queen of the Southern Sea sending a final blessing across her domain's edge before the travelers passed beyond her reach. The kinanti's verses -- eight syllables, eight syllables, the patient rhythm of hope sustained against all evidence -- carried across the water and dissolved into the volcanic haze:

*Kinanti nu jauh lumampah,*
*ti sagara ka sagara,*
*harepan nu henteu peugat,*
*sanajan bumi beulah...*

*Kinanti who journeys far, / from ocean to ocean, / hope that does not break, / even though the earth splits...*

The melody faded. The silence that replaced it was not empty but full -- full of resolve.

The silence settled. Not the silence of despair -- the silence of resolve. The silence of nine very different souls arriving, together, at the same understanding: that this was real, and this was now, and the only way out was through.

The leaf-boat sailed on. Battered, crowded, riding low in the water with too many bodies and too few supplies, its single outrigger holding it barely level against the corrupted swell. Behind them, the body of the Naga Laut sank into the volcanic deep. Around them, the Java Sea burned with the Shadow King's influence, its waters reddened, its currents fouled, its ancient creatures turned to instruments of malice.

Ahead, Krakatau's smoke rose like a dark pillar holding up the sky, and the fire in the caldera painted the underside of the cloud in shades of hell, and the mountain waited as it had always waited -- patient, hungry, old beyond the reckoning of any living thing, the throne and the prison and the forge of the greatest evil Nusantara had ever known.

Nine companions. One boat. One blade.

The Sunda Strait opened before them, and the fellowship sailed into the shadow of the mountain.
