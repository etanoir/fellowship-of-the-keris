## I. The Final Ascent

Tangga Kalajengking-na teu boga welas asih, sarta maranéhanana teu boga tanaga.

The Stairs of the Scorpion had no mercy, and they had no strength left to beg for it.

The steps were cut into the living rock of the caldera's western wall -- not carved with the geometric precision of the Kurcaci, whose stonework sang of purpose and devotion, but hacked with a crude, ancient brutality that suggested hands not interested in beauty. Hands interested only in passage. Each step was irregular: some barely wide enough for a single foot, others slanting so severely that the climber's weight shifted sickeningly toward the abyss at their left. The stone was volcanic basalt, dark as dried blood in the starlight, and its surface was pitted and sharp, tearing at their fingers when they grabbed for handholds, shredding the thin leather of Bagus's sandals until the soles were gone and he climbed barefoot, his toes bleeding, his jaw set.

They had been climbing for seven hours.

Kian Santang pressed his forehead against the cold stone and breathed. The air was thin here -- not the stifling, sulfurous atmosphere of the lowland ash fields, but a high, sterile cold that scraped the lungs like a blade drawn through cheesecloth. Each breath was a negotiation between the body's demand for oxygen and the mountain's reluctance to provide it. His thighs burned. His calves had progressed past burning into a numb, mechanical trembling that he could not stop and could not ignore. The Keris Murka was a weight on his back that defied physics -- heavier than a blade of its size should be, heavier than the iron and gold and dark *sakti* that constituted its physical form, as though the weapon understood where they were going and was dragging its heels like a child being led to punishment.

*Home,* the keris whispered. *You climb toward my home. Why do you fight what is inevitable? Let me guide you. Open the wrappings. Hold me. I will give you the strength to finish this climb.*

Kian Santang gritted his teeth and took another step. The voice was louder now than it had been in the lowlands, louder than it had been in the marshes, louder even than it had been at the Black Gate. Each step closer to the caldera of Krakatau was a step closer to the forge where the keris had been made, and the weapon knew its maker's fire the way a migratory bird knows its nesting ground -- with a certainty that transcended thought, that lived in the grain of the metal itself, in the pattern of the *pamor* folds that Empu Gandring had hammered into the blade in the First Age when the world was younger and darkness wore a crown.

"Kian."

Bagus's hand found his elbow. The grip was firm despite the trembling in both their bodies -- Bagus's palms were raw and bleeding from the stone, and the blood made his fingers slippery, but he held on with the stubborn determination that was as essential to his character as the brown of his eyes or the broadness of his honest face.

"Rest," Bagus said. "There's a ledge."

Kian Santang looked up. Five steps above them, the staircase widened into a shallow shelf in the cliff face -- perhaps three arm-lengths deep and five across, barely enough for two bodies lying side by side, but after seven hours of vertical agony, it might as well have been the royal *pendopo* of Padalarang, silk cushions and all.

Si Burik was already there.

The creature was perched at the ledge's far edge, his long fingers wrapped around his own knees, his pale eyes catching what little light the stars provided and reflecting it back in two cold, wet discs. He was not winded. He was not trembling. He had scuttled up the stairs with the fluid ease of a gecko ascending a wall, pausing only to look back at his two companions with an expression that shifted between pity and contempt and something older and hungrier than either.

"Rest... rest is good," Si Burik murmured as they hauled themselves onto the ledge. "Sleep if master wants. Si Burik watches. Si Burik keeps guard. *Gollum... gollum.*"

Kian Santang collapsed onto the stone. The cold of the basalt seeped through his thinning shirt and into the muscles of his back, and the relief was so profound that for a moment it overrode the keris's whispering, overrode the terror that had become the baseline condition of his existence, overrode everything except the pure, animal gratitude of a body given permission to stop.

Bagus sat beside him, their shoulders touching. The Tuyul pulled the last of their food from the cloth satchel -- two strips of dried fish, a handful of crushed rice cakes, a waterskin with perhaps three swallows remaining. He divided the food with a precision that was almost mathematical, giving Kian Santang the larger portion without comment, the way he always did, the way he had done since they were boys sharing stolen fruit on the riverbanks of Padalarang.

"Eat," Bagus said.

Kian Santang ate. The fish was salt and leather and nothing. He chewed and swallowed because Bagus had asked him to, and denying Bagus a simple request had become the one cruelty that Kian Santang could not bring himself to commit in a world where cruelty had become the currency of survival.

They sat in silence. Below them -- far, far below, a distance that the dark concealed but the body remembered with a visceral, vertiginous shudder -- the fires of the Buta army burned in the ash fields before the Black Gate. From this height, the flames were a spill of orange embers, tiny and scattered, like the remnants of a *wayang* performance after the audience has gone home and the *dalang* has packed his puppets and only the coals of the *blencong* lamp remain, guttering in the wind. But each of those tiny lights was a campfire surrounded by Buta Raksasa with iron axes and black shields, and beyond them, in the choking dark of the caldera's interior, the Shadow King sat on his throne of volcanic glass and listened to the whispers of his blade -- this blade, Kian Santang's blade, the Keris Murka that called to its master across the dark.

"Do you remember the river?" Bagus said.

Kian Santang turned his head. Bagus was looking out at the darkness, but his eyes were focused on something far beyond it -- on something that existed in a different geography entirely, a landscape of memory where the sun shone and the water was clean and two small Tuyul splashed in the shallows of the Citarum without understanding that the world contained anything sharper than river stones.

"Which part of the river?" Kian Santang asked, and his voice sounded strange to him -- thin and hoarse, scraped raw by the dry air and the weeks of near-silence.

"The deep pool. Below the waterfall. Where we used to dive."

Kian Santang closed his eyes. He could see it. The pool was jade-green and impossibly clear, fed by a small waterfall that dropped three meters from a moss-covered ledge, and the sunlight came through the canopy in shifting columns of gold that turned the water into something luminous, something sacred, a *telaga* of the kind that the old stories said the Bidadari bathed in when they descended from the heavens. He and Bagus had spent entire afternoons there, diving from the ledge, swimming until their lips were blue and their mothers shouted their names from the village paths above.

"I remember," Kian Santang said.

"The water was always cold. Even in the dry season. Do you remember how cold?"

"Cold enough to make your bones ache."

"But we always jumped anyway." Bagus paused. His voice was careful, deliberate, as though he were constructing something fragile from the syllables. "That's what I think about, Kian. When it gets bad. When the dark gets too close and *aing* can't breathe. I think about jumping into that cold water, and the shock of it, and then -- the way the cold became good. The way your body adjusted, and suddenly you weren't cold anymore, you were just... alive. More alive than you'd ever been."

Kian Santang opened his eyes. He looked at Bagus. The starlight was faint, but it was enough to see the outline of his friend's face -- the broad nose, the wide mouth, the deep-set eyes that held a kindness so fundamental that it seemed less a personality trait than a geological feature, something laid down in the strata of his being long before consciousness arrived to take credit for it.

"Why are you telling me this?" Kian Santang asked.

Bagus was quiet for a moment. Then: "Because *aing* made a promise. To your uncle. To Eyang Guru. To myself. That I'd bring you back. That whatever happened out here, I'd bring you home so you could jump in that pool again. And I intend to keep that promise, Kian. *Karuhun.* On the graves of my parents and the roots of every *rasamala* tree in Padalarang. I will bring you home."

Kian Santang's throat tightened. The keris, which had been whispering its dark seductions with the fluency of a practiced tempter, fell silent. Not because it had been defeated or overpowered, but because, for just this moment, there was something in the air between the two friends that the weapon did not understand -- a frequency of loyalty and love that lay outside its vocabulary of domination, a language that the Shadow King's fire had never learned to speak.

"*Aksama,*" Kian Santang whispered. *Forgive me.* "For bringing you here. For all of it."

"Mulah," Bagus said firmly. *Don't.* "You didn't bring me. I came. There's a difference, *Priya.* And I would come again."

At the edge of the ledge, Si Burik watched them.

His pale eyes moved between the two friends with an intensity that was almost painful to behold -- not the predatory attention of a creature calculating advantage, but the starved, bewildered hunger of a being who had once known this kind of closeness and had lost it so long ago that the memory had become an open wound rather than a comfort. His thin lips moved. He was speaking to himself, but the words were too quiet to hear -- fragments of a private argument that had been raging inside his ruined mind for years, decades, centuries, the two halves of his shattered soul grappling endlessly over a question that neither could answer: *Was the jimat worth what it cost?*

His fingers twitched. His gaze drifted to the bundled shape of the Keris Murka on Kian Santang's back, and his pupils dilated, and a thin thread of saliva descended from the corner of his mouth. *Jimat-ku...* the thought came, unbidden and irresistible, pushing aside the tenderness that the two friends' conversation had momentarily awakened. *The jimat that was mine. That should be mine. That will be mine again, yes, precious, yesss...*

But then Bagus laughed -- a short, startled laugh at something Kian Santang had murmured, a sound so warm and so unexpected in this vertical wasteland of stone and cold that it seemed to have been imported from another world entirely -- and Si Burik flinched. His hands came up to cover his ears, as though the sound of uncomplicated friendship was a frequency that caused him physical pain.

"Sleep," Si Burik hissed, rocking back and forth. "Sleep, sleep. Tomorrow we reach the tunnel. Tomorrow we go through. Sleep now. Si Burik watches. Si Burik... *gollum*... Si Burik is faithful."

Kian Santang's eyelids were heavy. The exhaustion was a tide that he had been holding back for days, and now, with the stone beneath him and Bagus's warmth beside him and the brief, fragile truce of the keris's silence, the tide broke through. His eyes closed. His breathing deepened. The last thing he felt before sleep took him was Bagus's hand finding his in the dark, gripping once -- firm, warm, anchoring -- and then releasing.

The stars wheeled overhead. The fires burned below. Si Burik watched them sleep, and his face was a battlefield.

---

Dawn did not come to the Tangga Kalajengking. There was no horizon from which the sun could rise -- only the cliff face above and the void below and a gradual lightening of the sky that seeped into the stone like water into cloth, turning the black to charcoal, the charcoal to a dull, metallic grey. The light was grudging, mean, given up by the atmosphere with visible reluctance, as though the sun itself was uncertain whether this part of the world deserved illumination.

Kian Santang woke to the sound of Si Burik's voice.

"Up... up... must go up now. The stairs end soon. The tunnel begins. Quick, quick, before the sun makes us visible. Quick, quick, master must hurry. *Gollum.*"

The creature was already on the stairs above them, balanced on a step so narrow that his long toes curled over its edge like a bird's claws on a wire. His urgency was palpable -- his body vibrating with a kinetic energy that seemed barely contained by his emaciated frame, his eyes darting between the two Tuyul and the sky with an anxiety that could have been genuine concern for their safety or the restless impatience of a schemer whose plan required a specific timetable.

They climbed.

The final three hundred steps were the worst. The staircase narrowed to a single body's width and steepened until it was nearly vertical, the steps becoming little more than notches in the cliff face, and the void at their backs pulled at them with a gravitational insistence that seemed personal, as though the abyss had developed an opinion about their passage and was registering its objection. Kian Santang climbed without looking down. He climbed without looking up. He climbed by looking at the stone directly before his face, at the individual crystals of feldspar and pyroxene that glittered in the basalt, at the tiny lichens that clung to the crevices in defiance of every biological principle, at the small, desperate ecosystem of mites and fungi that made its living in the cracks of the world. He focused on these miniature wonders because the alternative -- contemplating the grand scale of his circumstances, the enormity of the task, the impossibility of survival -- would have stopped his legs.

Bagus climbed behind him, close enough that Kian Santang could feel the warmth of his breath on the backs of his calves. The Tuyul's bare feet left smears of blood on the stone, but he did not complain. He had never complained. Not once, in all the weeks since Padalarang, through the marshes and the ash fields and the terror of the Black Gate, had Bagus uttered a single word of self-pity. It was not stoicism -- stoicism implied a suppression of feeling, a disciplined refusal to acknowledge pain. Bagus simply did not consider his own suffering noteworthy. He was here for Kian Santang. His discomfort was irrelevant to that purpose.

Si Burik reached the top first and peered over the edge with his pale eyes wide. "Here... here!" he whispered excitedly, beckoning with both hands. "The mouth! The tunnel mouth! Come, come, come!"

Kian Santang pulled himself over the final step and onto a narrow ledge that jutted from the cliff face like a lip. Before him, a hole in the rock opened -- not a cave mouth in the natural sense, not the organic erosion of water and wind, but a hole that seemed to have been bored into the mountain by something deliberate. Something that had wanted *in*. The opening was roughly circular, perhaps two meters in diameter, and its edges were smooth -- not the smoothness of carving but the smoothness of passage, of a surface rubbed by the repeated transit of a large, hard body over a span of time so vast that the stone itself had learned the shape of the thing that moved through it.

A smell came from the opening. Not the sharp, honest sulfur of the volcanic vents, but something older and fouler -- a layered, complex stench that spoke of biological processes occurring in the dark. Dry rot. The acrid musk of a predator's den. The sweet, cloying decay of organic matter left to decompose in an airless space. And beneath it all, threading through the other scents like a bass note through a discordant chord, the sharp, chemical tang of venom -- a smell that bypassed the nose entirely and went straight to the brainstem, to the ancient lizard-brain that had been cataloguing threats since long before the first human ancestors descended from the trees.

Kian Santang's body stopped. Not his legs -- his whole body. Every muscle locked. Every nerve fired its warning. This was not the conscious fear of the rational mind assessing danger. This was the primal, pre-linguistic *no* of an organism that has detected, at the cellular level, the presence of something that exists specifically to kill it.

"Si Burik," Kian Santang said, and his voice was very quiet. "What lives in there?"

Si Burik did not meet his eyes. "Nothing... nothing lives there. Old tunnel. Empty. Just... webs. Old webs. Spiders gone. Long gone. *Gollum, gollum.* Come. We must go through. Only way into the caldera."

"You're lying."

"Not lying! *Mulah* say that, master! Si Burik doesn't lie! Si Burik promised! Si Burik..." His voice trailed off. His hands were shaking. He looked at the tunnel mouth with an expression that was, for just a moment, nakedly terrified -- the terror of a creature who knows exactly what waits inside but who has made a calculation in which the prize outweighs the risk, provided the risk is borne by someone else.

Then the expression vanished, replaced by the servile grin, the wet, toothless smile that Si Burik wore like a mask. "Through the tunnel, into the caldera, destroy the *jimat*, yes? That is what master wants. Si Burik helps. Si Burik knows the way. *Trust* Si Burik."

Kian Santang looked at Bagus. His friend's face was pale, his nostrils flared, his eyes fixed on the dark opening with the wary attention of a man watching a sleeping snake.

"I don't like it," Bagus said simply. "*Karuhun*, Kian. My ancestors are screaming at me to turn around."

"Mine too," Kian Santang said. "But there is no turning around. The Black Gate is behind us. The army is behind us. There is only forward."

He stepped into the tunnel. The darkness swallowed him whole.

## II. The Tunnel of Nyi Blorong

Gua Kalajengking bauna siga kuburan kuno nu geus lila katutup.

The Scorpion's Lair smelled of ancient tombs long sealed from the world. The air hit them like a physical barrier -- thick and stagnant, dense with the accumulated foulness of centuries, so heavy that breathing it felt less like respiration than like drinking. Kian Santang's lungs protested. His body, already weakened by weeks of deprivation and the keris's relentless draining of his *bayu*, recoiled from the atmosphere the way skin recoils from fire.

He lit the torch.

It was their last torch -- a stub of resinous pine wrapped in a strip of cloth torn from his own shirt, soaked in the remnants of the cooking oil that Bagus had rationed with the grim precision of a siege commander. The flame was small and orange and desperate, its light reaching perhaps three meters in any direction before the darkness ate it. Beyond that radius, the tunnel was pure, impenetrable black -- the black of deep caves, of sealed chambers, of places where photons had never traveled and were not welcome.

The walls were volcanic tube. Kian Santang had seen such tunnels in the hills near Padalarang -- tubes formed by flowing lava, their interiors smoothed by the passage of molten rock, their surfaces glazed with a dark, glassy coating that reflected the torchlight in distorted, funhouse shapes. But those tunnels had been small, human-scaled, the kind of passages a boy might crawl through on a dare. This was something else. This tunnel was vast -- four meters wide and three meters high, its ceiling arched in a rough semicircle, its floor littered with debris. It extended ahead of them in a gentle downward curve, disappearing into the black at the limit of the torchlight, and the sense of depth -- of distance -- was staggering. This passage went deep into the mountain. Into the mountain's gut.

The walls were covered in a pale, phosphorescent fungus that gave off a sickly green luminescence -- not enough to see by, but enough to create the unsettling impression that the stone itself was alive, pulsing with a faint bioluminescence that seemed to respond to their presence. Where the torchlight touched the fungus directly, it dimmed, retreating into the crevices of the rock as though fleeing from the heat. Where the torchlight did not reach, it glowed brighter, and the green light crawled along the tunnel walls in patterns that might have been random and might not, might have been the growth patterns of a simple organism responding to moisture and temperature, or might have been something deliberate -- a map, a warning, a message left by something that had lived in this tunnel long before the fungus and long before the mountain.

Water dripped. The sound was irregular, percussive, each drop landing with a sharp *plunk* that echoed through the tunnel and returned from the darkness ahead as a diminished, slightly delayed duplicate, as though the tunnel were repeating the sound back to them in a mocking whisper. *Plunk. Plunk-plunk. Plunk.* The rhythm had no pattern, no periodicity -- a *gamelan* stripped of all beauty, all ceremony, all human intention, reduced to its barest percussive skeleton, stitching the minutes together into a fabric of slow, accumulating dread.

Si Burik moved ahead of them, ten paces in front, just at the edge of the torchlight's reach. He moved on all fours, his fingers and toes finding purchase on the smooth stone with the adhesive confidence of an animal perfectly adapted to this environment. His pale eyes did not need the torchlight -- they had adjusted to the tunnel's darkness instantly, dilating to absorb the faint phosphorescent glow, and they shone in the black like two cold coins at the bottom of a well.

"This way... this way..." he murmured, his voice a continuous, low-grade mutter that blended with the dripping water and the shuffling of their feet into a drone of ambient anxiety. "Follow Si Burik. Si Burik knows. Si Burik came this way before, long ago, when Si Burik had the *jimat*, when Si Burik was whole, when the world was different and the tunnels were clean. Not so much... stuff... then. Less... webs. But the way is the same. Si Burik remembers. *Gollum.*"

Kian Santang moved slowly, one hand on the wall to guide him, the other gripping the hilt of his short blade. The wall beneath his fingers was slick with moisture and the spongy texture of the fungus, and the sensation was deeply unpleasant -- like touching the skin of something dead and damp that had been left in the dark too long. Every few steps, his fingers encountered something different: a ridge in the stone, a groove, a channel carved by water or worn by passage. Once, his hand closed on something that crumbled at his touch, and he pulled his fingers away and saw, in the torchlight, the pale, fragile remnants of a bone.

Behind him, Bagus breathed in short, terrified gasps. The Tuyul had drawn his gardening knife -- a short, broad blade with a worn wooden handle, designed for cutting roots and trimming *padi* stalks, as far from a weapon of war as a teaspoon is from a battle-axe -- and held it before him in a two-handed grip that was equal parts martial determination and the desperate need to hold onto something solid.

"The air," Bagus whispered. "It's getting worse."

He was right. With each step deeper into the tunnel, the atmosphere thickened. New elements entered the olfactory landscape: a sharp, acrid musk that stung the eyes; the dusty, fibrous smell of old silk; a sweet, rotting undertone that spoke of meat left to liquefy in the heat. And beneath it all, growing stronger with every meter they advanced, the chemical tang of venom -- sharper now, more distinct, a smell that had graduated from subliminal warning to conscious alarm, a smell that Kian Santang's body recognized and responded to with a cascade of adrenaline that made his heartbeat spike and his muscles tighten and his mouth go dry.

"Si Burik," Kian Santang said again. "This smell. What is it?"

"Nothing! Just... old things. Dead things. Things that died long ago." The creature did not turn around. His voice was higher now, tighter, the servile calm fraying at its edges. "Everything dies in tunnels, master. No sun. No water. Things come in and don't come out. Natural. Normal. Si Burik has seen many tunnels. Many dead things. Nothing to fear. *Gollum, gollum.*"

Kian Santang stopped. He raised the torch. The flame guttered and dipped in a sudden current of air -- a breath from deep in the tunnel, as though the mountain had sighed -- and in the momentary brightening that followed, the light reached further than before. It reached the ceiling.

The ceiling was covered in webs.

Not the delicate, geometric constructions of garden spiders, not the untidy cobwebs that gathered in the corners of village houses and were swept away with a broom. These were structures of a different order entirely -- thick, ropey cables of grey silk stretched across the tunnel's ceiling in a dense, overlapping lattice that sagged under its own weight like the rigging of a ship in a dead calm. Each strand was as thick as a man's finger, and where the torchlight caught them, they gleamed with a sticky, wet sheen that spoke of freshness -- these were not old webs, not abandoned architecture, but maintained structures, tended and renewed by something that was still here, still present, still weaving.

"Webs," Kian Santang said flatly.

Bagus made a sound in his throat. It was not a word. It was the sound a body makes when every instinct it possesses is screaming a single command and the conscious mind is refusing to obey.

"Old webs!" Si Burik said quickly, too quickly. "Very old! From the old times! Before Si Burik! Before the Shadow King! Before everything! The creature that made them is gone. Dead. Long dead. Only the webs remain. Webs last forever in tunnels. No wind to break them. No rain to wash them. Just webs. Old, empty, harmless webs. *Gollum.*"

"These are not old," Kian Santang said. He reached up and touched one of the lower strands. It adhered to his finger instantly -- a fierce, tenacious grip that he had to wrench his hand to break. A thread of silk stretched between his fingertip and the web, thin and glistening, vibrating with the tension of a plucked string. "This silk is fresh. Something is still making them."

Si Burik was silent for three heartbeats. Then: "We should move faster."

They moved.

The tunnel descended. The webs grew thicker. What had begun as isolated cables stretched across the ceiling became a continuous canopy of silk that lowered the effective height of the tunnel by a full meter, forcing them to crouch as they walked. The strands caught in Kian Santang's hair, stuck to his arms, draped across his face with the revolting intimacy of wet fingers touching skin in the dark. He brushed them away with mounting revulsion, but they clung -- each strand releasing its grip only when torn, leaving behind a residue of sticky protein that dried on his skin like a second layer of cold, dead tissue.

The floor changed. The smooth basalt gave way to a surface that crunched underfoot -- a carpet of small, brittle objects that shattered and ground beneath their weight. Kian Santang lowered the torch.

Bones.

Small bones. Bird bones, rat bones, the delicate, curved ribs of bats and the hollow, air-filled bones of forest pigeons. Hundreds of them -- thousands -- scattered across the tunnel floor in a dense, ghastly layer. They were uniformly white, picked clean of flesh, gnawed and sucked dry by mandibles that had left tiny scoring marks on the surfaces. Among them, glinting in the torchlight, were the iridescent wings of beetles, the chitinous exoskeletons of insects, and the occasional skull -- tiny, fragile, staring upward from the bone-carpet with empty orbits that seemed to track the torchlight as it passed.

And then -- larger bones. A femur that was too long and too thick to belong to any creature smaller than a man. A ribcage, collapsed and splayed, still partially wrapped in tatters of cloth that had once been a garment. A skull -- round, smooth, unmistakably humanoid -- lying on its side in the carpet of smaller remains, its jaw missing, its eye sockets filled with the luminous fungus, glowing green from within like a lantern made of death.

"*Karuhun,*" Bagus whispered, and the word was not an exclamation but a prayer -- an appeal to the ancestors whose bones might be among these, whose spirits might still linger in this terrible place, watching their descendants walk through the charnel house where their bodies had been consumed.

"Buta," Kian Santang said, kneeling to examine the large skull. The cranial structure was heavy-browed, thick-jawed -- the skull of a Buta Raksasa, not a Tuyul. "This one was a Buta. And this --" he touched the ribcage. "This one wore cloth. Not a Buta. A man. Or a Tuyul."

"Many came into the tunnel," Si Burik said from ahead. His voice had dropped to a whisper -- not the theatrical whisper of a guide performing concern, but the genuine, involuntary quietness of a creature who knows that something is listening. "Many came. Few came out. Si Burik... Si Burik was one of the few. Si Burik was small. Si Burik was quick. Si Burik squeezed through the cracks where the big ones could not go. *Gollum.* Si Burik survived."

"What killed them?" Bagus asked. "What killed all of these?"

Si Burik did not answer.

They pushed deeper.

The tunnel branched. The main passage continued its gradual descent into the mountain, but secondary passages opened on both sides -- smaller tubes, barely a meter in diameter, their openings partially blocked by dense curtains of webbing that hung like the beaded curtains in a Sundanese *warung*. Through the gaps in the silk, the phosphorescent glow pulsed brighter, and from the depths of these side-passages came sounds that Kian Santang's ears registered but his mind refused to process: soft, chitinous rustlings; the delicate click of jointed legs adjusting on stone; a faint, rhythmic scraping, like a blade being sharpened against a whetstone, that might have been the sound of mandibles opening and closing in the dark.

The keris went silent.

This was the most terrifying thing that had happened yet. The Keris Murka, which had whispered to Kian Santang without ceasing since the moment it came into his possession -- coaxing, threatening, promising, seducing, arguing, pleading, commanding -- fell silent. Not the brief pause of a speaker drawing breath, not the tactical quiet of a tempter waiting for the right moment. This was a cessation. A withdrawal. The keris's consciousness retreating into itself like a sea creature withdrawing into its shell, pulling its dark presence inward until Kian Santang could barely feel it against his back, as though the weapon were trying to make itself small. To make itself invisible.

The Keris Murka -- the weapon of the Shadow King, forged in the fires of Krakatau, imbued with a *sakti* so vast and so malevolent that it had corrupted every soul that had ever touched it -- was *afraid*.

And if the keris was afraid, then what lived in this tunnel was something that even the Shadow King's power respected. Something older. Something that predated the forging, the kingdoms, the wars, the entire cosmological architecture of good and evil that the peoples of Nusantara had constructed to explain their world. Something that simply *was*, the way a volcano simply is -- not good, not evil, but elemental, a force of nature that existed before morality was invented and would endure long after morality was forgotten.

"We need to go back," Bagus said. His voice was barely audible. "Kian. We need to go back."

"There is no back," Kian Santang said. "Only through."

He held the torch higher and walked on, into the dark.

## III. The Betrayer's Path

Si Burik was wrong at every turning.

Kian Santang noticed it first. The creature, who had claimed intimate knowledge of the tunnel system, who had boasted of his previous passage through these depths, was hesitating at every junction. At the first fork, he paused for a long moment before choosing the left-hand passage, then stopped after twenty paces, sniffed the air, and doubled back. At the second junction, he chose the right, then stopped again, muttering to himself in a rapid, agitated stream of mixed Sundanese and the private, degraded language that he spoke when he believed himself unobserved -- a language of clicks and sibilants and wet, guttural sounds that bore no resemblance to any human tongue.

"*Left, left, it was left before... but the webs have changed... she has grown, yes, grown so much... the tunnels are different now... more tunnels... more webs... more, more...*"

"Si Burik," Kian Santang said. "Are you lost?"

The creature spun. His face was a mask of offended innocence -- brow furrowed, mouth downturned, hands spread in a gesture of wounded sincerity. "Lost? No! Not lost! Never lost! Si Burik knows every stone, every crack, every drip! Just... checking. Making sure. The tunnel has changed since Si Burik last came. Things grow. Things move. But Si Burik remembers. Si Burik always remembers. *Gollum, gollum.*"

Bagus touched Kian Santang's arm. When Kian Santang turned, the Tuyul's eyes communicated a message that did not need words: *He is lying. He has been lying since the stairs. We are being led.*

Kian Santang nodded, a minute movement that Si Burik, facing them, might or might not have seen. He studied the creature's face. In the wavering torchlight, it was a study in ruin -- the face of a being who had once been a Tuyul like them, a small, broad, brown-skinned person of the earth-villages, but who had been remade by centuries of the keris's corruption into something barely recognizable as the same species. The skin was grey and stretched tight over a skull that seemed too large, the eyes were too wide and too pale, the mouth was a lipless gash that revealed gums the color of raw liver. Only the hands retained something of the Tuyul's original shape -- broad, strong, earth-worker's hands -- but the fingers had elongated into spidery digits that moved with an independent, almost autonomous restlessness, as though each finger were a separate creature with its own agenda.

Those fingers were twitching now. Rhythmically. Compulsively. And their twitching was directed -- oriented, like compass needles, toward the bundled shape of the Keris Murka on Kian Santang's back.

"Si Burik," Kian Santang said. His voice was steady, but the effort of keeping it so was immense. "Look at me. Not at the keris. Look at my face."

The creature's eyes snapped to his. For a moment, the mask slipped -- the servile grin, the cringing deference, all of it fell away, and what Kian Santang saw beneath it was a face divided. Literally divided, as though two expressions were competing for the same muscles: on the left, a look of desperate, pleading need, the look of an addict who has been brought within arm's reach of his drug; on the right, a look of something almost human -- shame, perhaps, or the ghost of shame, the memory of what shame felt like before the addiction consumed the capacity to feel it.

"*Jimat-ku,*" Si Burik whispered, and the word came out involuntarily, a vocal tic, a spasm of the tongue. He caught himself immediately, shaking his head as though dislodging a wasp. "No... no... Si Burik said... Si Burik didn't mean... just... the memory. The memory of when it was Si Burik's. When Si Burik was whole. When Si Burik could remember... things. Names. Faces. The village. The water. The sun on the *sawah*..." His voice trailed off. His eyes lost focus, gazing at something invisible, something interior, and for a moment his ruined face softened with a grief so pure and so private that Kian Santang felt it like a blade between his ribs.

Then the moment passed. The mask returned. The grin, the cringe, the servile posture.

"This way," Si Burik said, and scuttled ahead into the dark.

They followed. Because there was nothing else to do.

The tunnel narrowed again. The webs grew so thick that they had to tear their way through them, ripping at the silk with their hands while the strands stuck to their skin and their clothes and their hair, coating them in a layer of adhesive filament that made them look, in the green phosphorescent glow, like creatures wrapped in shrouds. The air was nearly unbreathable now -- saturated with the chemical tang of venom and the musty, animal stench of a large predator's lair. The floor was no longer littered with bones; it was *paved* with them -- a continuous layer of skeletal remains compressed into a brittle, shifting surface that cracked and crunched beneath every step, and among the bones were other things: scraps of armor, fragments of weapons, the rusted remains of iron tools. Whatever lived here had been eating for a very long time.

Si Burik stopped.

He was twenty paces ahead, just at the edge of the torchlight, and his body language had changed. The nervous, scuttling energy was gone, replaced by a stillness that was almost catlike -- the poised, gathered alertness of a predator about to spring. His head was tilted to one side. He was listening.

"Master," he whispered. His voice was different now. The servile whine had been replaced by something harder, something that might have been urgency or might have been excitement. "Master must stay here. Si Burik will go ahead. Si Burik will... check. Make sure the way is clear."

"No," Kian Santang said. "We stay together."

"But master --"

"We stay *together*, Si Burik."

The creature stared at him. In the green-tinged darkness, his eyes were unreadable -- two pale discs reflecting the dying torchlight, betraying nothing of the calculation that was occurring behind them. Kian Santang met his gaze and held it, and in that moment, the silence between them was the silence of two chess players who both know the endgame has begun.

"Together, then," Si Burik said. But he said it to himself, not to Kian Santang, and his voice carried a sadness that was either the most convincing performance of his life or the last genuine emotion his corrupted soul was capable of producing.

They continued.

The tunnel opened into a wider passage. Si Burik moved faster now, pulling ahead of them despite Kian Santang's order, his pale body flickering in and out of the torchlight like a fish seen through murky water. Twenty paces. Thirty. Forty. The darkness ahead of them was absolute, and Si Burik was dissolving into it, his outline blurring, his pale skin merging with the grey-green glow of the fungus until only his eyes remained visible -- two bright points receding into the dark.

"Si Burik!" Kian Santang called out. "Come back! Stay in the light!"

The eyes stopped. Hung in the darkness like distant stars. Then, slowly, they began to move -- not forward, not backward, but sideways, sliding horizontally across the field of black with a smooth, deliberate motion that suggested Si Burik had found a side passage and was moving into it.

"Si Burik!"

A sound came from the darkness where the eyes had been. A sound that might have been a laugh or might have been a sob -- a choked, wet exhalation that contained both grief and release in equal measure, the sound of a creature shedding the last pretense of decency and surrendering to the hunger that had governed his existence since long before Kian Santang was born.

"*Aksama, master...*" the voice said, already faint, already distant, already swallowed by the tunnel's endless dark. "*Si Burik tried... tried to be good... but she is hungry... and the jimat calls... the jimat always calls...*"

Then silence.

Kian Santang and Bagus stood alone in the torchlight, surrounded by darkness and bones and webs and the chemical stink of something vast and terrible, and the silence pressed against them like a physical thing, like the weight of the mountain itself settling onto their shoulders.

"He's gone," Bagus said.

"Yes."

"He led us here on purpose."

"Yes."

Bagus was quiet for a moment. Then he squared his shoulders, gripped his gardening knife, and said, with the simple, devastating clarity of a friend who has accepted the worst and decided to face it anyway:

"Then we go forward, *Priya.* We go forward, and we trust the *Karuhun* to guide our feet, and if something comes for us in the dark, we fight it. Together."

Kian Santang looked at his friend. The torch was guttering now, its fuel nearly spent, the flame shrinking to a fragile, blue-orange tongue that would die within minutes. In its dying light, Bagus's face was the most human thing in the universe -- broad and afraid and absolutely resolved, the face of a gardener's son who had walked into the mouth of hell because his friend was there and leaving had never occurred to him as an option.

"Together," Kian Santang said.

They walked forward into the tunnel that Si Burik had opened and then abandoned, two small figures in a circle of fading light, moving through a darkness that breathed and clicked and waited.

## IV. The Lair

The tunnel opened, and the world became impossible.

Kian Santang felt it before he saw it -- the sudden absence of walls on either side, the change in air pressure that made his ears pop, the shift in acoustics from the intimate, close echoes of the tunnel to a vast, cathedral resonance that swallowed the sound of their breathing and returned it as a distant, ghostly whisper. The torchlight, which had been bouncing off tunnel walls three meters away, now reached into nothingness on all sides, finding nothing to illuminate, nothing to reflect from, its small flame suddenly pathetic and exposed in a space too large for it to comprehend.

They had entered the chamber.

Kian Santang held the torch above his head. The flame stretched, caught a draft from somewhere far away, and brightened briefly -- a flare that lasted two heartbeats, three -- and in that flare, the chamber revealed itself.

It was a natural cavern, formed where multiple volcanic tubes converged and the stone between them had collapsed or been excavated by something that preferred open spaces to tunnels. The floor was roughly oval, perhaps a hundred paces across at its widest, and it sloped downward from the entrance where they stood to a central depression that might once have been a lava pool but was now dry, its surface cracked and scaled like old skin. The ceiling was lost in darkness -- the torch's light could not reach it, could not even suggest it, and the absence of a visible upper boundary gave the chamber a vertiginous, bottomless quality, as though the space above them extended not just upward but *outward*, into some dimension that had nothing to do with the mountain's physical geometry.

The walls, where the torchlight touched them, were covered in webs.

Not the strands they had encountered in the tunnel -- those had been cables, individual lines stretched across a passageway. These were *structures*. The webs here formed a continuous fabric that covered every surface of the cavern walls in a thick, quilted layer of grey-white silk, draped and folded and layered like the funeral cloth of a giant, each fold sagging with the weight of trapped moisture and trapped things -- the dark, wrapped shapes of prey items, cocooned and suspended in the silk at various heights, their outlines suggesting bodies of different sizes, from the small, tight bundles of bats and rats to the longer, heavier shapes that could only be humanoid. There were dozens of them. Scores. A macabre gallery of the consumed, hanging in the webs like ornaments on a monstrous tree.

The web architecture told a story to those who knew how to read it. The oldest layers -- compressed against the stone, yellowed and brittle as parchment -- had been spun centuries ago, perhaps millennia, when the creature that dwelt here was young and its appetite was small. These ancient sheets were fine-spun, almost delicate, the work of a predator no larger than a dog. But the layers grew thicker and coarser as they built outward from the wall, each generation of silk bolder, more confident, laid down by an organism that was growing, decade by decade, century by century, expanding in mass and ambition with each successful kill, each absorbed life contributing not just sustenance but *sakti* -- the dark spiritual energy of its victims feeding the creature's impossible growth the way fire feeds on dry wood. The outermost layer, the one that caught the torchlight and reflected it back in wet, prismatic gleams, was spun from cables as thick as ship-rope, each strand embedded with fragments of the things it had trapped: splinters of bone, flakes of rusted iron, a shard of what might have been a gemstone, the iridescent scale of a serpent. The web was not merely architecture. It was a chronicle. A record of consumption written in silk and death and the slow, patient accumulation of centuries of uninterrupted feeding.

Hanging among the wrapped bodies, at irregular intervals, were objects that the silk had preserved with the incidental fidelity of amber preserving insects. Kian Santang's eye caught them in the stuttering torchlight: a helm of the old Kurcaci design, its iron pitted but its form unmistakable, still bearing the crest of the mountain-smiths who had forged it in some age when the Kurcaci still walked these tunnels as builders rather than prey. A shield, Buta-made, its black lacquer cracked but its boss still bright with the crude iron workmanship of the Shadow King's armories. A *keris* -- not the Keris Murka but an ordinary fighting blade, its sheath rotted, its *pamor* dull, the weapon of some forgotten warrior who had entered this tunnel with courage and a blade and left with neither. And further up, near where the wall met the invisible ceiling, shapes that were harder to identify -- bundles of silk so old and so tightly compressed that their contents had ceased to be recognizable as bodies and had become geological formations, fossilized in protein, mineralized in secretion, cenotaphs for the nameless dead who had been devoured before history thought to record their passing.

The floor was bones.

Not a carpet. A *geology* of bones. The remains were so numerous and so thoroughly mixed that they had ceased to be individual skeletons and become a landscape -- a terrain of calcified death that crunched and shifted underfoot like gravel, the upper layer composed of fresh, white bones still bearing fragments of sinew and dried flesh, the lower layers compacted into a dense, mineralized substrate that was halfway to becoming limestone itself. Walking on it was an obscenity. Every step was a desecration. Every footfall crushed someone's rib or someone's finger or someone's skull, and the sound -- that brittle, intimate snapping -- was the worst sound Kian Santang had ever heard, worse than the keris's whispers, worse than the Naga's roar, because it was the sound of individual lives reduced to material, of beings who had been mothers and fathers and children rendered into the substrate of a monster's larder.

In the depression at the cavern's center, the bones were different. They were not scattered but arranged -- or rather, they had accumulated in a pattern that suggested the habitual resting posture of something vast, something that curled its segmented body into the bowl of stone the way a cat curls into a basket, and the bones had settled into the grooves worn by centuries of this repose, filling the channels carved by armored legs and the shallow trench gouged by a dragging tail. The depression was a bed. A nest. And it was warm -- Kian Santang felt the heat rising from it in slow, invisible waves, the residual thermal signature of a body so massive that the stone itself retained its warmth for hours after the creature departed, the way a hearthstone holds heat long after the fire has died.

The creature was not in its nest.

This was worse than finding it there. To find a predator in its resting place was to know where it was, to locate the danger on the map of the mind, to assign it a position and therefore a distance, a vector, a calculable threat. But the nest was empty and the predator was somewhere else -- somewhere in the vast, unbounded darkness that surrounded them on all sides and above, somewhere in the cathedral of webs and bones and ancient, patient hunger -- and the *not knowing* was a kind of suffocation, a compression of the chest that had nothing to do with the air and everything to do with the mind's absolute refusal to exist in a space where the apex predator could be *anywhere*.

"*Gusti...*" Bagus breathed. The word was not an oath. It was a prayer -- the most sincere prayer the Tuyul had ever uttered, a single syllable thrown at the darkness like a stone thrown at a wave, inadequate and necessary and utterly human.

"The torch," Kian Santang said. "It's dying."

The flame had shrunk to a small, blue-edged wisp that clung to the last inch of pine resin with the desperate tenacity of a living thing that knows it is about to cease. The light it cast was barely sufficient to illuminate their own bodies -- beyond arm's reach, the darkness was absolute, a solid, dimensional blackness that pressed against the dying light like a predator pressing against the bars of a cage, waiting for the bars to give.

"We cross," Kian Santang said. "The exit must be on the far side. We cross the chamber and find it."

"In the dark?" Bagus asked.

"In whatever light we have."

They stepped forward. The bones cracked beneath their feet.

Kian Santang counted his steps. It was the only way to maintain direction in the featureless dark -- count the steps, keep them even, keep the wall at their right, use the slope of the floor as a compass. The central depression was lower than the edges; if they felt the floor dropping away, they had drifted too far inward. If they felt it rising, they were approaching a wall. Simple. Mechanical. A system that required no courage, no heroism, only the willingness to keep putting one foot in front of the other while every cell in the body screamed to stop.

Twenty steps. Thirty. The torch flame shrank to a blue point, a glow, a memory of light.

At forty steps, the torch flickered. Not the slow dimming of fuel exhausted but a sudden, lateral stutter -- the flame bending hard to the left as though shoved by an invisible hand, then snapping back, then bending again. Something was moving the air. Something was displacing the atmosphere of the chamber with a rhythmic, tidal regularity that could not be attributed to drafts or convection, because the movement was too slow, too deliberate, too measured. It was the breathing of something enormous, carried to them across the bone-field on currents of displaced air that smelled of musk and venom and the digestive secretions of a metabolism that had been processing flesh for longer than most civilizations had existed.

Kian Santang stopped. He held the torch perfectly still and watched the flame. Bend left. Straighten. Bend left. Straighten. The rhythm was steady -- approximately three seconds between each deflection. He counted. One-two-three-bend. One-two-three-straighten. The slow, wet breathing of a creature at rest -- deep and even, the breathing of a predator that does not dream because it does not need to. The creature was breathing somewhere in the darkness. Not hunting. Not yet. Resting. Waiting. Perhaps watching. Perhaps not needing to watch, because nothing that entered this chamber had ever left it, and watching was a courtesy that the Kalajengking had long since ceased to extend to its meals.

"Kian," Bagus whispered. "Do you hear that?"

He did.

From somewhere in the darkness above them -- far above, at a height that the torch had never reached, in the invisible ceiling of the cavern -- a sound. A chittering sound. A rhythmic, mechanical clicking, like the sound of a clock made of bone, its mechanism grinding through cycles of some alien, arthropod intention. Click-click-click. Pause. Click-click-click-click. Pause. The rhythm was irregular, unpredictable, and it shifted position as they listened -- moving along the ceiling with a lateral speed that suggested something large, something many-legged, something that clung to the stone above them and tracked their progress through the bone-field with the patient attention of a creature that has learned, through centuries of successful predation, that prey which enters the chamber does not leave.

*Skitter-skitter-click.*

The sound was closer. Not above them now -- to the side. To the right. Moving through the webs along the wall with a rustling, silken whisper that was somehow worse than the clicking, because it was the sound of something massive moving through a medium designed to be silent, something so large that even its most careful movements disturbed the webbing and sent tremors racing through the silk like ripples across the surface of a poisoned lake.

Then it stopped. The clicking ceased. The rustling ceased. The torch flame straightened and burned still, and the silence that followed was worse than any sound, because sound can be located, can be measured, can be catalogued and assigned a distance and a bearing -- but silence is everywhere and nowhere, and in this silence, the darkness itself seemed to thicken, to congeal, to compress around the two small figures in the bone-field until the air they breathed felt solid, felt occupied, felt as though they were drawing their breath through the body of something that had wrapped itself around them without touching, that was studying them from every angle simultaneously, that was tasting the shape of their terror the way a tongue tastes salt.

A single strand of web detached from the ceiling and drifted down through the torchlight. It fell slowly, almost lazily, turning as it descended, catching the flame's last light in a glistening spiral. It landed on Kian Santang's shoulder. He did not brush it away. He could not move. The strand was warm. Body-warm. It had been freshly cut -- severed from the web above them by something sharp, something that had reached out from its position in the dark and snipped the thread with surgical precision, with the casual, contemptuous artistry of a weaver trimming excess fiber. The creature had done this. The creature was directly above them, close enough to touch the web that spanned the ceiling, and it had cut a strand and let it fall and this was a *message* -- not a threat, not a warning, but an announcement: *I am here. I have been here. I was here before you entered and I will be here after you leave, which you will not, because nothing leaves.*

The torch went out.

The darkness that followed was not the absence of light. It was the *presence* of darkness -- a vast, breathing, sentient thing that descended upon them like a hand closing into a fist, obliterating not just their vision but their sense of space, their sense of direction, their sense of self. In the dark, the boundaries of Kian Santang's body dissolved. He could feel his hands and his feet and the keris on his back, but he could not locate them in relation to anything else -- could not say which direction was up, which was down, where the floor ended and the air began. He was a consciousness suspended in void, and the void was not empty.

He could smell it now.

The acrid musk was overwhelming -- a thick, animal reek that coated the inside of his nostrils and the back of his throat, that pushed past the olfactory system entirely and triggered the deep, autonomous systems of the body, the fight-or-flight machinery that evolution had built into the Tuyul genome over millions of years of being preyed upon by things with claws and mandibles and stingers. His heart rate spiked. His breathing accelerated. His pupils dilated uselessly in the absolute dark, straining for photons that did not exist.

"Bagus," he whispered. "Take my hand."

A warm hand found his in the dark. Gripped. Held.

"I'm here," Bagus said.

Something brushed Kian Santang's hair. Not the web -- he knew the web's texture by now, its cold adhesion, its fibrous cling. This was different. This was smooth and hard and jointed, and it traced a line from the crown of his head to the nape of his neck with the slow, exploratory precision of a finger running down a page of text, reading him, deciphering the topography of his skull the way a blind artisan reads the surface of an unfinished sculpture. The touch lasted three seconds. Then it withdrew -- lifted upward, retracted into the dark above, and the air where it had been was warm with the body heat of whatever limb had extended it.

Kian Santang did not scream. He could not. The scream was trapped somewhere between his diaphragm and his larynx, frozen by a terror so absolute that it had bypassed the nervous system entirely and gone straight to the bones, to the marrow, to the cellular memory of every ancestor who had ever been touched in the dark by something that intended to eat them. He stood perfectly still. Bagus's hand in his was a vice, a clamp, the only solid thing in a universe that had dissolved into heat and musk and the sound of his own blood roaring in his ears.

And then, from directly above them -- so close that Kian Santang felt the displacement of air on his upturned face, felt the heat of a body larger than his own radiating downward like the warmth from a low ceiling -- a sound.

Not clicking. Not chittering.

Breathing.

Slow, wet, rhythmic breathing. The respiration of a creature so large that each inhalation created a perceptible downdraft and each exhalation sent a wash of warm, venom-tainted air rolling across their faces. It was breathing them in. Smelling them. Tasting the chemistry of their fear on the molecules of air that their skin shed like dead leaves, learning the flavor of their terror the way a vintner learns the flavor of a wine -- with patient, professional expertise.

Kian Santang's hand moved to the short blade at his hip. Drew it. The steel made no sound coming free of the sheath, but the creature above them responded anyway -- the breathing paused, held, and the silence that followed was the loudest sound Kian Santang had ever experienced, a silence so dense with anticipated violence that it seemed to vibrate, to hum, to pulse with the frequency of something about to happen.

"Run," Kian Santang whispered.

They ran.

Bones exploded beneath their feet. The darkness around them erupted into sound -- a cascading, deafening cacophony of chitinous movement and silk tearing and something *enormous* launching itself from the ceiling, dropping through the air with a speed that the dark concealed but the body understood. Kian Santang felt the impact through the floor before he heard it -- a concussive *BOOM* that shook the bone-carpet and sent fragments flying, the sound of something the size of a water buffalo hitting stone from a height of twenty meters.

"SCREEEECH!"

The shriek tore the air apart. It was not a sound that any creature Kian Santang had encountered could make -- not the Buta's war-cry, not the Leak's ethereal scream, not the Naga Basukih's volcanic roar. This was something else entirely. This was the vocalization of an arthropod, produced by stridulation -- the scraping of specialized body parts against each other -- and amplified by a resonating chamber of chitin and hollow bone that turned the sound into a physical weapon, a blast of acoustic energy that hit Kian Santang's eardrums like a fist and sent him staggering sideways, his hands clapped over his ears, his blade falling to the bone-strewn floor.

In the aftermath of the shriek, as the echoes ricocheted through the chamber and the bones settled and the silence rushed back in to fill the void, the phosphorescent fungus on the walls flared.

It was a defensive response -- the organism reacting to the vibration of the shriek by releasing a burst of bioluminescent chemicals, a brief, convulsive brightening that turned the cavern walls from dim green to vivid, electric emerald for three seconds, four, five.

And in that light, Kian Santang saw the Kalajengking.

It was a scorpion.

The word is grotesquely insufficient. It was a scorpion the way a volcano is a hill -- the fundamental form was there, recognizable, but inflated to a scale that rendered the familiar monstrous. It stood in the center of the bone-field, its eight legs spread wide, each one as thick as a man's thigh and jointed in three places, ending in hooked claws that dug into the substrate of bones and stone with the casual destructive power of pickaxes driven by pistons. Its body was the size of a water buffalo -- no, larger, Kian Santang realized with a sick, vertiginous reassessment, larger than a buffalo, as long as a *perahu* -- a fishing boat -- from the tip of its pedipalps to the base of its tail. Its carapace was black obsidian, polished by centuries of movement through its silk-lined tunnels, and it shone with a wet, oily iridescence that caught the fungal light and scattered it in rainbow patterns across the bone-field like the light through a cathedral's stained glass, if the cathedral were a charnel house and the stained glass were made of nightmare.

Its pincers were open. They were massive -- each one the size of a man's torso, serrated along the inner edge with ridges of chitin that had been honed by use into cutting surfaces sharp enough to shear through bone and iron alike. They opened and closed with a slow, hydraulic patience that was more terrifying than any rapid movement could have been, because it communicated confidence -- the absolute, unshakeable certainty of a predator that has never been challenged, that has occupied the apex of its food chain for so long that the concept of threat has become abstract.

Its eyes. Eight of them, clustered in two rows of four above the mandibles -- not the compound eyes of an insect but the simple, camera-type eyes of an arachnid, each one the size of a man's fist, set in sockets of dark chitin. They burned. Not with reflected light, not with bioluminescence, but with an internal fire -- a cold, malevolent radiance that spoke of intelligence, of awareness, of a mind that was not mammalian, not even vertebrate, but was a mind nonetheless -- a vast, alien consciousness built from different materials and operating on different principles but capable of the one thing that made intelligence truly dangerous: intention.

*Not a serpent.* The thought cut through Kian Santang's terror with the cold precision of a blade. Si Burik had called her Nyi Blorong -- the Serpent Queen -- and Kian Santang had expected coils, scales, the sinuous horror of a great snake. But this was no serpent. This was something older, something armored and segmented and built for a different kind of killing. Si Burik had lied, or remembered wrong, or -- and this was worse -- had known exactly what waited here and had called it by the wrong name so that they would come.

This was the spawn of Nyi Blorong. Not the Serpent Queen herself -- even the Queen of Shadows had her proxies, her children, her distributed malice -- but something born from the Queen's essence, hatched from an egg of dark *sakti* laid in the mountain's gut in an age when the world was younger and its horrors were more honest. The Kalajengking had been here since before the Shadow King built his Black Gate, since before the Buta Raksasa carved their iron road through the caldera. It was the guardian of the high pass not because it had been appointed but because it had *chosen* -- chosen this tunnel, this chamber, this convergence of darkness and depth, as its hunting ground, and nothing that entered had the power or the right to dispute that choice.

And its tail.

The tail rose from behind the body in a segmented arc that curved upward and forward over the carapace, five segments of armored chitin, each one the diameter of a tree trunk, tapering to a final segment that held the telson -- the stinger. The stinger was the length of a man's forearm and the shape of a spearhead, translucent at its tip, and through that translucency, a yellowish fluid was visible -- venom, a quantity of venom sufficient to fill a drinking gourd, produced by glands that had been synthesizing their toxin for centuries, refining it through generations of kills into a compound that was less a poison than a molecular weapon, a substance designed at the chemical level to unmake the nervous system of any warm-blooded creature it entered.

The venom dripped. Each drop fell from the stinger's tip and hit the bone-strewn floor with a faint, hissing sizzle -- the sound of organic matter dissolving on contact, the sound of the venom beginning its work before it even reached a living body.

The fungal light faded. The darkness returned. But Kian Santang had seen enough. The image was branded into his retinas, into his mind, into the deepest, most primitive layer of his consciousness -- the image of a creature that should not exist, that violated every rational principle of biology and physics and mercy, and yet was here, was real, was breathing, was *looking at them* with eight burning eyes that saw in the dark the way sunlight sees through glass.

The Kalajengking charged.

## V. The Battle

Kian Santang threw himself sideways.

The movement was not thought. It was the autonomic lunge of a body that has been startled beyond the capacity of the conscious mind to process and has fallen back on the deep, survival-coded reflexes that evolution built into the Tuyul genome for exactly this moment -- the moment when something with claws and mandibles explodes from the dark with lethal intent. He hit the bone-strewn floor in a rolling dive, the bones shattering beneath his weight, and the Kalajengking's stinger struck the stone where he had been standing with a force that shattered the basalt and sent fragments spraying outward in a fan of razor-sharp obsidian.

"BAGUS! MOVE!" Kian Santang screamed, scrambling to his feet. His hands found the short blade on the floor -- groped for it in the dark, fingers closing on the cold steel with a desperate strength -- and he turned to face the creature.

He could not see it. But he could *feel* it -- the displacement of air as it moved, the vibration of the floor beneath its eight clawed legs, the percussive click-click-click of its pedipalps as it reset itself for another strike. It was fast. Impossibly fast for something so large. The biology was wrong -- an organism of this mass should have been slow, ponderous, limited by the square-cube law and the constraints of an exoskeleton that could not support its own weight without external assistance. But the Kalajengking was not bound by the laws that governed the natural world. It was a creature of *sakti*, born from Nyi Blorong's dark power, and the physics of its movement were the physics of nightmare, unconstrained by the rules that kept the mundane world orderly and comprehensible.

Kian Santang slashed at the darkness before him. The blade connected with something hard -- the chitin of a leg, perhaps, or the edge of a pincer -- and the impact sent a shock up his arm that numbed his fingers. Sparks flew. The short blade, good Sundanese steel, forged for clearing brush and butchering game, bounced off the Kalajengking's carapace without leaving a mark, the way a twig bounces off a stone wall.

The creature herded them. Kian Santang understood it with the sick clarity of a prey animal recognizing the pattern of its own death. The Kalajengking was not attacking randomly. It was driving them -- cutting off escape routes with sweeps of its pincers, blocking the tunnel mouths with its massive body, channeling their panicked movements toward the center of the chamber, toward the depression in the floor where the bones were deepest and the footing was worst. It was playing with them. The intelligence behind those eight burning eyes was enjoying this, savoring the familiar choreography of predator and prey, the ancient dance that always ended the same way.

It feinted. Kian Santang heard the rush of a pincer sweeping toward his left side and threw himself right -- exactly right, exactly where the creature wanted him, because the second pincer was already there, closing behind him like a gate, and he realized with a cold, sick certainty that the Kalajengking had predicted his dodge, had read the involuntary flinch of his muscles and calculated the trajectory of his evasion before his body had completed it. The creature was not merely fast. It was *strategic*. It understood the geometry of fear, the predictable vectors of panic, the way a terrified body will always lunge away from the threat it can see and toward the threat it cannot. It had hunted thinking prey for centuries, and it had learned.

The pincer clipped his hip. The impact spun him sideways, and he stumbled three steps before his feet found purchase on the shifting bone-carpet. The blow had not cut -- the serrated edge had passed over rather than through -- but it had measured him, tested his weight and his balance and his response time, and the creature absorbed this data with the clinical patience of a predator building a behavioral model of its prey. The next strike would be calibrated.

Kian Santang felt the creature pause. Not retreat -- pause. It settled back on its eight legs, its massive body lowering toward the bone-carpet, and the clicking of its pedipalps slowed from the rapid staccato of attack-mode to a measured, almost contemplative rhythm. Click... click... click. It was *watching* them. Observing. Allowing them the brief, illusory freedom of movement that a cat allows a mouse between strikes -- not from mercy but from the sophisticated cruelty of a predator that has learned that fear improves the flavor of the hunt.

"SCREEEECH!"

The creature struck again -- not with its stinger but with its pincers, a lateral sweep that Kian Santang felt coming as a rush of displaced air and dodged by millimeters, the serrated inner edge of the pincer passing so close to his face that he felt the wind of it on his cheek and heard the *snick* of chitin closing on empty air. He stumbled backward over the bone-carpet, fell, rolled, and came up facing the direction of the next attack, his blade raised in a guard position that was textbook silat and utterly, pathetically inadequate against an opponent that could crush him the way a child crushes a beetle.

In the darkness to his left, Bagus screamed.

Not a scream of pain -- a scream of action. The sound of a Tuyul who has weighed his options and found them wanting and has decided, in the absence of any good choice, to choose the brave one. Kian Santang heard the slap of bare feet on bone, heard the thick, meaty *thud* of Bagus's gardening knife hitting chitin, heard the gasping, sobbing war-cry of a friend who was not a warrior but had decided, in this moment, in this darkness, to become one.

"LEAVE HIM ALONE! LEAVE HIM! *AING* WON'T LET YOU! *KARUHUN,* I WON'T!"

The gardening knife sparked against the Kalajengking's foreleg. Bagus hit it again, and again, raining blows on the creature's armored limb with the desperate, untrained fury of a man attacking a mountain with a pickaxe. Each blow sparked. None penetrated. The chitin was too thick, too dense, a natural armor that had evolved -- or been *designed* -- to resist exactly this kind of assault.

The Kalajengking turned.

Its attention shifted from Kian Santang to the smaller, noisier target. Its eight eyes locked onto Bagus's position -- Kian Santang could see them now, eight points of cold fire swiveling in the dark, converging on the spot where the gardening knife was striking sparks from its leg -- and the creature moved with a sudden, convulsive speed that was less motion than teleportation, its massive body pivoting on its legs, its pincers snapping open.

The pincer caught Bagus by the shoulder.

The sound Bagus made was not a scream. It was a *gasp* -- a short, sharp, involuntary exhalation as the serrated edges of the pincer dug through his shirt and into the flesh beneath, as the hydraulic force of the claw closed with a pressure that could crush stone, and then he was rising -- lifted from the bone-strewn floor like a puppet yanked upward on its strings, his legs kicking in the air, his hands gripping the pincer's edge, his gardening knife clattering to the ground far below.

"Kian!" Bagus screamed, and now it was pain, now it was terror, now it was the cry of a friend who has been seized by something that intends to kill him and knows, with a certainty that transcends fear, that he cannot save himself.

The pincer tightened. Bagus screamed again. Blood, black in the darkness, ran down the chitin and dripped onto the bone-carpet below with soft, rhythmic patters.

"LET HIM GO!"

Kian Santang's roar tore from a place in his chest that he had not known existed -- a place below thought, below fear, below the keris's corruption and the exhaustion and the despair, a place where the only thing that mattered was the sound of his friend in pain and the knowledge that he had the power to stop it.

The power. The keris.

It pulsed against his back. Through the batik wrapping, through his shirt, through his skin, the Keris Murka radiated a heat that was not thermal but *intentional* -- the focused, directed warmth of a consciousness reaching out, pressing against the barriers that separated it from its bearer, offering itself with an urgency that bordered on desperation. The weapon *wanted* to be drawn. It was not whispering now -- it was *singing*, a low, subsonic vibration that resonated in Kian Santang's bones and teeth and sternum, a song without words that nevertheless communicated a single, overwhelming proposition: *Take me. Use me. I am the answer to this. I am the only answer.*

Kian Santang's hand moved toward his back. Stopped. His fingers trembled in the air between his body and the bundle -- trembled with the effort of holding still, of resisting the magnetic pull of the blade, because he knew. He *knew* what drawing the keris would mean. Eyang Guru had told him. Every time you draw it, it takes more. Every time the dark *sakti* flows through you, it carves its channels deeper, and the channels do not close. The keris was not a weapon. It was a contract. And each time Kian Santang signed it in blood and dark fire, the terms shifted further in the weapon's favor.

Bagus screamed again. The sound was weaker now, thinner, threaded with the breathless, fading quality of a body running out of air. The pincer was compressing his chest. His ribs were bending.

Kian Santang's hand closed on the wrapping.

*I know the cost,* he thought, and the thought was addressed not to the keris, not to the darkness, not to the Shadow King whose fire lived in the blade, but to himself -- to the small, honest part of himself that was still Kian Santang of Padalarang, the boy who jumped into cold water, the boy who ate stolen fruit, the boy who did not yet know that the world demanded payment for every act of salvation. *I know the cost, and I will pay it. Because the alternative is listening to him die.*

He drew the Keris Murka.

The blade came free of its batik wrapping with a sound like a breath being drawn -- a soft, silken *shhhhh* that was somehow more terrible than the Kalajengking's shriek, because it was the sound of something waking up. The keris's *pamor* pattern blazed to life -- the dark, folded lines of the blade igniting with a light that was not gold and not silver but a deep, poisonous violet, the color of bruised twilight, the color of the sky over Krakatau when the Shadow King's fires burned highest. The light was cold. It was the opposite of Eyang Guru's sacred fire -- not a warmth that comforted but a radiance that threatened, not a light that illuminated but a darkness that had learned to glow.

The Keris Murka filled the chamber.

Its light was not bright in the way of fire or sun. It was *present* in a way that brightness could not explain -- a pervasive, saturating emanation that stained the air itself violet, that turned the white bones to amethyst and the grey webs to bruised lavender and the phosphorescent fungus to a sick, gangrenous purple. It was the light of the Shadow King's power, unleashed by a bearer who had drawn the weapon not in service to its master's will but in defiance of it, and the contradiction -- the keris's dark *sakti* wielded for the purpose of love -- produced an illumination that was neither holy nor profane but something else, something raw and paradoxical and desperately, dangerously human.

The Kalajengking recoiled.

It dropped Bagus. The Tuyul fell four meters to the bone-strewn floor and hit with a sound that drove the air from his lungs and a cry that cut through the violet light like a blade through silk. But he was alive -- alive and conscious, rolling onto his side, clutching his bleeding shoulder, his eyes wide and white-rimmed in the keris's unholy glow.

The scorpion *screamed*. Not the hunting shriek, not the territorial challenge -- this was a different sound, a sound of pain, of outrage, of a creature that had ruled its domain unchallenged for centuries and was now confronted with something it recognized as a threat. The violet light of the Keris Murka burned it -- not its body, not its carapace, but its *eyes*. The eight burning orbs, designed to see in absolute darkness, to process the faintest photons of bioluminescence and translate them into targeting data, were overwhelmed by the keris's emanation. They flickered. Dimmed. The creature's head swayed from side to side, its mandibles clacking in agitation, its massive body retreating -- backing away from the violet light with the stumbling, disoriented movements of a predator that has been blinded.

But the retreat lasted only seconds. The Kalajengking's eyes adjusted -- the burning orbs recalibrating, their membranes thickening to filter the keris's radiance the way a pupil constricts against sudden sun. The creature stopped retreating. Its body lowered. Its pincers opened wide, and between them, the mandibles worked in a rapid, clicking sequence that might have been rage or might have been *recognition* -- the dawning comprehension that the small glowing thing before it was not merely prey with a bright weapon but something connected to the deeper powers of the mountain, to the dark *sakti* that the Kalajengking's own mother, Nyi Blorong, had seeded into this earth millennia ago. The creature knew the keris. Not the way a mind knows an object, but the way a body knows a scent -- cellularly, ancestrally, in the molecular memory of its chitin and its venom glands and the ancient, coiled intelligence behind its eight recalibrating eyes.

It knew the keris. And it was no longer afraid.

"Bagus!" Kian Santang shouted. "Can you move?"

"Yes... *aing*... yes." The Tuyul was on his feet, swaying, one hand pressed to his shoulder where the blood soaked through his fingers in dark, steady streams. His face was grey with pain and shock, but his eyes -- his brown, honest, stubborn eyes -- were clear. He was looking at the keris in Kian Santang's hand, and in his expression there was no fear of the weapon, no revulsion at its dark light, only gratitude -- the simple, overwhelming gratitude of a friend who has been saved.

"Run, Bagus. Go. Find the exit. Find the tunnel on the far wall."

"I won't leave you!"

"You *have* to! *Aing* commands you! Go -- find Eyang Guru -- find *anyone* --" Kian Santang's voice cracked. The keris was vibrating in his hand, singing with a frequency that bypassed his ears and entered his bones, and the sensation was not unpleasant -- that was the worst part, the keris *felt good*, felt *right*, felt like a key sliding into a lock, and the voice that had been whispering for weeks now spoke at full volume, a voice of thunder and dark honey:

*Yes. Hold me. Use me. This is what I was made for. This is what YOU were made for. Strike. Kill. RULE.*

"GO, BAGUS! NOW!"

Kian Santang turned from his friend and charged the Kalajengking.

The creature was recovering. Its eyes, initially overwhelmed by the keris's light, were adjusting -- the burning orbs dilating, constricting, recalibrating for the new illumination. It reared back on its hind legs, its pincers spread wide, its tail arching overhead with the stinger poised and dripping, and in the violet light of the keris, its carapace blazed with iridescent patterns that might have been beautiful if beauty were not the last thing on any sane creature's mind in this moment.

Kian Santang ducked beneath the pincers. He was small -- Tuyul-small, barely a meter tall -- and smallness, for once, was an advantage. The creature's attacks were calibrated for larger prey, its pincers sweeping at a height designed to catch Buta Raksasa and men, and Kian Santang slid beneath them on his knees, the bone-carpet tearing at his legs, the keris trailing violet light in an arc that painted the cavern in dark fire.

He drove the Keris Murka into the Kalajengking's underbelly.

The blade entered the creature's body at the juncture between two ventral plates -- a seam in the armor, a gap where the chitin thinned and the soft, vulnerable flesh beneath was exposed. The keris sank to the hilt. The sensation was profoundly wrong -- not the clean resistance of flesh against steel but a wet, yielding softness that seemed to pull the blade deeper, as though the creature's body were swallowing the weapon, and Kian Santang felt the keris's *sakti* discharge through the wound like venom through a fang, a surge of dark power that poured from the blade into the Kalajengking's body and met, within the creature's tissues, something equally dark and equally ancient.

The Kalajengking screamed.

The sound was beyond anything the cavern had produced -- a supersonic shriek that shattered webs and cracked stone and sent the phosphorescent fungus into a frenzy of panicked bioluminescence, the walls blazing green-white-green in stroboscopic pulses that turned the battle into a series of frozen images: Kian Santang beneath the creature, both hands on the keris, his face twisted with effort and revulsion. The creature's body convulsing, its legs slamming the bone-carpet in spasmodic, pile-driving blows. The webs along the walls shaking, tearing, shedding their grisly cargo of cocooned prey in a rain of silk and bones.

And the tail.

The tail came down.

It moved faster than thought -- a whipcrack of segmented chitin that crossed the five meters between the creature's arched back and Kian Santang's exposed body in a fraction of a heartbeat. The telson struck him in the chest. The stinger punched through his shirt, through his skin, through the muscle wall beneath, and the venom entered his body with a sensation that was not pain.

It was cold.

The cold was absolute. It was the opposite of the keris's dark fire, the opposite of the Naga's volcanic heat, the opposite of every warmth that Kian Santang had ever known. It was the cold of deep caves, of sealed tombs, of places where the sun has never reached and never will. It spread from the point of entry -- his chest, between the ribs, left of center -- outward through his body with a speed that outpaced his heartbeat, racing along his blood vessels and his nerves with a molecular efficiency that spoke of a toxin perfected over millennia of predation. First the chest: a frozen numbness that swallowed the pain of the puncture wound the way a lake swallows a thrown stone, replacing the sharp, honest agony of torn flesh with something worse -- the absence of sensation, the negation of feeling, as though the tissue around the wound had ceased to exist, had been subtracted from his body's accounting of itself and replaced with a void that was cold and empty and expanding. Then the arms: the cold raced outward along his brachial arteries and the nerve-bundles of his shoulders, and his arms went heavy, then leaden, then absent -- he could see them in the violet light of the keris that was slipping from his fingers but he could not *feel* them, could not locate them in the body's internal map, as though the venom were not merely numbing his limbs but erasing them, editing them from the narrative of his nervous system with the surgical precision of a scribe deleting lines from a manuscript. His fingers opened. Not because he willed them open but because the muscles that held them closed had been disconnected from the brain that commanded them. The keris fell. His legs folded. The venom reached his spine and the cold became a column, a vertical axis of ice that ran from the base of his skull to his sacrum, and the world tilted, and his knees gave way, and he was falling.

He fell.

The bone-carpet caught him. He lay on his back, staring upward at the cavern's invisible ceiling, and the violet light of the fallen keris painted the darkness above him in shades of poisoned twilight, and the Kalajengking loomed over him -- wounded, screaming, its ichor dripping from the gash in its underbelly -- but it did not strike again. It did not need to. The venom was doing its work. Kian Santang could feel it -- feel the cold spreading through his torso, feel his lungs slowing, feel his heartbeat becoming irregular, skipping beats, stumbling over its own rhythm like a runner whose legs have given out.

His vision blurred. The eight burning eyes above him became sixteen, became thirty-two, became a constellation of cold fire that wheeled slowly across his field of vision like stars seen from the bottom of a well.

*The pool,* he thought. *The cold water. Bagus said... the cold becomes good. The body adjusts...*

But this cold did not become good. This cold was final. This cold was the end of adjustment, the last temperature, the permanent condition of a body that would never again know warmth.

He tried to speak. His mouth formed the word, but no sound came -- only a thin, wet exhalation, a breath that tasted of iron and venom and the salt of tears he could not feel falling.

*Bagus.*

The darkness took him.

## VI. The Choices of Bagus

Bagus ran.

He did not want to. Every fiber of his body, every cell of his being, every ancestral voice in the deep, inherited library of his *karuhun* was screaming at him to go back -- go back to the chamber, go back to Kian Santang, go back and fight the impossible and die if dying was the price of staying. His legs ran and his heart ran back, and the contradiction tore him in half with a pain that was worse than the wound in his shoulder, worse than the venom-reek that burned his lungs, worse than the darkness that pressed against him from all sides like the hands of the dead trying to pull him down.

He had heard the scream. Not the scorpion's scream -- he had heard that too, a sound that would live in his nightmares until the nightmares themselves wore thin and died -- but the other scream. The smaller one. The human one. The short, sharp gasp that Kian Santang had made when the stinger found his chest, a sound so quiet and so private that it should have been lost in the cacophony of the battle but was instead the loudest thing Bagus had ever heard, louder than the gamelan, louder than the ocean, louder than the Naga's roar in the caves beneath the mountains, because it was the sound of his best friend being killed.

He had seen the keris fall.

In the violet light, he had seen Kian Santang crumple, had seen the small body fold like wet cloth, had seen the keris slip from the nerveless fingers and clatter to the bone-carpet, its light dimming from blazing violet to a dull, ember-like glow as it separated from its bearer's touch. And he had seen the Kalajengking loom over the fallen body, its wounded underbelly dripping ichor, its mandibles working, its stinger already reloading from the venom glands in its tail -- and he had understood, with a clarity that transcended thought, that there was nothing he could do.

Nothing.

He was a gardener's son from Padalarang. He had a gardening knife -- no, he didn't even have that anymore; the knife was back in the chamber, dropped when the pincer seized him. He had no weapons. He had no *sakti*. He had no ancient power, no enchanted blade, no secret knowledge that would turn the tide. He had only himself -- a small, wounded, terrified Tuyul with a dislocated shoulder and a heart that was breaking and legs that were running because running was the only thing left that he could do that wasn't dying.

*Find Eyang Guru,* Kian Santang had said. *Find help.*

There was no Eyang Guru. Not the resurrected sage in white -- the Dwija Putih was far away, across the mountains, beyond the reach of any voice Bagus could raise. But the instruction had not been literal. It had been a command to survive. To escape. To carry the knowledge of what had happened here out of the darkness and into the world where someone -- anyone -- might be able to act on it.

He looked back.

He should not have looked back. The tunnel mouth was still visible behind him -- a ragged circle of faint violet light, the dying glow of the fallen keris bleeding through the chamber's entrance like the last light of a sunset through a closing door. Through that aperture, diminished by distance and distortion, he could see it: the Kalajengking bending over the still form on the bone-carpet, its pincers reaching, its massive body eclipsing the violet glow as it lowered itself toward the prey it had claimed. And beneath it, barely visible, a shape so small and so still that it might have been a bundle of rags, might have been a discarded sack, might have been anything at all except what it was -- Kian Santang, his best friend, his *priya*, the boy he had followed into the dark because following was what love did when it could not lead.

The scorpion's spinnerets began to work. Even at this distance, even through the haze of tears and terror and the encroaching dark, Bagus could see the silk extruding -- thick, wet ribbons of grey-white fiber, catching the violet light, spiraling around the small body with the methodical, unhurried precision of a creature performing a task it had performed ten thousand times before. The wrapping had begun. The silk was closing over Kian Santang's face, over his closed eyes, over the mouth that had called Bagus's name as the last word it would ever speak.

Bagus's legs stopped.

He stood in the tunnel, one hand braced against the stone wall, the other pressed against the wound in his shoulder, and he watched his friend being wrapped in silk for the eating, and he could not move. Could not go forward. Could not go back. Could not do anything except stand in the dark and witness the destruction of everything he had promised to protect, and the pain of it -- the searing, breathtaking, annihilating pain of absolute helplessness in the presence of absolute loss -- was a physical force that bent him double, that folded him over his own knees, that drove the air from his lungs in a sound that was not a scream and not a sob but something between and beyond them both, a sound that the Sundanese language has no word for because the Sundanese language, for all its poetry, was built for a world where some griefs are too large for vocabulary.

"*Karuhun,*" he whispered, and the word was not a prayer for himself. It was a prayer for Kian Santang. For the ancestors to receive him. For the roots of the *rasamala* trees to recognize his *bayu* and draw it down into the earth of Padalarang where it belonged, far from this mountain, far from this darkness, far from the cold silk and the colder venom and the monster that knew nothing of the boy it was consuming -- knew nothing of the laughter, the diving, the stolen fruit, the hand extended in the dark, the voice that said *I will bring you home.*

*I will bring you home.*

The promise. The impossible, unbreakable, already-broken promise. Made on the graves of his parents, who were buried beneath the *rasamala* grove behind the village *pendopo*. Made on the roots of every tree in Padalarang, which were at this moment drawing water from the clean earth of the Citarum valley while their son's best friend lay wrapped in silk in a monster's larder half a world away. The promise was a rope and he was hanging from it over an abyss, and the rope was fraying, and his hands were bleeding, and the abyss was the knowledge that he had failed -- failed the promise, failed the friend, failed the simple, sacred, elementary task that had been the entire purpose of his presence on this journey: to bring Kian Santang home.

He remembered the *sawah*. The morning light on the rice paddies, the water reflecting the sky in a thousand small mirrors, and Kian Santang walking the levee beside him with his feet bare and his shirt open and his face turned up to catch the sun, and Bagus had thought, in that moment, with the casual, unexamined certainty of youth, *I will always be beside you. Wherever you go. Whatever you face. I will be there.* And the thought had not been heroic. It had not been a vow or a prophecy or a premonition of the darkness to come. It had been a fact, as obvious and as unremarkable as the sun on the water or the mud between his toes, a fact so deeply embedded in the architecture of his identity that questioning it would have been like questioning the color of the sky or the direction of gravity.

And now the fact was a lie. He was here, in the tunnel, running. And Kian Santang was there, in the chamber, dying. And the distance between them was growing with every step, and every step was a betrayal, and every betrayal was a nail driven through the living wood of the promise he had made.

The tunnel swallowed him.

He ran without light, without direction, without anything except the feel of the stone walls beneath his outstretched hands and the sound of his own breathing and the hot, thick tears that streamed from his eyes and blinded him -- though in this darkness, blindness was the universal condition, and tears made no difference to a man who could not see.

The tears did not stop. They came from a place deeper than the eyes, deeper than the grief, from the aquifer of accumulated devotion that a lifetime of friendship had laid down in the substrata of his soul -- every shared meal, every whispered secret, every silent moment of companionship on the riverbank or the *sawah* levee or the forest path, all of it liquefying under the heat of loss, all of it rising to the surface and spilling from his eyes in streams that carved channels through the grime and the venom-dust on his cheeks, two clean lines on a dirty face, two rivers on a ruined map.

He hit a wall. His shoulder -- the wounded one -- struck stone, and the pain detonated through his body like a bomb, a white-hot flare that blotted out thought and reduced him to a gasping, sobbing animal crouched in the dark, his forehead pressed against the cold stone, his hands clutching his shoulder, his mouth open in a silent scream that echoed only in the chambers of his breaking heart.

*Get up.*

The voice was not external. It was his own -- the voice of the steady, practical, endlessly capable self that had carried water and split firewood and tended the *sawah* and done every mundane, unglamorous thing that a life in a Sundanese village required, the self that did not deal in heroism or legend but in the simple, repeated discipline of doing what needed to be done, day after day, without complaint and without credit.

*Get up. Stand up. Move your feet. One foot. Then the other. Then the first again. That's all there is. That's all there's ever been.*

*But Kian --*

*One foot. Then the other. You cannot save him by dying here. You cannot keep the promise by breaking yourself against this wall. One foot, Bagus. Then the other. The promise is not over. The promise is not over until you decide it is over, and you have not decided. You will not decide. One foot.*

He got up.

He moved through the tunnel. His left arm hung useless at his side, the shoulder sending jolts of agony with every step, but he moved -- stumbling over bones, tearing through webs, bouncing off walls like a stone in a shaken box. The darkness was absolute. The silence behind him was absolute. No sound of pursuit. The Kalajengking had not followed him. It had what it wanted.

Time became meaningless. He walked, stumbled, crawled, walked again. The tunnel branched and he chose by instinct, by the feel of the air on his face, by the vaguest, most animal sense of *up* -- the sense that said the surface was in this direction, the air was marginally less foul in this direction, the stone was marginally cooler in this direction, and these marginal differences, accumulated over hundreds of steps, would eventually add up to daylight.

Or they would not, and he would die in the dark, and no one would ever know what had happened to Kian Santang.

He thought about the river. The pool beneath the waterfall. The jade-green water and the sunlight through the canopy and the shock of cold that became good, that became alive. He thought about Kian Santang's face when he jumped -- the moment of flight, the whoop of joy, the splash -- and he held that image in his mind like a torch, like a *blencong* lamp, like Eyang Guru's sacred fire, and he followed it through the dark.

*I made a promise,* he thought. *On the graves of my parents. On the roots of every rasamala tree in Padalarang. I will bring you home.*

*And if he is dead --*

He would not finish the thought. He crushed it underfoot the way he crushed the bones beneath his bleeding feet -- deliberately, violently, with the full weight of his refusal to accept the unacceptable. Kian Santang was not dead. The venom was slow. The wrapping was preservation, not consumption. There was time. There had to be time. And if there was time, then there was hope, and if there was hope, then the promise still held, and if the promise still held, then Bagus's feet must keep moving, one and then the other, through the dark, through the pain, through the grief that sat on his chest like a stone and made each breath a negotiation with despair.

He walked. The darkness was endless. His shoulder screamed. His tears dried. His feet bled.

He walked.

## VII. Si Burik's Triumph and the Darkness

Behind him, in the chamber of bones, the Kalajengking attended to its prey.

The creature moved with the methodical, unhurried efficiency of a predator that has lived long enough to develop ritual. Its pincers lifted Kian Santang's limp body from the bone-carpet -- gently, almost delicately, the serrated edges cradling rather than cutting, the hydraulic force modulated to handle without crushing. The venom had done its work. The Tuyul's body was limp, its limbs slack, its eyes closed, its breathing reduced to a shallow, barely perceptible flutter that would have been invisible to human eyes but was clearly detectable to the Kalajengking's sensory apparatus -- the fine hairs on its pedipalps that could feel the vibration of a heartbeat through six inches of silk.

It began to spin.

The silk extruded from the spinnerets at the base of its tail in thick, continuous ribbons -- not the dry, architectural silk of its tunnel-webs but a different formulation, a wetter, denser, more adhesive compound designed for wrapping prey. The scorpion turned Kian Santang's body in its pincers with the practiced precision of a *penjual kain* wrapping cloth, each revolution adding another layer of silk, each layer compressing the body within into a tighter, more secure cocoon. The silk was warm when it touched the skin -- body-temperature warm, almost comforting in its enveloping pressure -- and as it hardened in the air, it contracted slightly, molding itself to the contours of the body beneath like a second skin.

In seven minutes, Kian Santang was cocooned. The silk covered him from crown to toe, leaving only the faintest outline of his form visible beneath the grey-white wrapping -- the ridge of a nose, the hollow of an eye socket, the slight protrusion of hands crossed over a chest that still, barely, rose and fell.

The Kalajengking placed the cocoon against the cavern wall, among the other wrapped bodies that hung in its web-gallery. It did not distinguish between this prey item and the others. It did not know or care that the small, wrapped form contained the last hope of Nusantara. It simply stored its food and returned to the center of its chamber, its eight legs folding beneath its body, its tail curling around itself, its burning eyes dimming from active hunt-mode to the half-lidded, resting glow of a predator settling in to wait for digestion.

The Keris Murka lay on the bone-carpet where it had fallen. Its violet glow had faded to nothing. The blade was dark, inert, a piece of metal with no visible power -- a common keris, unremarkable, forgotten. The weapon that had seduced and corrupted and dominated every soul that had ever touched it lay in the bones and the dust like a discarded tool, and for the first time in its long existence, nothing reached for it. Nothing wanted it. The Kalajengking did not understand metal. The dead did not covet power. The darkness had no use for a blade.

The keris lay alone, and the silence that surrounded it was the silence of a thing that has been, for the first time in centuries, abandoned.

---

He came from the crevice like a creature born from the stone itself.

Si Burik's pale body emerged from a crack in the cavern wall that should not have been wide enough to admit a body of any size -- a fissure barely three hand-widths across, a crack in the basalt that connected to a network of smaller passages that threaded through the rock like the capillaries of a dying circulatory system. He had been there the entire time. Through the battle, through the screaming, through the violet fire and the venom and the wrapping, he had been wedged into the crack with his knees drawn to his chin and his eyes squeezed shut and his hands clamped over his ears, rocking, rocking, his mouth moving in a continuous, silent monologue that was half prayer and half self-justification and half mad, despairing apology to a conscience that had been eroded to a sliver but had not yet been entirely consumed.

*Didn't know she would be so big,* the monologue ran. *Didn't know. Si Burik thought... Si Burik remembered her smaller. From the old times. When Si Burik came through before. She was smaller then. Or Si Burik was bigger. When Si Burik had the jimat. When Si Burik was whole. She was afraid of Si Burik then. Afraid of the jimat's power. But now... now she is so big... and master is so small... and Si Burik is sorry, sorry, sorry, but the jimat, the jimat calls, the jimat must come back to Si Burik, it MUST...*

He crept across the bone-carpet. His movements were silent -- perfectly, terrifyingly silent, the silence of a creature that has spent centuries learning to move through spaces occupied by things that would eat him. He flowed over the bones without disturbing them, his long toes finding the gaps between femurs and ribs, his weight distributed so evenly that the brittle remains did not crack beneath him. The Kalajengking was resting, its eyes half-lidded, its breathing slow and rhythmic. Si Burik moved along the wall, staying in the deepest shadows, his pale skin absorbing rather than reflecting the faint phosphorescent glow.

He found the keris.

It lay on the bone-carpet ten paces from the Kalajengking's resting body -- a dark, slender shape among the white remains, unremarkable, inert. Si Burik crouched before it. His hands trembled. His breath came in short, rapid gasps that he could not control, that he had to stifle with his own fist jammed against his teeth.

"*Jimat-ku,*" he whispered. The word came out as a sob. "*Jimat-ku... at last... at last...*"

He reached for it.

His long fingers extended toward the blade -- shaking, straining, the tendons in his wrist standing out like wires. The tips of his fingers were three centimeters from the handle. Two. One.

He stopped.

His hand hung in the air above the keris, trembling, and his face -- that ruined, divided face, the face of a creature who had been two people for so long that the boundary between them had become a wound that never healed -- contorted with an expression of such agonized ambivalence that it was difficult to watch.

One hand wanted the keris. Every nerve in that hand, every fiber, every molecule was oriented toward the metal with a hunger so profound that it transcended desire and became a kind of gravity, a physical force pulling his fingers downward toward the blade.

The other hand -- the left, which he had pulled back and was now pressing against his own chest -- wanted something else. Something that he could not name because the naming would have required a vocabulary that the keris had spent centuries stripping from him, word by word, memory by memory, until his language was reduced to *jimat-ku* and *gollum* and the thin, reedy whisper of a mind that remembered kindness the way a blind man remembers color -- as an abstraction, a concept, a word without referent.

A drop of water fell from the ceiling and landed on his cheek.

He flinched. His hand jerked. And in the flinch, in the involuntary spasm of a body reacting to an unexpected stimulus, his fingers closed around the handle of the Keris Murka.

The blade awoke.

The violet light blazed -- not the full, saturating emanation that it had produced in Kian Santang's hand, but a pulsing, irregular glow that fluctuated with Si Burik's heartbeat, brightening and dimming, brightening and dimming, as though the keris were uncertain about its new bearer, testing him, tasting his *bayu* the way a snake's tongue tastes the air. Si Burik gasped. His eyes went wide. His body arched backward as the keris's power entered him -- not through the hand, not through the skin, but through the old channels, the pathways that the weapon had carved into his nervous system during the centuries of his previous ownership, channels that had never fully closed, scar tissue that the keris reopened with the casual brutality of a surgeon reopening a wound.

"*Jimat-ku...*" Si Burik breathed. "*Jimat-ku... yes... yesss... Si Burik remembers... Si Burik remembers the power... the beautiful power... gollum... gollum...*"

He cradled the keris against his chest. His body curled around it in a fetal posture, his knees drawn up, his arms wrapped around the blade, his face pressed against the cold metal of the handle with an expression of such terrible, broken ecstasy that it was indistinguishable from weeping. He rocked back and forth on the bone-carpet, and the violet light pulsed with his heartbeat, and the Kalajengking, ten paces away, stirred in its rest but did not wake.

Then Si Burik rose. He stood -- shakily, unsteadily, his emaciated frame swaying under the weight of the keris's renewed presence in his body -- and he turned toward the wall where the cocoons hung.

He crept to the cocoon that held Kian Santang.

The silk was thick and tight, molded to the Tuyul's body like a death-mask. Through the wrapping, the faintest outline of Kian Santang's face was visible -- the ridge of nose, the hollow of closed eyes, the still, silent mouth. Si Burik leaned close. His lips were centimeters from the silk that covered Kian Santang's ear.

"Master sleeps now..." Si Burik whispered, and his voice was the saddest sound in the world -- a sound that contained every betrayal, every broken promise, every moment of weakness that had led to this, and contained also the knowledge that the creature speaking it was beyond redemption, beyond repair, beyond the reach of any grace that the world might offer. "Master sleeps... and Si Burik takes... the *jimat*... but Si Burik... Si Burik is sorry, master. Si Burik tried. Si Burik *tried*."

A drop of liquid fell on the silk of the cocoon. It might have been water from the ceiling. It might have been saliva from Si Burik's eternally wet mouth. Or it might have been something else -- something that Si Burik's corrupted body should not have been capable of producing, something that required a capacity for grief that the keris should have destroyed long ago but had not, quite, not completely, not the last drop, not the very last.

It might have been a tear.

Si Burik pressed the keris to his chest and scuttled toward the far wall, toward the network of cracks and fissures that would take him away from the chamber, away from the sleeping predator, away from the cocooned body of the master he had betrayed -- and out, into the labyrinthine darkness of the mountain's interior, carrying the hope of Nusantara in his filthy, trembling hands.

---

And in the cocoon, in the wrapping of silk that sealed him from the world, Kian Santang's consciousness flickered.

Not woke. Not returned. Flickered -- the way a candle flame flickers in a draft, the way a drowning man's hand breaks the surface for one final, futile instant before the water closes over it forever. The venom held him. The cold held him. The darkness held him. But beneath the venom and the cold and the darkness, in the deepest chamber of his failing heart, a single thought persisted.

Not a thought of the keris. Not a thought of the mission, of the caldera, of the Shadow King, of the war that raged across Nusantara while he lay wrapped in silk in a monster's larder. Not a thought of failure or regret or the desperate mathematics of a cause that had been lost.

A thought of Bagus.

A small, brown face. Broad and honest and stubborn. A hand extended in the dark. A voice saying: *I will bring you home.*

The thought was not enough to save him. It was not a weapon, not a *sakti*, not a force that could break the silk or purge the venom or drive back the darkness that was closing over his mind with the patient, inexorable finality of a tide.

But it was warm.

In the cold of the venom and the dark of the cocoon and the silence of a world that had moved on without him, the thought of his friend was warm. And Kian Santang held onto it the way a drowning man holds onto a floating reed -- not because the reed can save him, but because holding is the last thing his hands know how to do, and even at the end, even in the deepest water, even in the final dark, the hands do not forget.

The web tightened. The cold deepened. The darkness was complete.

And in the chamber of bones, beneath the mountain, surrounded by the dead, the keris-bearer of Nusantara lay still, and the world above him turned, and the fires of Krakatau burned, and the war went on without him, and the night had never been so long.
