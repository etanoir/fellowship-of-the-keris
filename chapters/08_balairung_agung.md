# Bab 8: Balairung Agung
## (The Great Council)

---

The pendopo of Dieng stood at the highest point of the plateau, where the earth's breath rose warmest and the mist parted like curtains drawn back by unseen hands.

It was the grandest structure in all the highland temples -- not a building, precisely, for it had no walls, but a vast open pavilion raised upon a forest of carved teak pillars, each one as thick as a man's embrace, each one worked from base to capital with scenes from the old stories. Here, the armies of the first alliance marched in procession around the wood grain, their shields raised, their spears leveled against the Buta hordes that swarmed across the opposite face. There, the Bidadari descended from their mountain sanctuaries in flowing lines, their carved robes seeming to ripple in some wind trapped forever in the timber. And above it all, the roof soared upward in the *joglo* style -- four tiers of dark thatch and tile, each smaller than the one below, rising to a final peak that vanished into the low cloud like a mountain aspiring to become sky.

These stones had stood since the age of Ratu Boko, the king whose ambition built a palace that rivaled heaven itself — and whose curse ensured it would never be completed. The pendopo's pillars bore the same ancient chisel-marks, the same devotion to a grandeur that exceeded mortal reach. What Ratu Boko had begun in pride, the builders of Dieng had completed in humility — and perhaps that was why these stones endured where Boko's palace had crumbled to ruin on its lonely hill.

The floor was polished stone -- volcanic andesite, grey-green, fitted so precisely that a fingernail could not find the seams. It was warm. Not merely the warmth of a floor that has sat long in sunlight, but something deeper, something that rose from the living earth beneath, where the geothermal waters of Dieng threaded through rock in channels older than human memory. To sit upon that floor was to feel the pulse of the island itself, slow and immense, the heartbeat of Jawa transmitted through stone into bone.

It was upon this floor, in the grey light of an overcast morning, that the tikar mats had been laid in a great circle — a *mandala* arrangement, the sacred geometric form that mirrored the structure of the cosmos itself. The circle had no head and no foot, no seat of power and no seat of submission. Each position was equidistant from the center, where the keris would rest, the way all points on a mandala are equidistant from the divine source.

The arrangement was deliberate. In the courts of Mataram and Sriwijaya, a king sat elevated upon a singgasana throne, and his subjects arranged themselves below in careful gradations of rank -- the nearer to the throne, the greater the honor. But this was not a court. This was *sabha* -- the ancient practice of communal deliberation, older than any kingdom, rooted in the same volcanic soil as the rice terraces and the irrigation channels and the shared labor of the harvest. In sabha, all voices carried equal weight. The farmer spoke as freely as the prince. The question was not who held power, but what wisdom the gathering could produce when every perspective was heard, weighed, and woven into consensus.

The mats formed their circle. At the position of honor -- north-facing, where the morning light fell most gently -- sat Prabu Siliwangi, but he sat on the same level as every other delegate, his mat no thicker, his cushion no higher. This, too, was deliberate. The king presided, but he did not command. He was the *dalang* of the discussion -- the one who set the rhythm, gave voice to each character, and guided the story toward its resolution -- but the story, *tinangtu* by the gathering itself, belonged to everyone.

At the four cardinal points of the mandala circle, bronze censers held the sacred *homa* fire — the ritual flame that the *pandita* priests had kindled before dawn with prayers to the Tri Murti, feeding it with offerings of rice, sandalwood shavings, and kemenyan resin. The *homa* was older than any temple, older than any kingdom — it was the fire that connected the mortal world to the realm of the Dewata, carrying prayers upward in smoke and returning blessings in warmth. Its fragrance — resin and earth and something faintly sweet, like memory distilled to smoke — filled the pendopo and settled into the wood, into the stone, into the clothes and hair of every person present, so that for days afterward they would catch a trace of it upon their skin and remember this morning, this gathering, this moment when the fate of Nusantara turned upon the words spoken beneath a roof of teak and cloud.

At the southern edge of the pendopo, beyond the last row of pillars, a small gamelan ensemble sat cross-legged before their instruments. They played softly as the delegates took their places -- a piece in the *pelog* scale, measured and contemplative, the metallophones singing their bell-tones into the mist while the *rebab* drew a single sustained line of melody above them, thin and clear as a thread of silver. The music did not demand attention. It created atmosphere the way incense creates fragrance -- permeating everything, coloring the silence.

Then, at a nod from Prabu Siliwangi, the music ceased. The last note of the *rebab* hung in the air like a question, and was absorbed by the mist.

Silence settled upon the pendopo. The only sounds were the breathing of the assembled delegates, the distant hiss of a fumarole somewhere below the plateau, and -- impossibly faint, impossibly far -- the cry of an elang eagle circling the peaks above Dieng in the grey sky.

---

They had come from every corner of the archipelago, and to see them gathered in one place was to see Nusantara itself made manifest.

Prabu Siliwangi sat in the north position, cross-legged upon his mat, his back straight as the teak pillars that flanked him. He wore a simple white baju of raw silk over a kain batik of deep indigo -- the *megamendung* pattern of Pasundan, clouds layered upon clouds in gradations of blue, symbolizing patience, restraint, the wisdom to wait for clarity. His hair was white, his face lined by decades of governance and grief, but his eyes were dark and steady and fully present. He wore no crown. A true king, the Sundanese believe, needs no ornament.

To his right sat Eyang Guru Batara, the ancient sage, folded into stillness like a heron at the water's edge. He was very old -- how old, no one living could say with certainty, and the old man himself deflected questions on the subject with the practiced ease of someone who had been deflecting them for centuries. His white beard fell to his waist, and his robes, once the pale grey of mountain mist, were stained with the dust of long roads. His staff of gnarled waringin wood lay across his knees. He watched the assembly with eyes that seemed to see not only the people before him but the shadows behind them -- the long histories, the old alliances, the accumulated weight of *karma* — action and consequence, the cosmic ledger that recorded every choice and every cost — that had brought them all to this floor of warm stone.

Raden Wijaya sat to the left of Prabu Siliwangi, and even in stillness he carried the tension of a drawn blade. The exiled heir of Mataram wore the simple garb of the ranger -- dark cotton, travel-worn, a kain wrapped in the practical style of the road rather than the elegant drape of the court. His keris -- not the Keris Murka, but his own ancestral blade, the one he had carried through years of wandering -- hung at his hip. His face was hard and weathered, his dark eyes watchful, and he sat with the coiled alertness of a man long accustomed to sleeping with one ear tuned to danger. He had spoken little since arriving at Dieng. He spoke little always. But when he did speak, people stopped what they were doing and listened.

Dewi Anjani sat across the circle, and the delegates nearest her had unconsciously shifted their mats a few inches farther away, as mortals will in the presence of the numinous. She was Bidadari -- one of the ageless ones, the celestial spirits who had watched over Nusantara since before the first kingdom raised its first stone. She appeared as a woman of impossible beauty, but it was the beauty of a mountain seen at dawn, or a river in full flood -- something vast and inhuman wearing a human shape as a courtesy. Her skin held the pale gold of sunlight filtered through water. Her eyes were the deep amber of ancient tree resin, and they did not blink often enough. She wore armor beneath her selendang -- finely worked plates of silver and something harder, something that caught the grey light and broke it into spectra -- and a bow of pale wood lay across her knees, unstrung but present, as a warrior's weapon should be. In the stillness before the council began, her fingers traced a sequence of movements upon her knee — the sacred *mudra* gestures of the Balinese *legong*, the prayer-dance of the temples, each position an invocation, each transition a blessing. She carried within her the spiritual traditions of both Sunda and Bali, twin streams of devotion flowing through a single vessel.

Tumenggung Batu of the Kurcaci sat like a boulder that had chosen, against all geological precedent, to attend a meeting. He was short -- shorter even than the Tuyul, though far broader, his body a dense arrangement of muscle and bone that suggested he had been assembled from the same materials as the mountains he lived in. His skin was the dark brown of river clay, his beard a thicket of iron-grey wire, and his hands, resting upon his knees, were scarred and callused from decades at the forge. He wore a vest of chainmail over a leather jerkin, and a war hammer hung from his belt -- a brutal instrument of dark iron, its head the size of a coconut, its handle wrapped in sharkskin for grip. He had arrived at Dieng with a personal guard of twelve Kurcaci warriors, each one similarly built and similarly armed, and the plateau's stone paths had rung like anvils beneath their iron-shod boots for hours.

Gajahmada of Majapahit sat directly across from Prabu Siliwangi, and even seated he seemed to occupy more space than his body required. He was tall for a Javanese man, broad through the chest and shoulders, with the physical bearing of a career soldier tempered by the sharp, restless intelligence of a born statesman. His face was handsome and commanding -- strong jaw, high cheekbones, a mouth set in the habit of authority -- and his eyes burned with the particular fire of a man who believed, with every fiber of his being, that he knew how to save the world. He wore the formal court dress of Majapahit -- a *dodot* of crimson silk draped in elaborate folds, a golden *kuluk* headdress, a keris of exquisite workmanship thrust into the folds of his sash. He was here as a general, but he carried himself like a king, and several delegates had noted this with varying degrees of admiration and unease.

The Sriwijayan envoy -- a lean, dark-skinned man with the tattoos of a seafarer marking his forearms in patterns of wave and wind -- sat quietly, his expression guarded. He had brought with him a carved ivory cylinder containing reports from the Bugis seafarers of Sulawesi — those fearless navigators had confirmed the shadow movements in the eastern sea lanes, and their intelligence was grim: the Naga Laut were not merely patrolling but forming a living blockade, a chain of serpents stretching from the Selat Sunda to the Flores Sea. The Kediri horseman, sun-browned and still smelling faintly of horse, sat with his legs folded awkwardly beneath him, more accustomed to a saddle than a mat.

And then there were the Tuyul.

Kian Santang sat at the eastern edge of the circle, small and very still. He wore the best clothes he had -- a clean white baju, the blue Sundanese kain that Bagus had insisted on washing three times in the cold stream behind their lodging -- and he looked precisely like what he was: a young farmer from the highlands, out of his depth, surrounded by kings and warriors and legends made flesh. His hands were clasped in his lap. His face was pale. On the stone pedestal that stood in the exact center of the circle, equidistant from every seated delegate, rested a bundle of brown and gold batik cloth, folded upon itself, tied with a cord of woven palm fiber.

The Keris Murka.

Even wrapped, even bound, even blessed by Eyang Guru's whispered protections, the keris made itself known. Its presence was not visible but palpable -- a pressure in the air, a weight upon the chest, as though the atmosphere inside the pendopo had become slightly denser, slightly heavier, slightly harder to breathe. Several delegates had developed headaches since taking their seats. The Sriwijayan envoy rubbed his temples. Tumenggung Batu's eyes kept returning to the bundle with the involuntary fascination of a smith confronted with metal he cannot identify. And at the edges of awareness, just below the threshold of hearing, there was a whisper -- not words, not language, but something older, a vibration that hummed in the hollow bones of the skull and promised things that the conscious mind refused to translate.

Behind the easternmost pillars, partially hidden by a carved relief of the Ramayana, three small faces peered into the gathering. Bagus, Cepot, and Dawala had not been invited to the council -- the sabha was for leaders and representatives, not for farm boys and rambutan thieves -- but Cepot had located a vantage point behind the pillar that offered a clear view of the proceedings, and what Cepot located, Cepot occupied, and where Cepot went, Dawala followed, and Bagus had come because he had promised Eyang Guru to look after Kian Santang, and he was not about to let a few carved teak pillars and a question of protocol stand between him and that promise.

---

Prabu Siliwangi raised his hand, and the silence deepened. He spoke first a prayer — '*Om Swasti Astu. Awignam astu, demi Sang Hyang Widhi Wasa, nu ngalindungan Nusantara.*' (*Om, may all be well. Blessings upon us, by the Supreme Being, who protects Nusantara.*) The ancient invocation settled over the assembly like a blessing made of air.

"Brothers and sisters of Nusantara," he said. His voice was not loud, but it carried the way a river carries -- steadily, without effort, filling the space it was given. "I thank you for answering my summons. Some of you have traveled far. Some have traveled through danger. That you are here speaks to the gravity of the hour, and I will not dishonor your journey by speaking lightly."

He looked around the circle, meeting each delegate's eyes in turn. When his gaze reached Kian Santang, it rested there a moment longer -- a look of great gentleness, and great sorrow.

"The darkness stirs," Prabu Siliwangi continued. "I speak not in metaphor but in fact. In the east, the Shadow King moves again in Krakatau. His power grows. The Buta mass in numbers not seen since the age of the first alliance -- our scouts report armies gathering in the volcanic wastes, hosts beyond counting, armed and armored and driven by a will that is not their own. The nine Leak ride openly across the land, no longer hiding in shadow but hunting in the full light of the moon, and their presence spreads a sickness of the spirit wherever they pass. In the north, Empu Gandring, whom we trusted as keeper of the sacred forge at Merapi, has fallen to corruption. His craft, which once served the protection of the free peoples, now serves the Shadow King. He has turned his hammer against us."

A murmur passed through the circle. The Kediri horseman shifted on his mat. Gajahmada's jaw tightened.

"Word has reached us from beyond Jawa," Prabu Siliwangi continued, his voice steady as the stone beneath them. "The Balinese temple priests — the *pedanda* and the *pemangku* — have confirmed the spiritual omens. The *niskala*, the unseen world, is in upheaval. The offerings go unanswered. The sacred dances falter. From Sulawesi, the Bugis seafarers report shadow movements across the eastern waters. And from the distant shores of Papua, warriors of the mountain clans have sent their pledges — peoples who have never before entered the councils of the western kingdoms, but who feel, as we all feel, the trembling of the earth beneath our feet."

"And the source of it all," Prabu Siliwangi said, "the root from which this darkness draws its strength, the instrument through which the Shadow King projects his will across the archipelago -- sits among us now."

Every eye turned to the stone pedestal at the center of the circle. The batik-wrapped bundle seemed to absorb the grey light, returning nothing.

"Kian Santang of Padalarang," the king said gently. "Will you show the council what you carry?"

Kian Santang rose. His knees trembled, and he willed them to stop, and they did not. He crossed the short distance to the pedestal on legs that felt borrowed from someone else, and with hands that shook only slightly -- he was proud of that, later -- he untied the cord and folded back the layers of batik cloth.

The Keris Murka lay upon the stone.

It was smaller than the legends suggested. Slender. Seven sinuous curves in its blade -- seven *luk*, the number of dark completion -- and the pamor patterns that wound through the metal were not the familiar patterns that any empu would recognize. These were wrong. They shifted. In the grey light of the pendopo, the watered steel seemed to move, the dark and light layers of the pamor crawling across one another like smoke trapped in iron. The hilt was wrapped in blackened silver wire, and the *mendak* guard was carved -- or grown, it was hard to say -- in the shape of a serpent consuming its own tail.

No one spoke. The whisper in the air grew louder by a fraction, and the incense smoke in the nearest censer bent toward the blade, drawn by a gravity that had nothing to do with the physical world. The *homa* flames in their bronze basins flickered and leaned, as though even the sacred fire recognized what lay upon the pedestal and drew back from it.

Kian Santang stepped back to his mat, and sat, and clasped his trembling hands.

---

Eyang Guru Batara spoke.

He did not stand -- his age afforded him that courtesy -- but his voice, when it came, filled the pendopo the way the kemenyan smoke filled it: thoroughly, inescapably, reaching every corner.

"What you see before you," he said, "is the Keris Murka. The Keris of Wrath. It was forged by Raja Iblis in the deepest caldera of Krakatau, in the age before the age of kingdoms, when the Shadow King's power was at its zenith and the free peoples of Nusantara lived in chains of fear. Into this blade he poured his malice, his will to dominate, his hunger to rule every throne from Pasundan to Kutai. Seven pamor patterns are coiled into the metal -- each one a chain of binding, each one a corruption. And he forged nine lesser blades, nine shadow keris, and gave them to nine rulers whom he had seduced with promises of empire. Those nine became the Leak -- the shape-shifters, the night-terrors, bound forever to his will."

The old sage paused. His eyes moved across the assembly, and there was something in his gaze that was not merely ancient but eternal, as though he were not recounting history but testifying to events he had personally witnessed.

"The blade was cut from the Shadow King's hand by Raden Wijaya's ancestor, at the Battle of the Straits, when the first alliance broke his armies and cast his spirit back into the fire. But the keris was not destroyed. It was kept. It was hidden. And through the long centuries, it whispered, and it waited, and it found its way from hand to hand, leaving ruin in its wake, until it came to rest in the limestone caves of Gunung Sewu. There it lay in darkness for an age, and the Leak searched for it, and the Shadow King bent his will toward finding it, and it was found -- by chance, or by design, or by the keris's own dark will -- by a Tuyul of Padalarang who wandered too far and too deep."

Silence. The weight of it pressed upon the assembly like a hand upon the chest.

"I will speak plainly," Eyang Guru said, and his voice was the voice of stone. "The Keris Murka cannot be hidden. The Shadow King will find it, as he has always found it. It cannot be controlled -- every hand that grips this blade serves the blade, not the other way. It cannot be wielded for good, for there is no good in it; its every pamor is a thread connected to the will of Raja Iblis, and to draw it in battle is to open a door through which his power floods. It can only be destroyed."

He let the word settle.

"And it can only be destroyed in the fires where it was forged. In the deepest caldera of Krakatau. In the very heart of the Shadow King's domain."

---

The silence that followed Eyang Guru's words lasted the space of seven breaths. Then Gajahmada stood.

He rose in a single fluid motion, and the crimson silk of his dodot caught the grey light and held it, so that he seemed to glow faintly, a figure of fire and authority against the muted backdrop of stone and cloud. When he spoke, his voice was the voice of command -- resonant, rich, shaped by years of addressing armies and councils and kings.

"I hear the wisdom of Eyang Guru," Gajahmada said, "and I honor it. But wisdom without action is philosophy, and philosophy does not stop armies."

He turned slowly, addressing not Eyang Guru alone but the full circle, compelling each delegate to meet his gaze.

"My brothers and sisters, I speak to you not as a scholar of the old stories but as a soldier. As a general who has buried his men. As a servant of Majapahit, whose eastern borders burn as we sit here upon our mats debating history." His voice tightened. "The Buta have crossed the Brantas. Three villages in the lowlands are ash. The harvest has failed in the eastern provinces, not from drought but from blight -- a creeping corruption in the soil itself, spreading from the volcanic wastes. My people starve. My soldiers fight with broken weapons and empty bellies, and each day the enemy grows stronger while we grow weaker."

He gestured toward the keris on the pedestal. "And we possess the one instrument that could turn the tide. The weapon forged by the enemy himself -- the blade that commands the Buta, that binds the Leak, that channels the very power we seek to oppose. And we are told to throw it into a volcano."

Gajahmada shook his head. "I have sworn the Sumpah Palapa -- the sacred oath to unite all of Nusantara under one banner, one alliance, one protection. Not for empire. Not for glory. For survival. With the Keris Murka in the hands of a strong and righteous bearer, that unity could be forged. The Buta could be commanded to stand down. The Leak could be broken. The Shadow King's own weapon turned against him." He struck his fist into his open palm. "Why destroy power when power is exactly what we need?"

He stood a moment longer, his jaw set, his eyes blazing, and then he sat. And in the silence that followed, several delegates nodded -- not many, but enough. The Kediri horseman murmured assent. Even the Sriwijayan envoy's guarded expression had shifted toward something that might have been agreement.

It was a dangerous argument precisely because it was a compelling one.

Eyang Guru spoke into the silence, and his voice was gentle, which made it worse.

"Gajahmada," he said. "Your courage honors Majapahit, and your love for your people is beyond question. But you speak of wielding the keris as though it were a tool that answers to the hand that holds it. It is not. It is the hand that holds *you*." He paused. "The keris does not serve its bearer. The bearer serves the keris. Every pamor in that blade is a thread tied to the will of Raja Iblis. To draw it is not to command him -- it is to invite him in. He would flow through the blade and into you, and all your strength, all your righteousness, all your fierce love for Majapahit would become fuel for his purpose. You would not defeat the Shadow King. You would *become* him. The next Raja Iblis, wearing the face of a *wira*, and all the more terrible for it."

Gajahmada's face darkened, and for a moment the air between the general and the sage crackled with something unspoken.

Then Raden Wijaya spoke.

He did not stand. He did not raise his voice. He spoke from where he sat, quietly, with the authority of a man who has earned his words through suffering rather than rhetoric.

"My ancestor cut this blade from the Shadow King's hand," he said. "He was a great warrior. A good man. And when the battle was won and the Shadow King was cast down, he chose to keep the keris rather than destroy it. He told himself what Gajahmada tells us now -- that such power could be turned to good. That it could protect. That it would be different in his hands."

He looked up, and his dark eyes held a grief so old it had become a geography, a landscape within him.

"It was the greatest mistake in the history of Nusantara. The keris corrupted him. It corrupted his heirs. It brought the fall of the old Mataram kingdom, the scattering of my line, centuries of shadow and sorrow that echo still." His voice did not waver. '*Kula nyuwun pangapunten dhateng para karuhun*,' he added in *krama* Javanese — *I ask forgiveness of the ancestors* — the formal register carrying the weight of thirteen generations of shame. "I carry the blood of the man who made that choice, and I carry the shame of it. I will not see it repeated. Not by Gajahmada. Not by anyone."

The words fell into the circle like stones into still water, and the ripples reached every mat.

Dewi Anjani spoke next. She did not move, and her voice, when it came, held a quality that was difficult to describe -- layered, as though several voices spoke in perfect unison, a harmony of ages. She spoke in *basa luhur* — the highest register of Sundanese, the sacred tongue of the celestial realm — and then translated for those who could not follow.

'*Kami Bidadari parantos nyaksian ti jaman ka jaman,*' she said. (*The Bidadari have watched from age to age.*) "We watched when the keris was forged. We watched when it was wielded. We watched when it was lost, and when it was found, and when it was lost again. And in every age that touches this blade, the story ends the same way." Her amber eyes moved across the assembly, ancient and unblinking. "In fire. It is not a weapon. It is a disease. And the only cure is to return it to the source and unmake what was made."

Tumenggung Batu cleared his throat -- a sound like gravel shifting in a quarry.

"The Kurcaci know metal," he said. His voice was blunt, unadorned, the voice of a craftsman with no patience for oratory. "We have worked the forges of Gunung Sewu since before the first human kingdom raised a wall. I have held ten thousand blades in my hands. Keris, kujang, golok, tombak -- every weapon this archipelago has ever dreamed of. And I tell you that the pamor in that blade is *wrong*." He jabbed a thick finger toward the pedestal. "It is not of this world. No empu forged those patterns. No earthly fire could have laid that metal. It is a thing of the deep places, the dark places, and it has no business existing above ground." He folded his arms across his barrel chest. "Destroy it."

The Sriwijayan envoy spoke last among the respondents, and his voice carried the careful weight of a man accustomed to navigating between powers greater than his own.

"I do not dispute that the blade must be destroyed," he said. "But I must speak to the question of how. The sea routes between Jawa and Krakatau pass through the Selat Sunda, and the strait is no longer ours. The Naga Laut patrol those waters -- the great sea serpents that answer to the Shadow King's will. Our trading fleets have been driven from the western passages. The Sriwijayan navy, which once commanded the sea from Sumatra to Kalimantan, can no longer guarantee safe passage through its own waters." He spread his tattooed hands. "Even if we resolve to destroy the keris, the path to Krakatau is a path through the enemy's jaws. I bear also the reports of our Bugis allies of Sulawesi — their fastest *pinisi* ships have been turned back from the western straits three times this season. The blockade is complete."

---

The arguments had circled the pendopo like the smoke of the kemenyan -- drifting, interweaving, thickening. Every voice had been heard. Every perspective weighed. This was the sabha doing its ancient work, the slow and sometimes painful process of consensus, of collective wisdom emerging from individual conviction. But no consensus had emerged. The arguments for destruction were strong; the impossibility of the journey was equally strong; and Gajahmada's counterproposal, though refuted, still hung in the air like the ghost of a temptation, because it offered what every frightened heart craves -- a shortcut, a solution that did not require sacrifice.

Prabu Siliwangi let the silence breathe.

Then he spoke. "The sabha has heard all voices. The keris must be destroyed -- on this, the wisdom of the council is clear. The path to Krakatau is perilous beyond measure. An army cannot go -- an army would be seen, and met, and broken before it reached the coast. It must be a small company, moving in secret, bearing the blade through enemy territory by stealth rather than strength."

He paused. The silence in the pendopo became a living thing, a held breath, a weight.

"Then who shall carry it?"

No one moved.

The question hung in the warm, incense-heavy air, and every delegate felt its weight settle upon their shoulders and understood what it meant. It was not a question of courage. Every person in this circle possessed courage -- they would not be here otherwise. It was a question of sacrifice. To carry the Keris Murka to Krakatau was to walk into the Shadow King's domain, past his armies, past his Leak, past the Naga Laut and the Buta hordes, to the edge of a caldera of molten fire, with every step bringing the bearer closer to the keris's source of power and therefore deeper into its corruption. It was, by any rational assessment, a death sentence delivered in the form of a question.

Gajahmada studied his hands. The Sriwijayan envoy found something of deep interest in the polished stone between his knees. The Kediri horseman stared straight ahead with the fixed expression of a man willing himself to be invisible. Even Eyang Guru sat in silence, his ancient eyes closed, his lips moving in what might have been a prayer to Sang Hyang Widhi or calculation or both.

Behind the pillar, Bagus gripped the carved teak until his knuckles whitened.

The silence stretched. It became uncomfortable. It became painful. It became a thing with mass and texture, pressing down upon the circle, and still no one spoke, and the keris lay upon its pedestal in the center, patient as it had always been patient, waiting as it had always waited.

Then a voice spoke. Small. Clear. Slightly unsteady at the edges but firm in its center, the way a young tree is firm -- thin, but rooted.

"I will take it."

Every head turned.

Kian Santang stood. He had risen from his mat without deciding to rise -- his body had moved before his mind could counsel otherwise, propelled by something deeper than thought. He stood in the center of the council, barely reaching the shoulders of the seated humans around him, his bare feet on the warm stone, his hands at his sides, and he trembled. He trembled visibly, and he did not try to hide it, because hiding it would have been a lie, and the sabha deserved truth.

"I will take the keris to Krakatau," he said.

The council stared. Gajahmada's eyebrows rose. The Kediri horseman blinked. Tumenggung Batu leaned forward, as if to confirm that the voice had indeed come from this small person and not from the pillar behind him.

Kian Santang swallowed. '*Ingwang seja nyuhunkeun widi, Kangjeng Prabu,*' he said — *I respectfully request permission, Your Majesty* — the Sundanese *basa lemes* falling from his lips with the trembling formality of the young addressing the ancient. "I do not claim to be wira. I am not a yoda. I am not a king or a balapati or a sage. I am a farmer's nephew from the Pasundan highlands, and six months ago the most dangerous thing I had ever done was climb the waringin tree behind the lembur meeting hall. But I have carried this blade from Padalarang to Dieng, and I know what it is. I feel it. Every day, every hour, it whispers to me, and I have not listened, and I do not know why I have been able to resist it when stronger hands than mine could not, but I have. And if that means something -- if that is a sign that this task was given to me for a reason -- then I will see it through. I will take it. I do not know the way, and I do not know if I will survive, but I will take it."

His voice cracked on the last word, and he fell silent, and the silence that followed was of a different quality than the one that had preceded it. It was not the silence of avoidance. It was the silence of recognition -- the hush that falls when something true has been spoken, something so simple and so brave that it renders eloquence meaningless.

Prabu Siliwangi studied the young Tuyul for a long moment. His aged eyes were bright with unshed tears, though his face remained composed, because a king does not weep in council, not even when his heart requires it.

"I believe," Prabu Siliwangi said, "that this task was appointed to you, Kian Santang. And if you do not find a way, no one will."

---

Behind the pillar, Bagus moved.

He did not think about it. He did not weigh the consequences, calculate the risks, or compose an appropriate speech. He simply stepped out from behind the carved relief of the Ramayana, crossed the open floor of the pendopo in four strides, and planted himself beside Kian Santang with the immovable solidity of a man who has found the one place in the world where he is supposed to stand.

"I'm going with him," he said.

Several delegates turned with expressions of startled disapproval. Eyang Guru opened one eye. Cepot, still behind the pillar, was making frantic gestures that might have meant *come back* or might have meant *well done* -- with Cepot, the distinction was often academic.

"I made a promise to Eyang Guru," Bagus continued, his voice steady, his broad face flushed but resolute, "to look after Kian Santang. To walk with him and keep him safe. And I mean to keep it. I don't know anything about keris or Shadow Kings or the fires of Krakatau, but I know about Kian Santang. I know he doesn't eat enough when he's worried. I know he forgets to sleep. I know that someone needs to make sure he has clean water and decent food and a reason to keep walking when the walking gets hard. That's me. That's what I do. And you'll have to carry me out of this pendopo before I let him go alone."

Kian Santang looked at his friend. His eyes blurred with a warmth that had nothing to do with the geothermal stone beneath his feet.

Eyang Guru Batara rose. It took him a moment -- he was very old, and his joints protested the indignity of being asked to straighten after so long upon a mat -- but when he stood, he stood tall, and his eyes were clear and hard as quartz.

"I will guide them," he said. "My wisdom is old, and perhaps not as sharp as it once was, but what I have, I offer. I will walk with the keris-bearer as far as my knowledge and my strength can take him."

Raden Wijaya stood. He moved with the silent economy of the ranger, rising from his mat like a shadow detaching itself from a wall. He did not address the council. He crossed the circle, knelt before Kian Santang -- *knelt*, a prince of Mataram's blood, one knee upon the warm stone, his head bowed before a Tuyul who stood a full head shorter than his kneeling form -- and said: '*Ayo dulur — sampeyan gadah pedang kula.*' (*Come on, brother — you have my blade.*) The Javanese fell from his lips in a register that hovered between *ngoko* and *krama*, the intimate formality of a warrior swearing an oath to a comrade he has chosen freely.

The pendopo went still. This was not merely a pledge. It was a symbol that several of the older delegates recognized and many more felt in their bones without being able to name: the high brought low in service to the humble, the sword bent to the plowshare, the ancient wrong of Mataram's pride answered at last by Mataram's heir kneeling to the keris-bearer. Gajahmada watched with an unreadable expression.

Dewi Anjani rose. She moved like light on water -- without effort, without sound, as though gravity were a suggestion she had chosen to follow rather than a law she was bound to obey. She took the unstrung bow from her mat, and as she stepped forward, she strung it in a single fluid motion, the pale wood bending to her will, the string singing one sharp note as it seated itself.

'*Ingwang nyuhunkeun kahormatan pikeun ngajaga nu ngagaduhan keris,*' she said — the *basa luhur* falling from her lips like water over sacred stone. (*I request the honor of guarding the keeper of the keris.*) "You have my bow. The Bidadari stand with the keris-bearer."

Tumenggung Batu made a sound that was part grunt, part laugh, and entirely devoid of surprise.

"Well," he said, heaving himself to his feet. "If the Bidadari goes, the Kurcaci go. That's been the way of it since the First Age, and I'll not have it said that the mountain folk let the sky-dancers walk into danger alone." He unhooked his war hammer from his belt and raised it. "You have my hammer, small one. And the forges of Gunung Sewu stand behind it."

A beat of silence. Then Gajahmada moved.

He rose slowly, and there was a struggle visible in his face -- the war between his conviction and his conscience, between the soldier's pragmatism and the deeper understanding that had been growing in him since Raden Wijaya's words, since Eyang Guru's warning, since the quiet Mataram prince had laid bare the consequences of the choice Gajahmada had been championing. He stood, and he was silent for a long moment, and the council watched, and waited.

"I would have used it to save my people," he said. His voice was rough. "I still believe it could have been used. I still believe--" He stopped. He closed his eyes. When he opened them, something had changed, some wall had been breached, and behind it was not the general but the man -- tired, afraid, and honest.

"Perhaps," he said, more quietly, "perhaps destroying it *is* saving my people."

He drew his keris from his sash -- not the Keris Murka, but his own blade, the magnificent Majapahit keris with its golden hilt and its thirteen luk, the weapon of his rank and his oath. He held it up, point toward the thatched roof, and the polished blade caught the grey light and held it.

"You have my keris," he said. "And the oath of the Sumpah Palapa -- that Nusantara shall be united, and free. I will fight for that oath, even if the path is not the one I would have chosen."

From behind the pillar, a voice rang out with cheerful inevitability.

"Someone has to keep you lot from getting lost," Cepot announced, strolling into the circle with the insouciant confidence of someone arriving at a party he had been invited to all along. '*Naon deui atuh — kami mah siap!*' he added — *What else can I say — I'm ready!* — the *basa loma*, the casual Sundanese register of the streets and the market stalls, cutting through the ceremony like a cleaver through silk. "I've been to places none of you have been -- well, mostly orchards I wasn't supposed to be in, but the principle of navigation is the same. I'm in."

Dawala appeared behind him, round-faced and earnest, blinking in the sudden attention of the full council. He opened his mouth, closed it, looked at Cepot, looked at Kian Santang, and said: "What he said."

And despite everything -- despite the weight of the moment, the gravity of the quest, the shadow of the keris upon the stone -- a ripple of laughter moved through the pendopo, brief and human and necessary, the way rain is necessary after drought.

---

They stood together in the center of the circle. Nine.

Prabu Siliwangi rose from his mat and looked upon them, and his expression held the particular pain of a leader who sends others into the dark knowing he cannot follow. He raised his hands, palms outward, in the old Sundanese gesture of blessing — the *sembah agung*, the great reverence, *sinembah* not to the gods but from a Tohaan to those who would carry the world's hope upon their backs.

"Nine," he said. "Nine *priya*, to match the nine Leak who hunt the keris. Nine, to answer shadow with light, corruption with *wira* hearts, despair with the stubborn, unreasonable, unbreakable refusal to surrender."

His voice grew, filling the pendopo, carrying beyond the pillars and into the mist, as though the words were not merely spoken but inscribed upon the air.

"I name you *Pasukan Sembilan* -- the Nine Companions. You are *Persekutuan Keris* -- the Fellowship of the Keris. You carry with you the hope of every kingdom, every village, every sawah and every lembur from Sabang to Merauke. You carry our future in your hands. And you carry our blessing upon your heads."

A *Dangdanggula* rose from the assembled voices — the pupuh of grandeur and noble beauty, its opening notes sweeping upward like the smoke of sacred incense. The musicians had not been signaled. Perhaps they needed no signal. Perhaps the *Dangdanggula* called itself into being, as it had at every moment of consequence since the first songs were composed in the first courts of the first kingdoms. The voices wove together — Sundanese and Javanese, mortal and Bidadari, the rough bass of the Kurcaci and the silver tenor of the sage:

*Mangka ning rat raja kang sejati,*
*lir surya madhangi bumi,*
*ngajaga dharma lan satya...*

(*The true sovereign of the world / like the sun illuminating the earth / guarding righteousness and truth...*)

The melody was ancient and unsummoned, rising from the warm stone floor itself, from the earth beneath, from the volcanic heart of the island.

Prabu Siliwangi began to speak in ancient Sundanese -- the old language, the language of the first rice songs and the mountain prayers, words that even the most learned scholars could only partially translate. '*Om Swasti Astu, Sang Hyang Widhi Wasa, Tri Murti — Brahma, Wisnu, Siwa — lindungi ieu pasukan dina perjalanan siya ka sumber seuneu.*' The syllables were low and rhythmic, rising and falling like the contour of the highlands themselves, and as he spoke, the air in the pendopo seemed to thicken and clarify simultaneously, as though the blessing were not merely words but a substance, settling upon the nine like dew upon leaves at dawn.

The gamelan sounded.

The *bonang* chimed first -- a single, resonant tone that hung in the air like a star appearing in a dark sky. Then the *saron* answered, a lower register, steady as a heartbeat. Then the *kendang* drum, soft but insistent, the rhythm of marching, the rhythm of beginning. And over it all, the *rebab* drew its silver line of melody -- not the contemplative piece that had opened the council, but something older, deeper, the *Dangdanggula* melody taken up now by the instruments themselves, amplified, sanctified, until it became not music but invocation.

The nine stood in the center of the pendopo, and they were wildly, impossibly different. Four small Tuyul -- one frightened, one steadfast, one grinning, one blinking. One ancient sage leaning upon his staff. One exiled prince with a ranger's weathered face. One celestial warrior whose beauty was the beauty of mountains. One mountain chieftain built from stone and stubbornness. One proud general who had set aside his pride.

The mist of Dieng swirled around the open pillars, and for a moment -- just a moment -- the cloud parted above the pendopo, and a shaft of pale sunlight fell through the tiered roof and touched the stone floor at the center of the circle, and the nine stood in that light, and they cast nine shadows that stretched across the warm stone toward the nine cardinal directions of the Nusantara compass, and the gamelan played, and the *homa* fires burned at the four points of the mandala, and the keris lay upon its pedestal, dark and patient, waiting for the journey to begin.

Prabu Siliwangi spoke the final words.

'*Demi Sang Hyang Widhi — lumaku,*' he said. '*Lumaku kalayan berkah Pasundan, sareng sadaya rahayat merdéka Nusantara.*' (*By the Supreme God — go. Go with the blessing of Pasundan, and of all the free peoples of Nusantara.*) "Go with the strength of *gotong royong* -- the shared labor, the shared burden, the knowledge that no hand works alone. Go with the *dharma* of the righteous path, which no shadow can corrupt and no darkness can extinguish. The council is ended."

He paused, and when he spoke again, his voice was very quiet, meant for the nine alone.

"The quest has begun."
