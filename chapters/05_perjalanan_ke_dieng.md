# Bab 5: Perjalanan ke Dieng

## (The Journey to Dieng)

---

### I. The Long Road East

They walked for five days through the spine of Jawa, and the land changed beneath their feet as though the island itself were telling them they had left home behind.

On the first morning, the country was still kind. They descended from the beringin tree where they had sheltered and followed a narrow path along the lip of a river gorge, the water below rushing white over black volcanic stone. The highlands of Pasundan stretched behind them in layered terraces of green — *sawah* rice paddies climbing the hillsides in steps carved by generations of patient hands, each terrace a mirror of sky and cloud. Spice groves spread across the upper slopes like dark velvet, and the air carried the clean, sharp scent of wild pepper and cinnamon bark drying in the morning sun. This was still their country, still the land where Tuyul had lived and farmed and told stories under the stars since before the kingdoms rose.

Before they set foot upon the trail, Eyang Guru had instructed them in the old way. Bagus knelt at the forest's edge and placed a small *sesajen* — a woven bamboo tray bearing rice, flower petals, a sliver of palm sugar, and a pinch of kemenyan resin — upon a mossy stone. '*Awignam astu,*' he murmured, '*para karuhun nu ngajaga ieu wanantara, mugi pinareng idin sareng kasalametan*' (*Blessings upon us, ancestors who guard this deep wilderness, may we be granted permission and safety*). The offering was for the *karuhun* — the ancestral spirits who watched over the old paths — and for the forest spirits whose domain they were about to enter. It was *pamali*, forbidden, to enter sacred forest without asking leave.

Bagus led them. He moved through the forest with the sureness of one who had spent his whole life reading the language of trails — the bend of a fern indicating a water source, the particular hush of birds that meant a clearing ahead, the way certain mosses grew thicker on the sides of trees that faced the mountain streams. He carried their provisions on his broad back without complaint: a rolled *tikar* mat, a clay pot wrapped in banana leaves, a sack of rice that grew lighter each day. His golok, that heavy single-edged jungle blade, hung at his hip, its wooden handle worn smooth from years of use.

By the second day, they had climbed beyond the paddies into true *wanadri* — mountain rainforest, dense, ancient, cathedral-dark. The canopy closed above them like a green ceiling sixty meters high, and the light that filtered down was green and gold and strange. Lianas thick as a man's arm hung in spiraling loops from branches where orchids bloomed in silent extravagance — white and purple and a deep, almost obscene red. The air was thick with moisture and the scent of decomposition and growth intertwined, the twin breaths of the forest, death feeding life feeding death again. There was a presence in these ancient groves — the *bayu* of the living world, the breath-force that flowed through every root and vine and creature, as tangible as the humidity on their skin.

Bagus foraged as they walked. He dug wild *ubi* — yams with rough brown skin and pale, starchy flesh — from the soft earth near stream banks. He spotted a durian tree by its smell before any of them could see it, and they feasted on the pungent, custard-rich fruit, Dawala eating three whole segments and declaring it the finest meal of his life. He found salak hanging in low clusters, their reddish-brown snake-scale shells crackling open to reveal sweet, dry fruit inside. And from the barks and roots and leaves of a dozen plants whose names only he seemed to know, he brewed *jamu* — bitter, earthy herbal tonics that he made them all drink each morning.

"For strength," he said simply, pressing a bamboo cup into Kian Santang's hands.

Kian Santang drank. He tasted ginger, and turmeric, and something darker underneath — *temulawak*, perhaps, the Javanese wild ginger that the old healers used to purify the blood. The warmth spread through his stomach, but it could not reach the deeper cold that had settled in his chest since the night they fled. The keris hung at his belt in its sheath, wrapped in cloth, and still he could feel it — a constant, dull pressure, as though the blade were aware of him, as though it were listening.

He barely ate. The durian turned to ash on his tongue. The salak tasted of nothing. At night he lay on his back staring up through the canopy at the few stars visible between the leaves, and the stars shifted and swam and became red eyes, dozens of red eyes watching him from the darkness beyond the world, and a voice — not quite a voice, more a vibration in the bone, a humming in the blood — whispered things he could not quite make out but that left him shaking and cold when he woke.

The others noticed.

"Eat," Bagus said each morning, stern as a mother. Kian Santang took a few bites to satisfy him.

It was Cepot who fought the silence. Cepot, that impossible, talkative, sharp-witted creature, who seemed constitutionally incapable of letting a quiet moment survive. As they walked the narrow forest trails, he talked — and talked, and talked, and when even he ran out of his own observations, he turned to the old stories.

"Do you know," he said, stepping over a fallen log with an elaborate flourish, "that Semar was once a god? A true god — Sang Hyang Ismaya, brother of Sang Hyang Antaga, both of them sons of Sang Hyang Tunggal, the One Above All. But Semar chose to descend to earth. He chose to become ugly, fat, to look like a fool, so that he could walk among mortals and protect the Pandawa from harm. Imagine! A god who chose to look ridiculous. Now *that* is bravery. Forget your warriors with their muscles and their keris — the bravest thing anyone ever did was become a clown on purpose."

Dawala laughed — a bright, musical sound that scattered the birds in the canopy above. Even Bagus cracked a thin smile.

And Kian Santang, despite the weight on his chest, despite the whisper of the keris and the red eyes that haunted his sleep, felt the corner of his mouth lift. Just for a moment. Just enough.

"You see?" Cepot said, catching it. "The keris-bearer smiles. Write it down, Dawala, mark this date — I have achieved the impossible."

"You always say that," Dawala said.

"And I am always right."

Cepot fell quiet for perhaps thirty steps — a record of restraint — and then, as they crossed a stream over a fallen log slick with moss, he spoke again. "There is an old tale," he said, his voice shifting into the cadence of a *kawih bwatuha* — an ancient song — the rhythm he had learned from watching the *dalang* at village puppet shows, "of one called Lutung Kasarung — a prince cursed to wear the form of a black monkey, who proved his worth not through royal bearing but through patience and devotion."

Dawala's ears pricked. Even Bagus tilted his head, though he kept his eyes on the trail.

"Purbasari, the youngest princess, was cast out by her jealous sister Purbararang — stripped of her rank, her beauty marred by a curse, sent to live in the forest like an animal. And who came to her? Not a prince in golden armor. Not a warrior with a keris. A *lutung* — a black monkey, foul-smelling and ridiculous, who served her without asking for anything in return. He cleared the forest for her. He planted her a rice field overnight — the most beautiful *sawah* anyone had ever seen — while she slept. He proved that true *dharma*, true devotion, lives not in the form but in the heart."

He glanced at Kian Santang — a quick, sidelong look, sharper than his clowning usually permitted. "Nobility beneath a humble appearance, Kang. The gods love that trick. They play it all the time."

Kian Santang said nothing, but he felt the words settle into him alongside the cold, a small warmth, a seed.

On the third day the forest thinned, and they climbed. The air grew cooler, thinner. The trees were different here — shorter, wind-bent, their roots gripping volcanic soil that was dark and mineral-rich. They could see the peaks of the central volcanic chain rising above the forest line — Gunung Slamet to the east, vast and brooding, and closer, the elegant cone of Gunung Ciremai, its summit wreathed in cloud. Bagus paused at the ridge and murmured a prayer to the mountain spirit — '*Om Swasti Astu, Sang Hyang Giri, mugi berkahi lampah ingwang sadaya*' — the old invocation asking the mountain's blessing upon their passage. In the deep ravines, where the ancient jungle canopy stretched unbroken toward the distant blue ranges of Kalimantan — the great island of Borneo, where it was said the Kurcaci's distant kin had mined diamonds from rivers of black water since before the first *Saka* calendar was reckoned — the forest spirits were old and powerful, and no traveler passed without acknowledgment.

They were leaving Pasundan behind. Ahead lay the volcanic heartlands of central Jawa, and beyond that, if they survived, the high plateau of Dieng.

### II. The Dead Village

They found the village on the afternoon of the fourth day.

Or what had been a village. What was left could barely be called that.

It lay in a small valley between two forested ridges, a place where a stream widened into a natural clearing. There would have been perhaps twenty houses here — simple bamboo-frame structures with thatched *alang-alang* grass roofs, the kind of village that dotted the Javanese highlands like beads on a string, each one home to a few dozen families who farmed the terraces and tended their goats and lived their quiet, unremarkable lives.

Every house had been burned. Not just burned — torn apart, then burned. The bamboo frames were snapped like dry stalks, the thatch reduced to grey ash that stirred weakly in the wind. Pottery lay shattered across the ground. A loom, its frame cracked, still held the remnants of a half-woven cloth — a batik pattern, delicate brown and indigo, now stained with soot. A child's sandal, woven from pandanus leaf, lay in the middle of the path, abandoned. At the edge of the clearing, a small *sanggar* — a spirit shrine of stacked stones and weathered wood — had been toppled and trampled, its offerings of flowers and rice scattered in the mud. Even the *karuhun* had not been spared.

There were no bodies. That was almost worse. The absence pressed against them, a silence so heavy it had texture. But there were marks in the earth — deep gouges, wider than a man's hand, spaced the way footprints would be spaced if the creature making them were three meters tall and walked with the weight of a boulder. The tracks led from the forest on the north side and out again to the east, a path of destruction that had swept through the village like a river through a sand castle.

*Buta*.

Kian Santang knew the tracks from the old stories — the massive, flat-footed prints of the ogre-demons, children of darkness and volcanic rage, servants of the Shadow King. In the *wayang* tales, the Buta were grotesque and foolish, bellowing monsters that the heroes dispatched with ease. Standing in the ash of what they had done, with the smell of burned thatch and something underneath it — something older, sweeter, worse — filling his nostrils, Kian Santang understood for the first time how the wayang lied. How the *dalang* made monsters into comedy so that children would not scream.

This was not comedy. This was ash and silence and a child's sandal in the dirt.

Bagus crouched by one of the Buta tracks, his face set in hard lines. He touched the edge of the gouge, rubbed the soil between his fingers. "Three days old," he said quietly. "Maybe four. A raiding party — six Buta at least, maybe more. They came from the north." He looked east, where the tracks disappeared into the tree line. "Heading toward Ciremai."

Dawala stood very still, clutching his small rucksack to his chest with both hands. His round face, usually so open and cheerful, was drawn and pale. He did not speak. He looked at the child's sandal, and looked away, and looked back at it again, as though he were trying to understand something that refused to make sense.

Cepot, for once, said nothing. He walked slowly through the ruined village, his clever eyes taking in every detail — the scattered rice, the broken water jars, the scorch marks on the ground where something immensely hot had been pressed against the earth. He picked up a fragment of pottery, turned it over in his small hands, and set it down again gently, as though it were something sacred.

When he finally spoke, his voice was stripped of its usual theatricality. "We should go," he said simply.

They went.

Nobody told stories that evening. The jungle sounds filled the silence for them — the drone of cicadas, the distant call of a night-jar, the rustle of something moving through the undergrowth that might have been a mouse deer and might have been something else. Kian Santang lay awake long after the others slept, the keris pulsing against his hip like a second heartbeat, and in the red darkness behind his eyelids, he saw the village burning.

### III. Gunung Ciremai

They should not have stopped at the watchtower. Kian Santang would think this many times afterward — in the weeks and months that followed, in the long, pain-filled hours of his recovery — but thinking it changed nothing. They were exhausted. The slope of Gunung Ciremai was steep and the air was thin and night was falling, and when Bagus spotted the ruined stone structure on a rocky ledge above the tree line, it seemed like providence.

It was a *gardu jaga* — an old watchtower from the era of the Sunda Kingdom, built of fitted volcanic stone blocks that had endured where the mortar between them had not. Three walls still stood, chest-high on a man, and part of the floor remained — smooth flagstones carved with faint reliefs that centuries of rain and wind had worn almost to nothing. Moss covered everything. A ficus had rooted in one corner, its pale roots flowing over the stone like frozen water. At the base of the eastern wall, the faded outline of a *lingga-yoni* — the ancient symbol of Siwa's creative power, the union of heaven and earth — could still be discerned beneath the moss, testament to the Hindu kingdom that had once kept watch over these passes. From the ledge, they could see the dark forest falling away below them and, to the east, the distant suggestion of the central Javanese plains under a sky heavy with stars.

"Good sightlines," Bagus said approvingly. "We'll see anything coming up the slope."

"Anything we can see in the dark, which is nothing," Cepot observed, but he was already settling himself against a moss-covered wall with every appearance of comfort.

They built a small fire — too small, Kian Santang thought later, though it would not have mattered. Fire did not deter the Leak. They ate cold rice and the last of the wild yams, and Dawala fell asleep almost immediately, curled into a ball with his head on his rucksack, snoring softly. Bagus took first watch, sitting with his golok across his knees, his broad face turned toward the darkness below.

Kian Santang could not sleep. The keris was restless. He could feel it through the layers of cloth — a vibration, a heat, as though the metal were alive and agitated. The wound in his dreams was spreading into his waking hours now; even with his eyes open, he saw flickers at the edges of his vision — shapes that moved when he turned his head, shadows that were too dark, too deliberate. The stars above him blurred and sharpened and blurred again.

Midnight.

The keris screamed.

Not a sound — not truly. A frequency, a shuddering pulse of dark energy that Kian Santang felt in his teeth and his bones and the pit of his stomach. He sat bolt upright, his hand already on the hilt, and the world went silent. The cicadas stopped. The wind died. Even the fire seemed to flinch, its flames pulling inward as though trying to hide.

"Bagus—" Kian Santang began.

"I see them," Bagus whispered. He was on his feet, golok drawn, and his voice held something Kian Santang had never heard in it before: fear.

Three shapes were climbing the slope. They moved with terrible, unhurried purpose, shadows flowing up the volcanic rock face like dark water running uphill. Beneath them — around them — prowled their mounts: *Harimau Siluman*, spirit-tigers, massive and silent, their striped pelts rippling with a darkness that was not shadow but something deeper, an absence of light that seemed to drink the starlight around them. Their eyes burned amber in the darkness.

Three Leak riders.

Cepot was awake and scrambling backward, his usual wit replaced by a strangled sound that was not quite a word. Dawala woke, confused, terrified, pressing himself against the tower wall.

The keris pulsed. The keris demanded. Every fiber of Kian Santang's training — everything Eyang Guru had said, every warning — screamed at him not to draw the blade. But the Leak were coming, and his friends were unarmed against them, and the terror in his chest was a living thing, and in that moment of desperate, mindless fear, Kian Santang drew the Keris Murka from its sheath.

The world *changed*.

Color drained from everything — the fire went grey, the stone went grey, the stars became cold white points in a sky of ash. But the Leak — the Leak blazed into terrible clarity. They were not shadows now. The keris ripped away their concealment and showed Kian Santang what they truly were.

They had been kings.

He could see it — the remnants of what they had once been. Skeletal figures, taller than any living man, draped in the rotting remains of royal regalia. One wore a *makuta* crown of tarnished gold, its once-fine filigree bent and blackened. Another was wrapped in batik of extraordinary intricacy — a *parang rusak* pattern, the forbidden diagonal that only royalty could wear — but the cloth was faded to the color of dried blood, and it hung from the figure's frame like a shroud. The third wore armor of an ancient style, bronze plates laced with cord that had rotted to threads.

Their faces were their masks. The hollow-eyed, gaping-mouthed visages of the *topeng* Leak were not coverings but the actual contours of their skulls, frozen in expressions of agony — mouths stretched wide in screams that had lasted centuries, eye sockets burning with the pale fire of souls that could not die and could not rest. They were hideous beyond anything the *wayang* stories had prepared him for. They were pitiable beyond words.

And they could see *him*.

Through the keris's power, the veil between worlds was torn both ways. The three Leak turned their burning gazes upon Kian Santang with an intensity that was almost hunger, almost recognition. He felt the keris vibrate in his hand — felt it *reaching* toward them, drawn to the shadow keris each Leak carried, the lesser blades calling to their master's weapon like iron filings to a lodestone.

The nearest Leak surged forward. It moved impossibly fast — one moment at the edge of the ledge, the next inside the watchtower, its shadow keris raised high. Kian Santang tried to move, tried to raise the Keris Murka in defense, but his body was sluggish, dreamlike, as though the air had turned to water.

The shadow keris came down in a slashing arc and bit into his left shoulder.

Kian Santang screamed.

Cold. Cold beyond cold — not the cold of ice or mountain wind or river water, but the cold of *absence*, the cold of a soul being pulled from its moorings. It poured into the wound like liquid darkness, spreading down his arm, up his neck, reaching for his heart. He could feel his spirit flickering like a candle in a gale. The whispers he had been hearing for days erupted into a roaring chorus — *submit, yield, kneel, the blade is not yours it was never yours give it back give it BACK* —

He fell. The flagstones hit his back. The grey sky wheeled above him. The three Leak loomed, skeletal kings bending over a fallen child, and the shadow keris rose again—

### IV. The Ranger

Light.

Not the cold light of the Leak's eyes or the grey wash of the keris's revelation — but *warm* light, golden light, a flash that split the darkness like a blade through silk.

A figure erupted into the watchtower from the slope below — a man, tall, moving with a speed and fluidity that seemed impossible, as though his body were water taking human form. He held a keris in his right hand — a long, slender blade, eleven *luk* waves in its metal, and it caught the firelight and blazed.

His left hand was open, fingers splayed, and from his palm came a pulse of energy — *sakti*, the inner force, the spiritual power that the masters of *pencak silat* cultivated through decades of meditation and training. It struck the nearest Leak like a physical blow, hurling the skeletal king backward off its feet.

The man did not stop. He flowed into the space between the Leak and the fallen Tuyul with the effortless precision of the *aliran Mataram* — the fighting style of the old Mataram kingdom, which moved like river water: soft where the enemy was hard, hard where the enemy was soft, always turning, always redirecting, never meeting force with force but channeling it, reshaping it, sending it back.

The second Leak lunged. The man stepped aside — a half-turn, his bare foot pivoting on the flagstone — and his keris drew a line of golden light across the creature's arm. The Leak shrieked, a sound like tearing metal, and recoiled. The third Leak circled, and its *Harimau Siluman* mount snarled and leaped. The man dropped beneath the tiger's lunge with an impossible limberness, rose, and drove his elbow into the beast's flank with a strike that crackled with golden light. The spirit-tiger screamed and dissolved into black vapor.

The three Leak regrouped at the edge of the ledge. The man stood between them and the Tuyul, keris raised, his free hand glowing with faint golden radiance. He breathed steadily, easily, as though this were a morning exercise and not a battle against three undead kings.

"*Demi Sang Hyang Widhi* — pergi," he said. *By the Supreme God — go*. His voice was low and rough and carried an authority that was not volume but conviction. "Malam ini bukan malam kalian." *Tonight is not your night.*

The Leak hissed — a sound like steam escaping from volcanic rock. They lingered, their burning eyes fixed on Kian Santang where he lay, on the Keris Murka still clutched in his hand. The man took one step forward, and his sakti flared — brighter now, a pulse of golden energy that pushed the darkness back like sunrise.

The Leak turned. Their Harimau Siluman materialized beneath them, and they rode into the darkness, down the slope, away. The night swallowed them. The stars returned. The cicadas, tentatively, began again.

Bagus was on his knees beside Kian Santang. "He's cold," he said. "He's cold as stone. The wound—"

The man knelt swiftly. He sheathed his keris and placed his hand on Kian Santang's shoulder, and Kian Santang felt the warmth of that touch through layers of cold, faint and far away, like hearing music from the bottom of a well. The man's face swam above him — sharp features, dark eyes that were fierce and gentle at once, skin weathered by years of sun and wind and solitary roads. He wore a plain *baju hitam*, a black tunic of rough cotton, and his dark hair was tied back simply. He carried himself the way stone carries weight — without effort, without complaint, as though he had been built for it.

He tore away the fabric around the wound. Kian Santang heard Bagus draw a sharp breath. He could not see it himself, but he could feel it — the wound was not bleeding. It was worse than bleeding. From the slash on his shoulder, fine lines of darkness spread outward beneath the skin, like the roots of a black tree growing into his flesh.

The man reached into a leather pouch at his belt and drew out handfuls of crushed leaves and bark — Kian Santang caught the sharp scent of *daun sirih*, betel leaf, and something earthier, richer, a root he did not recognize. The man packed the herbs into the wound, pressed down hard, and spoke.

Not words — not at first. A *mantra*, a Javanese healing chant, spoken in the low, resonant cadence of old court language — the ancient Sanskrit-laced syllables that the *pandita* healers had passed down through generations since the age of the Hindu kingdoms. '*Om Dhanvantari namaha*,' the man intoned, invoking the divine healer, and the sacred syllables were unfamiliar, ancient, but Kian Santang felt them working — felt them enter his body through the wound like warm water into frozen earth. The spreading darkness paused. It did not retreat, but it stopped advancing. The roaring whispers in his mind quieted to a murmur.

"It will hold," the man said. "For a time."

He bound the wound with strips of cloth torn from his own tunic, working quickly and competently.

"Who are you?" Bagus asked. His golok was still in his hand, and his voice held the particular wariness of a man who has just been rescued and is not yet certain by whom.

The man looked at him. A half-smile crossed his face — there and gone, a flash of warmth in an otherwise severe expression. "My name is Raden Wijaya. Eyang Guru Batara sent me."

### V. By the Dying Fire

The fire had burned low. Raden Wijaya fed it with dry wood and bark until it crackled again, and in its orange light, the four Tuyul looked very small and very young.

Kian Santang lay wrapped in the *tikar* mat, the Keris Murka — sheathed now, the man had insisted on sheathing it immediately, his hands firm and unafraid — beside him on the stone. The cold was still in his shoulder, a deep, patient ache that whispered at the edges of his consciousness, but the herbs and the mantra held it at bay. He was awake, though his vision blurred at the edges.

Raden Wijaya sat across the fire from them, cross-legged, his long keris laid across his knees. He said little. When he spoke, it was in short, direct sentences, as though words were a resource he was unwilling to waste.

"Eyang Guru sent me three days after you left Pasundan," he said. "I have been walking the roads between here and the Dieng Plateau, watching for you. Watching for them." He nodded toward the darkness where the Leak had vanished. "There are more of them now than there were a month ago. The Shadow King stirs. His Buta range further every week. The Leak ride openly." He looked at the keris beside Kian Santang. "They are drawn to the blade. The closer you carry it to civilized lands, the more of them will come."

"And you?" Cepot asked. His voice was careful, measuring. "Who are you, Raden? Rangers don't fight like that. Rangers don't carry *sakti* that burns gold."

Raden Wijaya looked at Cepot for a long moment. "I am what Eyang Guru needs me to be," he said. "A guardian of the wild places. A watcher on the old roads." He paused. '*Ayo dulur, ora usah kuwatir*,' he added quietly — *Come on, brothers, do not worry* — the Javanese falling from his lips in the easy *ngoko* register of one warrior speaking to comrades. "My ancestors were kings of Mataram. I am not a king. I am a man with a keris and a duty."

He did not elaborate. He turned instead to Kian Santang.

"The wound is serious," he said, and his voice softened. "The Leak's shadow keris has planted a shard of cold in you — a fragment of their master's will. It will grow. It is growing now. You can feel it."

Kian Santang nodded. He could feel it — a numbness spreading from his shoulder down his left arm, cold fingers reaching toward his heart. And with the cold came visions: a throne of obsidian in a vast red hall, a figure of smoke and wrath offering him a crown, a voice that said *why carry what you could command?* The visions were seductive. That was the horror. They were not nightmares. They were invitations.

"If we do not reach Dieng within two days," Raden Wijaya said, "the cold will reach his heart, and he will be beyond any healing I can give. Only Prabu Siliwangi has the power to draw out a Leak shard. He waits at Dieng with Eyang Guru. The king has prepared the *tirta* — sacred water drawn from the holy springs — and the old healing mantras. It is the only way."

"Two days?" Bagus said. "Dieng is three days' march from here at best."

Raden Wijaya stood. "Then we do not march," he said. "We run."

---

### VI. The Race to Dieng

They ran.

Through the rest of that night and all the next day and into the following night, they ran and walked and staggered and ran again, and the world became a fever-dream of volcanic landscape and the sound of their own ragged breathing.

Raden Wijaya set the pace, and the pace was brutal. He led them down from Gunung Ciremai by a path that no map recorded — a smuggler's trail, perhaps, or an older thing, a route remembered by the land itself, worn into the volcanic rock by centuries of feet now forgotten. They descended into a valley where sulfurous hot springs steamed between boulders crusted with yellow mineral deposits, and the air stank of rotten eggs, and the water in the streams was warm to the touch and tasted of metal. Fumaroles hissed from cracks in the earth, sending up pale columns of steam that drifted across the trail like ghosts.

Kian Santang walked as long as he could. The cold in his shoulder spread with each hour — down his arm to his fingertips, up his neck toward his jaw. His left hand went numb first, then his forearm. The world took on a shimmer, a doubling, as though he were seeing two realities layered atop each other: the volcanic trail and, superimposed upon it, a darker place, a throne room of black stone where something vast and terrible waited with the patience of a mountain. In his delirium, the boundary between the *Triloka* — the three realms of existence, upper world and middle world and underworld — seemed to dissolve, and he walked through all three at once.

When he stumbled for the third time and did not rise, Raden Wijaya picked him up without a word and carried him. The Tuyul was small — barely reaching the man's chest when standing — and Raden Wijaya bore his weight as though it were nothing, striding up volcanic slopes with Kian Santang cradled against his chest, the keris between them, its dark energy pulsing against the ranger's sakti like two heartbeats out of rhythm.

Bagus carried the provisions. Cepot and Dawala walked side by side, too exhausted for conversation, their small legs churning over the rough terrain. Dawala, who had never walked more than half a day in his life before this journey, moved in grim silence, his round face set in an expression of determination that would have been comical in other circumstances and was, in these, quietly heroic.

Cepot, even at the edge of his endurance, found words. "One foot," he muttered, more to himself than anyone. "Then the other foot. Then the first foot again. It's not complicated. Semar walked further than this, and he was twice as fat."

The landscape changed. They climbed out of the sulfurous valley and into a high, rolling plateau, and the world became something from a dream — or a myth. The Dieng Plateau opened before them in the pre-dawn half-light, and all four Tuyul — even Kian Santang, fading as he was — saw it and were struck silent.

It was a high tableland, ringed by the dark silhouettes of ancient volcanic peaks that rose from the mist like the stumps of mountains that had once been taller, grander, closer to heaven. The ground was warm beneath their feet — geothermal heat seeping up through the thin soil, so that even in the cool mountain air, the earth itself was alive with gentle warmth. Mist lay across the plateau in sheets and ribbons, and through the mist, the ruins of ancient *candi* emerged like stone flowers — Hindu temples from an age so distant that their builders' names had been lost to history, their carved facades worn smooth by centuries of rain and wind but still standing, still beautiful, still sacred.

They passed Candi Arjuna and Candi Semar — small, elegant temples with tiered roofs of carved stone, their walls adorned with reliefs of gods and heroes that the moss had half-consumed. Stone *naga* serpents flanked the entrance stairs, their hoods spread in eternal vigilance, and above the doorways the fierce face of *kala*, the time-devourer, gazed down upon all who sought entry. The temples stood in a clearing of short, tough grass, and the mist curled around their bases, and they seemed less like buildings than like prayers made solid — stone voices speaking to a sky that might or might not be listening.

And the lakes. *Telaga Warna* — the Colored Lake — appeared through a break in the mist, and Kian Santang, barely conscious, opened his eyes and saw it and felt his breath catch despite everything. The lake's surface was a mosaic of impossible color: turquoise and emerald and gold and a deep, luminous violet, the colors shifting and blending as the mineral-rich water caught the first light of dawn. Steam rose from its surface in delicate wisps. The reflection of the surrounding peaks trembled on the water like the memory of a painting.

"Almost there," Raden Wijaya said. His voice was tight. Kian Santang could hear the strain in it now — not physical exhaustion, though the man had carried him for hours, but urgency, the contained fear of someone who knows the margin is razor-thin.

Kian Santang tried to speak. The cold had reached his chest. He could feel it pressing against his heart — not pain exactly, but a pressure, a constriction, as though something were wrapping itself around the core of him. The whispers were louder now, nearly coherent: *you are already mine, little bearer, you have been mine since you touched the blade, since before you touched the blade, since before you were born* — and beneath the whispers, a deeper sound, a pulse, the heartbeat of something immense and ancient stirring in its volcanic sleep.

"The keris," he managed. "It's... calling..."

"I know," Raden Wijaya said. "Hold on. Do not listen. Think of your home. Think of the paddy fields. Think of your uncle's face."

Kian Santang tried. He thought of Uncle Kian's workshop, the smell of iron and coconut oil, the sound of the hammer on the anvil. He thought of Bagus splitting firewood in the morning light, of Cepot's ridiculous laugh, of Dawala stuffing his face with *ketupat* rice cakes at the harvest festival. He held these images like a man holding a candle in a hurricane, and the darkness pressed in from every side, and the candle flickered, and flickered, and held.

### VII. The Gate of Dieng

The sun rose.

It came up over the eastern peaks in a blaze of gold and amber, and the mist on the Dieng Plateau caught the light and turned to luminous gauze, and through it — through the golden haze of dawn on this high, sacred, impossible place — Kian Santang saw the great temple complex.

It rose from the plateau like something that had grown rather than been built — a central *candi* of dark volcanic stone, taller and grander than the smaller temples they had passed, its tiered roof climbing toward the sky in diminishing steps, each level carved with scenes from the ancient epics: the Ramayana, the Mahabharata, the stories that had shaped the soul of Nusantara for a thousand years. Smaller temples flanked it on either side, and the whole complex was set within a stone wall that was itself carved with patterns of lotus flowers and *naga* serpents and the repeating geometric precision of the old sacred geometries.

It was beautiful. It was beyond beautiful. It was *sanctified* — a place where the boundary between the visible world and the world of spirit had been worn thin by centuries of prayer and offering and devoted attention, until the two worlds overlapped like hands pressed together in prayer. Kian Santang could feel it even through the cold — a warmth that was not thermal but spiritual, a radiance that pushed against the darkness in his chest the way dawn pushes against night.

And at the gate, two figures stood.

Eyang Guru Batara was the first — unmistakable, his white robes bright against the dark stone, his silver hair lifting in the mountain wind, his ancient face creased with an expression that held worry and relief and fierce, complicated love. He leaned on his staff of ironwood, and his eyes found Kian Santang in Raden Wijaya's arms, and his lips moved in what might have been a prayer — '*Om Swasti Astu, Sang Hyang Widhi, sinindungan anak ieu*' — or simply a name spoken too softly to hear.

Beside him stood a figure Kian Santang had never seen but recognized immediately, the way one recognizes a mountain or a river — not from memory but from the simple, undeniable fact of presence. He was old, older even than Eyang Guru, but where the sage's age was weathered and sharp, this man's age was *composed* — layered, settled, like sediment becoming stone. He was tall for a Sundanese, and he stood with the straight-backed dignity of one who had borne the weight of a kingdom and set it down without losing his posture. His robes were white and gold, simple but immaculate. His face was broad and kind and carried the particular serenity of someone who has seen the worst that the world can offer and chosen, deliberately and with full knowledge, to remain gentle.

Prabu Siliwangi. The wise king of Pasundan. The last of the great rulers of the old alliance, living now in exile among the temples of Dieng, holding the light against the gathering dark.

"Bring him to me," said Prabu Siliwangi, and his voice was deep and kind, the voice of a man accustomed to being obeyed not through fear but through trust. "The shadow has not yet reached his heart."

Raden Wijaya climbed the last steps and carried Kian Santang through the temple gate. The warmth of the sacred ground rose through the stone, through Raden Wijaya's body, into Kian Santang's own fading flesh. He felt gentle hands receive him — many hands, warm hands — and he was laid upon smooth stone that held the heat of the earth itself.

Above him, Prabu Siliwangi's face appeared, and the old king placed his palm on Kian Santang's forehead, and the touch was like sunlight, like the first warm rain after a long drought, like coming home. '*Om Tri Murti namaha*,' the king murmured — the invocation of the three supreme gods, Brahma the creator, Wisnu the preserver, Siwa the transformer — and the ancient Sanskrit syllables carried a power older than any kingdom, older than the stone temples themselves.

The keris screamed.

Not the dark pulse of before — a true scream, a shriek of thwarted malice, as the sacred energy of Dieng and the ancient power of Prabu Siliwangi's touch met the cold darkness in Kian Santang's shoulder and *opposed* it. The two forces clashed inside him, and Kian Santang arched his back and cried out, and for one terrible moment the cold surged — *you are MINE* — and the warmth answered — *he is not* — and then—

Then the darkness softened. Not defeated. Not driven out. But held. Contained. Pushed back from his heart, back toward the wound, back into the shard that the Leak's blade had planted.

Kian Santang's vision blurred. The golden light of dawn, the carved stone of the temple, the faces of his friends — Bagus, steady and exhausted; Cepot, grey-faced but still standing; Dawala, tears on his round cheeks — all of it swam and faded. He heard the keris whispering still, but faintly now, as though from a great distance, muffled by the sacred warmth of this place.

He heard Eyang Guru's voice, very close: "Rest now, child."

He heard Prabu Siliwangi, further away, speaking to Raden Wijaya: "You brought him in time. Barely, but in time."

He heard Bagus say, "Will he live?"

He did not hear the answer.

Darkness took him — but it was a different darkness now. Not the cold, grasping dark of the Leak's wound or the red-eyed void of the keris's whispers. This was warm darkness, soft darkness, the darkness of a child falling asleep in a safe place after a long and terrible day. The darkness of healing. The darkness of hands that hold you while the broken things inside you slowly, slowly begin to mend.

Kian Santang slept.

Above him, the sun continued to rise over Dieng, and the ancient temples stood in the golden light as they had stood for a thousand years, patient and sacred and enduring, and the mist burned away, and the colored lake shimmered, and somewhere in the volcanic depths far to the west, in the molten heart of Krakatau, something stirred and seethed and whispered the bearer's name.

But that was far away. And this was Dieng.

And for now — for this one fragile, precious moment — the light held.
