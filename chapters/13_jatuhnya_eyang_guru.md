# Bab 13: Jatuhnya Eyang Guru
## (The Fall of the Elder)

---

The mouth of the cave opened onto the southern coast of Jawa like a wound in the side of the world, and the light that poured through it was so bright, so mercilessly vivid after the hours of darkness underground, that Kian Santang stumbled on the threshold and fell to his knees on the black volcanic sand.

He did not get up.

Behind him, the others emerged one by one from the limestone dark -- eight figures stepping into the salt wind and the crashing white noise of the Indian Ocean, blinking against the sun like creatures born from stone. The sea cliffs of the southern coast rose on either side of the cave mouth in sheer walls of dark basalt, two hundred meters of vertical rock draped with ferns and wind-bent pandanus trees that clung to the stone with the desperate tenacity of living things that had chosen an impossible place to grow. Below, the ocean threw itself against the cliff base in great rolling detonations of white water, the waves arriving from the open Indian Ocean with the accumulated force of a thousand kilometers of unbroken fetch, and the sound of their breaking filled the air with a continuous, thundering exhalation that was less a noise than a pressure -- a weight upon the chest, a vibration in the teeth and bones.

Kian Santang knelt on the black sand and stared at the cave mouth behind him and waited for the ninth figure to emerge.

He waited.

He waited until Bagus knelt beside him and placed a hand on his shoulder, and he waited still, and the cave remained dark, and the ninth figure did not come.

---

Eyang Guru Batara was gone.

The knowledge settled over the fellowship like ash from a distant eruption -- slow, silent, suffocating. Each of them had seen it. Each of them carried the final image branded into the dark behind their eyes: the old sage standing on the stone bridge above the chasm, his teak staff blazing with white fire, the Naga Basukih coiled beneath him in a fury of scales and flame and ancient wrath. The bridge cracking. The stone giving way. And then the fall -- the sage and the serpent locked together, plunging into the abyss in a spiral of fire and shadow that shrank and shrank and vanished into a darkness so deep it seemed to have no bottom, only a silence that swallowed the last echoes of the old man's voice and gave nothing back.

He had spoken one word as he fell. One word, hurled upward against gravity and fire like a stone thrown at the heavens.

*Pergi.*

Go.

And they had gone. Raden Wijaya had seized Kian Santang by the collar and dragged him bodily from the edge of the chasm, and the fellowship had run -- stumbling, weeping, choking on sulfurous air -- through the last tunnels of Gunung Sewu until the light of the southern coast broke over them and the sea wind struck their faces and the world reassembled itself around them in all its cruel, indifferent beauty.

And Eyang Guru Batara was not among them.

His atma -- his eternal soul, the spark of the divine that had animated his mortal form for centuries upon centuries -- had departed. The ancient teachings of the Weda spoke of this: the body is the chariot, the atma is the rider, and when the chariot breaks, the rider does not perish but passes onward, carried by the current of karma through the great wheel of samsara toward whatever birth or liberation awaited. Eyang Guru had lived many lives, had accumulated merit through ages of service and sacrifice and the patient tending of the sacred flame. His karma was a river of gold. Wherever the wheel carried him -- to the celestial realms of Swarga-loka, to rebirth in a form worthy of his accumulated wisdom, or to the final liberation, the moksha that released the atma from the cycle entirely -- he had earned his passage. He had paid in full.

But the knowledge of this did not ease the grief. Understanding the theology of death is not the same as surviving the death of someone you love. The chariot was broken, and the rider was gone, and the road ahead was darker for his absence.

---

They walked west along the coast because Raden Wijaya said to walk west, and because none of them could think of a reason not to, and because standing still felt like drowning.

The southern coast of Jawa was a landscape designed by a god who understood both grandeur and cruelty -- who knew that beauty, applied at the wrong moment, could cut deeper than any blade. The sea cliffs marched westward in a procession of dark stone, their faces carved by centuries of wave and wind into arches and pillars and cathedral spaces where the ocean boomed and echoed. Black sand beaches opened between the headlands, narrow crescents of volcanic grit strewn with driftwood and the bleached shells of nautilus and giant clam. Coconut palms lined the shore, their trunks curved by the relentless ocean wind into postures of permanent supplication, their fronds rattling in the salt breeze with a dry, papery sound that was almost like speech. The sky was immense -- a dome of equatorial blue unmarked by cloud, and beneath it the Indian Ocean stretched to the horizon in a sheet of hammered silver that hurt the eyes.

It was beautiful. It was unbearable.

They walked in silence for the first two hours. The kind of silence that is not the absence of words but the presence of something too large for words to hold -- a silence that pressed outward against the skin, that made the chest tight and the throat raw, that sat behind the eyes like a stone too heavy to weep around. Each of them carried it differently. Each of them grieved in the particular, irreducible language of their own heart.

Kian Santang walked at the rear of the column, and the keris on his back was a leaden weight.

He had not spoken since the cave. His face, sun-darkened and drawn, wore an expression that was not grief exactly but something adjacent to it -- a blankness, a withdrawal, as though the part of him that felt things had retreated deep inside and pulled the shutters closed. He walked with the mechanical steadiness of a man whose body has learned to function in the absence of his will, his bare feet marking the black sand with prints that the incoming tide would erase within the hour. He did not look at the sea. He did not look at the sky. He looked at the sand directly before his feet, and he walked, and the keris whispered.

It had been whispering since the bridge. Since the moment Eyang Guru fell, the blade had found a crack in Kian Santang's defenses -- a fissure opened by grief, widened by guilt, and the whisper poured into it like water into limestone, patient and corrosive and inexorable.

*He died for nothing.*

The thought arrived with the keris's particular signature -- not quite a voice, not quite his own thought, but something between, something that occupied the border between suggestion and certainty the way twilight occupies the border between day and night. It felt true. That was the keris's genius. It did not lie. It took the truths you were most afraid of and held them up to the light and said: *Look. You already know this.*

*He came on this quest because of you. He left his sanctuary, his manuscripts, his quiet garden on the Dieng plateau, and he followed you into the dark because you were too young and too small and too weak to carry what you carry. And now he is dead, and you are alive, and the keris is still on your back, and nothing has changed except that the wisest man in Nusantara is gone and you remain.*

Kian Santang's hands trembled at his sides. He pressed them against his thighs and willed them still.

*You will fail. You know this. You have always known this. A Tuyul farmer carrying the Shadow King's blade to the fires of Krakatau -- it is a story that ends only one way. Eyang Guru knew it. That is why he looked so sad when he thought you were not watching. He saw the ending. He knew you would not be enough.*

"Kang."

Bagus's voice. Quiet. Close.

Kian Santang looked up and found his friend walking beside him -- not ahead, not behind, but precisely alongside, matching his pace step for step, his broad, sturdy body a warm presence at Kian Santang's left shoulder. Bagus did not speak again. He did not ask what was wrong, did not offer comfort, did not attempt to fill the silence with words that would have been inadequate and would have known themselves to be inadequate. He simply walked. He was simply there.

In Bagus's hands was a bamboo water container, its cap removed. He held it out without comment.

Kian Santang took it. The water was warm from the sun, tasting of bamboo and faintly of the mineral springs where it had been filled, and it moved down his throat and into his body, and it was not enough, and it was everything.

At midday, Bagus built a small fire in the shelter of a sea cliff and cooked rice in a clay pot with dried fish and a handful of wild kangkung he had pulled from a stream bank as they passed. He set the food before Kian Santang on a banana leaf, arranged with the quiet care of a *sesajen* -- an offering laid at a temple altar -- and sat beside him and ate his own portion and said nothing. Kian Santang managed four mouthfuls. Bagus did not count them, did not urge more, did not display the tender ferocity of earlier journeys when he had scolded and cajoled and mothered. He understood -- with the wordless intelligence that was his deepest gift -- that this grief was not the kind that could be fed away. He could only be present. He could only make sure that the water was offered and the rice was warm and the fire did not go out.

It was enough. It was the bravest thing Bagus had ever done, and he did not know it was brave, and that was why it was.

---

Cepot was silent.

This fact alone told the fellowship more about the depth of their loss than any eulogy could have. Cepot -- who had talked without ceasing through five days of mountain trail and three days of underground passage, who had filled every silence with stories and every story with commentary and every commentary with jokes that were terrible and necessary and sometimes, despite everything, genuinely funny -- Cepot walked the black sand coast with his mouth closed and his clever eyes fixed on the middle distance, and the silence that came from him was so unnatural, so profoundly wrong, that it made the others' grief sharper by contrast, the way a single broken string makes the dissonance of an entire gamelan impossible to ignore.

On the afternoon of the first day, Dawala fell into step beside him.

Dawala had been crying. He had been crying since they emerged from the cave, openly and without shame, the tears cutting channels through the dust and ash on his round, gentle face. He wept the way he did everything -- fully, without reservation, with the whole of his considerable heart committed to the act. He had loved Eyang Guru's stories. In the evenings around campfires in Gunung Sewu, the old sage had told tales of the first age -- of Bidadari descending from the clouds, of Kurcaci forging the first gongs in the heart of the mountains, of Tuyul *wira* -- heroes -- who had stood against darkness with nothing but courage and cleverness and the stubbornness of the earth itself -- and Dawala had listened with his mouth open and his eyes shining, and he had asked questions that the others were too proud to ask, and the old man had answered every one with the patience of someone who understood that curiosity was sacred.

Now Dawala walked beside Cepot, his face wet, his breath hitching in the small, regular spasms of someone who has cried so long that the crying has become automatic, and he said, "*Rahayu, Kang Cepot?*" The gentle Sundanese *basa loma* of one friend to another. *Blessings -- are you all right, Kang Cepot?*

Cepot did not answer for a long time. Long enough that Dawala began to wonder if he had been heard. Then Cepot spoke, and his voice was stripped of its usual theatrical polish, bare and rough as unfinished wood.

"Even Semar wept when the Pandawa fell."

Dawala looked at him.

"In the Bharatayudha," Cepot said, and his voice was quiet in a way that Dawala had never heard it, "when the great warriors die on the battlefield of Kurusetra -- Bima and Arjuna and Nakula and Sadewa, one by one, each one a hero, each one beloved -- Semar watches. The clown-god. The divine fool. He watches them fall, and he weeps. Not because he does not understand death -- he is a god, he understands everything -- but because understanding does not help. Knowledge does not blunt grief. You can know that the atma passes onward, that the wheel of samsara turns, that the karma of a good life carries the soul toward light -- and still feel the thread snap when a good man falls into the dark."

He looked at Dawala, and his eyes -- those sharp, knowing, comedian's eyes that missed nothing and mocked everything -- were wet.

"*Jadi nya, Dawala. Kami oge ceurik. Catetkeun.*" In the easy, unadorned Sundanese of their shared childhood. *So yes, Dawala. Even I weep. Mark it down.*

Dawala took Cepot's hand, and Cepot let him, and they walked that way for a while -- two small Tuyul on a vast black beach, hand in hand, the ocean crashing to their left and the cliffs rising to their right and the sky above them blue and enormous and indifferent.

---

Raden Wijaya led.

He moved at the front of the column with the grim, efficient stride of a man who has set grief aside the way a soldier sets aside a wound -- not because it does not hurt, but because others are watching, and they need to see someone who is not falling apart. His face was stone. His dark eyes swept the trail ahead, the cliff tops, the tree line, reading the landscape for threats with the automatic vigilance that decades of rangership had burned into his nervous system. He chose their path. He set their pace. He decided when they stopped and when they moved and where they sheltered for the night. He made decisions because decisions needed to be made, and because if he stopped making them, he would have to feel what he was not allowing himself to feel, and the feeling would be large enough to swallow him.

At night, when the others slept in the shelter of an overhanging cliff -- their bodies curled on the black sand, the fire burning low, the ocean's breathing filling the darkness with its constant, restless rhythm -- Raden Wijaya sat apart.

He chose a flat stone at the cliff's edge, where the sea spray reached him in a fine, salt mist, and he sat with his back straight and his hands on his knees and his face turned toward the open ocean. He did not speak. He did not move. He watched the sea, and the sea watched him back -- that dark, heaving expanse of the Indian Ocean, lightless except for the thin curve of the moon's reflection on the swell, and in its darkness and its vastness and its absolute indifference to human grief, he found something that was not comfort but was at least proportion. The sea did not care that Eyang Guru Batara was dead. The sea did not care about the keris or the quest or the fate of Nusantara. The sea simply was -- enormous, ancient, continuing -- and in its continuation there was a truth that Raden Wijaya needed: the world goes on. It goes on without the people you love. It goes on without you. And the duty remains.

He sat at the cliff's edge until the moon set, and then he returned to the camp and lay down in the dark, and if his breathing was uneven for a while before sleep came, no one was awake to hear it.

---

In the dim light of the second evening, a sound rose above the ocean's voice -- thin, clear, almost unbearably beautiful.

Dewi Anjani was singing.

She stood at the water's edge where the waves spent themselves in thin, hissing sheets of foam on the black sand, her bare feet in the retreating water, her face turned toward the open sea. She sang a *Mijil* -- a *kawih sasambatan*, the ancient lament song, the pupuh of sorrow, the form that the Sundanese reserved for grief and loss and the aching beauty of things that have passed from the world. Its structure was spare and descending: ten syllables, then six, then ten, then ten, then six, then six -- each line stepping downward like a mourner descending temple stairs, each phrase shorter than the one before, as though the song itself were diminishing, fading, following the beloved into the silence. The melody mimicked tears falling -- a high note breaking, sliding downward through quarter-tones and microtones that no tempered scale could capture, each descent a small death, each return to the higher register a breath drawn against the weight of sorrow.

*Mijil ing ratri kang sepi,*
*tanpa sang guru,*
*cahya geni suci ical,*
*kantun kenangan ing ati,*
*sira wus mulih,*
*mulih mring luhur.*

*Emerging in the silent night,*
*without the teacher,*
*the sacred fire's light has vanished,*
*only memory remains in the heart,*
*you have returned,*
*returned to the heights above.*

The words were in a dialect so ancient that even its vowels had changed, but the meaning lived in the melody rather than the language, and each of them understood it in their own way. It was a song about light. About the way light enters a dark room through a crack in the wall and falls across the floor in a thin, bright line, and how that line of light is enough -- not to banish the darkness, but to prove that darkness is not all there is. It was a song about the persistence of good things in a world that destroys them. About memory as a form of defiance. About the atma's journey beyond the veil of the mortal world, ascending through the realms toward the light that had always been its home.

One by one, the fellowship stopped what they were doing and turned toward the sound.

Bagus, stirring the evening's rice, let the wooden spoon rest against the rim of the pot. Cepot sat down on the sand and drew his knees to his chest. Dawala's tears, which had finally slowed, began again -- but differently now, not the tears of devastation but the tears that come when grief is given a shape, a container, a melody that says: *Yes. This is real. This happened. And it mattered.*

Even Gajahmada, who had been pacing the tree line with the coiled restlessness of a general without an army, went still.

Kian Santang listened, and the keris on his back fell silent. Not quiet -- silent. As though the blade, for all its ancient malice, recognized something in the Bidadari's Mijil that it could not corrupt, could not twist, could not whisper against. The pupuh moved through the salt air and through his grief, and it did not dissolve the grief -- nothing could dissolve it, nothing should -- but it gave the grief room to breathe. It made the weight bearable for the length of a song. The descending melody, each phrase falling like tears, spoke the truth of loss without flinching from it -- and in that honesty, in the Mijil's refusal to console or prettify, there was a strange and terrible comfort.

Tumenggung Batu sat apart from the others, his broad back against a boulder, his thick fingers working at something in his lap. He had been working at it since the cave mouth -- a piece of grey limestone, dense and fine-grained, that he had picked up from the beach on the first morning. His tools were a small iron chisel and a hammer no larger than his fist, and he worked with the focused, meditative precision of a master craftsman for whom creation was not an act of will but of listening -- the stone telling his hands what it wanted to become.

It was becoming a staff. A miniature teak staff, no longer than a man's hand, carved with such fidelity that the grain of the wood was rendered in stone -- each whorl and ridge and knot reproduced in grey limestone with an attention to detail that bordered on devotion. At its crown, where Eyang Guru's staff had borne the carved head of a garuda with folded wings, Tumenggung Batu's chisel was shaping the same figure -- the eagle, wings tucked, head bowed, an expression of fierce, patient vigilance carved into its tiny stone face.

This was how the Kurcaci grieved. They made things. Where other peoples wept or sang or sat in silence, the Kurcaci took stone and metal and wood and shaped them into memorials -- small, precise, imperishable objects that held the memory of the dead the way a warangka holds a blade. Every Kurcaci carried a collection of such objects -- palm-sized carvings of lost friends, fallen kin, remembered mentors -- in a leather pouch at their belt. The pouch was called a *kantong kenangan*, a sack of memories, and a Kurcaci's kantong told the story of every loss they had endured and every love they had survived.

Tumenggung Batu carved, and the chisel tapped, and the tiny garuda emerged from the stone grain by grain, and the Bidadari sang her Mijil, and the ocean breathed, and the fellowship grieved together on the black sand coast of southern Jawa while the stars came out above them one by one, as though the sky, too, were remembering something it had lost.

---

On the third morning, Gajahmada spoke.

They were breaking camp in the grey light before dawn, the ocean a dull pewter expanse beneath a sky that had not yet decided whether to be clear or clouded. Raden Wijaya was crouched by the fire pit, banking the coals with sand. The others were gathering their few possessions -- waterskins, rice sacks, the diminishing bundle of dried fish that Bagus rationed with the stern economy of a man who understood hunger as a practical problem rather than an abstract one.

"We have lost our guide," Gajahmada said. His voice carried the flat, declarative quality of a general making a field assessment. "Our wisest counsel. The only one among us who had walked the deep roads of the spirit realm and returned. Perhaps we should reconsider the path ahead." He paused. "*Kula gadhah usul,*" he said, shifting into the formal *krama* of the Javanese court -- a register that lent weight and legitimacy to what followed. "Majapahit's armies could still use the keris. One decisive battle --"

Raden Wijaya looked up from the fire.

He did not speak. He did not need to. The look he gave Gajahmada was not anger -- it was something older and harder than anger, something that had been refined in decades of solitary rangership into a tool as precise as a blade. It said: *I have heard this argument before. I have heard it from men stronger and wiser than you. I have heard it from the voice inside my own blood, because the blood of kings runs in me and the desire to use power is my inheritance. And I have set it aside, every time, because using this blade is not victory. It is surrender by another name.*

The corruption reminded them all of Calon Arang, the widow-witch of Girah whose rage had transformed her into a vessel of black magic -- a warning from the oldest tales that grief perverted becomes destruction. The keris offered the same bargain that had corrupted Calon Arang: power in exchange for surrender, strength in exchange for the soul. Empu Gandring had taken that bargain. The nine kings before him had taken it. And the whisper in Gajahmada's voice -- the faint, hungry edge beneath his reasonable words -- told Kian Santang that the general was closer to that bargain than he knew.

Gajahmada held the look for three heartbeats. Then his jaw tightened, and he turned away, and the matter was closed -- for now. But Kian Santang, watching from the edge of the camp with the keris whispering at his back, saw the seed that had been planted. Gajahmada was not wrong. That was the danger. He was not wrong about the armies, not wrong about the threat, not wrong about the desperate arithmetic of a war being lost. He was only wrong about the solution. And the wrongness of the solution did not make the rightness of the problem any easier to bear.

---

On the fourth day, they reached the fishing village.

It appeared around a headland like something from a dream -- a cluster of bamboo houses on stilts, built on a crescent of pale sand where a river met the sea. *Jukung* -- outrigger canoes with carved wooden outriggers and patched cotton sails -- were drawn up on the beach in a row, their narrow hulls resting on log rollers, their bows decorated with painted eyes that stared outward at the sea with an expression of perpetual astonishment. Fish drying racks stood in the sun, heavy with silver-scaled *ikan teri* and split yellowfin, and the air smelled of salt and smoke and the sweet, fermented pungency of *terasi* -- shrimp paste curing in clay pots in the shade.

The fishermen were wary. Eight strangers arriving from the east, on foot, bearing weapons -- two of them not human, one impossibly tall and luminous, another short as a child and broad as a barrel -- this was not the kind of traffic that came to a southern coast fishing village. But Raden Wijaya spoke to the headman in the Javanese of the coastal people, low and steady, and coins changed hands -- silver keping, Majapahit currency that Gajahmada produced from a leather purse with the resigned efficiency of a man paying for something he considered beneath his station.

Two jukung were prepared for the crossing. The fellowship would sail west and then northwest, around the tip of Jawa and across the open strait to the western coast of Sumatra, where Ngarai Sianok lay hidden in the Minangkabau highlands. It was a voyage of several days, through waters that were not merely dangerous but sacred.

"The Laut Selatan," the headman said, using the old name for the Indian Ocean. He was a thin, leathery man with hands like knotted rope, and he spoke with the careful deliberation of someone delivering a warning he had delivered many times. "The domain of Nyai Roro Kidul. The Queen of the Southern Sea." He looked at each of them in turn, and his gaze lingered on their clothing. "No green. Nothing green. Not a thread, not a leaf, not a stone. She takes those who wear her color. The sea opens beneath them and closes above, and they are hers."

They checked their garments. Dawala, who had been wearing a faded green sash since Gunung Sewu, removed it without protest and left it on the beach. Dewi Anjani plucked a spray of green leaves from the clasp of her traveling cloak and let them fall.

The headman nodded, satisfied. "And *sesajen*," he said. "Offerings. You do not cross the Laut Selatan without asking permission."

Bagus prepared the sesajen with the practiced hands of a Tuyul who had grown up in a world where the boundary between the physical and the spiritual was not a wall but a veil. He wove small boats from banana leaves, their sides folded up into shallow hulls, and filled them with the offerings: white rice and yellow rice, frangipani blossoms, sticks of kemenyan incense, a scattering of small coins, and a pinch of the red-and-white *kembang setaman* -- the mixed flower petals that were the oldest offering in Nusantara, older than temples, older than kingdoms, the first gift that the first people had given to the first sea.

They launched at dawn. The jukung slid from the black sand into the shallows with a hiss of wood on wet grit, and the ocean received them -- not gently, not roughly, but with the impersonal authority of something so vast that gentleness and roughness were equally irrelevant. The outriggers bit the water and the patched sails caught the morning wind and the two canoes moved south, then west, then out -- out past the headland, past the reef line where the water changed from green to blue, past the last marker buoy where a fisherman had tied a faded red pennant to a bamboo pole, and into the open Indian Ocean.

Bagus set the sesajen on the water one by one, leaning over the jukung's low gunwale to place each banana-leaf boat on the swell with the care of a man laying offerings at a temple. The tiny vessels caught the current and drifted outward, their incense sending thin threads of smoke into the salt air, the frangipani blossoms white against the dark water. They bobbed on the swells, growing smaller, drifting apart, until they were scattered across the ocean's surface like prayers released into the vastness of the world.

"*Nyai Roro Kidul,*" Bagus murmured as he released the last one, "*sing kulo suwun berkah lan perlindungan kanggo perjalanan kulo.*" An old Javanese prayer, spoken in the respectful *krama* of one addressing a power beyond mortal station. *Queen of the Southern Sea, I ask your blessing and protection for our journey.*

The ocean did not answer. But the wind steadied, and the swell smoothed, and the jukung moved westward through water that was deep and dark and very, very old.

---

The voyage lasted two days, and each hour of it was saturated with the *jaladri* -- the vast sea.

The Indian Ocean was not like the Java Sea of the northern coast -- that warm, shallow, island-studded water where trading vessels had sailed for millennia and the worst dangers were pirates and seasonal storms. The Indian Ocean was something else. It was open water -- true open water, with no land between the southern coast of Jawa and the ice-bound shores of a continent so distant it might as well have been a myth. The swells that crossed it were enormous, slow-moving mountains of dark water that lifted the jukung to their crests and slid them down into their troughs in a rhythm that was hypnotic and faintly terrifying. The color of the sea changed with the sun -- deep blue at noon, almost black, the blue of extreme depth, the blue of water that was a thousand meters deep and held things in its darkness that no surface dweller had ever seen; then, as the afternoon light shifted, the blue softened to indigo, to violet, to a bruised purple that merged with the sky at the horizon in a line so ambiguous it seemed less like a boundary than a negotiation.

The salt was everywhere. It crusted on their lips, dried white on their skin, stiffened their hair into stiff, rough ropes. The spray that came over the jukung's bow tasted of the mineral essence of the planet -- of dissolved stone and ancient bone and the chemical memory of every river that had ever emptied itself into the sea. The smell was ozone and brine and the faint, organic sweetness of plankton blooming in the warm surface waters.

At night, the sea transformed.

Kian Santang saw it first -- a faint blue-green glow in the water alongside the jukung's hull, a luminescence that seemed to come from within the sea itself rather than from any external light. He leaned over the gunwale and looked down, and the ocean beneath him was alive with light. Phosphorescent plankton, billions of microscopic organisms, each one producing its own faint spark of cold blue fire, and together they turned the sea into a field of underwater stars. The jukung's wake was a river of light -- a brilliant, shifting trail of blue-green phosphorescence that stretched behind them across the dark water like a road of luminous mist. When the oars dipped, they pulled arcs of light from the surface, and each droplet that fell back was a tiny comet, blazing for an instant before the dark water absorbed it.

Dawala hung over the side of the jukung with his face inches from the surface, his eyes wide, the phosphorescent glow reflected in his pupils like green fire.

"It is like the sky fell into the sea," he whispered.

Cepot, beside him, said nothing. But he looked at the glowing water, and then he looked at the sky where the real stars burned in the equatorial darkness -- the Southern Cross low on the horizon, the broad sweep of the Milky Way like a river of milk across the vault of heaven -- and for a moment the world was symmetrical, stars above and stars below, the jukung sailing between two infinities, and the grief that had silenced Cepot's tongue did not disappear but shifted, made room for something else. Wonder. The stubbornness of wonder, which persists even in the presence of sorrow, which is not the opposite of grief but its companion.

"*Meureun aki bakal resep ieu,*" Cepot said at last, very quietly, in the soft Sundanese of memory. *Perhaps the old man would have liked this.*

It was the first thing he had said in a day that sounded like Cepot. Dawala smiled -- a wet, fragile, genuine smile -- and Cepot almost smiled back, and the phosphorescent sea blazed blue-green beneath them, and the stars turned overhead, and the jukung sailed on through a world that was, for one brief hour, more beautiful than it was cruel.

---

On the morning of the second day at sea, something rose from the deep.

Dewi Anjani felt it first. She was seated at the bow of the lead jukung, her long legs folded beneath her, her luminous eyes fixed on the western horizon where the coast of Sumatra was a faint blue suggestion against the sky. She went still -- stiller than her usual Bidadari stillness, which was considerable -- and her hand rose slowly from her knee and extended toward the water, palm down, fingers spread, in a gesture that was part greeting and part question.

"*Aya ma nu sumping,*" she said, in the *basa luhur* that was her natural register -- the highest Sundanese, each syllable resonant with spiritual authority. *Something comes.*

The water to their starboard side began to move. Not with the random motion of waves or current, but with purpose -- a rising, a displacement, as though something immense were ascending from the depths with the slow, deliberate patience of a thing that had never needed to hurry. The surface bulged. The dark water paled as the shape beneath it rose, and the phosphorescence that had dimmed with the dawn blazed again, concentrated around the ascending form in a corona of blue-green fire that outlined its contours against the deep -- and the contours were enormous.

Dawala gasped. Gajahmada's hand went to his keris. Tumenggung Batu reached for his war hammer.

"No," Dewi Anjani said. Her voice was calm. Certain. "No weapons. This is not a threat."

The Naga Laut surfaced.

It rose from the Indian Ocean like a living island, water cascading from its body in sheets and rivers, and the morning sun struck its scales and shattered into a thousand points of iridescent light. It was vast -- longer than both jukung laid end to end, its body as thick as the oldest banyan in the forests of Pasundan. Its scales were the blue-green of deep ocean water, each one the size of a man's shield, layered and overlapping with the architectural precision of roof tiles on a Minangkabau rumah gadang, and they shifted color as the light moved across them -- blue to green to a deep, opalescent teal that seemed to contain its own luminescence, a glow that was cousin to the phosphorescent plankton but older, richer, a light that had been burning in the deep since before the first human launched the first boat upon the first sea.

This sea-serpent was kin to the Naga Basukih of the mountain depths, yet utterly different -- where the fire-serpent was rage and magma and the blind fury of the volcanic deep, the Naga Laut was patience and current and the vast, encompassing intelligence of the ocean. If the Naga Basukih was Siwa's wrath -- the destroyer-aspect of the divine -- then the Naga Laut was Wisnu's grace -- the preserver, the sustainer, the force that held the world together between the fires of creation and the darkness of dissolution.

Its head rose above the water three body-lengths ahead of the jukung, and it turned to regard them with eyes the size of temple gongs -- round, ancient, the amber of fossilized resin, with vertical pupils that contracted in the sunlight to thin black lines. Those eyes held no malice. They held something far more unsettling: intelligence. A vast, patient, unhurried intelligence that had watched the ages pass from the ocean's floor and had found them, on the whole, neither surprising nor particularly interesting.

Until now.

Now the ancient eyes rested on the lead jukung with an attention that was specific and deliberate, and Kian Santang felt, with a certainty that bypassed thought and arrived directly in his bones, that the creature was looking at him. At the keris on his back.

Dewi Anjani spoke.

She spoke in a language that Kian Santang had never heard -- a tongue of liquid consonants and long, rolling vowels that seemed to move through the air the way water moves through a riverbed, finding its own path, shaping itself to the contours of whatever it touched. It was not Sundanese, not Javanese, not any human language. It was older. It was the language that the Bidadari had spoken to the creatures of the deep in the first age, before the kingdoms, before the keris, before the shadow. The language of the sea itself, rendered in syllables that mortal throats were never meant to produce.

The Naga Laut listened. Its great head swayed gently on the swell, its amber eyes half-closing, and when Dewi Anjani finished, it responded -- not in words, but in a sound that rose from its body like the lowest note of a gamelan gong, a vibration so deep it was felt rather than heard, resonating in the bones of the jukung's hull and in the chests of everyone aboard.

"It is a guardian," Dewi Anjani said, turning to the others. Her expression was one that Kian Santang had not seen on her before -- not awe, exactly, but a kind of reverence, the expression of a being who was herself ancient discovering something more ancient still. "A servant of Nyai Roro Kidul. The Queen has sent it to guide us. The currents ahead are treacherous -- there are reefs and riptides that would dash these boats to splinters. The Naga will lead us through."

The great serpent turned westward, its body uncoiling across the surface of the sea in a long, sinuous curve that caught the morning light and blazed with iridescent fire, and it began to move. Slowly. Patiently. At the pace of two small wooden boats with patched cotton sails, carrying eight grieving travelers toward a coast they could not yet see.

The jukung followed.

They sailed in the Naga Laut's wake for the rest of that day -- through channels between submerged reefs where the water ran white and violent on either side but lay smooth and dark in the path the serpent carved, through cross-currents that would have spun the jukung like leaves in a gutter but parted before the creature's passage like curtains drawn aside by an invisible hand. Once, a squall rose from the south -- a wall of black cloud and driving rain that swept toward them with the speed of a charging cavalry line -- and the Naga Laut raised its head and sang. The sound was unlike anything Kian Santang had ever heard. It was not music. It was not speech. It was a vibration that seemed to address the storm directly, and the storm -- impossibly, undeniably -- divided. It split around them like a river around a stone, the rain hammering the sea to their left and right while the jukung sailed through a corridor of calm air, the sky above them a narrow strip of blue between two walls of grey.

"I have sailed these waters for thirty years," said the fisherman who crewed the lead jukung, and his voice was not afraid but awed, which was worse. "I have heard the old men speak of the Naga Laut. I never believed them."

"*Percados,*" said Dewi Anjani, in the highest Sundanese -- the *basa luhur* that brooked no contradiction. *Believe.*

They reached the Sumatran coast at sunset, and the Naga Laut guided them into a sheltered bay where the water was clear and green and warm, and the beach was white sand, and the forest that began at the tide line was the densest, greenest, most overwhelmingly alive forest that Kian Santang had ever seen. The serpent surfaced one last time -- its great head rising from the shallows, its amber eyes regarding them with that same vast, patient intelligence -- and then it sank. The water closed over it without a ripple, and it was gone, and the sea was just the sea again, and the bay was just a bay, and if it were not for the iridescent scale the size of a dinner plate that had lodged in the jukung's outrigger during the crossing, Kian Santang might have doubted any of it had happened.

Tumenggung Batu pried the scale free and held it up to the fading light. It blazed blue-green, then teal, then a deep, luminous gold.

"For the kantong kenangan," he said, and tucked it into his leather pouch alongside the small stone staff. A memory of the sea. A memorial of wonder.

---

They traveled inland.

The forest of western Sumatra was a world unto itself -- denser, wetter, more extravagantly alive than even the highland jungles of Jawa. The trees were enormous -- dipterocarps with trunks as wide as houses, their canopy so high above the forest floor that the light that reached the ground was green and watery and dim, filtered through so many layers of leaf and vine and epiphyte that it seemed to arrive exhausted, having spent itself on the long journey down. The air was thick with moisture and the scent of growth -- a rich, complex, layered smell of wet earth and rotting wood and flowering vines and the sharp, resinous tang of tropical hardwoods sweating sap in the heat. Birds called from the canopy in voices that ranged from the melodic to the frankly absurd -- the rising, fluting song of a *cucak rawa*, the harsh, mechanical cry of a hornbill, the eerie, descending whistle of something unseen that might have been a bird or might have been the forest itself, exhaling.

On the second day inland, the ground began to rise. The forest thinned. The air cooled. They climbed through a zone of *wanadri* -- mountain forest -- where the trees were shorter and draped in moss so thick it transformed every branch into a green, dripping sculpture, and then they emerged above the clouds, and the Minangkabau highlands spread before them in a panorama of green valleys and terraced rice paddies and sharp volcanic peaks, and at the center of it all, like a wound in the earth -- like a door into the mountain's heart -- lay Ngarai Sianok.

Kian Santang stopped walking. Behind him, one by one, the others stopped too.

The canyon was a revelation.

It opened in the highland plateau without warning -- one moment they stood on a broad, grassy ridge overlooking the terraced valleys, and the next the earth fell away beneath them in a sheer drop of a hundred meters, the cliff faces plunging down to a river that glinted silver far below. The canyon was narrow -- no more than two hundred meters across at its widest -- and it curved through the highlands in a long, sinuous arc, like a keris blade laid upon the land. The walls were vertical, in places overhanging, and they were green. Not the ordinary green of vegetation clinging to stone, but a green so intense, so saturated, so luminously, impossibly alive that it seemed to produce its own light -- a green that was not a color but a statement, an assertion of vitality so fierce that it made the forests they had just walked through seem pale and tentative by comparison. Ferns erupted from every crack in the stone in great, cascading fountains of frond and stem. Orchids bloomed in clusters along the cliff faces -- white, purple, deep crimson -- their roots anchored in the vertical stone as though gravity were a suggestion they had chosen to decline. And the waterfalls -- there were dozens, perhaps scores, perhaps too many to count -- threads and ribbons and great white curtains of water pouring over the cliff edges and falling, falling, falling through the green air to the river below, filling the canyon with a sound that was not quite roar and not quite song but something between -- a continuous, shimmering exhalation that vibrated in the chest and blurred the boundary between hearing and feeling.

Mist rose from the canyon floor in slow, ascending columns, and where the mist met the waterfalls, it caught the light and held it -- prismatic, fractured, scattered into rainbows that arced across the canyon's width and vanished and reformed and vanished again, so that the air itself seemed to shimmer with color, with light, with a beauty so concentrated it was difficult to look at directly.

But this canyon was more than stone and water and green.

The air here was different. Kian Santang felt it the moment he reached the canyon's rim -- a thickness, a density, a saturation of *bayu* -- spiritual energy -- so intense that breathing felt like drinking, each lungful arriving heavy with something that was not oxygen but was just as essential. The plants on the canyon walls were not merely lush -- they were ancient. Ferns whose species had been extinct for centuries in the lowland forests grew here in profusion, their fronds unfurling in spiral patterns that suggested not evolution but intention. Flowers bloomed in colors that had no names in any language Kian Santang knew, colors that existed only here, in this place, as though the canyon had invented them for its own pleasure.

"Time moves differently in this place," Dewi Anjani said. Her voice was hushed. Her luminous eyes were wide, and in them, for the first time since Kian Santang had known her, he saw something that looked like home. "I have heard of this canyon from the eldest of my people. They said it was the last place in the mortal world where the veil between the realms is thin enough to walk through. I did not entirely believe them." She paused. "I was wrong."

They descended.

A stairway had been cut into the canyon wall -- not carved by human hands, but shaped by something that understood stone the way a poet understands language, coaxing it into forms that seemed to grow from the rock rather than being imposed upon it. The steps were narrow, slick with mist and moss, and they spiraled down the cliff face in a long, curving descent that took them through curtains of waterfall spray and past ledges where orchids bloomed in clouds of white and pink and through narrow passages where the canyon walls pressed close on either side and the green light deepened to emerald, to jade, to a luminous underwater darkness that was not dark at all but merely a different kind of light.

At the bottom, the river received them.

The water was clear -- so clear that the riverbed, five meters below, was visible in every detail: smooth stones in shades of grey and amber, aquatic plants waving gently in the current like green banners in a slow wind, and small silver fish darting between the stones with the quick, purposeful movements of creatures that had somewhere important to be. The water itself was cool but not cold, and when Kian Santang cupped it in his hands and drank, it tasted of minerals and rain and something else -- something sweet, something that had no name, something that made his body, exhausted and grief-hollowed and salt-crusted from the sea crossing, feel suddenly and overwhelmingly alive.

They followed the river into the canyon's depths, and the canyon deepened around them, and the green walls rose higher, and the waterfalls multiplied, and the mist thickened, and the air grew heavy with *bayu*, and with each step, Kian Santang felt the world outside -- the grief, the war, the whisper of the keris, the terrible weight of the quest -- grow thinner, more distant, as though the canyon were a vessel filling with something that displaced everything else. Peace. Not the absence of trouble, but the presence of something older and stronger than trouble. A peace that did not deny grief but held it, the way a mother holds a weeping child -- firmly, gently, with the absolute authority of love.

And then they came around a bend in the river, and she was there.

---

Nyai Roro Kidul stood at the water's edge where the river widened into a deep, still pool fed by a waterfall that fell from such a height that by the time it reached the pool, it had dissolved into mist -- a curtain of white vapor that hung in the air behind her like a veil, like a throne, like the breath of the mountain made visible.

She was not a Bidadari. She was not human. She was something older than either -- something that had existed before the categories, before the distinctions between mortal and immortal, earth and sky, flesh and spirit. She was the sea made woman. She was the deep made conscious. She was the oldest power in the southern hemisphere of Nusantara, and she stood at the edge of a pool in a hidden canyon and regarded the eight travelers who had come to her door with eyes the color of the ocean at its deepest -- a green so dark it was almost black, a green that held light within its darkness the way the sea holds the sun's rays in its depths, transforming them into something richer and stranger than what entered.

Her skin was dark -- the deep, warm brown of the earth beneath the sea, of volcanic stone polished by millennia of tidal motion -- and it seemed, like the canyon itself, to produce its own luminescence, a faint, shifting glow that was not the golden light of the Bidadari but something cooler, bluer, the phosphorescent shimmer of deep water. Her hair was black and long -- impossibly long, falling past her waist, past her knees, pooling on the stone at her feet like a river of dark water -- and it moved. Even in the still air of the canyon, her hair moved, flowing and shifting in slow, sinuous currents as though it were submerged in an invisible sea, as though the element she truly inhabited was not air but water and the air merely accommodated her presence the way a host accommodates a guest whose power vastly exceeds their own.

She wore a *kebaya* of green silk -- the deep, saturated green of her domain, the green that fishermen feared and revered, the green of the Laut Selatan itself. The kebaya was embroidered with patterns of waves and coral and creatures of the deep, rendered in silver thread so fine that the embroidery seemed to move with her breathing, the waves cresting and falling, the sea creatures drifting through the fabric like shadows through water. Beneath the kebaya, a *batik sarong* of ocean patterns -- not the geometric precision of Javanese batik but something more fluid, more organic, the wax-resist patterns flowing and curving and spiraling in designs that suggested currents and tides and the great circular movements of the ocean itself.

She radiated power. Not the aggressive, conquering power of a king or a general, but the patient, encompassing, absolute power of the sea -- the power that does not need to assert itself because it is already everywhere, already in everything, already so vast that assertion would be redundant. It was the kind of power that made Dewi Anjani -- a warrior-princess of the Bidadari, a being of considerable might and ancient lineage -- bow. Not in submission, but in recognition. In acknowledgment of something that preceded her and would outlast her and existed on a scale that made her own considerable power seem, by comparison, like a candle flame beside the sun.

"You are expected," said Nyai Roro Kidul. Her voice was the sound of the sea in a shell -- distant, enclosed, resonant, carrying within it the echoes of every wave that had ever broken on every shore. "I have watched your crossing. The Naga brought you safely."

"Nyai," Raden Wijaya said, and bowed. The word was not a title but an acknowledgment -- the old Javanese term of respect for a woman of supernatural power, spoken with a deference that Kian Santang had never heard in the ranger's voice before.

"Raden Wijaya. Son of Mataram. You carry your ancestors' burden with more grace than they did." Her dark green eyes moved across the fellowship, resting on each of them in turn, and each of them felt the weight of her regard -- not the weight of judgment but the weight of comprehension, of being entirely and completely known. "The Bidadari warrior. The Kurcaci chieftain. The general of Majapahit. The Tuyul companions." Her gaze found Kian Santang, and it lingered.

"And the keris-bearer," she said. "The small one who carries the heaviest weight."

She stepped forward, and the pool rippled at her feet though she had not touched the water, and the mist parted before her like a curtain drawn aside by an invisible hand.

"I knew Eyang Guru Batara," she said. "He was old when I was young, and I am very old." She let the words settle. "He came to this canyon once, long ago, when the world was different and the darkness was still sleeping. We sat beside this pool and spoke of the future, and he told me that a day would come when a small person would carry a great darkness toward the fire that made it, and that the road would be longer and harder than anyone could foresee." Her eyes, fathomless and green, held Kian Santang's. "He did not tell me that the small person would be so young. Or so afraid."

She paused, and her voice softened with something that might have been the ocean's own grief. "His atma has passed beyond the veil. The wheel of samsara turns for all beings, even for one as old and wise as he. But the karma of his final act -- the sacrifice on the bridge, the light poured out in defiance of the dark -- that karma is a golden thread woven into the fabric of the world. It does not die with the body. It endures. It sustains. And it will sustain you, keris-bearer, in the hours ahead, when the darkness is deepest and the whisper is loudest."

Kian Santang felt his throat tighten. The keris on his back pulsed once -- a single, sharp throb of dark energy -- and then went quiet, as though the blade had attempted to speak and been silenced by something stronger.

"You are weary," Nyai Roro Kidul said, and it was not a question. "Your bodies are broken and your spirits are wounded and the grief you carry is heavier than the blade. The canyon will restore you. Its springs will heal your flesh. Its air will quiet the voices that trouble your rest." She looked at Kian Santang, and the look was gentle and terrible and old. "You will need both your body and your spirit whole for what lies ahead. Rest now. Eat. Sleep. Let the canyon hold you for a time."

---

They rested.

The canyon of Ngarai Sianok unfolded around them like a gift -- like the answer to a question they had not known how to ask. Springs rose from the canyon floor in pools of crystal water that was warm without being hot, infused with minerals that sank into their skin and worked through their muscles and bones with a slow, deep, penetrating warmth that dissolved the accumulated damage of weeks of hard travel -- the bruises, the blisters, the joint-deep ache of sleeping on stone, the particular exhaustion of bodies that had been running on will alone after the reserves of strength had been spent.

Tumenggung Batu lowered himself into one of the springs and sat motionless for an hour, the warm water rising to his granite chin, his flint-grey eyes closed, his broad body relaxing for the first time since the fellowship had formed. When he emerged, the lines of pain that had been carved into his face since Gunung Sewu had softened, and he moved with an ease that made him seem, briefly, less like a boulder and more like the mountain stream that had, over centuries, shaped the boulder into something almost graceful.

They ate. Food appeared -- not carried by visible hands, not prepared in any kitchen they could see, but simply present, laid out on broad banana leaves beside the springs as though the canyon itself were offering sustenance. The rice was golden -- not the white rice of the lowlands or the red rice of the highlands but a deep, warm gold that caught the light like metal, and it tasted of sunlight and clean earth and something ineffably sweet, as though it had been grown in soil that had never known drought or blight or the shadow of any dark thing. Fruits lay beside it -- rambutans the size of fists, their hairy red shells opening to reveal flesh of impossible sweetness; mangosteens whose purple husks concealed white segments that tasted of every good fruit that had ever grown; and others, fruits without names, fruits that existed only here, their flavors beyond description, beyond comparison, beyond the capacity of any tongue to catalogue.

There was water, too, served in cups of carved stone that were cool to the touch and warm to the lips, and the water tasted -- Kian Santang struggled for the word and could not find it. It tasted the way starlight looks. It tasted the way the Mijil had sounded. It tasted like the purest essence of the thing that water is before it becomes water -- the idea of water, the platonic form of clarity and life and quenching, distilled into a liquid that filled his mouth and his throat and his chest and his blood and left him feeling, for the first time in weeks, that his body was not a burden but a home.

He slept.

He slept on a bed of moss beside the pool where the waterfall mist drifted over him like a blessing, and for the first time since the caves of Gunung Sewu, the keris was silent. Not whispering. Not murmuring. Not pressing its cold suggestions against the edges of his consciousness like a hand pressing against a locked door. Silent. The ancient magic of Nyai Roro Kidul's canyon wrapped around the blade like water around a stone, dampening its voice, muffling its malice, creating a space of quiet in which Kian Santang's mind could rest without defense, without vigilance, without the constant, exhausting effort of resisting the whisper that never stopped.

He dreamed of his mother's kitchen. Of nasi timbel steaming in banana-leaf wrappings. Of rain on a thatched roof. Of Bagus sleeping in the next room, snoring gently. Of the sound of a pestle in a stone mortar, grinding spices in the early morning -- turmeric and coriander and the warm, sweet bark of cinnamon -- and the smell filling the small house with a warmth that was not temperature but love.

He woke with tears on his face and the grief still present but changed -- no longer the crushing, suffocating weight of the coast road but something gentler, something that coexisted with breath, something he could carry without being carried under. The grief remained. It would always remain. But the canyon had given him room beside it. Room to breathe. Room to continue.

---

On the evening of their rest -- a period that felt like days but was, Dewi Anjani said with quiet certainty, only hours in the world beyond the canyon -- Nyai Roro Kidul sent for Kian Santang.

The summons came not in words but in the movement of the mist -- a thin tendril of white vapor that detached itself from the waterfall's curtain and drifted toward him with the deliberate, purposeful motion of a living thing, coiling around his wrist like a cool, damp hand. He followed it. Through the canyon's green twilight, past the sleeping forms of his companions, past the springs and the moss-beds and the flowering walls, to a place he had not seen before -- a pool at the canyon's deepest point, where the walls rose so high on either side that the sky above was a narrow ribbon of darkening blue, and the waterfalls that fed the pool fell from such heights that they arrived as rain, as mist, as the ghost of water rather than water itself.

Nyai Roro Kidul stood at the pool's edge. In the twilight, her green kebaya was the darkest thing in the canyon, darker than the stone, darker than the water, darker than the coming night. Her hair moved in its phantom currents, flowing around her shoulders and down her back in slow, serpentine waves. Her eyes, when she turned to him, were the green of the deep ocean at the moment before full dark -- the last green, the green that exists when all other color has been swallowed by the night.

"There is something you must see," she said.

The pool before her was still. Perfectly, impossibly still -- not the temporary stillness of water between gusts of wind, but an absolute, crystalline stillness that seemed to reject motion as a concept, as though this water had decided, at some point in its long history, that it was done with ripples and waves and the restless agitation of surface tension, and had simply stopped. Its surface was a mirror -- not the imperfect, shifting mirror of ordinary water but a true mirror, flawless, depthless, reflecting not the sky above but something else.

"This is the Cermin Laut Selatan," Nyai Roro Kidul said. "The Mirror of the Southern Sea. It shows what the sea knows. And the sea knows everything, Kian Santang. Every ship that has ever sailed upon it, every body that has ever sunk beneath it, every prayer that has ever been whispered at its shore. The sea forgets nothing. The sea forgives nothing. And the sea sees what is, and what was, and what may yet be -- if the courage to see it can be found."

She looked at him, and her expression was not unkind, but it was not comforting either. It was the expression of the ocean itself -- vast, ancient, holding within its depths both the terrible and the beautiful, making no distinction between them because the sea does not distinguish. The sea simply is.

"Kneel," she said. "Look."

Kian Santang knelt at the water's edge. The moss was damp beneath his knees. The mist from the high waterfalls settled on his shoulders like a mantle. Behind him, Nyai Roro Kidul stood like a green shadow -- silent, immense with contained power, her presence filling the canyon the way the sea fills a bay, quietly, completely, leaving no space unoccupied.

He looked into the pool.

The water's surface was perfectly still, and it reflected not the narrow ribbon of sky above the canyon, not the green walls, not his own face kneeling at its edge. It reflected something else. Something that moved in the mirror's depths with a slow, purposeful clarity that was not reflection but vision -- not the world as it was but the world as it could be, as it might be, as it would be if certain choices were made and certain roads were walked to their end.

And behind him, on his back, the Keris Murka pulsed.

Not the slow, patient, confident pulse of the coastal road, not the insistent whisper of the grief-days, not the cold assertion of power that had been the blade's constant companion since the night above Telaga Warna. This was different. This was rapid. Erratic. A stuttering, arrhythmic throb that felt, against his spine, like a heartbeat seized by fear.

The keris was afraid.

For the first time since its forging in the fires of Krakatau, for the first time in all the long centuries of its patient, hungry, corrosive existence, the Keris Murka -- the weapon of the Shadow King, the blade that had corrupted nine kings and broken a world -- was afraid.

Of what the mirror would show.

Kian Santang knelt at the water's edge, and Nyai Roro Kidul stood behind him like the sea itself standing sentinel, and the mirror waited, still and fathomless, and the keris trembled against his back like a living thing that had seen its own ending written in water and could not look away.

He leaned forward.

He looked.
