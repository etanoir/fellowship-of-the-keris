# Bab 1: Tanah Sunda
## (The Land of Sunda)

---

There is a place in the heart of the world where the mountains hold the sky upon their shoulders, and the earth gives back everything it is given.

It lies in the western highlands of Jawa, where the Pasundan plateau rises from the lowland plains like a great green altar set before heaven. To the north, the broken crown of Gunung Tangkuban Perahu sleeps against the clouds — that ancient mountain whose peak, so the stories say, was kicked asunder by a prince in his grief, overturning his great canoe upon the sky so that it rests there still, hull upward, forever sailing nowhere. Beyond the rice terraces rose the flat-topped mountain that the old tales said Sangkuriang had kicked upside-down in his rage when denied his mother's love — Gunung Tangkuban Perahu, the overturned boat, forever pointing its hull at the sky. The *dalang* told the story every Seren Taun: how the young hero had fallen in love with his own mother, Dayang Sumbi, without knowing her, and how she had set him the impossible task of damming the Citarum and building a great boat before dawn, and how, when she tricked him by pounding her rice mortar to make the roosters crow early, he had kicked the unfinished vessel in fury, and it landed upside-down upon the highland, and there it remains — a mountain shaped like an overturned *perahu*, a monument to the rage of thwarted love. To the east, the perfect cone of Gunung Ciremai watches over the land like a sentinel carved from blue stone and morning light. Between these guardians, the highlands unfold in an endless cascade of green — a green so deep and so alive that travelers from the coastal kingdoms have been known to weep upon first seeing it, believing they had stumbled upon the gardens of the Bidadari themselves.

This is Tanah Sunda. The land of light. The land of water and rice and stone.

The sawah climb the hillsides in broad, shining terraces, each paddy reflecting the sky in its still water so that from above, the hills appear to be set with a thousand mirrors, each one holding a fragment of cloud. When the rice is young, the terraces glow with a green so tender it seems to tremble. When the rice is golden and heavy with grain, the hills burn amber in the afternoon sun, and the breeze moves through the stalks in long, slow waves, and the whole land breathes. The Citarum river threads its silver way through the valleys below, fed by a hundred unnamed streams that tumble down from the volcanic heights, cold and clean and tasting faintly of mineral and moss. Along its banks, the wild melati grows in thick white clusters, and its perfume — sweet, heavy, almost unbearably gentle — drifts through the villages in the early mornings before the mist has fully lifted, so that to wake in Tanah Sunda is to wake inside a fragrance.

And everywhere, the sound of water. Water falling over stone. Water standing patient in the paddies. Water singing in the bamboo channels that the farmers have built across the centuries, an intricate lattice of irrigation that feeds every terrace, every field, every garden, from the highest slopes to the lowest valley floor. The people of this land understood water the way other peoples understood war — completely, and with devotion.

These were the highlands of the Tuyul.

Now, you must set aside what you think you know of that name. The old grandmothers of the coastal cities tell their children tales of the *tuyul* — small, mischievous spirits summoned by dukun to steal coins and trinkets from the unwary. Those stories are half-truths worn smooth by centuries of misunderstanding, like river stones that have forgotten their original shape. The Tuyul of the Pasundan highlands were no spirits and no thieves. They were a people — small, yes, the tallest among them barely reaching the chest of a grown Javanese man, but broad-shouldered and brown-skinned and solid as the earth they tended. Their feet were wide and tough, calloused from a lifetime of walking barefoot through the warm mud of the sawah. Their hands were clever with tools and instruments. Their eyes were large and dark and quick to laugh, and their laughter, once begun, could outlast a rainstorm.

They were farmers, first and always. They grew rice of a dozen varieties, each one suited to a different terrace, a different altitude, a different angle of the sun. They grew cassava and sweet potato in the red volcanic soil of the upper slopes. They tended groves of coconut and banana and rambutan and mangosteen, and their gardens exploded with chili and lemongrass and ginger and galangal and turmeric, so that even the smallest Tuyul kitchen smelled like a spice merchant's dream. They raised fish in the deeper paddies and kept chickens that roamed freely through the lembur, pecking at the ground beneath the raised wooden houses and crowing with absolute impunity at whatever hour pleased them.

They were, in a word, content.

Their lembur dotted the highlands like clusters of brown and gold amid the green — small villages of twenty or thirty families, each one centered around a common yard where the children played and the elders sat on woven tikar mats, drinking *wedang* and trading stories that everyone had heard a thousand times but pretended to hear fresh, out of courtesy and because the telling mattered more than the tale. Their houses were *rumah panggung* — raised wooden structures standing on sturdy teak pillars, their floors a full arm's length above the ground to keep out the damp and the crawling things and, more importantly, to let the cool air circulate beneath in the heat of the dry season. The roofs were steep, thatched with dried palm fronds or, in the wealthier homes, tiled with terracotta that turned the color of burnt cinnamon in the rain. The walls were woven bamboo, light and strong, and the verandas — every house had a veranda — faced east, so that the first thing a Tuyul saw upon waking and stepping outside was the sun rising over the mountains, gilding the mist, promising another good day.

They kept to themselves, the Tuyul. They had little interest in the affairs of the great kingdoms — the intrigues of Mataram, the ambitions of Sriwijaya, the wars that periodically set the lowlands ablaze. Such matters belonged to the Big Folk, the full-sized humans who built their empires of stone and steel and seemed, to the Tuyul, to be forever restless, forever wanting something beyond the next hill. The Tuyul wanted nothing beyond the next harvest. Their ambitions were seasonal: plant well, tend well, harvest well, eat well, and give thanks to Dewi Sri, the goddess of the rice, who had blessed them with abundance since the beginning of memory. At the *kabuyutan* — the sacred *mandala* at the village edge where the oldest trees stood sentinel over mossy stones and the air hummed with the presence of the *karuhun*, the ancestor spirits — they left *sesajen* of flowers and rice and betel at every turning of the Saka calendar, for the Tuyul understood that the *pertiwi* did not belong to them; they belonged to the land, and the land remembered everything.

It was, by any reasonable measure, a paradise.

And in this paradise, in a lembur called Padalarang that sat upon a gentle slope overlooking a wide valley of sawah, there lived a young Tuyul who was not entirely content.

---

His name was Kian Santang.

He was twenty-five years old, which among a people who commonly lived to see a hundred and fifty harvests, made him barely more than a youth — old enough to be trusted with the family sawah, young enough to be forgiven for not yet having taken a wife, though the aunties of Lembur Padalarang had begun to comment on this with increasing frequency and diminishing subtlety. He stood four feet tall in his bare feet, which was respectable for a Tuyul man, and he was lean where most Tuyul were round, angular where they were soft, as though he had been carved from a slightly different wood. His skin was the warm brown of polished mahogany. His eyes were the color of river stones held up to firelight — brown with flecks of amber that caught the sun when he tilted his head. His hair was thick and black and perpetually unruly, defying the coconut oil that his late mother had taught him to apply each morning, a habit he maintained less for vanity than for memory.

He was, the village agreed, a good young man. Polite. Dutiful. He tended his uncle's sawah with care, paid his respects to the elders, contributed his labor during the communal planting and the communal harvest. He wore simple but well-made clothes — a white linen shirt, clean and pressed, and a sarong of dark blue cotton that he wrapped in the Sundanese fashion, knotted at the waist. He was, in all outward respects, a proper Tuyul of Padalarang.

But there was something in him that did not fit.

He read. This, in itself, was not unusual — many Tuyul could read, and the village had a small collection of manuscripts kept in the meeting hall. But Kian Santang did not merely read. He devoured. He had found, in his uncle's house, a collection of *lontar* — old palm-leaf manuscripts, their edges brown and brittle, their script the ancient Sundanese alphabet that few now bothered to learn. He had taught himself to read them, slowly, painfully, by candlelight, and the stories they held had cracked something open inside him. Tales of the old wars. Chronicles of the alliance that defeated Raja Iblis. Accounts of Tuyul warriors who had marched alongside the kings of Mataram, small but unbreakable, standing firm when larger soldiers fled. Descriptions of lands beyond the highlands — the great temple of Barabudur with its thousand stone Buddhas, the spice ports of Maluku where the air burned with clove and nutmeg, the endless blue waters of the Laut Banda where ships sailed for months without sight of land. And farther still — rumors of the deep forests of Kalimantan where the rivers ran black with tannin and the Dayak people built longhouses that stretched the length of a village, of the seas beyond Sulawesi where the Bugis sailors navigated by stars the Tuyul had never named, of eastern islands so remote that their mountains breathed fire into waters no Sundanese boat had ever crossed.

He wanted to see these things. He would never say so aloud — it would have been considered deeply eccentric, a kind of mild social illness — but the wanting was there, lodged in his chest like a seed that would not stop growing.

And he practiced pencak silat.

This was odder still. The Tuyul were not a martial people. They had fought in the old wars, yes, but that was ancient history, and the modern Tuyul viewed combat training with the same bemused tolerance they might show a neighbor who collected unusual beetles — harmless, certainly, but why? Yet every morning, before the sun had fully cleared the ridge, Kian Santang would stand in the small yard behind his rumah panggung and move through the forms his uncle had once taught him, long ago, before his uncle had grown strange. The stances. The blocks. The fluid, circular movements that mimicked the flow of water and the strike of the snake. He was not especially skilled — he had no teacher now, and the lontar manuscripts that described the higher forms were maddeningly vague — but he was persistent, and there was a grace in his movement that surprised even him, as though his body remembered something his mind had never learned.

His rumah panggung stood at the eastern edge of Lembur Padalarang, where the village gave way to a grove of tall bamboo and, beyond that, to the descending terraces of the sawah. It was a modest house, smaller than those of the wealthier families closer to the village center, but beautifully made. His grandfather had built it — each joint fitted without nails, each beam of teak aged and strong, the veranda railing carved with a pattern of rice stalks and flowing water that caught the light in the early mornings and made the wood seem almost alive. From the veranda, Kian Santang could see the entire valley — the green and gold patchwork of the sawah, the silver thread of the river, the blue mountains beyond — and on clear mornings, when the mist burned off early, he could see all the way to the smudged purple line of Gunung Ciremai on the eastern horizon, impossibly distant, impossibly beautiful, impossibly out of reach.

This morning, however, he had no time for gazing. Today was Seren Taun.

---

The harvest festival came once a year, at the turning of the season, when the last of the rice had been cut and bundled and carried singing from the fields, and the granaries stood fat and full, and the land lay open and breathing beneath the wide sky. It was the most important day in the Tuyul calendar — a day of thanksgiving, of feasting, of music and dance and unrestrained communal joy. Every lembur in the highlands celebrated it, but Lembur Padalarang, which was blessed with particularly fertile sawah and a particularly talented corps of cooks, celebrated it with a fervor that bordered on the competitive.

By mid-morning, the village common was transformed.

Long mats of woven pandanus had been laid across the packed earth, end to end, forming a great communal table that stretched from the banyan tree at one end of the common to the carved wooden gate at the other. Upon these mats, the women of the lembur were laying out food in quantities that suggested either extraordinary generosity or a profound inability to estimate serving sizes — most likely both. There were mountains of *nasi timbel*, each portion of fragrant rice wrapped tight in a banana leaf and steamed until the leaf's green sweetness had seeped into the grain. There were platters of *lalapan* — raw cucumber, fresh basil, long beans, cabbage leaves, all crisp and bright, meant to be eaten with the hands, dipped in sambal so fiery it made the eyes water and the spirit sing. There were whole fried fish, golden and crackling, their skin crisped in coconut oil until it shattered at the touch. There were bowls of sayur asem — tamarind vegetable soup, sour and savory and deeply restorative. There were clay pots of rendang that had been simmering since before dawn, the beef falling apart in its dark, aromatic coconut sauce. And at the far end of the mats, presided over by the eldest grandmother of the lembur — a formidable woman named Nini Anteh who claimed to be a hundred and forty-two and who no one dared contradict — there were vats of *kolak*, sweet coconut milk simmered with palm sugar and banana and sweet potato, thick as liquid gold and twice as precious.

The children had been circling the food mats since sunrise, darting in to steal bites when they thought the grandmothers were not looking. The grandmothers were always looking. Wooden spoons cracked against small knuckles with unerring precision, accompanied by scolding that was fierce in volume and entirely hollow in intent, because the grandmothers had been stealing bites from festival mats when they were young, and their grandmothers had scolded them, and this, too, was tradition.

At the center of the common, a group of young men had assembled the angklung — dozens of bamboo instruments, each one a frame of tubes cut to different lengths, designed to produce a single note when shaken. Played alone, an angklung was a modest thing. Played together, in the hands of a trained ensemble, twenty or thirty instruments interlocking their notes in rapid, rippling succession — the sound was extraordinary. It was the sound of rain falling on a forest canopy. It was the sound of water running over stones. It was the sound of bamboo itself, alive and singing, as though the instruments remembered being trees and were calling out to the groves they came from.

They began to play as the sun reached its zenith, and the music poured through the lembur like warm water, loosening everything it touched. The elders nodded along, keeping time with subtle movements of their heads. The younger women began to dance — not the formal court dances of the lowland palaces, but *jaipongan*, the Sundanese social dance, all fluid shoulders and quick, playful footwork, the dancers' hands tracing curves in the air like calligraphy, their hips punctuating the rhythm with sudden, joyful accents that drew cheers and clapping from the crowd. The drummers responded, the *kendang* driving the tempo faster, and the angklung players shook their instruments with increasing abandon, and the whole common pulsed with a single, communal heartbeat. And somewhere in the rising melody, a clear voice began an *Asmarandana* — the pupuh of love and longing, its lilting cadence winding through the angklung notes like a vine through trellis:

*Sunda nu kaasih kami / lemah cai lalita pisan / giri héjo jauh melengkung / ...*
(*Sunda whom we love / land and water beautiful beyond measure / green mountains curving in the distance / ...*)

The voice rose and fell with the aching sweetness that only the Asmarandana could carry — the pupuh whose very name meant "the fragrance of love" — and several of the older women wiped their eyes, though whether from the beauty of the song or the smoke from the rendang fires, no one asked.

Ki Sunda, the village elder — a stout, kind-faced Tuyul of ninety-eight years who wore his white *iket* headcloth with the quiet dignity of a man who had earned his title through decades of patient service — stood before the offering table and raised his hands for silence. The music softened. The dancers stilled. The children stopped mid-bite, cheeks bulging.

Before the offering table, the *sesajen* had been laid with care: cones of yellow rice, betel leaves and areca nuts, flowers of five colors, small cups of *tirta* holy water drawn from the spring at the *kabuyutan*. Ki Sunda's voice carried the particular gravity that Tuyul elders cultivated like a crop. He lifted his palms skyward in the ancient gesture of *sinembah* — the reverence made manifest.

"*Om Swasti Astu. Awignam astu namo sidham*," he intoned — the blessing that had opened every ceremony in these highlands since before the first *candi* temple rose from the Pasundan earth. "*Pinareng ku Sang Hyang Widhi, tinangtu ku karuhun*" — *permitted by the Divine, determined by the ancestors.* "We give thanks to Dewi Sri, mother of the rice, keeper of abundance, she who feeds us from the body of the pertiwi. We give thanks to the *karuhun*, the ancestors who planted these fields before us and whose spirits watch from the *kabuyutan*. *Tumuwuh sangu di sawah, sumebar berkah di lembur.* The harvest is good. The granaries are full. Our children are fat." He paused, glancing at the children, several of whom were indeed remarkably fat. "We are blessed. The *dharma* of the land has been honored. *Mulah kami lali ka pertiwi.* Let us eat, and in eating, remember — every grain is a gift. Every meal, a mercy."

"*Awignam astu*," murmured the crowd — *may there be no obstacles* — and then, with the decisiveness of a people for whom eating was both sustenance and high art, they descended upon the food.

Kian Santang sat near the edge of the gathering, a banana leaf of nasi timbel balanced on his knee, watching the celebration with a warmth that was genuine and an attention that was, perhaps, a degree more watchful than the occasion required. He ate slowly — the rice fragrant, the sambal volcanic, the fried fish perfect — and smiled at the right moments and clapped for the dancers and complimented Nini Anteh on her kolak, which was indeed transcendent, and he was, to all appearances, entirely present.

But his eyes kept drifting to the far side of the common, where a particular rumah panggung sat apart from the others, its veranda shadowed, its door half-open.

Uncle Kian's house.

---

Uncle Kian — Kian Perkasa was his full name, though no one in the lembur had called him that in decades — was Kian Santang's mother's brother, and he was the most unusual Tuyul in the memory of Lembur Padalarang.

He had left.

This, in itself, was almost inconceivable. Tuyul did not leave. They were born in their lembur, they grew in their lembur, they married someone from a neighboring lembur (which was daring enough), and they died in their lembur, and they were buried in the hillside above the sawah, and their grandchildren planted flowers on their graves, and the cycle continued. The idea of simply walking away — of crossing the highlands, descending to the coastal plains, and venturing out into the wider world of the Big Folk and their bewildering affairs — was so foreign to the Tuyul temperament that their language did not even have a proper word for it. They called it *ngelana* — wandering — and they said it the way one might say *illness* or *misfortune*, with a sympathetic wince and a quiet hope that it would not prove contagious.

Uncle Kian had gone wandering some sixty years ago, when he was already past middle age, which made it worse. A young Tuyul might be forgiven a restless season; an elder should have known better. He had been gone for three years — three years during which the lembur had alternated between mourning him as dead and condemning him as mad — and when he returned, walking up the path from the valley one ordinary morning as though he had merely been visiting a neighboring village, he was... different.

He was thinner. His eyes, once merry, held a new sharpness, a wariness that surfaced at odd moments — when a shadow fell unexpectedly, when a stranger passed through the village, when the night was very dark and the wind came from the east. He had brought back objects — strange things, foreign things. A curved knife from Sulawesi. A carved wooden mask from Bali. A compass of Zheng He's design, its needle forever pointing north. And he had stories — extraordinary stories of underground rivers and ancient ruins and encounters with creatures that the Tuyul preferred to believe existed only in wayang tales.

But there was one thing he had brought back that he never showed anyone. Something he kept wrapped in a length of old batik cloth, brown and gold, folded in upon itself in his private room. Something he touched, late at night, when he thought no one could hear. Kian Santang had heard him, more than once, through the thin bamboo wall that separated their rooms in the years they lived together — the soft rustling of cloth, and then silence, a long silence, and then his uncle's breathing, slow and strange, as though he were in a trance.

Once, Kian Santang had heard him whisper to it. Just a single word, repeated.

*"Milik-ku."*

Mine.

Now, at a hundred and thirty, Uncle Kian was white-haired and thin as bamboo, still quick on his feet but hunched, as though carrying a weight that no one else could see. He emerged from his house halfway through the festival, blinking in the sunlight, and the lembur greeted him with the exaggerated cheer reserved for elderly relatives whose behavior required collective forgiveness. He smiled — or attempted to; his smiles, these days, looked like something he was remembering rather than feeling — and accepted a banana leaf of food and sat at the edge of the common, near Kian Santang, and ate almost nothing.

His right hand, Kian Santang noticed, never left his chest. Beneath the folds of his shirt, pressed against his ribs, there was a shape — something long and flat, wrapped in cloth, held close. His uncle's fingers curled around it the way a mother holds a sleeping child: tender, automatic, absolute.

"Eyang," Kian Santang said softly, using the term of respect, "are you well?"

Uncle Kian looked at him with those sharp, shallow eyes, and for a moment, something surfaced in them — affection, perhaps, or sorrow, or the ghost of the jovial uncle who had once taught a small boy the first forms of pencak silat in the yard behind the house.

"Well enough," he said. "Well enough, boy. Eat your rice."

He turned away, scanning the crowd, searching for something or someone, and Kian Santang felt the familiar ache of worry settle into his chest like a stone dropped into still water.

---

The stranger arrived in the late afternoon, when the sun had softened to amber and the shadows of the banyan tree stretched long across the common.

He came up the path from the valley — the same path that wound down through the sawah terraces to the river and the lowland road beyond — and he was tall. Impossibly, absurdly, magnificently tall by Tuyul standards, his head and shoulders rising above the crowd the way Gunung Tangkuban Perahu rose above the foothills. He walked with the slow, deliberate gait of a man who had been walking for a very long time and intended to walk a great deal further, leaning on a staff of dark teak wood, its surface worn smooth by decades of use. A cloak of deep indigo hung from his shoulders, travel-stained and heavy, and beneath it, Kian Santang caught a glimpse of a simple shirt of undyed cotton and a sarong of old batik — the brown and black patterns of the north coast, parang rusak, the broken blade design that only certain men were permitted to wear.

His face was weathered — brown skin creased and folded by sun and wind and years beyond easy counting — and a beard of pure white cascaded down his chest, long enough to tuck into his belt, though he let it hang free. Upon his head he wore an *iket*, a batik headcloth tied in the Sundanese fashion, its tails hanging to one side. His eyes, beneath brows as thick and white as his beard, were extraordinary: dark, deep-set, and lit from within by a brightness that had nothing to do with the fading sun. They were the eyes of a man who had seen the world's wonders and the world's horrors in equal measure and had somehow retained the capacity to be astonished by both.

The children saw him first and ran toward him, shouting.

"Eyang Guru! Eyang Guru Batara!"

They knew him. Every Tuyul child in the Pasundan highlands knew the stories of the wandering sage, the ancient *empu* who appeared in the lembur once in a generation, bringing kembang api — fireworks — that painted the night sky in colors that no earthly flame could produce, and stories that made the wayang tales seem pale by comparison. He was human — fully human, one of the Big Folk — but he was something more than that, too. He was an empu, a master of the spiritual arts, a smith of keris blades whose knowledge spanned centuries and whose purposes were his own. It was said that in his youth he had wandered far — from the highlands of Sunda to the temple-mountains of Bali, where he had studied with the *balian* priests and learned the rites of the *homa* fire ceremony and the mysteries of the *lingga-yoni*. His path had wound through the sacred groves and smoking peaks of a dozen kingdoms before he took up his wandering staff and never set it down again. The Tuyul respected him. The Tuyul trusted him, as much as they trusted any outsider, which was to say: considerably, but with reservations.

Eyang Guru Batara looked down at the children swarming around his legs and smiled, and the smile transformed his face from something ancient and stern into something ancient and kind, which was a different thing entirely. He reached into his cloak and produced, from some unseen pocket, a handful of small paper cylinders. He tossed them gently into the air, and they burst in tiny pops of colored light — red, gold, green — that hung for a moment like jeweled insects before fading. The children shrieked with delight.

But above the smile, his eyes were grave.

He moved through the festival with the ease of a man accustomed to being both welcomed and watched, nodding to the elders, accepting a cup of wedang jahe from the women of the kitchen, pausing to listen to the angklung with an expression of genuine pleasure. But he was looking for someone, and it did not take him long to find him.

Uncle Kian had risen from his seat the moment the tall figure appeared at the edge of the common. His face had gone through a rapid succession of expressions — surprise, relief, fear, and something that might have been resignation — before settling into a mask of careful calm. He moved through the crowd toward the old sage, and the two of them met at the far edge of the common, beneath the eaves of the village meeting hall, where the torchlight did not quite reach and the shadows pooled like dark water.

They spoke in low voices.

Kian Santang watched from across the common, his nasi timbel forgotten on his knee. He could not hear their words — the angklung played on, the crowd laughed and chattered, the children chased the last sparks of Eyang Guru's kembang api — but he could read their bodies. His uncle stood rigid, one hand pressed to his chest where the wrapped shape lay hidden. Eyang Guru leaned close, one hand on the old Tuyul's shoulder, speaking with an urgency that seemed at odds with everything Kian Santang had ever heard about the wandering sage's legendary patience. At one point, Uncle Kian shook his head sharply — a refusal, or a denial — and Eyang Guru's grip on his shoulder tightened, and the old empu's face, caught for a moment in the flicker of a distant torch, was not kind at all. It was afraid.

Kian Santang had never seen fear on a face like that — a face that seemed built to contain every emotion except that one — and the sight of it sent a cold wire threading through his stomach.

Then Uncle Kian turned and walked away, back toward his house, moving quickly, his hand still pressed to his chest. Eyang Guru watched him go, standing motionless beneath the eaves, his staff held before him like a pillar, and in the torchlight his shadow stretched behind him, long and dark and ancient, and it did not look like the shadow of a man. It looked like the shadow of something older. Something that remembered the world when it was young.

---

Night fell over Lembur Padalarang the way it always did in the highlands — swiftly, gently, and with a sudden deepening of the sky that turned the stars from suggestions into declarations. The festival continued by torchlight and by the warm glow of oil lamps hung from the eaves of every house, and the lembur shimmered like a constellation that had fallen to earth and decided to stay.

The dalang arrived at dusk. He was a Tuyul man from a neighboring lembur — Pak Rahmat, old and thin and possessed of a voice that could shift from the thunderous rage of a demon king to the silken whisper of a princess in the space of a single breath. He set up his screen — a white cloth stretched between two poles — and behind it, he arranged his puppets: flat figures of painted leather, pierced and jointed, mounted on sticks of buffalo horn. The *blencong* oil lamp was lit and hung above the screen, and its flame cast a warm, unsteady light through the leather figures, projecting their shadows onto the cloth in shapes that were at once flat and impossibly alive.

The crowd gathered. Children sat cross-legged in the front, eyes wide. The elders settled behind them on their mats, and the young couples drifted to the edges of the crowd where the darkness offered a degree of privacy that the daylight did not.

Pak Rahmat began to chant. The story tonight was from the Mahabharata — the exile of the Pandawa brothers, those five righteous princes cast out of their kingdom by the treachery of their cousins, forced to wander the forests for thirteen years. It was a story of injustice and endurance, of good men tested by a world that did not reward goodness easily, and the dalang told it with a passion that made the shadows on the screen seem to breathe. In his telling, the old patterns of *dharma* and *karma* moved like invisible currents beneath the tale — the great cosmic wheel of action and consequence, the *samsara* of suffering through which even the noblest souls must pass before finding *moksha*, the final liberation. The gamelan struck the gong at each turning of the tale, and the sound hung in the night air like a prayer that had forgotten its words but remembered its meaning.

Kian Santang sat at the back of the crowd, watching the wayang. The leather puppets moved across the lit screen — Arjuna drawing his bow, Bima raising his mighty club, the demon Rahwana laughing from his throne of bones — and the blencong flame trembled in the night breeze, and the shadows trembled with it, and the line between the story and the world grew thin.

He watched, but he did not see the puppets.

He saw his uncle's hand, pressed flat against his chest.

He saw Eyang Guru Batara's face, caught in torchlight, wearing fear like a foreign garment.

He felt — and this was new, this was strange, this was a sensation he had no name for — a coldness. Not the cool of the highland night, which was familiar and welcome. Something else. A chill that seemed to come from nowhere and everywhere, as though the warm air of Pasundan had been punctured, just slightly, just enough, and through that puncture, something was breathing. Something distant. Something patient. Something that had been waiting for a very long time.

The angklung played on, soft now, accompanying the dalang's chant. The blencong flickered. The shadows of the wayang danced their eternal dance — good against evil, light against dark, the same story told a thousand thousand times because it was always true and never finished.

Kian Santang pulled his sarong tighter around his shoulders and looked toward his uncle's house. The door was closed. The windows were dark. But he could see, at the edge of the common, the tall silhouette of Eyang Guru Batara, standing alone, watching the wayang with eyes that did not seem to be watching the wayang at all. The old empu's gaze was turned eastward, beyond the screen, beyond the torchlight, beyond the lembur — toward the dark line of the mountains and whatever lay past them.

And the night breeze stirred the melati blossoms along the river, and their perfume rose through the village, sweet and heavy and sad, as though the flowers, too, sensed what was coming.

The wayang played on.

The shadows danced.

And in his house at the edge of Lembur Padalarang, Uncle Kian sat alone in the dark, holding something wrapped in old batik cloth, pressing it to his heart like a wound, whispering to it, rocking gently back and forth, as the music of the festival drifted through his walls and the stars turned slowly overhead and the world — the wide, dangerous, beautiful world beyond the highlands of Pasundan — drew one breath closer.
