# Bab 14: Ngarai Sianok
## (The Enchanted Canyon)

---

The canyon had no entrance that the eye could find, and yet they were inside it.

One moment the fellowship had been walking through ordinary forest -- secondary growth, dense and humid, the trees hung with strangler figs and the understory thick with ferns that reached to their waists. The next moment the trees parted, the ground dropped, and the world opened beneath them like a wound in the earth's green skin, and they stood at the rim of Ngarai Sianok.

It was not a gorge. It was not a valley. It was a revelation.

The canyon walls fell away on either side in sheer curtains of stone -- a hundred meters, two hundred, the depth difficult to judge because the rock itself defied simple measurement. The cliff faces were layered in strata of color that no geologist could have predicted: ochre and rust and the deep violet of crushed berries, veined with white quartz and green copper deposits that caught the late afternoon light and threw it back in patterns that resembled, if one looked too long, the brushstrokes of a vast and patient painter. Waterfalls cascaded from ledges at every level, some thin as silk threads, some broad as rivers turned on their sides, their water falling in slow arcs that the wind pulled into mist before they reached the canyon floor. The mist hung in the gorge like dupa smoke in a mountain sanctuary -- drifting, layering, glowing with the golden light that filtered down from the open sky above. There was something sacred in it, the haze of offerings rising from the earth's own altar, and Kian Santang felt the presence of the *hyang giri* -- the mountain spirit -- ancient and vast, breathing through the stone, watching through the water, holding the canyon in a grip that was not possessive but protective, the way a mother holds a sleeping child.

And the sound. The sound of Ngarai Sianok was not the roar of water or the echo of stone. It was a chord -- a sustained, layered, endlessly shifting harmony of falling water and wind in stone channels and the calls of birds that existed nowhere else in Nusantara, birds whose songs threaded through the canyon acoustics and emerged transformed, multiplied, braided into a music that was not quite natural and not quite supernatural but something in between. Something enchanted.

They descended by a path that appeared to have been carved from the living rock by hands that understood stone the way a carver of wayang understands leather -- not merely cutting but shaping, coaxing the material into forms that seemed to have been waiting within it all along. The steps were narrow but sure, each one fitted to the next with a precision that exceeded masonry. Ferns grew from the cracks between them, and small orchids clung to the rock face at eye level, their blooms the pale green of sea glass, trembling in the updraft from the canyon floor.

Kian Santang walked with one hand on the rock wall and the other pressed against the batik-wrapped bundle at his hip. The keris had been quiet since they entered the canyon's influence -- not silent, never truly silent, but subdued, its constant whisper dampened to a murmur so faint he could almost believe it was his own pulse. The canyon's *bayu* -- its spiritual force -- pressed against the keris's darkness the way water pressure presses against a diver's lungs: not violently, but absolutely, with a force that could not be resisted because it came from every direction at once. He whispered a prayer of gratitude to the *karuhun*, the ancestral guardians whose spirits dwelled in the deep places of the earth, asking their protection as the path steepened and the canyon walls closed in like the hands of the old ones themselves.

Bagus walked behind him, steadying himself with both hands, his broad face tilted upward at the canyon walls with an expression of such unguarded wonder that Cepot, three steps below, had stopped making jokes about the height. Even Cepot was quiet. The canyon demanded it -- not through threat but through presence, the way a great work of art demands silence simply by being what it is.

Raden Wijaya led the descent. He moved with his usual economy, but even his ranger's composure had softened at the edges. His dark eyes, normally scanning for danger with the automatic vigilance of long training, had taken on a different quality -- not relaxed, exactly, but opened, as though the canyon had found some locked room in him and turned the key. "*Edan tenan*," he muttered under his breath -- the Javanese battle-tongue, Ngoko, stripped of all formality, the language a warrior uses when something overwhelms even his discipline. Behind him, Dewi Anjani walked with her hand trailing along the stone wall, her amber eyes half-closed, and the faintest luminance played beneath her skin, as though the enchantment of the canyon was calling to the celestial light within her.

Tumenggung Batu climbed down with the sure-footed confidence of a creature born in mountains. His iron-shod boots found purchase on the narrow steps without hesitation, and his expression -- usually set in the permanent skepticism of a craftsman who trusted nothing he had not forged himself -- carried something that approached reverence. He touched the canyon stone with his thick, scarred fingers, and murmured something in the old Kurcaci tongue, and Kian Santang heard the word for *good stone* repeated three times, like a prayer.

Gajahmada descended last. His face was difficult to read. The general had said little since they entered the forest approach, and his silence was not the awed silence of the others but something more guarded -- the silence of a man who feels an enchantment working upon him and resists it, not because the enchantment is hostile, but because submission of any kind is foreign to his nature. His dark eyes moved restlessly across the canyon, measuring, assessing, and his jaw was set in the particular way it set when his thoughts were at war with one another.

They reached the canyon floor as the last light of the day turned the western cliff face to gold.

And she was waiting for them.

---

Nyai Roro Kidul stood at the edge of a *talaga* -- a sacred pool -- where three waterfalls converged, and the water at her feet was perfectly still.

This should not have been possible. The waterfalls poured into the pool from three directions, their cascades thundering against the rock, throwing spray high enough to wet the ferns on the upper ledges. The water should have churned. It should have foamed and roiled and surged outward in turbulent currents. Instead, it lay flat as a mirror -- a circle of absolute stillness at the center of the falling water, reflecting the canyon walls and the darkening sky above with a clarity that surpassed the original, as though the reflection were more real than the thing reflected.

The Queen of the Southern Sea stood upon a stone at the pool's edge, and the mist parted around her the way it parts around no mortal thing.

She was beautiful. It is necessary to say this plainly, because the word beautiful, overused and cheapened by a thousand lesser applications, was the only word that applied, and here it carried its full original weight -- the beauty that is not decorative but elemental, the beauty of deep water and high weather and the slow turning of stars. Her face was the face of the sea: calm on the surface, fathomless beneath, capable of infinite gentleness and infinite violence in the same breath. Her skin held the luminous brown of sunlight seen through a wave's crest. Her hair fell unbound past her waist, black as the ocean at midnight, and it moved -- not in the wind, for there was no wind that touched it, but with its own slow, tidal rhythm, as though currents invisible to every eye but hers ran through the air around her body.

She wore a kebaya of sea-green silk, fitted close to her form, fastened with kerongsang brooches of gold wrought in the shapes of seahorses and nautilus shells. Her kain was batik tulis of the highest order, its pattern a *hujan riris* -- the drizzle motif -- drawn not with the geometric repetitions of the workshop but with a design that seemed to have been drawn by the sea itself: wave upon wave, the pattern never quite repeating, each fold revealing new depths, new configurations of indigo and white and the green of deep water where sunlight fails. Her eyes were the green of the southern ocean on a clear day -- not the pale green of shallow water but the dark, saturated green of the deep, the green that holds within it blue and black and something that is not color at all but depth made visible.

She looked at them, and the look was like the sea: it received everything and reflected it back transformed.

"*Rahayu, sadayana nu sumping ka wanantara titipan hyang giri*," she said -- the archaic Sundanese of the old courts, elevated beyond even the highest *basa luhur*, a register that had not been spoken aloud in centuries, each word carrying the weight of tidal authority. *Blessings upon all who arrive in the deep wilderness entrusted by the mountain spirit.* Her voice carried the quality of water over stone -- liquid, continuous, shaped by what it touched. "You have come far. And you carry a heavy burden. Rest now. The canyon will hold you."

The eight companions stood before her, travel-worn and grief-raw -- for Eyang Guru Batara's absence was a wound that had not yet closed, a gap in their formation that each of them felt differently but all of them felt constantly -- and the weight of the road seemed, for the first time since they had left Dieng, to ease.

Not to lift. The weight was still there. The keris was still there. Krakatau was still ahead, and the miles between here and there were filled with Buta and Leak and darkness. But for a space -- a day, perhaps two, perhaps a time that the canyon measured differently than the world outside -- the weight settled into something bearable, and the fellowship breathed, and rested, and were held.

---

On the second night, Nyai Roro Kidul came to Kian Santang.

He had been sitting at the edge of the still pool, watching the water in which nothing moved. The canyon was dark above him, the cliff walls black against a sky studded with more stars than he had ever seen -- the enchantment stripped the air of haze, and the stars burned with a cold intensity that made them seem closer, as though the canyon were a well dug not downward but upward into the heavens.

She appeared without sound, without approach. One moment the stone beside him was empty; the next, she was there, seated upon it with the composed stillness of a figure carved in jade, her sea-green eyes catching the starlight and holding it.

"*Geura*, come," she said, and the single Sundanese word -- spoken in the archaic imperative, the command of queens -- carried more authority than any sentence in any language Kian Santang had ever heard. "There is something you must see."

She led him along the pool's edge to a place where the stone had been worn into a natural basin -- a shallow depression in the rock, wide as a man's reach, smooth as polished marble. It was empty. Dry. The ferns grew thick around it, and the three waterfalls fell on every side, their sound a curtain that enclosed this place in its own sphere of white noise, separate from the world.

Nyai Roro Kidul drew from her sash a conch shell -- large, spiraling, its surface the nacreous pink of a sunrise seen from beneath the waves. She walked to the nearest waterfall and held the shell beneath the cascade, and the water filled it, and when she returned to the stone basin, she poured.

The water that flowed from the conch was not the white, churning water of the falls. It was something else. It poured in a smooth, unbroken stream, perfectly clear, and where it touched the stone basin it did not splash but spread -- evenly, silently, filling the depression to the brim and then going still. Absolutely, perfectly still. The surface became glass. It became mirror. It became something more than mirror -- a surface that did not merely reflect light but received it, absorbed it, and returned it in forms the original light had never taken.

"This is the Cermin Laut Selatan," Nyai Roro Kidul said. "The Mirror of the Southern Sea. It shows what was, what is, and what may yet be." She looked at him, and her green eyes held a warning as old as the tides. "*Poma, mulah salah hartikeun, Kian Santang*," she said -- *Take heed, do not misunderstand, Kian Santang* -- in the basa luhur of the ancient queens. "The mirror does not counsel. It only reveals. What you see is not instruction. It is not prophecy. It is possibility -- and possibility is the most dangerous thing in the world, because it wears the face of certainty."

She gestured toward the still water. "Look."

Kian Santang knelt at the basin's edge and looked into the mirror.

---

The water showed him the past.

He saw the great alliance of the first age, and it was more terrible and more magnificent than the stone reliefs in Siliwangi's temple had suggested. Armies stretched across a landscape of volcanic fire -- Bidadari in silver armor descending from the sky like falling stars, Kurcaci in obsidian plate advancing in formations so tight they moved like single organisms, Tuyul fighters in leather and bronze holding the flanks with a ferocity that belied their small stature, human cavalry charging through corridors of flame. The Battle of the Straits. Krakatau erupting behind them, the sky ripped apart by fire, and at the center of it all, a young prince of Mataram -- Raden Kartawijaya, Raden Wijaya's ancestor, his face fierce and proud -- driving his keris into the hand of Raja Iblis and severing the dark blade from the shadow king's grip. The Keris Murka falling. The Shadow King screaming -- a sound the mirror transmitted not as sound but as sensation, a vibration in Kian Santang's bones that made his teeth ache. The obsidian throne cracking. The shadow king dissolving into smoke, driven downward, downward into the fire.

The image shifted. The water rippled once and went still.

The mirror showed him the present.

Gunung Merapi. The sacred volcano, once a place of pilgrimage and prayer, now transformed into a fortress of dark industry. In the caldera halls, forges burned with fire that was not orange but black -- a fire that consumed light rather than producing it. And before those forges stood Empu Gandring, and the sight of him was worse than anything the mirror had yet shown, because it was the ruin not of a stranger but of a legend. The great empu -- the master smith, the keeper of sacred craft, the man whose name Kian Santang had heard spoken with reverence in every lembur from Pasundan to Bali -- stood hunched over his anvil, and his face, which had once held the patient wisdom of a man who understood that metal was a living thing, was twisted now into something barely recognizable. His eyes burned with a feverish light. His lips moved constantly, muttering, arguing with voices only he could hear. His hands, which had once shaped blades of extraordinary beauty, now hammered forms into being that were obscenities of the smith's art -- weapons of cruelty, instruments of domination, and worse: flesh. Buta Raksasa emerging from the forge-smoke, their bodies assembled from corrupted *sakti* and dark iron and the stolen life-force of captured peoples. An army being bred in the bowels of the mountain, growing, massing, waiting.

The image shifted again. Kian Santang gripped the basin's edge.

He saw Pasundan. His own highland country, its terraced sawahs green and golden in the afternoon light, and for one moment his heart leaped because it was home, it was the landscape of every memory he treasured -- but the moment broke, because the image was not a memory. It was now. The Buta came across the eastern ridge in a black tide, their crude weapons raised, their tusked mouths open in war-cries that the mirror rendered as a pressure in his chest. He saw Lembur Padalarang -- his lembur, his home, the place where he had grown rice and climbed waringin trees and drunk wedang jahe on cold evenings -- and the thatched roofs were burning. The sawah terraces were trampled. Small figures ran through the smoke -- Tuyul, his people, his neighbors -- and the Buta were among them, and the mirror showed everything, spared nothing, and Kian Santang's hands shook on the stone, and the water did not ripple, because the mirror did not care about his grief. It only revealed.

The image shifted a final time.

The mirror showed him the future.

One future. One possibility. But it felt as real as the stone beneath his knees.

He saw Krakatau. The volcano in full eruption -- not the destructive fury of a natural event, but something shaped, directed, the fire itself becoming an instrument. The keris rising. A hand -- small, familiar, his own hand -- lifting the Keris Murka above the caldera's rim. The blade descending into the fire. A blinding light, white and absolute, swallowing everything -- the mountain, the sea, the sky -- and in that light, the annihilation of the darkness. The pamor patterns unwinding. The seven corruptions burning away. The keris unmaking itself, and taking the Shadow King's power with it, forever.

Hope surged in him. It could be done. It could be --

The light faded.

The mirror showed another future.

The same caldera. The same hand. But in this vision, the hand did not release the keris. The fingers tightened. The blade turned. And the fire of Krakatau did not consume the keris but flowed into it, filling the seven pamor patterns with volcanic power, and the hand that held the blade began to glow with a light that was not white but red, deep as forge-coals, and the figure holding the keris rose -- rose above the caldera, above the smoke, above the shattered rim of the mountain -- and stood against the burning sky in silhouette, and the silhouette was his.

Kian Santang. On the obsidian throne of Krakatau. The Keris Murka in his right hand, its blade blazing with seven fires. A crown of shadow and volcanic glass upon his brow. His eyes -- his kind, frightened, ordinary eyes -- burning red as embers, and in them no fear, no doubt, no grief.

No grief.

That was the worst of it. The figure on the throne was not suffering. It was not enslaved, not puppet-mastered, not screaming against its chains. The Kian Santang on the obsidian throne sat with the calm, settled authority of one who had found his purpose. His face held peace. His hands were steady. He looked out across a Nusantara that knelt before him -- every kingdom, every lembur, every last human and Tuyul and Kurcaci bowing in silent submission -- and his expression was one of deep, satisfied contentment. The wars were over. The Buta obeyed him. The Leak served him. The sea serpents lay still in their trenches, awaiting his command. One ruler. One will. One peace -- enforced, absolute, unbreakable.

The keris whispered. Not in the vision, but in his hand, the real keris, the one wrapped in batik cloth at his hip, pulsing against his body with a warmth that was almost affectionate: *You could end all suffering. All war. One ruler. One peace. Is that not what you want? Is that not what they all want -- the farmers, the mothers, the children who cry in the night? An end to fear? You could give them that. You could give them everything.*

And the Kian Santang on the throne smiled.

Kian Santang recoiled from the mirror.

He threw himself backward from the basin's edge with a violence that sent him sprawling on the wet stone, his hands clawing at the ferns, his chest heaving, his eyes wide and blind with terror. Not the terror of threat. The terror of recognition. The figure on the throne had not been a stranger wearing his face. It had been him -- the same heart, the same desires, the same love for his people turned inward and twisted until protection became possession, until care became control, until the humble farmer who wanted nothing but his garden became the tyrant who would cultivate the world.

Nyai Roro Kidul caught him. Her hands closed on his shoulders -- strong hands, cool as ocean water -- and steadied him, and the contact broke the vision's hold the way a bucket of seawater breaks a fever.

"Breathe," she said. Her voice was the tide: steady, rhythmic, ancient. "Breathe, Kian Santang."

He breathed. His body shook. The keris whispered, and he could not tell where the whisper ended and his own heartbeat began.

"I saw --" he started, and could not finish.

"I know what you saw." Her voice carried no judgment. The sea judges nothing. "You saw what the keris wants you to become. The throne it has been building for you since the moment it touched your hand."

He looked up at her, and his eyes were wild. "The worst part," he whispered. "The worst part was that I looked -- that he looked -- *content*."

Nyai Roro Kidul released his shoulders and settled beside him on the stone, her legs folded beneath her, her batik kain spreading across the rock like a tide pool filling. When she spoke, her voice was quieter, meant for him alone.

"That you feared it means you are not yet lost," she said. "The keris promises peace. But the peace of one ruler is not peace. It is the stillness of the sea floor, where nothing moves because everything has drowned."

---

They sat together at the edge of the still pool, the mirror of the Southern Sea dark and quiet now, reflecting nothing but the stars. The waterfalls fell around them, and the canyon breathed, and the night was very old.

"I must tell you something," Nyai Roro Kidul said. "So that you understand. So that you do not feel alone in what you carry."

She turned her gaze to the water, and her sea-green eyes held a depth that was not metaphorical but actual -- layers upon layers of memory, compressed by centuries into something denser than stone.

"The Shadow King came to me once. Long ago, in the age before the first alliance, when his power was uncontested and the peoples of Nusantara lived under his shadow. He came to the southern waters. He walked upon the waves -- the only being who has ever done so without my permission -- and he stood before my throne beneath the sea, and he offered me the keris."

She paused. The waterfalls fell. The ferns trembled.

"He offered me dominion. Not merely over the southern waters, which were already mine, but over all waters. Every ocean, every river, every rain that falls, every spring that rises. And through the waters, dominion over the land, for no land exists without water, and no life persists without the sea. He said I would be queen of everything. Queen of the archipelago, queen of the world beyond it, queen of every living thing that drinks or swims or sails."

Her voice did not change in volume, but something in its quality shifted -- a deepening, as though the ocean itself were speaking through her, vast and dark and honest.

"I wanted it."

The words fell into the silence like stones into deep water.

"I wanted it, Kian Santang. Do not imagine that I stood before his offer and felt nothing. I am the sea. I am hunger. I am the force that erodes mountains and swallows kingdoms and grinds stone to sand across the centuries. The desire for more is not foreign to my nature -- it *is* my nature. Every tide that rises yearns to rise higher. Every wave that breaks upon the shore wishes it could break further."

She looked at him, and in her eyes the green had darkened to the color of the deep trenches, the abyssal places where no light reaches and the pressure crushes everything to its essential form.

"If I had taken the keris, I would have become queen of all waters, all lands, all peoples. And I would have been terrible." Her voice dropped to a murmur that was somehow more audible than a shout. "Beautiful and terrible as the tsunami. As the earthquake beneath the sea. As the wave that rises higher than the coconut palms and swallows the coast and leaves nothing -- not the houses, not the boats, not the children playing in the shallows. All would love me. All would worship me. And all would despair."

The silence that followed was absolute. Even the waterfalls seemed to fall more quietly, as though the canyon itself were listening.

"I refused," she said. "Not because I lacked desire. But because I recognized the desire itself as the trap. The keris does not corrupt through force. It corrupts through truth -- it finds the truest thing in you, the deepest want, the most righteous hunger, and it feeds that hunger until it becomes insatiable. My love for the sea became the desire to be all seas. Your love for your people will become the desire to rule all peoples. The keris does not lie, Kian Santang. It tells you exactly what you want. And that is why it is the most dangerous weapon ever forged."

Kian Santang sat with these words, and they settled into him the way water settles into stone -- slowly, finding every crack, filling every hollow.

Then, without fully understanding why, he reached for the batik-wrapped bundle at his hip. He unwound the cloth with trembling hands. He held the keris -- sheathed, its warangka of ancient sandalwood smooth beneath his fingers -- and extended it toward Nyai Roro Kidul.

"If you... if you would take it, Nyai," he said. His voice was unsteady. "You are powerful enough. You could carry it to Krakatau. You could cast it into the fire. You have already refused it once -- you know its tricks, you know its whisper. You could resist it where I --" His voice cracked. "Where I am not sure I can."

Nyai Roro Kidul looked at the keris in his outstretched hands. For a long moment she was still -- so still that she might have been a statue, a figure carved from living coral, unbreathing, unchanging. And in that stillness, Kian Santang saw something move behind her eyes: the desire. Ancient and oceanic and vast as the southern waters. The wanting that she had just described to him, alive in her still, unfaded by centuries of refusal.

She could take it. She was strong enough. The knowledge was there in her eyes, clear as the mirror that had shown him his darkest future.

Then she smiled. And the smile was the saddest thing Kian Santang had ever seen -- sadder than the burning of Padalarang, sadder than the loss of Eyang Guru, because it was the sadness of someone who understood themselves completely and bore that understanding without flinching.

"I could," she said. "And I would carry it. And somewhere between this canyon and Krakatau, I would use it. Not from weakness. From strength. From the certainty that I could wield it better than anyone else, that my will was strong enough, that my cause was just enough. And that certainty would be my undoing." She reached out and placed her cool hands over his, and gently -- with a gentleness that contained the restrained force of every tide that has ever turned -- she pushed the keris back toward him.

"It must be you, Kian Santang. Small and gentle and wanting nothing but your garden. You are the keris's antidote. Not because you are strong enough to resist its temptation, but because the thing it tempts you with -- power, dominion, the throne -- is so far from what you truly want that the distance itself becomes your shield. The keris cannot corrupt a desire that does not exist. It can only amplify what is already there."

She released his hands. The keris settled against his hip, and its whisper resumed -- faint, patient, constant.

"You will doubt yourself," she said. "Every day, every hour, the keris will show you versions of yourself that you fear you could become. But remember this, child of Pasundan: you looked upon the obsidian throne, and you did not want it. You saw yourself crowned in darkness, and you recoiled. Hold onto that recoil. It is more precious than any weapon, any power, any throne. It is the truest thing you have."

---

In another part of the canyon, in the hollow between two waterfalls where the sound of falling water created a chamber of white noise that drowned all other sound, Gajahmada walked.

He could not sleep. He had not slept properly since the fellowship entered Ngarai Sianok, and the enchantment that soothed the others only sharpened his unease. There was something in the canyon's peace that grated against him -- not because it was false, but because it was genuine, and genuine peace was something Gajahmada had not permitted himself since the day the Buta crossed the Brantas and the eastern villages began to burn. Peace, to a general at war, is an indulgence. Rest is a tactical decision, not a state of being. And the canyon's insistence on serenity felt, to him, like a hand pressing gently but firmly against his chest, urging him to sit, to breathe, to let go -- and he could not let go. He would not. Not while Majapahit bled.

The keris whispered to him.

It was not the first time. The whisper had begun on the road from the bridge -- the terrible bridge where Eyang Guru had fallen, and the fellowship had been reduced from nine to eight, and the old sage's staff had cracked like a gunshot against the stone and the darkness had taken him. Since that day, the keris had found Gajahmada's frequency. Not the general's ear -- his mind. His ambition. His love.

Because that was what it was: love. Gajahmada loved Majapahit the way a father loves a child -- fiercely, possessively, with a willingness to sacrifice anything, including himself, for its survival. And the keris knew this. The keris tasted this love the way the sea tastes the river that feeds it, drawing the fresh water into the salt, dissolving the boundary between devotion and desire.

*You could save them*, the whisper said. Not in words. In images. In feelings. In the slow, insidious certainty that seeped into his thoughts like water through limestone. *The Tuyul cannot carry this burden. He is small. He is afraid. He will fail, and when he fails, Majapahit will fall, and the blood of your people will be on his small, trembling hands. But you -- you have the strength. You have the will. You have the Sumpah Palapa. Take the keris. Carry it yourself. Not to Krakatau -- why destroy power when power is what Nusantara needs? Carry it to the battlefield. Raise it against the Buta. Unite the archipelago not through councils and debates and the slow, maddening consensus of *sabha*, but through the clean, decisive exercise of righteous force. One will. One blade. One Nusantara.*

Gajahmada stood at the edge of Kian Santang's sleeping area. He did not remember walking there. The Tuyul slept fitfully on a bed of ferns, the batik-wrapped keris tucked against his body, his small hands curled around it protectively even in sleep. Bagus slept beside him, close enough to touch, his broad back turned toward the night, his breathing deep and steady.

Gajahmada looked at the keris.

His hand twitched.

It was a small motion -- a flexing of the fingers, an involuntary opening and closing of the fist, the body's rehearsal of an action the mind had not yet sanctioned. He stared at his own hand as though it belonged to a stranger, watching the fingers curl and uncurl, and the keris whispered, and the whisper was shaped exactly like his love for Majapahit, and he could not tell where one ended and the other began.

"The canyon sees all hearts, General."

The voice came from behind him, quiet and layered with that peculiar harmonic that marked the Bidadari -- not quite a single voice, not quite many, but something in between. Dewi Anjani stood in the shadow of the canyon wall, her amber eyes catching the starlight. She was still armored -- she slept in her armor, if she slept at all -- and her moonstone blade hung at her hip, and her face held no accusation, only a vigilance that was older than his ambition and colder than his pride.

"*Mulah diganggu anu keur hees*," she said -- *One must not disturb those who sleep* -- in the elevated *basa luhur*, the highest Sundanese register, the language of celestial beings addressing mortals, each word carrying the formal weight of a decree pronounced from the silver courts above the clouds. "Be careful what you reach for."

Gajahmada turned from the sleeping area. His face was dark with a fury that was not directed at Anjani but at himself -- at the hand that had moved without his permission, at the desire that grew in him like a vine through a wall, cracking the stone of his discipline from within. He said nothing. There was nothing to say that would not be a confession or a lie. He stalked past her into the dark, his boots ringing on the canyon stone, and she watched him go, and her ancient eyes held neither judgment nor mercy, only the clear, cold sight of one who has watched ambition consume better men than Gajahmada across the long centuries.

She said nothing more. She did not need to.

The warning had been spoken, and the night recorded it, and the canyon remembered.

---

On the morning of their departure, Nyai Roro Kidul summoned the fellowship to the place where the still pool met the river's head -- the point where the enchanted waters gathered themselves and began to flow south, toward the coast, toward the sea, toward Krakatau.

She stood upon the stone where they had first seen her, but she was changed. She wore a different kebaya -- this one the deep blue-black of the ocean at midnight, sewn with threads of silver that traced the constellations of the southern sky across the silk. Her hair was bound now, woven into a single braid that fell like a rope of black water down her back, and at her brow she wore a circlet of white coral set with a single pearl the size of a quail's egg, luminous and faintly green.

Before her, on a stone slab covered with a *boeh siang* -- a sacred daylight cloth -- of indigo batik, lay eight objects. Sesajen -- offerings. Gifts. But not the sesajen of flowers and rice and incense that the Tuyul knew from lembur ceremonies. These were artifacts of a power older than kingdoms, each one carrying the faint hum of the canyon's enchantment, each one made for the hand that would receive it.

She called them forward one by one.

"Kian Santang."

He approached, and she placed in his hands a phial. It was small -- no larger than his thumb -- and it was made of sea glass: glass that the ocean had shaped through decades of tumbling in the surf, its edges smoothed, its surface frosted, its color the pale blue-green of shallow water over white sand. Inside the glass, something shone. Not liquid. Not flame. A light. A cold, clear, steady light that had no visible source, that neither flickered nor dimmed, that illuminated nothing outside the glass but burned with an intensity that suggested it could illuminate everything, if it chose.

"Light of the old stars," Nyai Roro Kidul said. "Captured in the foam of creation, when the first waves broke upon the first shores and the sky was so young that the stars wept at their own brightness. This light remembers the world before shadow." She folded his small fingers around the phial. "It will shine when all other lights fail."

Kian Santang placed the sea glass phial beside the Telaga Warna phial that Prabu Siliwangi had given him -- two talismans of light, one from the mountain, one from the sea. The keris whispered its displeasure, faint and petulant, and he ignored it.

"Bagus."

The stocky Tuyul stepped forward, his face solemn but his eyes bright, and Nyai Roro Kidul placed in his cupped hands a pouch of woven sea-silk, small enough to fit in his palm. Inside was soil. Dark, rich, fragrant soil that smelled of the canyon floor after rain -- of fern and fungus and the deep, sweet decay that is not death but transformation, the earth's patient alchemy of turning fallen things into growing things.

"Soil of Ngarai Sianok," she said. "Plant this in any earth, no matter how barren, and things will grow. Fruit and flower and leaf. You carry the garden with you, Bagus. Wherever you go, the green follows."

Bagus held the pouch against his chest, and his broad face cracked into a smile so wide and so genuine that even Tumenggung Batu looked away, slightly embarrassed by its nakedness. "*Nembah pisan, Nyai*," Bagus said softly -- *I bow deeply, Nyai* -- in the respectful *basa lemes* that one uses when speaking to elders and those of high station, the Sundanese register of deference and gratitude.

"Raden Wijaya."

The ranger knelt, and she placed across his open palms a coil of rope. It was thin -- no thicker than a woman's finger -- and it shimmered with the nacreous sheen of raw silk, but this was not silk spun by any worm. It was sea-silk: byssus, the thread of the noble pen shell, harvested from the deep waters of the southern ocean, woven by hands that knew the sea's own tensile strength. It was light as air in his hands. It was also, as Raden Wijaya tested with a sharp tug, completely unbreakable.

"Woven from the anchor-threads of creatures that hold fast against the strongest currents in the world," she said. "It will not break. Not by blade, not by fire, not by the strength of any Buta. Use it well."

"Dewi Anjani."

The Bidadari warrior stepped forward, and the two ancient beings -- the Queen of the Sea and the daughter of the Sky -- regarded each other with a recognition that transcended introduction. They had met before. They had perhaps always been meeting, across the centuries, in the places where water and sky touch.

Nyai Roro Kidul placed in Anjani's hands three arrows. They were slender and perfectly balanced, fletched with the feathers of a bird Kian Santang could not identify -- iridescent black, shifting to green at certain angles -- and their tips were not iron or bronze but something darker, denser, pitted with tiny craters. Meteorite iron. Metal that had fallen from the sky, forged by no earthly fire, carrying the *sakti* of the void between stars.

"These will fly true against any darkness," Nyai Roro Kidul said. "No enchantment can deflect them. No shadow can swallow their path. Three arrows, Dewi Anjani. Use them wisely, for there will be no more."

Anjani accepted the arrows with a bow that was formal, ancient, and between equals.

"Tumenggung Batu."

The Kurcaci chieftain stomped forward, his iron boots ringing on the stone, and Nyai Roro Kidul placed in his broad, scarred hand a stone. It was small, dark, unremarkable -- a lodestone, rough-surfaced, the kind of stone a child might pick up and throw into a river without a second glance. But when Tumenggung Batu held it, it pulled. Not toward the north, as a common lodestone would. It pulled south and east, insistently, like a living thing straining toward something it loved.

"It points toward Krakatau," she said. "Always. Through fog, through storm, through the deceptions of the Leak and the illusions of the dark craft. This stone knows where your enemy dwells, and it will not lie."

Tumenggung Batu turned the stone in his fingers, feeling its pull, and grunted his approval -- the highest compliment in the Kurcaci emotional vocabulary.

"Gajahmada."

The general stepped forward, and his face was guarded, his dark eyes wary. He had not spoken of his midnight vigil at the edge of Kian Santang's sleeping place, but the canyon had seen, and the queen had listened.

Nyai Roro Kidul held out a belt. It was gold -- not the bright, garish gold of ornament, but the deep, warm gold of ancient metalwork, its surface patterned with the wave-and-wind motif of the southern ocean. It was wide enough to sit comfortably at the waist, and its clasp was a pair of interlocking nautilus shells, each one spiraling inward toward its center with the mathematical perfection that nature achieves and artifice can only envy.

"Wear this," she said, and her voice carried a note that was new -- not the warmth she had shown the others, but something more complex. Compassion, and caution, and the particular firmness of a healer who administers medicine that the patient does not yet know they need. "It will protect you."

Gajahmada took the belt. As he fastened it around his waist, something shifted in the air around him -- a subtle lessening of pressure, as though a hand that had been slowly tightening around his chest had eased its grip. The keris's whisper, which had been a constant companion since the bridge, dimmed. Not silenced. Dimmed. Reduced from a voice to a murmur, from a murmur to a hum, from a hum to something that could, with effort, be ignored.

He looked at Nyai Roro Kidul, and in his eyes was a gratitude he could not speak, because to speak it would be to admit the need, and Gajahmada was not yet ready to admit how close the need had come to consuming him.

She met his gaze and said nothing. The sea does not explain its currents.

"Cepot. Dawala."

The two young Tuyul approached together -- Cepot with his usual swagger, slightly undermined by the awe that the canyon and its queen had finally succeeded in pressing into his irrepressible features, and Dawala at his heels, round-faced and wide-eyed, looking as though he had wandered into a legend and was still trying to find the exit.

Nyai Roro Kidul placed in each of their hands a dagger. They were identical in craft -- short-bladed, single-edged, hilted in dark hardwood wrapped with silver wire. The blades were old metal, Nusantara steel, forged in an age when the smiths still knew the songs that made iron remember its purpose. They were small. They were also, as Cepot discovered when he ran his thumb experimentally along the edge, extraordinarily sharp.

"Ow," said Cepot.

"Sharp as truth," Nyai Roro Kidul said, with the ghost of a smile. "And truth, in the days ahead, will be the sharpest weapon of all."

Dawala held his dagger with both hands, his face luminous with a solemnity that made him look, for the first time in the fellowship's acquaintance, like a warrior rather than a boy.

---

The boats were waiting at the river's head.

They were called *lembut* -- the gentle ones -- and in their dormant form they were nothing more than leaves. Large leaves, as long as a man's forearm, broad and thick-veined, the deep green of old growth forest. Nyai Roro Kidul's attendants -- silent figures who moved through the canyon like currents through water, barely visible, more presence than person -- had placed three leaves upon the river's surface.

The leaves became boats.

It was not a transformation in the dramatic sense -- no flash of light, no crack of thunder. It was a becoming. The leaves touched the water, and the water accepted them, and they grew. The veins became ribs. The broad surface became hull. The stem became prow, curving upward and forward with the elegant sweep of a jukung sailing canoe. Within the space of a breath, three boats rocked gently at the river's edge -- each one large enough to carry three passengers and their provisions, each one the pale green of its leaf-origin, each one buoyant and stable and alive in a way that wood and canvas could never be.

The fellowship loaded their few possessions. Kian Santang and Bagus and Cepot took the first boat. Raden Wijaya and Dewi Anjani and Tumenggung Batu took the second. Gajahmada and Dawala took the third, and the extra space held the provisions that the canyon had provided -- dried fruit, smoked fish, rice wrapped in banana leaves, gourds of fresh water. Food for the journey south. Food for the road to Krakatau.

They pushed off from the bank. The current took them gently -- not rushing, not pulling, but guiding, the way a mother's hand guides a child's first steps. The canyon walls rose on either side, immense and layered, their strata of color catching the morning light. Waterfalls fell in silver threads from the upper ledges. The birds of Ngarai Sianok sang their impossible songs, and the sound echoed from wall to wall, multiplying, braiding, until the canyon itself seemed to be singing -- a farewell, a blessing, a last gift of beauty before the world demanded its price.

Kian Santang looked up.

Nyai Roro Kidul stood on the cliff edge above them, a hundred meters up, at the point where the canyon rim met the open sky. Her midnight-blue kebaya caught the wind -- a wind that touched nothing else, that moved no leaf, no fern, no strand of any other hair. It was her wind. The sea's wind. It lifted her braid and spread it behind her like a banner of black water against the morning sky.

She sang.

It was a *kinanti* -- a *kawih bwatuha*, one of the ancient songs, the pupuh of longing and hope whose very name means *waiting*, whose measured verses carry the ache of separation and the stubborn faith that what is loved will be found again. Her voice carried down the canyon in a sustained, unwavering line of melody shaped by the kinanti's strict prosody -- eight syllables, then eight, then eight, the pattern repeating like waves against a shore, each line carrying its regulated vowel endings the way the sea carries salt: invisibly, essentially, inseparably. The melody was not pelog, not slendro, but something older, something that predated the formalization of music into systems and scales. It rose and fell with the rhythm of waves. It carried the smell of salt and the sound of deep water.

*Kinanti nu mapay cai,*
*leumpang jauh mapay sagara,*
*hate nu nyimpan harepan,*
*sok sanajan poek jalan...*

*Kinanti who follows the water, / walking far toward the sea, / a heart that keeps hope, / even though the road is dark...*

The kinanti's musicality was unlike any other pupuh form -- neither the martial drive of pangkur nor the philosophical weight of sinom, but something gentler and more persistent, a melody that did not demand attention but earned it through patience, each verse building upon the last like waves accumulating on a rising tide. It was the sea itself, given voice, singing farewell to travelers who were leaving its protection and entering the world of fire.

The words, woven through the kinanti's formal structure, were in the archaic Sundanese of the old courts, but the meaning was clear in the way that the meaning of the sea is always clear, even to those who have never seen it: *Go well. Be brave. Remember the water. The sea waits for all travelers, at the end of all journeys, and the sea does not forget.*

The kinanti followed them down the canyon. It echoed from the stone walls, layered upon itself, became a choir of one voice singing in harmony with its own reflections. The waterfalls provided its accompaniment. The birds fell silent, deferring to the queen.

As the kinanti faded, a new melody rose -- an *asmarandana*, a *kawih lalanguan* -- a song of pleasure and beauty, whose name itself means *the feeling of being in love*, whose lilting eight-line stanzas carry the particular ache of beauty glimpsed in the moment of leaving. Nyai Roro Kidul's voice shifted into its more intimate register, the asmarandana's shorter phrases and sweeter intervals weaving through the canyon like silk through water:

*Asmarandana ti sagara,*
*caina herang ti giri,*
*nu ayu ngarangkul bumi,*
*ngajaga anu leumpang...*

*Asmarandana from the ocean, / clear water from the mountain, / the beautiful one embraces the earth, / guarding those who walk...*

The asmarandana was beauty made audible -- the canyon itself transformed into an instrument, each waterfall a note, each stone wall a resonance chamber, the whole vast gorge singing with the queen's voice until the boundary between singer and landscape dissolved entirely.

Cepot, for once in his life, said nothing. Dawala wept openly. Bagus gripped the gunwale of the leaf-boat and stared straight ahead, his jaw set, his eyes bright. Raden Wijaya bowed his head. Dewi Anjani closed her eyes, and the luminance beneath her skin pulsed once, faintly, in answer. Tumenggung Batu cleared his throat several times and blamed the canyon mist. Gajahmada listened with his hand on the golden belt at his waist, and something in his hard face softened, and for a moment the general disappeared and the man was visible -- vulnerable, mortal, afraid.

Kian Santang whispered to Bagus as the melody faded: "*Kami kudu terus, priya*" -- *We must go on, companion* -- in the easy *basa loma* of old companions, the informal Sundanese of equals, of childhood, of trust too deep for formality.

The songs faded. The canyon walls began to recede, falling away on either side as the river widened, the stone giving way to earth, the enchanted strata of color yielding to ordinary brown soil and common green vegetation. The waterfalls thinned, then ceased. The impossible birds fell behind. The light changed -- from the golden, filtered radiance of the canyon to the flat, white glare of the open sky.

The air changed.

They felt it on their skin -- the shift from enchantment to the mortal world. The air grew heavier. Warmer. Thicker, as though the atmosphere itself carried a burden it had been spared within the canyon's walls. And at the edges of the warmth, at the very limit of perception, a scent: sulfur. Faint and distant, carried on a wind from the east, from the direction of the volcanoes, from the direction of Krakatau. The scent of fire sleeping. The scent of the enemy.

The river widened further. The current quickened. Ahead, through the thinning trees, the fellowship could see the flat glitter of open water -- the coast, the Java Sea, and beyond it, barely visible on the eastern horizon, a smudge of dark cloud that was not cloud but smoke, rising from a source that lay below the curve of the earth, beyond the Sunda Strait, in the place where their journey would end.

Kian Santang sat in the prow of the first leaf-boat. The keris lay against his hip, wrapped in its batik cloth, whispering. The sea glass phial hung at his chest beside the Telaga Warna phial, two points of captured light against the growing dark. The golden belt circled Gajahmada's waist, holding back a hunger the general had not yet named. The lodestone in Tumenggung Batu's pocket pulled south and east, patient, insistent, pointing the way.

He thought of the mirror. The obsidian throne. His own face, content in darkness. The crown of shadow upon his brow. The red eyes that had once been his -- kind, frightened, ordinary -- transformed by a power that promised everything and delivered only dominion.

He swore silently, in the privacy of his own heart, in a whisper that no ear could hear and no mirror could show: *I will not become that.*

The keris heard him. The keris always heard him.

*You will*, it whispered back. And the whisper was patient, and gentle, and absolutely certain.

The leaf-boats carried the fellowship out of the enchanted canyon and into the widening river, and the mortal world closed around them like water over a stone, and ahead lay the sea, and the strait, and the fire, and the end of all things, or the beginning.
