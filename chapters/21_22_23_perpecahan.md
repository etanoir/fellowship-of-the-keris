# Bab 21: Pengorbanan

## (The Sacrifice)

---

### I. The Cairn

The silence after battle is a sound unto itself.

It is not the absence of noise but the presence of aftermath -- the creak of cooling stone, the drip of blood from wounds not yet tended, the ragged breathing of the living taking stock of what the dead have paid. The seven who remained stood among the ruins of the watchtower on the volcanic island's eastern ridge, and the silence pressed against them with a weight that had nothing to do with the air and everything to do with the shape that lay at their center, motionless on the black stone, still wearing the expression of a man who had made his choice and was satisfied with the terms.

Gajahmada of Majapahit. General. Warrior. Keeper of the Sumpah Palapa.

Dead.

His body lay where it had fallen -- between Kian Santang and the last Buta Raksasa, his sword still in his hand, his blood darkening the volcanic rock in a pattern that, in the strange logic of violence, resembled a map of the very archipelago he had sworn to unite. The wound that had killed him was terrible -- a blow from the Buta's stone mace that had caved in the left side of his breastplate and the ribs beneath -- but his face was oddly peaceful. Not serene, not at rest, nothing so gentle. It was the face of a man who had spent his entire life calculating odds and had, at the final moment, found the arithmetic acceptable: his life for the keris-bearer's. A clean trade. A soldier's bargain.

Kian Santang looked at that face and thought of samsara -- the great wheel of death and rebirth that the old resi priests taught, the cycle that turns and turns through kalpas beyond counting. Gajahmada's atma, his eternal self, had departed this body. Where on the wheel would it alight next? In what form would his courage and his pride and his terrible, beautiful capacity for both betrayal and redemption be born again? The thought was not comfort, exactly. But it was continuity -- the assurance that what Gajahmada had been would not simply end, that the wheel would carry him forward, that the karma of his final act -- the shield of his body between the darkness and the light -- would shape whatever came next.

Bagus was the first to move. He knelt beside the body and straightened Gajahmada's cloak with careful hands, pulling the fabric across the ruined armor, covering the wound the way one draws a blanket over a sleeping child. His broad face was wet, though whether from tears or the salt spray that drifted up from the cliffs below, he did not say. He placed Gajahmada's sword across the general's chest, both hands folded over the hilt, and stepped back.

Raden Wijaya stood apart, gripping his injured arm. The cut ran from shoulder to elbow -- a Buta blade had caught him during the fighting -- and the blood seeped through his fingers in slow, steady pulses. He watched Bagus tend the body, and his jaw worked, and his dark eyes held the particular anguish of a commander who has lost a man under his watch and knows that the strategic calculus that permitted the loss does not diminish its cost by a single grain.

Dewi Anjani moved among the wounded. Her hands, slender and sure, pressed strips of cloth torn from her own cloak against Tumenggung Batu's cracked armor where the broken edges had cut into his shoulder. The Kurcaci chieftain sat on a fallen pillar and endured her ministrations with the rigid patience of a man who would rather bleed to death than admit he needed help, his stone-grey face fixed in an expression that might have been pain or might have been something else entirely.

Cepot sat on the ground with his back against a wall, pressing a wadded cloth to the gash on his forehead. Blood had run down the left side of his face and dried in a dark crust along his jaw, giving him the appearance of a small, disreputable warrior-poet who had lost an argument with a door. Dawala crouched beside him, wrapping a bandage around Cepot's forearm where a second, smaller cut wept quietly.

"It should have been me standing there," Cepot said. His voice was flat, emptied of its usual theatrics, and the absence of performance was more disturbing than any grief. "Not him. He was worth ten of me."

"Don't," Dawala said quietly. "Don't do that."

"*Mulah kitu, Cepot,*" Bagus added softly -- *Don't be like that, Cepot* -- the basa loma carrying the gentle weight of a friend who would not let another friend diminish himself in grief.

Kian Santang stood at the edge of the ruin, looking out across the dark water toward the shape on the horizon that was Krakatau. The keris hummed against his hip -- a low, satisfied vibration, the purr of something that had fed well on violence and death and wanted more. He could feel the blade's awareness reaching toward the volcanic island, straining like a compass needle drawn to its pole. So close now. Close enough that the connection between the keris and its place of forging resonated in Kian Santang's bones, a frequency that made his teeth ache and his vision blur at the edges.

He had not wept. He was not sure he could. The keris had been eating at him -- not his body but his capacity for the softer things, the human things, the tears and trembling that belonged to a person who had not spent weeks carrying a blade that whispered of empires and ashes. The grief was there. He could feel it pressing against the walls of something inside him, a dam that held. But he did not know if the dam was his own strength or the keris's influence, and the uncertainty was itself a kind of horror.

Gajahmada had died for him. Gajahmada, who had wanted the keris -- who had looked at it with the hunger of a man who sees the instrument of his ambition and cannot look away -- had, in the end, placed his body between that ambition and a small Tuyul from Padalarang. The keris had whispered to Gajahmada for weeks. It had offered him everything. And he had said no. Not with words -- Gajahmada was not a man of words but of actions -- but with his body, with his blood, with the final arithmetic of sacrifice.

That was worth weeping for. Kian Santang pressed his marked palm against his chest and breathed.

---

### II. The Stone Marker

It was Tumenggung Batu who built the cairn.

This surprised no one who understood the Kurcaci, and everyone who did not. The chieftain and the general had argued from the moment they had met -- a grinding, persistent friction that had accompanied the fellowship like the sound of stones rubbing together in a river, constant and abrasive and, in retrospect, a kind of music. They had disagreed about tactics, about routes, about the proper way to fortify a camp, about whether ale or rice wine was the superior drink, about matters so trivial that Cepot had once suggested they were arguing for the pure animal joy of it, like two rams butting heads on a mountainside.

But when it came to the cairn, Tumenggung Batu worked alone.

He gathered stones. Not the loose rubble of the ruined watchtower -- those were compromised, cracked, unworthy -- but solid pieces of volcanic basalt that he prised from the island's bedrock with his bare hands, his thick fingers finding the seams in the stone with an instinct that was deeper than knowledge, that lived in his blood and his bones and in the ancient compact between the Kurcaci and the earth. Each stone he examined, turning it in his palms, reading its weight and grain the way a jeweler reads a gem, and those that met his standard he carried to the place where Gajahmada lay.

The others watched. They did not help. They understood, without being told, that this was a Kurcaci rite -- the building of the stone, the housing of the dead -- and that to intrude would be to diminish it.

Tumenggung Batu built the cairn with the precision of a master mason and the tenderness of a man constructing a cradle. Each stone was placed with deliberation, each joint fitted with a craft that required no mortar, only the understanding of pressure and weight and the willingness of stone to bear stone. The cairn rose around Gajahmada's body in courses of dark basalt -- a shelter, a monument, a final fortress for a man who had spent his life building fortresses of another kind.

When it was done -- when the last stone was placed and the cairn stood complete, a low dome of dark rock on the island's ridge, facing east toward the dawn that Gajahmada would never see again -- Tumenggung Batu knelt. He pressed both palms flat against the cairn's surface and spoke in Kurcaci, the deep-throated language of stone and forge, and the words were formal and measured and held the weight of centuries.

Then he took his war-hammer and, with a delicacy that seemed impossible for so blunt a tool in so broad a hand, he carved. The iron head struck the basalt in precise, controlled taps, and the stone yielded to the Kurcaci's intent, and letters formed -- clean, sharp, permanent -- on the cairn's eastern face.

**GAJAHMADA DARI MAJAPAHIT**

**Dia Menepati Sumpahnya.**

*Gajahmada of Majapahit -- He kept his oath.*

The chieftain stood. He looked at his work. His craggy face held nothing that the surface world would recognize as emotion, but his hands, hanging at his sides, trembled with the faintest vibration -- the Kurcaci grief, that deep-earth tremor that shook the foundations rather than the facade.

"He was a stubborn, arrogant, insufferable man," Tumenggung Batu said. His voice was rough as uncut stone. "He was the finest warrior I have known." He paused. "I will miss arguing with him."

He turned and walked away from the cairn, and no one spoke, and the wind that blew in from the Selat Sunda carried the smell of sulfur and the distant rumble of a mountain that had not finished with them yet.

---

### III. The War Council

Raden Wijaya called the council at midday, when the sun was a pale disc behind the volcanic haze and the heat pressed down on the island like a hand.

They gathered in the roofless shell of the watchtower, sitting on fallen stones and broken pillars, a ragged assembly of the wounded and the grieving. Seven where nine had set out. Seven where eight had stood that morning. The arithmetic of the quest, counted in losses.

Raden Wijaya's arm was bound now -- Dewi Anjani had cleaned and dressed the wound with herbs from her diminishing supply, and the bleeding had stopped, but the arm was stiff, and when he moved it, the pain showed in the tightening around his eyes that he could not quite suppress. He stood before them because sitting was a concession he was not prepared to make, and he spoke with the measured clarity of a man who has inventoried the situation and found it very nearly hopeless but not quite.

"We are seven," he began. "Wounded. Low on supplies. Our position is known to the enemy -- the Buta patrol that attacked us will have been expected to report. When it does not, others will come. More Buta Raksasa. Possibly Leak." He let the words settle. "We are on a volcanic island in the Sunda Strait, half a day's sail from Krakatau. The keris must reach the caldera. That has not changed. Nothing changes that."

"What has changed," Dewi Anjani said, "is our capacity to force a passage." She sat with her silver blade across her knees, her golden eyes scanning the strait beyond the ruined walls. The ancient Bidadari looked no different than she had at the outset of the quest -- her beauty untouched, her bearing unchanged -- but there was a weariness in her voice that spoke of something deeper than physical fatigue, a wearing-down of the spirit that even immortals could feel when the weight of mortal suffering accumulated beyond a certain threshold. "Krakatau is a fortress. The approaches are watched. The waters around it are patrolled by creatures we have not yet faced. A company of seven, three of whom are wounded, cannot fight its way to the caldera."

"Not as a single force, no," Raden Wijaya agreed. He paused, and in the pause Kian Santang saw the shape of a plan forming behind the prince's dark eyes -- the strategic mind working, turning possibilities like pieces on a papan catur board, testing configurations, discarding and selecting with the cold precision of a man raised from birth in the art of war. "But perhaps we need not."

He knelt and drew in the volcanic dust with his finger: the island chain, the strait, the bulk of Krakatau to the southeast. "The enemy expects us as a group. A fellowship. Nine became eight, now seven, but still a company, moving together, fighting together. That is what they will prepare for. That is what they will guard against." He drew a line from their island toward Krakatau. "What if we give them what they expect -- a company, visible, aggressive, making noise -- while the real stroke falls from a direction they are not watching?"

Tumenggung Batu leaned forward, his cracked armor creaking. "A diversion."

"A diversion," Raden Wijaya confirmed. "The main group -- five of us -- moves north along the island chain. We engage the Buta patrols. We make ourselves seen. We draw their attention, their forces, their eyes. We become the threat they expect." He drew a second line, thin and curving, hugging the western edge of the strait. "And while every Buta and Genderuwo in the strait is watching us, a small team -- two at most -- slips through the unguarded western approach and reaches Krakatau."

Silence. The weight of the plan settled over them -- its logic, its cost, its terrible efficiency.

"Who goes to the mountain?" Cepot asked. His voice was quiet, the gash on his forehead dark with dried blood, and the question was not strategic but personal. He already knew the answer. They all did.

Raden Wijaya looked at Kian Santang.

Kian Santang looked at the ground.

---

### IV. The Silent Plan

The debate continued as the sun tracked westward through the haze. They argued about numbers -- could three reach the mountain? Should they risk more? They argued about routes -- the western approach was less guarded but the currents were treacherous, the water heated by submarine volcanic vents. They argued about timing -- when to launch the diversion, how long to sustain it, at what point the deception would fail.

Kian Santang did not argue. He sat on the edge of the circle, his hand resting on the bamboo case that held the keris, and he listened, and he watched, and he was already decided.

He had been decided since Gajahmada fell.

The logic was simple. It was the same logic that had whispered at him for weeks, growing louder with each loss, each wound, each life spent in proximity to the blade he carried. The keris destroyed everything it touched. Not with violence -- or not only with violence -- but with its gravitational pull, its capacity to warp the will, to bend the good toward corruption and the brave toward ruin. Eyang Guru had died protecting it. Gajahmada had died defending its bearer. How many more? How many of the faces in this circle would be staring sightlessly at the sky before the caldera was reached?

Like Malin Kundang who denied his own mother and was turned to stone for his ingratitude, those who betray the bonds of love invite a curse that no keris can lift. Kian Santang understood this now with a clarity that cut deeper than any blade: the keris did not merely corrupt with power -- it severed bonds. It turned love into leverage, loyalty into liability. Every companion who walked beside him became a hostage to the blade's hunger. And the cruelest betrayal was not Gajahmada's grasping hand but the keris's patient art of making love itself dangerous.

None. None, if he went alone.

He began, quietly, to prepare. Not obviously -- he did not pack his bag or check his supplies in view of the others. He simply noted, as the afternoon wore on, where things were. The water skins, hung in the shade of a ruined wall. The remaining provisions -- dried fish, rice wrapped in banana leaf, a small pouch of salt. The phial of starlight from Nyai Roro Kidul, tucked into the inner pocket of his tunic, warm against his chest. The outrigger boat, beached on the island's western shore, half-hidden among the rocks.

He noted these things, and he waited, and he made no argument when Raden Wijaya proposed that two should make the crossing, because he had no intention of taking anyone with him.

Bagus noticed.

Of course Bagus noticed. Bagus had been watching Kian Santang since Padalarang -- since before Padalarang, since the days when watching Kian Santang meant making sure he didn't fall into the irrigation ditch while reading lontar manuscripts or forget to eat because a passage about ancient metallurgy had consumed his attention for six hours. Bagus had spent his entire life calibrating his awareness to the frequency of Kian Santang's moods, and the frequency had changed -- a subtle shift, a tightening, the particular stillness of a man who has made a decision and is waiting for the cover of darkness to execute it.

Bagus said nothing. He watched Kian Santang not-watch the outrigger boat, and not-count the water skins, and not-check the provisions, and he understood everything, and he said nothing at all.

He began, quietly, to prepare his own pack.

---

### V. The Last Vigil

Night fell over the Sunda Strait with the swiftness of the tropics -- the sun drowning in the western haze, the sky shifting from amber to violet to black in the space of minutes. The volcanic smog that drifted from Krakatau blotted the stars, leaving only occasional gaps through which the Southern Cross burned with a cold, distant clarity that seemed to belong to another world entirely.

They built no fire. The risk was too great. Instead they lay in the ruins, wrapped in what remained of their cloaks and blankets, and the warmth of the volcanic rock beneath them was a strange comfort -- the earth's heat rising through the stone, a reminder that the island was not dead ground but the shoulder of a sleeping giant that might stir at any moment.

They slept in shifts. Raden Wijaya took the first watch, standing motionless on the highest point of the ruin, his silhouette barely distinguishable from the broken stones around him, his wounded arm held close to his body. Dewi Anjani took the second, her golden eyes moving across the dark water with that deeper sight that perceived currents and intentions invisible to mortal vision. Tumenggung Batu took the third, sitting with his war-hammer across his knees, his stone-grey body blending with the rock until he was indistinguishable from the ruins he guarded.

Kian Santang did not sleep.

He lay on his back, the bamboo case beside him, and he looked at each sleeping face in turn, and the looking was a kind of farewell that he did not allow himself to name.

Raden Wijaya. The prince of Mataram who had relinquished his claim to a throne because the quest demanded it -- who had walked away from the power and privilege of the Aliran Mataram with the quiet decisiveness of a man who understood that some duties outweighed all others. In sleep, the severity of his features softened, and he looked young -- younger than Kian Santang had ever seen him look -- and the vulnerability of that sleeping face was almost unbearable.

Dewi Anjani. Ancient beyond reckoning, luminous even in rest, her silver hair spread across the dark stone like spilled moonlight. She had chosen this. She had descended from the mountain sanctuaries of the Bidadari, where she might have waited out the age in immortal safety, and had walked into the mortal world with its pain and its blood and its terrible, beautiful brevity. She had chosen mortality's risk. She had chosen to fight.

Tumenggung Batu. The Kurcaci chieftain who had left his mountain halls, his forge, his people, to accompany a fellowship of surface-dwellers on a quest that the deep-earth folk had every reason to consider someone else's problem. He slept upright, his cracked armor still on, his broad hands resting on his war-hammer, and even in sleep his jaw was set, and the stone-grey planes of his face held the particular stubbornness of a man who had decided to see a thing through and would not be moved by any force short of geological.

Cepot. The gash on his forehead had been bandaged, and in sleep his mobile face was still, the constant performance laid down like a mask removed, and beneath it was something that Kian Santang had always known but that Cepot himself would never admit: a goodness. A deep, persistent, unshakeable goodness that the wit and the complaints and the theatrical cowardice were built to protect, the way a shell protects the soft creature within.

Dawala. Round-faced, gentle, brave in the particular way that the fearful are brave -- not through the absence of terror but through the daily, exhausting decision to act despite it. He slept curled on his side with his pack clutched to his chest like a child clutching a stuffed toy, and his breathing was even and trusting, and Kian Santang's throat tightened.

Bagus.

Always Bagus.

His oldest friend slept on his back, his golok laid beside him in easy reach, his broad chest rising and falling with the steady rhythm of a man whose conscience was clear and whose loyalty was not a decision but a condition, like the beating of his heart. Bagus, who had followed him out of Padalarang without hesitation. Bagus, who had stood over him in the Gua Sewu caves and swung his golok at things that belonged in nightmares. Bagus, who had never asked for any of this and had never once complained.

Kian Santang closed his eyes.

The keris whispered. It always whispered. But tonight the whispers were different -- not the seductive visions of power, not the dark promises of empire. Tonight the keris whispered of futility. *They will die for you,* it breathed. *One by one, as Gajahmada died. As Eyang Guru died. You carry death with you, little Tuyul. You are the plague, and they are the village. Every step you take beside them is a step closer to their graves.*

He gripped the phial of starlight through his tunic. The warmth pulsed against his chest -- faint but true, a small clean flame against the vast dark.

*I know,* he thought. *That is why I go alone.*

He waited for dawn.

# Bab 22: Perpecahan

## (The Breaking)

---

### I. Before Dawn

The sky was still black when Kian Santang rose.

He moved with the careful silence of a man who has rehearsed each motion in his mind -- the slow shift of weight from hip to feet, the incremental straightening, the first step placed on bare rock where no loose stone could betray him. The keris, wrapped in its bamboo case, he lifted with both hands and slung across his shoulder, settling the strap into the groove it had worn over weeks of carrying. The blade stirred at his touch -- a quickening, a recognition -- and the whispers shifted register, from the despair of the night to something almost eager, almost hungry. It knew what he was doing. It approved. The keris wanted to go to Krakatau. It did not care who carried it there.

The water skin was where he had marked it. He lifted it from the wall, testing the weight -- two-thirds full, enough for a day if he was careful. The provisions he gathered were meager: a portion of dried fish, a small packet of rice in banana leaf, a handful of dried cassava chips that he suspected had been Dawala's. He would need little. The crossing was half a day's paddle. Whatever happened after he reached the mountain would not require provisions.

The phial of starlight rested against his chest, warm and steady. The mark of the oath on his palm glowed faintly in the darkness -- the pale scar from the lingga, the promise made flesh. He did not look at it. The oath bound him to the quest and to his companions. He was keeping the first part. The second part, he was breaking. The knowledge of this sat in his chest like a stone.

He needed a moment. Just a moment. He knelt in the darkness and found a strip of lontar leaf in his pack -- dried and stiff, the kind used for writing in the old way. He had no stylus, but the tip of his belt knife served, and he scratched words into the leaf with the slow deliberation of a man writing a last letter.

*Aku harus pergi sendiri. Keris ini menghancurkan semua yang disentuhnya. Jangan ikuti aku. Selamatkan Nusantara dengan cara lain. -- Kian.*

*I must go alone. The keris destroys all it touches. Do not follow me. Save Nusantara another way. -- Kian.*

He placed the lontar strip on the stone where Raden Wijaya's head had rested, weighted it with a pebble, and turned away.

The path to the western shore wound through a gully between two ridges of hardened lava, the rock sharp-edged and treacherous in the dark. Kian Santang picked his way down with the cautious steps of a Tuyul raised in the terraced sawah of Padalarang, where every child learned to navigate steep, slippery ground before they learned to read. The volcanic rock was warm beneath his bare feet. The air tasted of sulfur and salt. Ahead, he could hear the sea -- the slow surge and retreat of waves against the island's western flank, a rhythm that in other circumstances might have been soothing.

The outrigger was where he had marked it: a small fishing boat, probably left by island fishermen months or years ago, its hull of carved wood weathered but sound, its single bamboo outrigger still firmly lashed. It was built for one, maybe two. It would serve.

He grasped the bow and pushed. The hull scraped against the black sand, the sound obscenely loud in the pre-dawn stillness, and he froze, holding his breath, listening. Nothing. The ruins above were silent. He pushed again, and the boat slid free of the sand and into the dark water, and the warm sea received it with a gentle rocking that was almost welcoming.

He was knee-deep in the water, one hand on the gunwale, preparing to swing himself aboard, when the voice came from behind him.

"You're not very good at sneaking, you know."

---

### II. The Promise

Kian Santang's hand tightened on the gunwale. He did not turn. He did not need to. He would have known that voice in absolute darkness, in a hurricane, at the bottom of the sea. He had been hearing it since before memory -- across the sawah paths of Padalarang, across schoolyard games and harvest festivals and the long quiet evenings when the angklung music drifted down from the hills and the fireflies rose from the rice paddies like earthbound stars.

"Bagus." His voice was barely a whisper. "Go back."

Bagus stood on the beach. He was already packed -- his golok at his belt, his provisions in a bundle on his back, his sandals in his hand because he, too, knew the treachery of volcanic rock in the dark. He had not just woken. The moonlight that filtered through the haze caught the set of his jaw, the calm readiness in his posture, the complete absence of surprise. He had been awake the whole night. He had watched Kian Santang not-sleep, and he had packed his own bag in the darkness, stone by stone, and he had followed his friend to the shore because that was what he did. That was what he had always done.

"Go back," Kian Santang said again. "I wrote a note. I explained--"

"I read your note."

"Then you know--"

"I know you wrote a very noble goodbye on a piece of dried leaf and tried to sneak off in the dark like a thief. Yes." Bagus walked to the water's edge. The waves lapped at his feet. His voice was steady, uninflected, the voice of a man stating facts. "I also know that if I go back to the camp and sit down and wait, you will paddle that boat into the most dangerous waters in Nusantara, land alone on a volcanic island crawling with Buta, and try to climb a mountain of fire by yourself. And you will die. Not heroically. Not nobly. You'll die because you'll have no one to watch your back when you stumble, and no one to hand you water when the heat takes your strength, and no one to talk sense into you when the keris fills your head with its lies." He paused. "I'm not going to let that happen."

"Bagus, you can't--"

"You didn't ask me. I decided." Bagus stepped into the water. The sea rose around his ankles, his calves. He walked to the boat and placed his hand on the gunwale, opposite Kian Santang's, and his grip was firm and his eyes were clear and he did not look away. "Back in Lembur Padalarang, when Eyang Guru told me to look after you, I made a promise. *Ingwang moal ngantunkeun andika* -- I will not leave you. And a Tuyul keeps his promises."

The words hung in the warm salt air between them -- the Sundanese basa lemes of a solemn vow, the respectful register reserved for promises that bind the soul. The waves lapped. The keris hummed. Krakatau smoldered on the horizon, a darker mass against the dark sky, its summit crowned with a faint red glow that pulsed like a heartbeat.

"You'll die," Kian Santang said. His voice cracked on the word, and the crack was the first fissure in a dam he had been building for weeks -- the dam of duty and distance and the deliberate numbness that the keris encouraged, the dam that let him walk toward death without trembling. The crack spread. "This isn't the sawah, Bagus. This isn't chasing Dawala out of the rambutan trees or pulling Cepot out of a ditch. This is Krakatau. I'm carrying the most dangerous weapon in Nusantara toward the most dangerous place in Nusantara, and everyone who comes with me--" His voice broke entirely.

"Maybe," Bagus said. His tone had not changed. "Maybe I'll die. But you'll definitely die alone. Two Tuyul are better than one." A pause. The ghost of something that in better times would have been a smile. "That's just math."

Kian Santang's eyes filled. The tears came, and they were not the keris's doing and not the phial's doing but his own -- the grief for Gajahmada and Eyang Guru and the world he had left behind in Padalarang, the terror of the mountain ahead, the overwhelming, staggering, almost unbearable fact of Bagus standing in the dark water refusing to let him go alone. The tears ran down his face and dropped into the warm sea and were lost.

He nodded.

He could not speak, so he nodded, and Bagus nodded back, and together they pushed the outrigger into deeper water and climbed aboard -- Kian Santang at the bow, Bagus at the stern, the way they had sat in the wooden boats on the Citarum River as boys, when the world was small and safe and the worst danger was tipping over and getting a mouthful of muddy water.

They took up the paddles. The blades bit the dark sea. The outrigger slid forward, nosing into the warm current of the Sunda Strait, and behind them the volcanic island receded -- the ruins, the cairn, the sleeping figures of their companions -- growing smaller with each stroke until it was just a dark shape among dark shapes, and then nothing, and then gone.

Ahead, Krakatau waited.

---

### III. The Message

Raden Wijaya found the lontar strip at first light.

He had woken to the grey pre-dawn and known immediately that something was wrong. The quality of the silence was different -- a subtraction, a thinning, the way a gamelan sounds when a player has stopped and the remaining instruments rearrange themselves around the absence. He rolled onto his side and saw two empty sleeping places where there should have been two small forms, and the knowledge of what had happened arrived complete and fully formed, requiring no deduction, no search, no denial.

He read the note. His face did not change as he read it -- the severity of his features had already been set by years of command, of absorbing bad news and worse odds and converting them into action. But something moved behind his eyes, a sequence of emotions so rapid they overlapped: anger that Kian Santang had gone without consultation, then understanding of why he had, then something that was not quite admiration and not quite grief but occupied the territory between them -- a recognition of courage, even foolish courage, even the courage of a boy barely out of youth carrying a cursed blade toward a burning mountain because he believed it was the only way to stop people he loved from dying.

And Bagus had followed him. Of course Bagus had followed him. The sun rose in the east and Bagus followed Kian Santang. These were equivalent certainties.

He gathered the remaining five.

They stood in the grey dawn light -- Dewi Anjani, Tumenggung Batu, Cepot, Dawala -- and Raden Wijaya held up the lontar strip and read it aloud, and the words fell into the morning air like leaves from a dying tree.

Word of the fellowship's quest had already spread beyond these burning islands. Bugis trading prau had carried the tale from port to port along the northern coasts; Maluku spice merchants had heard whispers of it in harbors as far east as the Banda Sea. The story of a fellowship walking toward fire to unmake a blade of darkness had become a rumor on the lips of sailors and fishermen across the archipelago -- and now, at this moment of fracture, those distant witnesses could not know that the fellowship they spoke of in hushed tones was splitting apart on a nameless volcanic rock, its two smallest members paddling alone toward the mountain of their undoing.

Cepot was the first to react. "That idiot," he said, and his voice was thick with something that was not anger. "That absolute, complete, magnificent idiot. He's gone to get himself killed, and he's taken Bagus with him, and I wasn't even awake to call him an idiot to his face." He turned away. His shoulders shook once, and he controlled them, and when he turned back his eyes were bright but his jaw was set. "We go after them."

"No," Raden Wijaya said.

The word was quiet and absolute. It landed on the group like a stone on still water, and the ripples of it spread outward -- disbelief, protest, a dozen arguments forming and dying in the space of a breath.

"*Boten,*" he repeated in the ngoko Javanese of a man beyond courtly pretense, the blunt register of a warrior making a field decision. "He has chosen his path. We must choose ours." He looked at each face in turn, holding each gaze long enough to communicate something that words would have diminished. "If we follow -- if all seven of us converge on Krakatau in a single group -- we give the enemy exactly what they expect. A target. A company to surround and destroy. But two Tuyul in a small boat, moving quietly, approaching from the west while every eye is turned elsewhere--" He paused. "That is the slimmest of chances. Our task is to make it less slim."

Dewi Anjani understood first. Her golden eyes narrowed, and she looked north, across the island chain toward the dark waters where Buta patrols moved in their heavy war-boats. "The diversion."

"The diversion," Raden Wijaya confirmed. "We go north. We engage. We make enough noise, draw enough eyes, command enough attention that two small Tuyul in an outrigger might -- might -- reach the mountain unnoticed. This is not retreat. This is the other half of the quest."

He held up the lontar strip. "Kian Santang wrote, 'Save Nusantara another way.' This is how. If we fight well enough, if we draw the enemy's gaze hard enough and hold it long enough, we give him his chance. That is what Gajahmada died to protect. That is what we will protect now."

Dewi Anjani looked at the strait, at the distant glow of Krakatau. "It is a slim hope."

Tumenggung Batu stood. His cracked armor caught the grey light, and the claw-marks scored across his breastplate were a record of every fight he had survived to reach this moment. "Slim hope is still hope," he said. "The *Pangkur* teaches this -- determination against the odds, the stubborn stride that does not turn back. We walk it now."

Cepot wiped his eyes with the back of his hand. He looked at Dawala. He looked at the distant mountain where his two closest friends were paddling toward fire and death. He squared his small shoulders and set his jaw, and when he spoke, his voice held something new -- not the performance, not the wit, but the thing beneath, the bedrock.

"Besides," he said, "I've always wanted to punch a Buta Raksasa."

Dawala reached into his pack and pulled out a piece of dried cassava. He chewed it slowly, thoughtfully, as though the act of eating were a form of offering to the unseen karuhun -- the ancestral spirits who watched over their descendants even in these darkest hours. "For Kian Santang," he said quietly. "For Bagus."

Raden Wijaya sheathed his keris. "We move north within the hour. Pack light. We fight today."

---

### IV. Two Paths

The morning split in two.

**On the western strait**, Kian Santang and Bagus paddled in silence.

The outrigger was small -- barely four meters bow to stern, its hull narrow and low in the water, its single bamboo outrigger cutting a thin wake through the dark sea. They sat facing forward, their paddles dipping and pulling in a rhythm that they found without discussion, the way they had always found rhythm together -- in the planting of rice, in the beating of the angklung, in the wordless coordination of two people who have known each other so long that communication has been refined beyond language into something cellular and automatic.

The Sunda Strait unfolded before them. The water was warm -- warmer than it should have been, heated by the volcanic activity beneath, and in places it steamed, thin wisps of vapor curling from the surface like the breath of something submerged. The current was strong, pulling them southeast, toward Krakatau, and they worked with it, angling the outrigger to ride the flow rather than fight it. Pumice floated on the surface in scattered patches -- grey, porous, light as dried sponge -- and bumped against the hull with a soft, persistent knocking that sounded, in Kian Santang's keris-addled awareness, like something tapping to get in.

Krakatau grew before them. At dawn it had been a silhouette against the grey sky, a triangular darkness crowned in smoke. Now, as the morning advanced and the distance closed, it resolved into detail -- terrible detail, each feature sharpening like a face emerging from shadow. The lower slopes were black, hardened lava flows ridged and cracked like the skin of something ancient and burned. The middle reaches were scarred with vents and fissures from which steam and gas erupted in continuous plumes. The upper caldera was wreathed in cloud -- not natural cloud but volcanic smog, dense and dark and lit from beneath by the glow of magma, so that the summit seemed to burn with a cold fire that was not fire but the reflection of the world's molten interior.

The keris sang.

Not whispered -- sang. A low, sustained note, felt in the bones rather than heard with the ears, a frequency that vibrated in Kian Santang's sternum and radiated outward through his ribs and spine. The blade knew where it was. It recognized the volcanic signature of Krakatau the way a river recognizes the sea toward which it flows -- not with thought but with the certainty of physics, of magnetism, of things returning to their source. The pamor patterns in the metal were responding to the mountain's fire, the same fire in which they had been forged, and the resonance was growing with every paddle stroke, a triumphant hum that said: *Home. Home. Home.*

Kian Santang gripped his paddle harder and did not listen.

---

**On the northern island chain**, Raden Wijaya led his five into battle.

They encountered the first Buta Raksasa patrol before midday -- a war-boat crewed by eight of the massive creatures, patrolling the channel between two volcanic islets. Raden Wijaya did not avoid them. He walked to the highest point of the nearest islet and stood in full view, his keris drawn, and he shouted across the water in the ngoko register of old Javanese -- a challenge, a taunt, a declaration of presence as loud and unmistakable as a war-gong struck at dawn. "*Mrene, asu! Iki sing kok goleki!*" -- *Come, dogs! This is what you seek!*

The Buta came.

They beached their war-boat and charged up the slope with the mindless aggression of creatures bred for violence and nothing else, and Raden Wijaya and his four companions met them in a fighting line on the ridge -- a fighting retreat, carefully planned, drawing the Buta deeper into the islet's interior while Dewi Anjani's arrows picked them off from the flanks and Tumenggung Batu anchored the center like a boulder in a river.

They fought. They bled. They held.

And when the last Buta fell, Raden Wijaya did not rest. He moved to the next islet, the next visible point, the next place where he could be seen. He was painting a target on himself and his company -- a bright, loud, unmissable target that said: *Here. We are here. Come find us.*

Cepot fought beside Dawala with a ferocity that surprised them both. The witty Tuyul, who had spent the entire quest converting terror into comedy, found that the conversion could run the other way -- that the same energy that powered his jokes could power his blade, and that the fear that had dogged him since Padalarang, when alchemized by the right desperation, became something very close to fury. He was not skilled. He was not graceful. But he was fast, and he was stubborn, and he fought for the two friends he could not see, paddling toward fire on the far side of the strait.

"*Mulah hayang kabur, jurig!*" Cepot snarled at a Buta twice his height -- *Don't think about running, demon!* -- the basa loma of Padalarang erupting from him like a battle cry, and Dawala, hearing it, laughed despite everything, because the sound of home -- even home's rudest register -- was a shield against the dark.

---

### V. The Breaking Complete

The sun climbed. The strait shimmered with heat.

In the outrigger, Kian Santang and Bagus paddled on. Krakatau filled their vision now -- the entire southern horizon consumed by the volcanic island, its mass blocking the sky, its slopes radiating a heat they could feel on their faces from a league away. Dark shapes moved on the water around them -- shadows beneath the surface, long and sinuous, things that might have been volcanic eels or might have been something worse. The keris's song was a constant pressure now, a vibration that made Kian Santang's teeth hum and his vision pulse at the edges.

Behind them, faintly, carried on the warm wind, the sounds of battle: the clash of metal, a distant shout, the bass roar of a Buta Raksasa. Their friends were fighting. Their friends were bleeding. Their friends were buying them this passage with every wound they took and every blow they struck.

The fellowship was broken. The nine who had sworn the oath at the temple were scattered -- one dead beneath a cairn, two in a boat on dark water, five fighting on a chain of volcanic islands. The circle was shattered. The formation was gone.

But the quest continued.

And somewhere in the depths of Kian Santang's exhausted mind, a *Mijil* began -- not sung aloud, but felt, the pupuh of parting and grief, its melancholy rhythm matching the dip and pull of the paddles. Mijil: the form of separation, of longing, of the sorrow that comes when those who love each other must walk different roads. Its melody wound through him like the current wound through the strait, carrying him forward even as it mourned what was left behind.

Kian Santang looked at Krakatau. It was so close now that he could feel its heat on his face, could see the individual lava flows on its slopes -- rivers of molten red crawling down the black rock with the slow, inexorable patience of geological time. He could hear the deep volcanic rumble, a sound that was less heard than felt, a vibration that lived in the belly and the bones and the deep places of the chest where breath originates. The keris's whispers had become a single, sustained tone, and the tone was a word, and the word was:

*Welcome home.*

He closed his eyes. He gripped the paddle.

Behind him, Bagus said quietly: "One stroke at a time, Kian. One stroke at a time."

Kian Santang opened his eyes. He pulled. The paddle bit the dark water. The outrigger slid forward.

One stroke at a time.

# Bab 23: Seberang Selat Sunda

## (Across the Sunda Strait)

---

### I. The Cauldron

The Sunda Strait was trying to kill them.

Not with malice -- or not only with malice. The strait was a collision of forces that had been at war since the tectonic plates first ground against each other beneath the Java Sea: the Indian Ocean pushing from the south, the Java Sea pressing from the north, and beneath both, the volcanic plumbing of Krakatau heating the water and sending convection currents spiraling upward in unpredictable, churning columns that caught the outrigger and spun it like a leaf in a whirlpool. The sea was not one temperature but many -- warm here, scalding there, and in places where the submarine vents opened directly onto the seabed, the water hissed and bubbled with a violence that sent Bagus hauling on the paddle to steer them clear, his broad arms burning with effort.

Pumice was everywhere. It floated on the surface in rafts of grey stone, some pieces no larger than a fist, others as big as the outrigger itself, and the boat scraped and knocked against them with a constant, maddening percussion. The bamboo outrigger caught on a larger piece and the hull tilted, water slopping over the gunwale, and Kian Santang threw his weight to the opposite side while Bagus drove his paddle down like a lever and wrenched them free. They rocked. They steadied. They paddled on.

The sky above was not sky. It was a permanent twilight -- volcanic haze so dense that the sun was reduced to a pale smear of light without warmth or definition, a ghost of itself, drowning in particulate ash. The haze was not grey but brown, tinged with the sulfurous yellow of volcanic gases, and it pressed down on the water with a weight that was almost physical, thickening the air, shortening the breath. Kian Santang's lungs burned. Each inhalation tasted of sulfur and hot metal and something else -- something organic, something rotten, the smell of a world being unmade at its foundations.

And Krakatau.

Krakatau filled the world.

It was no longer a mountain on the horizon. It was the horizon. The volcanic island rose from the strait like a thing pulled upward from the seabed by a fist of fire, its slopes climbing in steep, brutal angles to the caldera that crowned its summit, and the caldera was open -- a vast wound in the earth's crust from which smoke and gas and the red glow of magma poured in continuous, churning violence. The lower slopes were black -- hardened lava, ancient and recent, layered in flows that overlapped like the scales of some monstrous serpent, each flow representing an eruption, a convulsion, a chapter in the mountain's long history of destruction. Higher, the black stone gave way to red -- living lava, still molten, glowing in cracks and rivers and broad, slow-moving sheets that crawled down the mountainside with the patient hunger of something that could not be stopped, only delayed. Steam erupted from a hundred vents, jetting into the haze with a hissing roar that was audible even from the water. The mountain trembled -- a constant, low vibration, as though the earth itself were shivering with the effort of containing what burned within.

Kian Santang looked at it and felt the vertigo of scale -- the disorienting smallness of a single being confronting a force that operated on a geological timeline, that had been here before the first humans sailed these waters and would be here long after the last human had gone.

"We're actually going to land on that," Bagus said, from behind him.

It was not a question. It was not a complaint. It was the observation of a man cataloguing reality with the blunt accuracy that had always been Bagus's greatest strength and most infuriating quality -- the inability to look away from what was true, no matter how much the truth might benefit from a little evasion.

"Yes," Kian Santang said.

"All right," Bagus said. And paddled.

---

### II. The Keris Speaks

The assault began at the edge of Krakatau's shadow.

There was a point -- a specific, measurable point -- where the water changed. The currents shifted, the temperature spiked, and the volcanic haze thickened until visibility dropped to a dozen boat-lengths, and the world contracted to a small sphere of brown air and dark water with an outrigger at its center and a mountain somewhere ahead, felt rather than seen. And at that point, the keris stopped singing and started shouting.

Not in words. Not exactly. The keris had never truly spoken in language -- its communications were more fundamental than that, impressions and emotions and images delivered directly into the nervous system, bypassing the ears and the rational mind, arriving in the consciousness fully formed, like memories of things that had never happened. But what had been a whisper for weeks, a murmur for days, a song for hours, became, at the edge of Krakatau's domain, a roar.

It showed him power.

Not the abstract concept of power but its specific, tangible manifestation: Kian Santang on a throne of black stone, the keris in his hand, its pamor patterns blazing with a dark light that illuminated a throne room carved from the interior of the volcano itself. Before him, the armies of Nusantara knelt -- not in fear but in awe, because the man on the throne was not a tyrant but a king, a true king, beloved and wise, who had taken the keris's power and used it to unite the archipelago as no one had ever united it before. The kingdoms at peace. The Buta destroyed. The Leak broken. Every island from Sabang to Merauke joined under a single banner, a single will, a single vision. And at his side, his companions -- not dead, not corrupted, but honored, elevated, given the rewards their loyalty deserved. Bagus as his chief counselor. Raden Wijaya as his general. Cepot and Dawala as lords of their own domains. The vision was beautiful. It was complete. It was a lie, and Kian Santang knew it was a lie, but the knowing did not diminish its pull any more than knowing a flame is hot diminishes its heat.

It showed him failure.

The mountain -- this mountain, the one ahead of them now -- and Kian Santang climbing it alone because Bagus had fallen, struck down by a Buta arrow, his body lying on the black volcanic stone with his eyes open and his golok still in his hand, and Kian Santang screaming his name, and no answer coming. And the others -- Raden Wijaya cut down on the northern islets, Dewi Anjani's silver light extinguished, Tumenggung Batu buried in the rubble of a collapsed cave, Cepot and Dawala lost, lost, small and frightened and lost in a darkness that would never lift. All of it his fault. All of it because he had come here, because he had carried the keris into the Shadow King's domain, because he had been too proud or too stupid or too determined to stop when stopping might have saved them.

It showed him the easy path.

*Keep me,* the keris said. *Turn the boat. Sail away. You need not climb the mountain. You need not face the fire. Keep me, and live. Keep me, and be loved. Keep me, and the whispers will become a song, and the song will become silence, and you will never be afraid again.*

Kian Santang's hand moved. Without his permission, without his will, his right hand released the paddle and reached for the bamboo case at his shoulder, and his fingers found the wrapping, and the keris blazed with a heat that was not pain but pleasure, a dark warmth that flooded up his arm and into his chest and promised him everything, everything, if he would only--

He seized the phial of starlight.

His left hand found it through his tunic, closed around it, and pulled it free. The phial was small -- a vessel of white crystal no larger than his thumb, containing a light given by Nyai Roro Kidul in the depths of the southern ocean, a fragment of the first starlight that had ever touched the waters of Nusantara. He held it against his chest, and the light pulsed -- faintly, gently, with none of the keris's overwhelming force, but with a quality that the keris could not match and could not counterfeit.

Truth.

The light was true. It did not promise. It did not seduce. It simply was -- warm, clean, steady, like sunlight on a sawah at dawn, like the lamp in a window at the end of a long road, like the hand of a friend in the dark. It did not silence the keris. The visions did not stop, the roar did not diminish. But the light gave Kian Santang something to hold onto that was not the blade's lies -- a fixed point, a reference, a reminder that the world contained things the keris could not corrupt because they were too small and too real and too good for corruption to find a purchase.

He held the phial and breathed. The visions flickered and faded. His right hand withdrew from the bamboo case and found the paddle again.

Behind him, Bagus had not stopped paddling. He had not spoken. He had not even turned around. He had felt the outrigger shift as Kian Santang released his paddle, and he had compensated -- adjusting his stroke to keep the boat straight, absorbing the imbalance without comment, without alarm -- because he had known, with the certainty of a lifetime's observation, that the keris would try and that Kian Santang would fight and that the fight would be won, and that his role was not to intervene but to keep the boat on course until Kian Santang came back to himself.

The paddle bit the water. The outrigger moved on.

---

### III. The Garden

"Did I ever tell you about my garden?"

Bagus spoke into the silence that followed the keris's assault -- not the oppressive silence of the strait, which was full of hissing vents and grinding pumice and the mountain's constant rumble, but the interior silence, the stillness inside Kian Santang's skull where the keris's roar had been and where now there was only the ringing echo of a battle narrowly won.

"Your garden," Kian Santang said. His voice was hoarse. His hands shook on the paddle.

"The soil Nyai Roro Kidul gave me," Bagus continued, his tone conversational, unhurried, as though they were sitting on the porch of his family's rumah panggung in Padalarang rather than paddling toward an active volcano in a boat the size of a moderate bathtub. "The enchanted soil. I've been thinking about what to plant."

Kian Santang pulled. The paddle cut the dark water. Ahead, Krakatau loomed, vast and terrible, its slopes crawling with fire.

"Kangkung, obviously," Bagus said. "And bayam. Maybe some terung -- the long purple ones that your mother used to slice thin and fry with sambal. And tomatoes. I've always wanted to grow tomatoes. Real ones, not the sad little things they sell at the market in Cianjur."

The water steamed around them. A vent erupted to their left, sending a column of scalding vapor into the haze. Bagus adjusted his stroke without breaking his sentence.

"And a rambutan tree. A proper one. Remember the rambutan tree behind Pak Amin's house? The one Dawala fell out of and cracked his tooth? I want one like that, but bigger. Big enough that the branches hang over the fence and the neighbors can pick the fruit without asking, the way it should be."

Kian Santang's hands steadied on the paddle. The shaking subsided, slowly, like ripples in a pool settling back to stillness. He pulled. Bagus talked.

"I've been thinking about the layout. The sawah terraces behind the house -- we could extend them. Run an irrigation channel from the spring above the lembur. The enchanted soil will help, but you still need good water. Rice first -- always rice first, that's what my father said -- and then vegetables around the edges, and fruit trees on the upper terrace where they'll get the morning sun."

The volcano's heat pressed against their faces. The air shimmered. Dark shapes moved in the depths below the outrigger -- sinuous, massive, turning slowly in the heated water like thoughts forming in a fevered mind.

"And angklung," Bagus said. "I want to hang angklung tubes from the rambutan tree. So when the wind blows through the garden, the whole place makes music. Can you imagine? You're working in the sawah, and the wind picks up, and the whole garden starts playing. Not a proper melody -- just notes, random, but beautiful the way random beautiful things are."

Kian Santang closed his eyes for a moment. Behind his eyelids, the keris offered its visions -- the throne, the crown, the armies kneeling. And Bagus's voice offered another vision entirely: a garden. Kangkung and bayam and a rambutan tree with angklung tubes in its branches, and rice paddies fed by a spring, and the taste of nasi timbel on a rainy day, and the sound of the Seren Taun harvest festival drifting down from the hills on a warm evening.

The keris's vision of empire could not compete with Bagus's vision of a garden.

It was absurd. It was the most absurd thing Kian Santang had ever experienced -- the Shadow King's instrument of domination, the blade that had corrupted kings and toppled kingdoms, rendered mute by a Tuyul farmer's daydream about rambutan trees and kangkung. But that was the truth the keris could not understand, the truth that lived in Bagus's steady voice and unhurried manner and the absolute, unshakeable simplicity of his desires. Bagus did not want power. He did not want empire. He wanted a garden. He wanted to plant things and watch them grow. He wanted to feed people. And the wanting was so pure, so rooted, so devoid of the ambition and fear on which the keris fed, that the blade's whispers slid off it like water off stone.

"When this is over," Bagus said, "I'm going to plant the biggest garden Lembur Padalarang has ever seen."

Kian Santang opened his eyes. Krakatau burned before them, massive, terrible, filling the world with fire and smoke and the promise of destruction.

"You will," he said. "I believe you."

They paddled on.

---

### IV. Black Sand

The shore of Krakatau was the end of growing things.

The outrigger's hull scraped against black sand and came to rest. The sound it made was wrong -- a dry, grating hiss, like skin drawn across heated metal -- and the sand itself was wrong, too fine, too uniform, too warm. It was not the sand of beaches where children play and fishermen drag their boats. It was ground stone, volcanic glass pulverized by eruptions and deposited in a layer so fine and so dark that it absorbed the dim light rather than reflecting it, and the black strip of shore seemed to swallow the edge of the sea rather than meet it.

Kian Santang stepped out of the boat.

His bare feet sank into the black sand, and it was warm -- blood-warm, the warmth of something alive, the residual heat of the earth's interior conducted upward through layers of volcanic rock and ash to the surface. The warmth spread through his soles and up his legs, and for a moment the world tilted.

The keris blazed.

Not the gradual intensification of the crossing, not the slow build from whisper to song to shout. This was instantaneous -- a detonation of dark energy that erupted from the bamboo case and flooded through Kian Santang's body like a wave breaking over a sea-wall, and in the wave were visions and voices and the full, concentrated malice of the Shadow King's awareness crashing down on him like a spotlight, like a fist, like the weight of the mountain itself pressing on a single point. The keris was home. The keris was on the island of its forging. The keris was screaming with a joy that was indistinguishable from hunger, and the Shadow King -- that dispersed, diminished, endlessly patient intelligence that waited in the caldera above -- felt it, and turned his attention toward the shore, and *looked*.

Kian Santang fell.

His knees hit the black sand. His hands hit the black sand. The phial of starlight swung on its cord against his chest and pulsed, and the keris pulsed back, and between the two pulses he was ground like grain between millstones, and the world was light and dark and light and dark, and he could not breathe, and the mountain roared.

Hands caught him.

Strong, broad, familiar hands -- hands that had pulled him out of irrigation ditches and steadied him on slippery sawah paths and caught his arm when the keris's weight made him stumble on mountain trails. Bagus gripped his shoulders and hauled him upright and held him, and Bagus's voice was in his ear, close and solid and real.

"I've got you."

The world settled. The tilting ceased. The keris's scream subsided to a roar, and the roar to a growl, and the growl to the constant, throbbing hum that Kian Santang had carried since Padalarang. The Shadow King's attention was still there -- he could feel it, a pressure at the top of his skull, a heat behind his eyes -- but it was searching, probing, not yet certain of what it had felt. Two Tuyul were small. Two Tuyul were below the threshold of what the Shadow King expected.

"I'm all right," Kian Santang said. He was not all right. But he was standing, and Bagus was beside him, and the mountain was above them, and these three facts constituted the minimum viable conditions for continuing.

They pulled the outrigger above the tide line and wedged it between two slabs of hardened lava. Bagus secured the provisions and the water skin with the meticulous care of a man who understood that these small practical things -- food, water, shelter -- were the scaffolding that held the larger enterprise together. Without the scaffolding, the quest was not heroism but suicide.

Kian Santang looked up.

The mountain rose above them in a nightmare of geology. The lower slopes were ancient lava flows, hardened into ridges and channels and frozen rivers of black stone, their surfaces cracked and fissured by centuries of thermal stress. Higher, the flows were younger -- darker, sharper-edged, some of them still warm to the touch, some still glowing faintly in their deepest cracks with the dull red light of magma not yet fully cooled. Steam erupted from vents scattered across the slope in random patterns, the jets hissing upward with a violence that made the ground tremble. The air was thick with sulfur -- a choking, eye-watering miasma that burned the throat and coated the tongue with a taste like old pennies and rotten eggs. Nothing grew. Not a blade of grass, not a lichen, not a moss. The mountain had burned the possibility of growth out of the stone, and what remained was a landscape of absolute desolation -- black rock and red fire and the ceaseless, grinding sound of a planet consuming itself from within.

And above it all, the caldera. A ragged rim of rock at the summit, half-hidden in the permanent cloud of volcanic smog, lit from below by the glow of the magma lake that churned in the volcano's throat. That was where the keris had been forged. That was where it must be unmade. The caldera was far -- a thousand meters of ascent through terrain that would challenge a mountain goat, let alone two Tuyul from the lowland paddies of Padalarang.

"There," Kian Santang said. He did not need to point.

---

### V. The Way Up

They studied the mountain.

Below them, the black sand beach curved away in both directions, meeting the dark water of the strait in a line so precise it might have been drawn with a ruler. Above them, the slopes climbed at an angle that steepened with altitude, the lava flows layered and angled like the scales of a dragon's back, each one presenting a maze of ridges and channels and sudden drop-offs that would have to be navigated by feel as much as by sight. Dark shapes moved on the upper slopes -- Buta sentries, posted along the eastern and southern ridges, their massive silhouettes visible against the glow of the lava flows. Genderuwo scouts, smaller and faster, flickered between the rocks in the middle reaches, their movements quick and erratic, the hunting patterns of predators covering known territory.

But the guards were looking outward.

Kian Santang saw it, and Bagus saw it at the same moment: the sentries faced the strait, the eastern approach, the direction from which any attack or incursion would logically come. They watched the water. They watched the island chain to the north, where -- and Kian Santang's heart clenched as the realization struck -- where Raden Wijaya and his company were fighting, bleeding, drawing every eye. The diversion was working. The enemy's attention was fixed on the loud, visible, impossible-to-ignore disturbance that five warriors were creating among the northern islets, and the western face of the mountain -- the face that Kian Santang and Bagus now studied from the shadow of a lava ridge -- was less watched. Not unwatched. But less.

A path existed.

It was not a path in any conventional sense -- no trail, no markers, no evidence that any being had ever walked it by choice. It was a geological accident: a channel carved into the western slope by an ancient lava flow, now cooled and hardened, its walls high enough to provide cover from observation above, its floor rough but navigable, winding upward through the least-watched quadrant of the mountain in a series of switchbacks that would, if the rock held and the vents did not block the way and the sentries did not look down at the wrong moment, bring them to the caldera rim.

"That," Bagus said, pointing with his chin.

"That," Kian Santang agreed.

They shouldered their packs. Kian Santang adjusted the bamboo case on his back -- the keris was vibrating, pulling upward, straining toward the caldera with a force that was almost physical, as though the blade were a lodestone and the magma above were its pole. Bagus checked his golok, his water skin, his bundle of provisions. He tightened his sandal straps. He rolled his shoulders once, settling his pack.

"Ready?" Kian Santang asked.

"No," Bagus said. "Let's go."

---

### VI. The Climb

They climbed into the fire.

The lava channel provided cover but not comfort. The walls radiated heat -- a deep, dry, relentless heat that sapped moisture from the air and from their bodies, and within the first hundred paces their clothes were dark with sweat and their throats were parched. Kian Santang rationed the water: a mouthful every hundred paces, no more, swished and held before swallowing, the way the old farmers in Padalarang had taught him to manage thirst during the long days in the sawah. Bagus followed the same discipline without being told.

The rock beneath their feet was treacherous -- sharp where it had fractured, smooth where volcanic glass had formed, and in places so hot that their sandals smoked and they had to move quickly, skipping from cooler stone to cooler stone like children crossing a creek on stepping stones. The sulfur stink was overwhelming, and they tore strips from their tunics and tied them across their faces, breathing through the cloth, which helped a little and not enough.

They climbed in silence. Not the heavy silence of the keris -- that was there, constant, the blade's voice a pressure in Kian Santang's skull that he had learned to carry the way one learns to carry pain, by refusing to give it the attention it demanded. This was a different silence -- the working silence of two people engaged in a task that required the full commitment of their bodies and most of their minds, the silence of pure effort, of one foot placed before the other on uncertain ground, of hands finding holds on hot stone and pulling, pulling, pulling upward against gravity and heat and the accumulated weight of everything they carried, physical and otherwise.

Bagus climbed behind Kian Santang, and his presence was a wall at the keris-bearer's back -- not a wall of stone or iron but of something more durable, something that the mountain's fire could not melt and the keris's whispers could not crack. Bagus was there. Bagus had always been there. And the simple fact of his presence -- his breathing, his footsteps, the occasional scrape of his golok against the rock wall -- was the thread that kept Kian Santang tethered to himself, the rope that prevented the keris from pulling him into its vision of the world, the anchor that held.

From below, faintly, carried on the hot wind that rose from the strait, the sounds of battle drifted up to them. Metal on metal. A distant roar that might have been a Buta or might have been the sea. A sound that might have been a war-cry in a voice that might have been Cepot's, high and defiant and furious.

Their friends. Fighting. Bleeding. Giving them this.

Kian Santang climbed.

---

### VII. The Last Look

They rested once, at a point where the lava channel widened into a small depression sheltered from the wind and hidden from above by an overhang of cooled magma. The stone here was merely warm rather than hot, and they sat with their backs against the wall and drank sparingly and said nothing for a long time.

Kian Santang looked back.

Below them, the western slope fell away in a tumble of black rock to the shore, and beyond the shore, the strait -- dark water streaked with foam and steam, dotted with pumice, stretching away to the north and west. The island chain was visible as a series of dark shapes against the haze, and on one of those shapes -- which one, he could not tell -- his friends were fighting. He could no longer hear the battle. The distance and the mountain's noise had swallowed it. But they were there. He knew they were there.

Beyond the islands, lost in the haze and the distance and the curvature of the earth, lay the world he had come from. The highlands of Pasundan. The green valleys where the sawah terraces glowed gold in the morning sun and the irrigation water caught the light and scattered it in a thousand small mirrors across the hillsides. The lembur of Padalarang, where the houses stood on stilts above the rice paddies and the smoke from the cooking fires rose into air that smelled of cloves and wood and the particular sweetness of steamed rice. The angklung groves, where the bamboo instruments hung from their frames and made music when the wind blew, a sound so gentle and so rooted in the landscape that it seemed to grow from the earth itself.

He could see it. Not with his eyes -- the haze and the distance made that impossible -- but with the deeper sight that belongs to those who are far from home and carry it within them. He could see the mist in the valleys at dawn, and the fireflies rising from the paddies at dusk, and the face of his mother standing in the doorway of their house, waiting for him to come home from the sawah, and the sound of the Seren Taun drums, and the taste of nasi timbel wrapped in banana leaf, and the feeling of cool water on tired feet after a long day in the fields.

He carried that image. He placed it in his heart beside the phial of starlight -- his home, his reason, his answer to every dark whisper the keris had ever breathed.

"Bagus."

"Yes?"

"Thank you."

Bagus looked at him. His broad face was streaked with ash and sweat, his tunic was torn, his sandals were half-burned through, and there was a blister on his right palm where the hot rock had caught him. He looked exactly like what he was: a Tuyul farmer from Padalarang who had no business being on a volcano and who was here anyway, because his friend was here, and that was reason enough.

"You can thank me with rambutan," Bagus said. "When we get home."

He stood. He offered his hand. Kian Santang took it.

---

### VIII. Into the Smoke

They turned to the mountain.

Above them, the caldera rim was visible now -- a jagged line of dark rock against the lurid glow of the sky, closer than it had been, still impossibly far. The path wound upward through steam and shadow and the constant, grinding vibration of a mountain that was never still, never quiet, never at peace. The keris pulled upward. The phial of starlight pulsed against Kian Santang's chest. Between the two forces, he stood -- small, frightened, determined -- and looked at what lay ahead.

The lava channel narrowed. The heat intensified. The air shimmered and warped, and through the shimmer, the slopes of Krakatau rose in terraces of fire and darkness, and the path was there -- faint, treacherous, threading between the sentries that looked outward and the vents that hissed their scalding warnings.

Kian Santang looked back one last time.

The strait. The islands. The world he was fighting for -- not the grand, abstract world of nations and kingdoms, but the small, specific world of a lembur in the Pasundan highlands where rice grew in terraced paddies and angklung music drifted through misty valleys and a garden waited to be planted. That world. His world.

He turned to the mountain.

"*Hayu, Bagus. Kami teruskeun.*" -- *Let's go, Bagus. We continue.* The Sundanese came naturally, the basa loma of two friends on a road together, the language of home carried into the fire.

"Right behind you, Kian. Always."

They climbed into the smoke. Two Tuyul -- small, barefoot, carrying between them a cursed blade and a water skin and a golok and a phial of starlight and the memory of home -- ascending a volcano of fire and shadow, moving upward through the heat and the haze toward the caldera where the keris had been forged and where it must be unmade. Behind them, across the dark strait, the distant sounds of battle faded and were gone, and the mountain closed around them, and the world narrowed to the next handhold, the next step, the next breath.

The smoke swallowed them.

And the mountain burned on.
