# Bab 3: Bayangan (Shadows)

---

The weeks after Uncle Kian's departure settled over Lembur Padalarang like sediment in still water. Days passed. The rains came and went. The sawah terraces filled and turned to mirrors, reflecting the pale skies of the highland wet season. Life, as it always does in the small places of the world, resumed its gentle rhythm — and Kian Santang tried, with all the quiet determination of his nature, to let it carry him along.

He tended his garden. He swept the veranda of the rumah panggung each morning, shaking out the tikar mats and hanging them over the carved railing to air. He fed the chickens and collected their warm eggs in a rattan basket. He repaired the bamboo fence that the monsoon winds had loosened. He left fresh *sesajen* at the small shrine beside his door — flowers and rice and a cup of water, offerings to the *karuhun*, the ancestor spirits, asking for their protection in the old way, the way his mother had taught him before the fever took her. These were the tasks that had shaped his days since boyhood — small rituals of order and care — and he performed them now with a diligence that bordered on desperation, as though by keeping the world around him perfectly maintained, he could prevent it from unraveling.

But the keris sat in its teak chest in the back room, wrapped in its cloth of ancient batik, and no amount of swept floors or mended fences could make him forget it was there.

He did not touch it. He obeyed Eyang Guru's warning absolutely. But he did not need to touch it to feel its presence. It was like a second heartbeat in the house — faint, persistent, slightly wrong in its tempo. In quiet moments, when the evening rain drummed on the atap roof and the village settled into sleep, he could hear it. Not words, exactly. Not even a voice. Something deeper, more insidious — a hum at the edge of consciousness, a low vibration in the bones of his skull, like a tuning fork pressed to the base of his spine. It whispered of things he could not quite name: vast landscapes seen from impossible heights, the taste of ash, the feeling of a hand closing slowly into a fist.

His dreams had worsened. The vision of Krakatau returned nightly — the black caldera, the rivers of molten stone, the figure on the obsidian throne whose eyes burned like embers — and now there were new images layered upon the old. He dreamed of armies marching through burning forests. He dreamed of nine riders moving through darkness on beasts that were not horses. He dreamed, once, that he held the keris unsheathed and the seven luk of its blade writhed like a living thing in his grip, and the pamor patterns crawled up his arm like vines of black iron, and he woke gasping, his hand clenched around nothing, his skin cold with sweat. Each morning he rose feeling as though his *bayu* — his vital breath, the life-force that flowed through every living being — had been slowly siphoned in the night, drawn out of him like water drawn from a well by an unseen hand.

He had lost weight. His neighbors noticed. Mbok Tari from the food stall at the crossroads pressed extra portions of nasi timbel on him when he passed, clucking her tongue. Old Pak Jaya, who tended the communal fish pond, looked at him sideways and muttered about young men who studied too much and ate too little.

Only Bagus said it plainly.

---

Bagus was the son of Pak Rahmat, who had been the Kian family's gardener and groundskeeper for as long as anyone could remember. Where Kian Santang was slight and fine-featured — the delicate build of old Tuyul nobility — Bagus was broad and solid, stocky through the shoulders and thick in the arms, with the sturdy frame of a Tuyul built for labor rather than courtly pursuits. His hands were calloused from years of gripping the cangkul hoe and the arit sickle, his dark skin weathered by sun and rain, and his honest brown eyes sat in a round, open face that could not have dissembled if his life depended on it. He was slightly older than Kian Santang, though you would not have known it from their manner together — Bagus deferred to Kian out of old habit and genuine affection, but there was a steadiness to him, a rootedness, that made him seem like the elder in all the ways that mattered.

He loved three things above all others: good food, growing things, and his friend Kian Santang. The order shifted depending on how recently he had eaten.

They had grown up together — playing in the sawah mud as children, climbing the ancient waringin trees at the lembur's edge, learning pencak silat side by side under Guru Saleh's strict and patient eye. Where Kian was quick and fluid, a dancer in the *jurus* forms, Bagus was a wall — slow to move but immovable once planted, his low stances rooted to the earth as though he had grown there. In sparring, Kian would circle and feint and strike like water; Bagus would absorb, deflect, and wait, and when his counter came it landed like a falling tree. They complemented each other perfectly, and they both knew it, and neither needed to say so.

It was during their morning silat practice on the clearing above the village — the flat ground beneath the old cemara pines where the mist hung like gauze in the early light — that Bagus finally spoke.

They had been drilling the *langkah* footwork patterns, moving through the familiar sequences with the ease of long practice. But Kian Santang was off. His timing faltered. His transitions were sluggish, his strikes arriving a half-beat late, and twice he stumbled on footwork that he had mastered years ago. When Bagus swept his leading leg — gently, a touch that should have prompted an easy recovery — Kian went down hard on the wet grass and lay there a moment, breathing, staring up at the canopy.

Bagus squatted beside him.

"*Rahayu, Kang?*" he asked — *Peace be with you, brother?* — in the easy, informal register of lifelong friends. Then, dropping into the blunt and loving Sundanese that only a lifelong companion can manage: "You look terrible. You eat like a bird. You sleep like — well, I do not think you sleep at all. And just now you moved like Pak Jaya after too much tuak." He paused. "What is wrong, Kian?"

Kian Santang sat up. He looked at his friend's broad, worried face, and he wanted to tell him everything — the keris, the dreams, the whispering in the dark — but Eyang Guru's warning sealed his lips. The old sage had been specific: tell no one. Not until we understand what we are dealing with.

"I have not been sleeping well," Kian said, which was true. "Bad dreams."

Bagus studied him with the frank, undeceivable gaze of a man who has known you since you were both knee-high to a sawah dike.

"Bad dreams," Bagus repeated. "Since your uncle left."

"Since then. Yes."

"And the old man. The one who came from far away. Eyang — what was his name?"

"Eyang Guru Batara."

"He told you something." It was not a question.

Kian Santang said nothing. Bagus nodded slowly, as though the silence itself were an answer — which, between the two of them, it was.

"Well," Bagus said, standing and offering his hand, "whatever it is, you will tell me when you are ready. And until then, you will come to my mother's house tonight and eat a proper meal. She is making rawon, and she will not accept refusal. Neither will I."

He pulled Kian Santang to his feet with the effortless strength of a man who shifted fifty-kilo sacks of rice for amusement.

Kian Santang managed something like a smile. It was the first in weeks.

"*Nembah, priya*," he said quietly — *I bow to you, companion* — and the warmth in the old Sundanese phrase carried more than its simple words could hold.

---

Eyang Guru Batara came back on a night of storms.

It was the kind of storm that the Pasundan highlands breed in the deep of the wet season — not the gentle afternoon rains that feed the sawah, but a great churning darkness that rolled down from the shoulders of Gunung Tangkuban Perahu like the wrath of old gods. Lightning split the sky in branching white veins — the Tuyul elders called it *cagak Batara Guru*, the trident of Lord Shiva hurled from the upper realm of the Triloka — and thunder came not in single claps but in long, rolling detonations that shook the floorboards of the rumah panggung and set the oil lamps swaying on their chains.

Kian Santang was alone. He sat by the hearth in the main room, the fire burning low, a cup of cold *wedang* forgotten beside him. The keris whispered. It always whispered louder in storms, as though the electricity in the air fed something in the metal. He was trying not to listen. He was failing.

The knock came sharp and urgent — three raps, then two, then one. Uncle Kian's old signal. Kian Santang crossed the room in three quick strides and threw the door open.

Eyang Guru stood in the downpour, and the sight of him drove all other thoughts from Kian Santang's mind. The old sage looked as though he had aged a decade in the weeks since their last meeting. His white hair hung lank and dripping. His robes, once immaculate, were torn and mud-spattered. A long, raw scratch ran from his left temple to his jaw, barely healed. But it was his teak staff that told the true story — the ancient wood, dark as old iron, was scarred with deep gouges and what looked like burn marks, as though something with claws of fire had raked across it again and again.

"Inside," Eyang Guru said. His voice was hoarse but steady. "Quickly."

Kian Santang pulled him in, barred the door, and led him to the hearth. He built the fire up until it roared and crackled, filling the room with warmth and orange light. He fetched blankets. He heated water and brewed jamu pahitan — the bitter herbal tonic, thick and dark as swamp water, that the old traveler had always favored — and pressed the cup into the sage's trembling hands.

Eyang Guru drank deeply. The bitterness did not make him flinch. He drank as a man drinks who has been running for days and has forgotten the taste of anything but fear and dust. When the cup was empty, he set it down, drew a long breath, and looked at Kian Santang with eyes that held a grief older than the mountains.

"I know what it is," he said. "I know the full truth of the keris. And I wish with all my heart that I did not."

The fire popped. The storm howled against the walls. Kian Santang sat down across from the old man, and the world he had known — the small, safe, beloved world of Lembur Padalarang — began, quietly and irrevocably, to end.

---

"In the first age of Nusantara," Eyang Guru began, and his voice took on the cadence of a dalang — the ancient storyteller's rhythm, measured and deliberate, as though the words themselves were wayang puppets being placed one by one upon the screen of firelight — "before the kingdoms of men had risen, before the great temples were even dreamed of, there came a being from beyond the world."

He stared into the fire as he spoke, and the flames painted moving shadows across his weathered face.

"He was not born of earth or sea or sky. He came from the spaces between worlds — the dark intervals that separate one reality from the next — drawn by the spiritual energy of the archipelago. For Nusantara is not like other lands, Kian. The *sakti* that flows through these islands, through every stone and root and river, is among the most potent in the world. The *bayu* of the earth is strong here — the Triloka press close together in these islands, and the boundary between the middle world and the realms above and below is thinner than silk. It called to him. And he came."

"Raja Iblis," Kian Santang said quietly.

"Raja Iblis. The Shadow King. He took the form of a great king — beautiful and terrible, clad in robes of volcanic glass, crowned with a diadem of black iron — and he built his fortress in the molten heart of Gunung Krakatau, where the earth's fire burns closest to the surface of the world. There, in chambers cut from living obsidian, he practiced the forbidden arts. Not the craft of the true empu — not the sacred metallurgy passed down from Sang Hyang Widhi through generations of faithful smiths. No. He took that sacred art and inverted it. Corrupted it. He forged a keris — *ginawé ku kagelapan* — made by darkness itself — using the darkest techniques: human bone folded into the steel, the blood of murdered kings quenching the blade, rituals spoken in a language that predates human speech."

Eyang Guru's voice dropped lower.

"He poured his own essence into the metal. His shadow-soul. His will to dominate. Seven pamor patterns he coiled into the blade — seven luk in its shape — each one a chain of mastery over a different aspect of the world. The first pamor commands fear. The second commands loyalty. The third shatters the will. The fourth corrupts memory, so that the bearer forgets who he was. The fifth grants sight beyond sight — but only of suffering. The sixth bends the natural world, turning beast and earth to the bearer's purpose. And the seventh..."

He paused. The fire crackled.

"The seventh is the pattern of binding. It links the keris to the nine lesser blades — the nine shadow keris that Raja Iblis forged afterward and gave to nine great kings of Nusantara. Those nine rulers were seduced by promises of empire, and when they took the blades, they were consumed. Their bodies twisted. Their *atma* burned away. They became the Leak — shape-shifting night-terrors, neither alive nor dead, bound to the Shadow King's will forever. The nine riders of darkness. The Leak Sembilan."

Kian Santang felt the blood drain from his face. In the back room, the keris pulsed once — a slow, heavy throb, like a heart beating in its sleep.

"The Shadow King was defeated," he said, clinging to the one part of the story he knew. "The great alliance — the Bidadari, the Kurcaci, the old kingdoms — they cast him down."

"They did." Eyang Guru nodded slowly. "At terrible cost. The war consumed an age. The *wanantara* — the deep wilderness — burned from Sumatra to Papua. The seas boiled around Krakatau when the final assault came. The Shadow King's body was shattered — destroyed utterly — but his spirit could not be killed. Not while the Keris Murka exists. For the keris IS his power, Kian. It is his anchor to this world. As long as the blade endures, the Shadow King endures — diminished, bodiless, a thing of smoke and fury — but not gone. Never gone."

"And the keris was lost."

"Lost. Carried from the battlefield by a soldier who did not understand what he held. Passed from hand to hand across the centuries, leaving a trail of madness and ruin wherever it surfaced. Until it disappeared entirely — fell into the limestone caves of Gunung Sewu, where your Uncle Kian found it, by chance or by fate, and where he bore it for decades without understanding its full nature. The keris was patient. It was waiting."

The storm lashed the windows. Rain drove through the gaps in the shutters, hissing on the hearthstones.

"Waiting for what?" Kian Santang asked, though he was not sure he wanted the answer.

Eyang Guru looked at him.

"To be found by the Shadow King's servants. To be returned to Krakatau. For if the Keris Murka is brought back to the caldera where it was forged, the Shadow King will have power enough to rebuild his form, to walk the world again in terrible majesty, and no alliance of the free peoples will be strong enough to cast him down a second time."

Silence fell between them. Kian Santang could hear his own heartbeat. He could hear the keris.

"But there is worse news still," Eyang Guru said, and his voice cracked on the words like old wood splitting. "Empu Gandring — you know the name?"

"The great keris-smith. Your friend."

"My colleague. My brother in craft, or so I believed." The grief in Eyang Guru's voice was raw and vast. "He has been corrupted. I do not know when it began — perhaps decades ago, perhaps longer. He went to Gunung Merapi, to the eastern volcanic ranges, and there he has built a fortress. A second seat of shadow, Kian. He breeds Buta Raksasa in the depths — giant demon warriors, stronger and more cunning than the common Buta hordes. He raises an army for the Shadow King. And he seeks the keris. His spies, his creatures, his corruption — all of it bent toward one purpose: finding the Keris Murka and returning it to Krakatau."

Kian Santang stared at the old man. The room felt smaller. The walls felt thinner. Beyond them, the storm raged, and somewhere in the darkness, things moved that had no right to exist.

"Then what do we do?" he asked. "Can we hide it? Bury it again? Throw it into the sea?"

Eyang Guru shook his head. "It cannot be hidden. It calls to its master and to his Leak. It will always be found. It cannot be buried — the earth itself rejects it, and in time it would work its way back to the surface. It cannot be drowned — the sea would cast it upon the shore. And it cannot be broken or melted by any earthly fire." He paused, and his eyes were terrible. "It can only be unmade in the fire where it was forged. In the caldera of Krakatau itself."

The words fell into the room like stones into deep water.

"Krakatau," Kian Santang repeated. His voice sounded very far away to his own ears. "You want me to carry this thing to the heart of the Shadow King's own domain."

"I do not want it," Eyang Guru said gently. "The world requires it."

"But why me?" The question burst from Kian Santang's chest like a trapped bird. "I am nobody, Eyang. I am a Tuyul from a lembur so small it does not appear on the trade maps. I have never left the Pasundan highlands. I know nothing of war, nothing of great deeds, nothing of the wide and terrible world you are describing. Why not a king? A warrior? A — a great empu, like yourself?"

Eyang Guru leaned forward, and the firelight caught the deep lines of his face, and for a moment he looked not old but ancient — a being who had watched centuries turn like pages.

"That," he said softly, "is precisely why it must be you. Do you not understand? The great kings would covet the keris's power. The warriors would be seduced by its promise of strength. Even I —" He stopped, and something passed across his face that might have been fear. "Even I would be tempted. The keris feeds on ambition, Kian. On the desire for dominion, for mastery, for the power to shape the world according to one's will. And every heart that desires great things is vulnerable to it."

He reached across and gripped Kian Santang's thin wrist.

"But you, Kian — you are small. You desire no dominion. You love nothing more than your garden, your lembur, your quiet mornings sweeping the veranda. The keris will find less purchase in a heart that wants nothing but to tend a few rows of kangkung and watch the rain fall on the sawah. That is not weakness. In this matter, against this enemy, it is the greatest strength in all of Nusantara."

Kian Santang looked down at his own hands — small, brown, unremarkable. The hands of a gardener. Not a hero.

"I am afraid," he said.

"Good," said Eyang Guru. "*Mulah sieun ku kasieun* — do not fear the fear itself. The fearless are the first to fall. Fear is the mind reminding you that what you carry matters. It is your *dharma* now, Kian — your sacred duty, *tinangtu ku Sang Hyang Widhi* — determined by the Divine, laid upon you not by choice but by the turning of the cosmic wheel. And *dharma* carried with fear is still *dharma* fulfilled."

---

They might have spoken further — of routes and plans and the impossible geography between Pasundan and the Sunda Strait — but the world did not grant them the luxury of time.

It began with the dogs.

Every dog in Lembur Padalarang began to howl at once — not the usual barking at a passing civet cat or a neighbor's wandering goat, but a high, thin, keening sound, ragged with terror. The sound rose from every corner of the village, from every yard and alley, a chorus of animal fear that lifted the hair on Kian Santang's arms.

Then the chickens went silent. Every henhouse, every coop, every roosting bird — all of them, in a single moment, stopped making any sound at all. It was as though something had pressed a hand over the mouth of the world.

The temperature dropped. Not gradually, not the gentle cooling of a highland evening, but a sudden, sharp plunge — the warmth draining from the room as though a door had been opened onto a place where warmth had never existed. The fire in the hearth guttered and shrank, its flames turning pale blue, and the shadows in the corners of the room grew deeper, denser, as though they were gathering themselves into shapes.

Eyang Guru was on his feet instantly. His hand closed around his scarred teak staff, and the old wood began to glow — faintly, reluctantly — with a pale amber light, as though it were remembering what it was made for. He murmured something under his breath — '*Om Namah Shivaya*' — the ancient invocation to the Destroyer, the prayer spoken in moments of mortal peril since before the first *candi* was raised on Nusantara's soil.

"Stay low," he whispered. "Do not go to the window."

But Kian Santang was already moving. Something pulled him — not the keris, not this time, but a deeper instinct, the animal need to see the shape of the thing that hunts you. He crossed to the window and peered through the gap in the wooden shutters.

The rain had stopped. The storm clouds still hung low and black over the highlands, but the rain itself had ceased, and in the unnatural stillness, the sawah terraces gleamed like plates of dark metal under the faint light of the hidden moon. The village lay below, its thatched and tiled roofs huddled together, and not a single lamp burned in any window. It was as though Lembur Padalarang had collectively held its breath.

And on the ridge above the sawah — the long, bare ridge that separated the terraces from the forested slopes of the upper hills — there was a figure.

It sat mounted on something that was not a horse. The creature beneath it was low-slung and massive, moving with the fluid, rolling gait of a great cat, and even at this distance — two hundred paces, perhaps more — Kian Santang could see the faint luminescence of its eyes, a sick yellowish green, the color of phosphorescence on rotting wood. A Harimau Siluman. A spirit-tiger. Twice the size of any natural beast, its stripes shifting and blurring as though its very form were uncertain, caught between the material world and something else.

But it was the rider that seized his gaze and held it.

Tall. Far too tall for any mortal frame. Shrouded in robes that seemed woven from the darkness itself — not cloth but shadow made tangible, rippling in a wind that did not blow. Its shape was wrong in ways that Kian Santang's eyes refused to fully process — proportions that shifted when he tried to focus, angles that should not have existed on a living form. Where its face should have been, there was only a deeper darkness beneath a hood, a void, an absence — and within that absence, two points of cold light that might have been eyes or might have been something far worse.

The figure turned its head.

Slowly. Deliberately. Scanning the village below with the patient attention of a predator that knows its prey is near but has not yet found the scent.

In the back room of the rumah panggung, the Keris Murka pulsed. Not a whisper this time but a throb — dark, hot, urgent — like a heart suddenly quickening. The batik wrapping could not muffle it. The teak chest could not contain it. Kian Santang felt it in his molars, in his sternum, in the soft place behind his eyes. The keris knew. It recognized what rode the ridgeline. It was calling to it — or the rider was calling to the keris — and for one terrible instant, the boundary between the two impulses dissolved entirely.

A hand seized his shoulder and wrenched him back from the window.

Eyang Guru's face was inches from his own, the old sage's eyes wide and fierce in the amber glow of his staff.

"They cannot see the keris yet," Eyang Guru hissed. "But they can feel it. Its presence, its resonance — like a scent on the wind. The longer we stay here, the closer they will come to finding it. Do you understand?"

Kian Santang nodded. His mouth was dry. His hands were shaking.

"We must leave," Eyang Guru said. "Tonight. Now."

"Leave?" The word came out cracked and small. "Leave Padalarang?"

"Leave everything. Take only what you can carry. Take the keris — wrap it well, keep it close to your body, do not let it touch your skin. We go south, into the *wana* — the deep forests — away from the roads. I know paths through the *wanadri* that even the Leak may not follow."

Kian Santang stood in the center of the room. The room where he had been born. The room where his mother had sung him to sleep with kidung lullabies. The carved wooden panels, the faded batik hangings, the stone hearth blackened by generations of cooking fires — every surface held a memory, and every memory was an anchor, and the thought of cutting himself free of all of them at once was a pain so sharp it stole his breath.

"There is one thing more," Eyang Guru said, and his voice softened. "You must not go alone. The keris whispers, and on a long road, in the dark, without a companion to remind you who you are — it will find its way into your heart. You need someone beside you. Someone whose spirit is simple and true, whose heart the keris cannot easily twist."

He did not say the name. He did not need to.

"Bagus," Kian Santang said.

"Find him. Wake him. Tell him what you must — but quickly. We leave within the hour."

Kian Santang moved through the house like a man in a dream. He took his silat traveling clothes — the dark practical garments of woven cotton, close-fitting and quiet. He took his betel pouch, though he did not chew. He took the small carved figure of Dewi Sri, goddess of rice and prosperity, that had stood on the family altar since before his birth — the *arca*, the sacred image, small enough to fit in his palm, worn smooth by generations of hands that had touched it in prayer. He took a waterskin and a pouch of cold rice wrapped in banana leaf.

And he took the keris.

He opened the teak chest with hands that trembled only slightly, and there it lay in its batik wrapping — and even through the cloth he could see it faintly, the dark shape of the blade, the seven sinuous curves, and the pamor patterns that moved like oil on water. He lifted it. It was warm. It should not have been warm. He tucked it into the sash at his waist, the batik cloth between the blade and his skin, and felt it settle against his hip with a familiarity that was deeply, profoundly wrong.

At the door, he stopped.

He looked back once at the rumah panggung — the lamplight flickering across the wooden walls, the fire dying on the hearth, the shadows pooling in the corners like dark water. His whole life, contained in a few small rooms. The smell of woodsmoke and clove and rain-soaked earth.

Outside, the night waited. On the ridge, the shadow rider prowled the darkness on its spirit-tiger, searching, patient, tireless. The rain had begun again — a thin, cold drizzle that fell on the sawah of Pasundan and turned the terraces into sheets of black glass.

Kian Santang pulled the door shut behind him, stepped into the rain, and went to find Bagus.

He did not look back again.
