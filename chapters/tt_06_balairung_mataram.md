## (The Great Hall of Giri Mataram)

## I. The Approach to Giri Mataram

Giri Mataram di kajauhan warnana siga menyan nu geus kolot sarta poek -- asep nu geus tiis, poho kana asalna tina seuneu suci.

The city of Giri Mataram, seen from the western ridge of the Kediri highlands, was the color of ancient, stagnant incense smoke -- ash that had forgotten its origins in sacred fire. It lay in the cupped palm of a volcanic valley like a jewel that had been buried too long in earth and had lost its luster, its once-brilliant facets dulled to the flat opacity of tarnished silver. The morning mist that should have risen from the terraced rice fields in white, curling tendrils -- the breath of the land, the *bayu* of the living earth -- instead hung motionless above the rooftops, a grey shroud that did not lift even when the sun crested the eastern mountains and threw its rays, weakened and yellowed, across the valley floor.

Raden Wijaya drew his horse to a halt at the ridge's edge and looked down upon the city of his ancestors. Beside him, the Dwija Putih sat astride a white stallion that Arya Kamandanu's men had provided -- a *kuda turangga* of Kediri breeding, its flanks the color of fresh cream, its mane unbraided and flowing free as befitted a horse bearing a sage. The beast had accepted its rider without protest, without so much as a stamp or a toss of its head, as though it recognized in the Dwija Putih something older and more commanding than any rider it had ever known. Behind them, Dewi Anjani rode with the fluid grace of wind made flesh, her pale silver bow slung across her back, her amber eyes scanning the valley below with an expression that hovered between vigilance and sorrow. Tumenggung Batu rode with the grim, compressed determination of a man who had never truly been comfortable on horseback and was not about to admit it; his war hammer lay across the saddle before him, and his thick legs gripped the horse's flanks with enough force to forge iron.

Arya Kamandanu brought his *kuda* alongside Raden Wijaya's mount. The Tumenggung of Kediri had ridden through the night without complaint, his patrol of twenty horsemen fanning out behind them across the ridge in a formation that was half honor-guard and half battle-screen. His face was dark with the dust of the road and the weight of things he had seen in the border lands -- villages emptied by fear, fields left unharvested, the charred remains of watchtowers that had fallen to Buta raiding parties. The silver filigree on his breastplate was tarnished, and the bells in his horse's mane had been muffled with strips of torn cloth, silenced for the sake of stealth.

"*Sira* sees it, Raden," Arya Kamandanu said quietly, following the prince's gaze down into the valley. "The city sleeps, but it is not the sleep of peace. It is the sleep of the drugged, the poisoned. We have watched from the borders these many months, and with each passing moon, the sickness deepens."

Raden Wijaya said nothing for a long moment. He felt the morning air upon his face, thin and sharp with the altitude of the ridge, carrying with it the faintest trace of sulfur from the distant volcanic chain and the nearer, more insidious scent of decay -- not the honest decomposition of leaves and soil, but something deeper, something spiritual, the smell of a kingdom's *bayu* (life force) being slowly drained from its veins.

*This is my home,* he thought, and the thought was a blade twisting in his chest. *This is the city where my father's father's father sat upon the throne of Hayam Wuruk, where the gamelan played the Srepegan at dawn and the court poets composed their kidung in the shadow of the waringin. Where the batik-makers laid their copper stamps upon white cotton in patterns that told the story of Mataram's glory -- the Parang Rusak, the broken blade, the symbol of a kingdom that could not be defeated. And now it lies in its own filth, drugged and whispering, its king a puppet, its glory a memory.*

He closed his eyes against the sting of something that was not quite tears -- he had not wept since the breaking of the Persekutuan at the Selat Sunda, and he would not weep now -- but that was closer to the geography of grief, a landscape of loss that he carried within him as a mountain carries its own weight, so deeply embedded in his structure that to remove it would be to crumble.

"*Duh Gusti*," he said softly, the invocation addressed not to anyone present but to the ancestors whose bones lay in the royal crypts beneath the *kadaton* (palace). "*Duh Gusti*, what have they done to you?"

The Dwija Putih spoke. His voice was calm, but beneath the calm was something that burned, the way a banked fire burns beneath a layer of ash -- contained, patient, waiting for the breath that would set it blazing.

"They have done what darkness always does, Raden. It has found the crack in the stone and poured itself in. Ki Surasenta is the instrument, but the hand that wields him belongs to Empu Gandring, and the will behind Gandring belongs to the Shadow King himself. Prabu Jayabaya has been walled away from the world inside the fortress of his own mind, and the bricks of that fortress are fear, and doubt, and the slow, sweet poison of whispered counsel that tells him what he wants to hear rather than what he needs to know."

The White Sage turned his golden eyes upon the valley, and for a moment those eyes blazed with a light that made the morning sun look dim and uncertain by comparison. "But the wall can be broken. It *will* be broken. I did not walk through the fire beneath the Jembatan Batu and return from the halls of death to be stopped by a whisperer in silk."

Tumenggung Batu shifted in his saddle, the leather groaning beneath his dense weight. "How many *bayangkara* (royal guard) does the city hold?" he asked, addressing Arya Kamandanu with the blunt practicality of a man who counted threats the way a mason counts stones -- by weight and by number.

Arya Kamandanu's jaw tightened. "The *bayangkara* number perhaps five hundred, but they are shells. Ki Surasenta has replaced the old captains with men who answer to him alone -- men whose eyes are empty and whose loyalty is purchased with *menyan* smoke and the dark *mantra* that he chants over them in the small hours. They move like puppets. They eat, they stand guard, they obey commands, but there is nothing behind their eyes. I have seen it, Tumenggung. I have watched good soldiers -- men I trained beside, men I drank tuak with around the campfire -- become hollow things, *wayang* without a *dalang*, shadows walking in the shape of men."

"Then we carve through them," Tumenggung Batu said, his hand closing on the shaft of his hammer.

"*Mulah*," the Dwija Putih said sharply. "They are not enemies, Tumenggung. They are prisoners. The spell can be broken at its source, and the source is not the soldiers but the spider who sits beside the throne. Cut the thread, and the puppets will remember they are men."

Tumenggung Batu grunted -- a sound of grudging acceptance, like a boulder settling into its final position. "Then let us cut the thread. Every moment we sit here admiring the view, the spider spins another yard of silk."

Raden Wijaya looked at the Dwija Putih. "Eyang, the city gates will be barred to us. Ki Surasenta's men control the outer walls. We cannot simply ride through the front gate and announce ourselves."

The White Sage smiled, and it was a smile that contained no humor but a great deal of fire. "The gates of death did not hold me, Raden. These gates are stone and iron, and stone and iron remember the hand of the righteous."

---

They descended from the ridge along a track that wound through terraces of abandoned rice paddies -- the *sawah* of the outer territories, once the green, breathing lungs of the kingdom, now dry and cracked and choked with weeds. The irrigation channels that had been the pride of Mataram's engineers -- a network of stone-lined waterways as old as the kingdom itself, carrying snowmelt from the mountain to the paddies in an unbroken flow that mirrored the flow of *dharma* through a righteous land -- were clogged with silt and debris, their waters reduced to a sluggish trickle of brown liquid that smelled of sulfur and neglect.

Raden Wijaya rode past a field where the rice stalks stood upright but dead, their husks empty, their grain stolen by some blight that was more than natural. The stalks were bleached white, like bones. He thought of the Mataram he had known as a child, before his family's exile -- the green oceans of rice that stretched from the palace walls to the horizon, bowing in the wind like a congregation of the faithful, their grain heavy and golden and sweet with the promise of the harvest. He thought of the farmers who had tended those fields, their backs bent in the *gotong royong* (communal labor), singing the planting songs that his nurse had taught him, songs that bound the community to the land and the land to the sky and the sky to the gods in a chain of mutual obligation that was the foundation of civilization itself.

All gone. All silent. The fields were ghost-fields now, tended by no one, yielding nothing.

"The *bayu* has been sucked from the earth," Dewi Anjani said, her voice carrying the harmonic resonance of wind chimes stirred by a mournful gust. Her golden eyes darkened as she extended her senses into the damaged land. "The spiritual ecology is in collapse, *Manusa*. The *karuhun* of the soil -- the ancestral spirits who guard the fertility of the earth -- have retreated to the deep places. They hide from the corruption that flows from the *kadaton*. Without them, the rice will not grow, the water will not flow, and the land will die. It is dying already."

"How long?" Raden Wijaya asked.

"Months. Perhaps a season. Perhaps two. The land is patient, but its patience is not infinite. When the last *karuhun* departs, the soil will become as the ash-fields of Krakatau -- dead stone that will grow nothing for a thousand years."

Raden Wijaya's hand tightened on his reins until the leather creaked. He thought of the Keris Nagasasra at his hip, its dark iron pulsing with a warmth that felt like urgency. The blade knew. The ancestral weapon of Mataram could feel its kingdom dying, and its pamor patterns stirred against the sheath like the restless sleep of a dreamer caught in nightmare.

They passed through the outer villages in silence. The houses -- once cheerful structures of teak and bamboo, their walls hung with fresh batik, their courtyards shaded by frangipani trees whose blossoms fell like fragrant snow -- were shuttered and dark. Smoke rose from a few chimneys, thin and listless, as though even the cooking fires lacked the will to burn. Dogs lay in the dust of the roads, too lethargic to bark. A child peered from behind a doorframe with eyes that were too old for its face -- not frightened, but *empty*, as though some essential light had been drawn from it, the way a leech draws blood.

Arya Kamandanu rode with his hand on his *tombak* (spear), his horsemen spreading into a defensive formation that covered their flanks and rear. "The people know we are here," he murmured to Raden Wijaya. "Word travels, even in a land of whispers. Ki Surasenta will know before we reach the outer walls."

"Let him know," the Dwija Putih said, and his voice carried the deep resonance of stone struck by stone, the sound of tectonic forces engaging. "Let him tremble in his silk and prepare his excuses. The reckoning comes."

The outer walls of Giri Mataram rose before them, a ring of grey volcanic stone that encircled the city in an embrace that had once been protective and was now merely confining. The walls were ancient -- laid in the time of Hayam Wuruk, their blocks of andesite fitted with the same precision as the temple stones of Prambanan, each one carved with the protective *rajah* (sacred symbols) that had been placed there by the court *pandita* to ward against evil. But the *rajah* were faded now. The carved sigils were obscured by lichen and by a dark stain that crept up the stonework from the base, a discoloration that looked less like weather damage than like the marks left by something that had clawed at the walls from beneath the earth.

The gate of the outer wall was closed. Two *bayangkara* stood before it in armor of bronze and leather, their spears crossed in the formal posture of denial. They were young men -- barely more than boys -- but their eyes had the flat, vacant quality that Arya Kamandanu had described: not hostile, not alert, but simply *absent*, as though the men behind those eyes had gone elsewhere and left their bodies standing at attention.

The Dwija Putih dismounted. He walked toward the gate with his staff striking the paving stones in a rhythm that was deliberate and measured, each impact sending a pulse of sound through the still air that resonated in the chest like the deepest note of a *gong ageng*. The white of his robes was blinding against the grey of the walls and the grey of the guards and the grey of the sky, a radiance that did not merely reflect light but generated it, as though the Sage were wrapped in a garment woven from the substance of dawn itself.

The *bayangkara* raised their spears. "The gate is closed," one said, his voice mechanical, reciting words that had been placed in his mouth by another. "By order of Ki Surasenta, *patih* of the realm. No visitors. No messengers. The King receives no one."

The Dwija Putih stopped three paces from the crossed spears. He looked at the young soldiers with an expression of infinite patience and, beneath the patience, an authority so vast that it made the stone walls of Mataram seem temporary by comparison.

"The King will receive *ingwang*," he said. His voice was quiet, but it filled the space before the gate the way sunlight fills a room, reaching every shadow, every corner, every hidden place. "And the *patih* who closes this gate in the name of fear does not speak for the throne. He speaks for the shadow that sits upon the throne's shoulder."

He raised his staff. The golden light at its tip flared -- not with the blinding intensity he had shown in the Wanantara, but with a warm, steady glow, like a lantern held up in a dark room. The light fell upon the faces of the *bayangkara*, and something stirred behind their vacant eyes. One of them blinked. The other lowered his spear by a fraction, his hand trembling as though the muscles of his arm were fighting against instructions that came from two different sources.

"Open the gate," the Dwija Putih said, and it was not a command but a liberation -- a permission granted to men who had forgotten they could choose.

The *bayangkara* looked at each other. The one who had blinked reached up and rubbed his eyes with the back of his hand, a gesture so human, so ordinary, that it struck Raden Wijaya like a blow to the heart. Then, slowly, the young soldier uncrossed his spear and stepped aside.

The gate opened.

## II. Entering the Kadaton

The city of Giri Mataram within the walls was worse than the countryside had suggested. It was a place where beauty had been preserved in the amber of neglect, its outlines still visible but its substance rotted away from within, the way a magnificent tree can stand for years after its heartwood has been consumed by termites, maintaining the shape of grandeur long after the reality has departed.

The streets were wide and paved with the same grey andesite as the walls, laid in patterns that replicated the *kawung* motif of Javanese batik -- circles within circles, a geometric meditation on the eternal cycle of life and death and rebirth. But the patterns were obscured now by drifts of fallen leaves that no one had swept, by the detritus of markets that no one attended, by the accumulated filth of a city that had stopped caring for itself the way a sick man stops washing, stops eating, stops maintaining the rituals of the living.

The market square, which Arya Kamandanu told them had once been the beating heart of the city -- a cacophony of color and commerce, where the spice traders from the eastern islands displayed their mountains of clove and nutmeg and cinnamon beside the silk merchants of the coastal kingdoms, where the *jamu* healers mixed their herbal remedies in copper pots and the *topeng* dancers performed their masked dramas for crowds of laughing children -- was empty. The market stalls stood like skeletons of their former selves, their canvas awnings rotting, their display tables bare. A single vendor sat behind a counter of dusty wood, selling nothing, his eyes fixed on a point in the middle distance that no one else could see. A cat walked through the square with the slow, deliberate gait of the only living creature that had not surrendered its autonomy.

Raden Wijaya rode through the market square with his jaw set so tightly that the muscles of his temples ached. He remembered this square. He remembered it as a child of six, sitting on his father's shoulders, watching the *wayang wong* performers dance the Ramayana beneath the great waringin tree at the square's center. He remembered the smell of *satay* smoke and melting palm sugar and the jasmine garlands that the flower sellers hung from every post and lintel. He remembered his father's laughter, a sound he had not heard since the exile, a sound that lived now only in the chambers of his memory, growing fainter with each passing year, the way a gamelan melody fades as you walk away from the *pendopo* where it is played.

The waringin tree was still there, but it was dying. Its leaves, which should have been a deep, glossy green -- the green of eternity, for the waringin is sacred, the tree beneath which the ancestors dwell -- were yellowed and sparse. Its aerial roots, which should have cascaded from the branches in a curtain of living rope, hung limp and dry, like the fingers of a dead man. Someone had tied a strip of white and yellow cloth around the trunk in the traditional manner of marking a sacred tree, but the cloth was old and frayed, and the prayers tied to it in small paper packets had gone unanswered and unread.

"That tree has stood for three hundred years," Raden Wijaya said, his voice barely above a whisper. "My ancestors planted it when they laid the first stones of the *kadaton*. It is the *jiwa* (soul) of the city. If it dies..." He did not finish the sentence. He did not need to.

The Dwija Putih looked at the dying waringin with an expression that was not sorrow but something harder, fiercer -- the expression of a healer who looks upon a patient's wound and sees not only the damage but the cure, and the cost of the cure, and accepts both without flinching.

"It will not die," the White Sage said. "Not today."

They rode through the inner streets, past the temples and the *pura* (shrines) that lined the processional way to the *kadaton*. These were ancient structures of stone and teak, their walls carved with the epics -- the Ramayana, the Mahabharata, the Panji cycle -- their courtyards paved with smooth river stones, their gates surmounted by the *kala* (demon face) carvings that served as both ornament and guardian. But the temples were dark. No priests tended the sacred fires. No offerings of flowers and rice and incense sat upon the stone altars. The *kala* faces, which were meant to be terrifying in the service of protection -- the grotesque masks that frightened evil spirits away from sacred spaces -- looked merely sad now, abandoned guardians staring out at a world that had forgotten why they were carved.

The *kadaton* of Giri Mataram rose before them at the end of the processional way, a complex of buildings and courtyards and gardens that occupied the highest ground in the city, its terraced stone platforms ascending in three tiers toward the central *pendopo* and the *Balairung Agung* (Great Hall) where the kings of Mataram held court. The architecture was magnificent even in its decay -- a fusion of the Hindu-Javanese temple style with the practical *joglo* construction of the lowland palaces, producing structures that were both monuments and living spaces, as grand as any temple but as warm as any home. The *naga* (serpent) carvings that flanked the stairways were masterworks of stone craft, their scales individually rendered, their coiled bodies suggesting both the power of the underworld and the protective embrace of the cosmic serpent who holds the earth upon his back. The fountains that should have played at each terrace level were dry, their basins filled with dead leaves and the dark, stagnant residue of water that had evaporated long ago.

Dewi Anjani dismounted at the base of the first terrace, and as her feet touched the stone of the *kadaton*, she drew a sharp breath.

"The corruption is dense here," she said, her luminous skin dimming visibly, the way a lamp dims when the air around it thickens with smoke. "*Bayu* moves through the stone like a diseased river. I can feel the *mantra* -- the dark chants that Ki Surasenta weaves in the hours before dawn, binding the palace to the Shadow King's will. It is like walking through a spider's web made of smoke and whispered lies."

She touched the carved *naga* that flanked the stairway, and her fingers came away coated in a fine, grey dust that was not stone dust but something else -- something that clung to the skin with an oily tenacity and smelled faintly of burnt offerings made to the wrong gods.

"Can you still sense the *bayu* beneath?" the Dwija Putih asked, watching her with an expression of clinical attention.

"It is there," Dewi Anjani said. "Deep. Buried beneath layers of filth. The original *rajah* placed upon these stones by the court *pandita* are still intact, still pulsing with the *sakti* (power) of the ancestors who blessed them. Ki Surasenta has not been able to erase them -- he has only covered them, the way one might cover a living flame with wet cloth. The fire still burns, but it cannot breathe."

"Then we give it air," the Dwija Putih said.

They ascended the first terrace, then the second. At each level, more *bayangkara* stood in their vacant poses, spears upright, eyes empty. The Dwija Putih passed them without pausing, and as he passed, the golden light that emanated from his staff -- not directed at them, merely ambient, the natural radiance of the Sage's renewed *sakti* -- touched their faces like a warm breath. Some of them stirred. One man lowered his spear and looked around him with the dazed expression of a man waking from a long, drugged sleep, blinking at the daylight as though he had forgotten what it was.

Tumenggung Batu walked beside Raden Wijaya, his iron-shod boots ringing on the stone steps with a percussive authority that was its own kind of announcement. The Kurcaci's broad face was set in the expression that Raden Wijaya had come to recognize as his deepest fury -- not the explosive, volcanic anger that the mountain-folk were known for, but the slow, tectonic compression of rage that builds over time, the pressure that precedes an earthquake.

"Look at this place," Tumenggung Batu said, his voice a low rumble, like the sound of distant stone-fall in a quarry. "I remember Giri Mataram. I came here fifty years ago, in my father's time, when Prabu Jayabaya was young and the alliance with the Kurcaci was still fresh. The stonework was maintained. The *naga* carvings were polished monthly. The fountains sang. It was a place where even a mountain-dweller could feel the *dharma* in the walls, the way you feel the warmth of a forge-fire in good iron." He kicked a piece of fallen masonry with his boot, sending it clattering down the terrace steps. "Now look at it. The stone is cracked. The carvings are weeping. The fountains are dust. It is a forge gone cold, a mountain hollowed out. Someone has taken the fire from this place, and when you take the fire from a forge, you take its purpose."

The corridor of the third terrace was lined with carved reliefs depicting the great kings of Mataram in chronological succession -- a stone chronicle of the dynasty that stretched back to the age of the first kingdoms. Here was Hayam Wuruk in his prime, standing with one foot upon a conquered Naga, his keris raised in triumph. Here was Ken Arok, the founder, rising from the ashes of civil war to forge a new kingdom from the fragments of the old. Here was the great queen Tribhuwana, her face serene, her hands extended in the *mudra* of justice, her kingdom at peace beneath her rule.

The reliefs had been defaced.

Not physically -- not with hammer and chisel, not with the crude violence of a conqueror who seeks to erase the past. The damage was subtler and more insidious. A dark film coated the stone, an oily residue that obscured the features of the carved kings, blurring their faces into anonymity, smearing their achievements into vagueness. It was as though someone had breathed upon the reliefs with a breath of corruption, fogging them the way breath fogs a mirror, making the past unclear, uncertain, deniable.

Raden Wijaya stopped before the relief of Hayam Wuruk. He reached out and touched the stone, and his fingers found the contours of his ancestor's face beneath the film of corruption. He could feel it -- the sharp line of the jaw, the broad forehead, the eyes that even in stone seemed to carry the weight of empire and the burden of righteousness. He rubbed at the film with his thumb, and a small patch of clean stone emerged, revealing the carved eye of the ancient king, staring out with an expression of quiet, implacable judgment.

"*Kula nyuwun pangapunten dhateng para karuhun*," Raden Wijaya whispered -- *I ask forgiveness of the ancestors* -- the *Krama Inggil* falling from his lips like a prayer spoken in a temple where the priests have fled. "I have come too late. I should have come sooner. I should have come before the rot set in, before the spider wove his web across your faces. But I am here now. *Inggih*, I am here."

The Dwija Putih placed his hand upon Raden Wijaya's shoulder. "You are not late, Raden. You are precisely on time. The hour was not ripe before. The fire had not been rekindled. But it burns now." His golden eyes met the prince's dark ones. "And you carry it within you, as your ancestor carried it before you. Not in the blade at your hip, but in the blood that flows through your veins. The blood of Mataram. The blood of kings."

They turned toward the entrance of the *Balairung Agung*.

The doors were massive -- twin panels of aged teak, each one the height of three men, carved with the royal insignia of Mataram: the Garuda clutching the Naga in its talons, the eternal struggle between sky and earth, light and darkness, dharma and adharma. The bronze fittings were green with verdigris, and the teak itself had darkened from its original honey-gold to a deep, sullen brown that was almost black, stained by years of the *menyan* smoke that seeped from within.

Two *bayangkara* stood before the doors, but these were different from the guards at the outer walls and the terraces. These men were fully under Ki Surasenta's influence -- their eyes were not merely vacant but actively hostile, lit with a dull, reddish gleam that was not a reflection of the light but something that burned from within, fed by the dark *mantra* that the advisor had woven into their minds. They crossed their spears as the party approached, and their movements were not mechanical but purposeful, the movements of men who had been given specific instructions and intended to carry them out.

"The *Balairung* is closed," one of them said, and his voice was not his own -- it was deeper, oilier, carrying the inflections of another speaker, as though the words were being spoken through him by a mouth elsewhere. "The Prabu rests. The *patih* has commanded that none shall disturb the royal meditation."

The Dwija Putih planted his staff upon the stone and drew himself to his full height. He was tall -- taller than Raden Wijaya, taller than the guards, taller, it seemed in that moment, than the teak doors themselves -- and his white robes blazed with a light that cast no shadows.

"*Ingwang* am the Dwija Putih," he said, and his voice was the voice of the volcano -- not the eruption, but the deep, subsonic vibration that precedes it, the voice of the mountain itself, ancient and patient and absolutely, utterly irresistible. "I have walked the halls of *Arcapada* and returned. I have spoken with the *Dewata* in the halls of sacred fire. I do not ask permission of *sewaka* (servants) to enter the halls of kings."

He raised his staff, and the golden light that blazed from its tip was not warm but *hot* -- a searing, purifying radiance that struck the dark *mantra* in the guards' minds like a hammer striking a chain. The red gleam in their eyes flickered, guttered, and went out. One guard dropped his spear. The other stumbled backward, his hands going to his temples, a cry of confusion escaping his lips.

The doors of the *Balairung Agung* swung open.

## III. The Great Hall -- First Confrontation

The Great Hall of Giri Mataram was the color of ancient, stagnant incense smoke, and it smelled of the same -- the thick, clotted sweetness of *menyan* resin burned continuously for years in a space with no ventilation, the fragrance long since curdled from sacred to suffocating. It was a smell that sat in the nostrils like a physical substance, coating the throat, dulling the senses, wrapping the mind in a gauze of torpor and forgetfulness. Beneath the *menyan* were other smells: stale jasmine, its petals brown and curling on neglected offering trays; the musty, animal scent of old silk too long unwashed; and beneath it all, faint but persistent, the acrid bite of fear -- the chemical excretion of human bodies under sustained spiritual duress, a smell that no amount of incense could entirely mask.

The hall was vast. Its dimensions seemed to exceed what should have been physically possible within the walls of the *kadaton*, as though the space had been stretched or distorted by the dark *sakti* that permeated it, the way a nightmare distorts the familiar rooms of a house. The teak beams of the ceiling soared upward into a gloom so dense that it was impossible to tell where the wood ended and the darkness began; the carved *naga* and *garuda* that adorned them were visible only as shapes, suggestions of form within the murk, their scales and feathers lost in a century of soot from the *menyan* censers that stood at intervals along the walls, their bronze mouths breathing grey plumes into the stagnant air.

The floor was polished marble -- *kancana* (golden) marble from the quarries of the southern mountains, its surface veined with patterns of black and amber that, in better days, had been maintained to a mirror shine, reflecting the torchlight and the gamelan musicians and the dancing girls and the ambassadors of a dozen kingdoms in its golden depths. Now the marble was dull, filmed with the residue of smoke and neglect, its golden veins obscured by a patina of grey that made the floor look like the skin of a sick man.

Along the walls, in niches carved for the purpose, stood the *gamelan Sekaten* -- the sacred orchestra of the court, its instruments arranged in the traditional formation: the paired *saron* and *demung* metallophones, the great *gong ageng* suspended in its wooden frame, the *bonang* chimes in their graduated rows, the *kendang* drums, the single *rebab* on its spike. The instruments were still beautiful -- the bronze of the *saron* keys was tarnished but not corroded, the carved teak frames retained their exquisite proportions -- but they were silent. No musician sat before them. No mallet had struck them in months, perhaps years. They waited in their niches like prisoners awaiting release, their silence a condemnation more devastating than any accusation.

And at the far end of the hall, upon a *singgasana* (throne) of obsidian and gold, sat **Prabu Jayabaya**, King of Mataram.

He was a man who should have been in the fullness of his power. He was of an age when the Javanese kings traditionally reached their prime -- past the impetuosity of youth, not yet diminished by the ravages of age, the body still strong, the mind sharpened by experience, the authority of the throne settled into the bones as naturally as the grain settles into the wood of a tree. Raden Wijaya remembered his uncle as such a man -- broad-shouldered, deep-voiced, his laugh like the *gong ageng* itself, a sound that filled rooms and lifted spirits and reminded everyone within earshot that the throne of Mataram was occupied by a living king and not a painted statue.

The man on the throne was a ghost of that memory.

Prabu Jayabaya was thin -- not merely lean but wasted, his royal robes of heavy silk hanging from his frame the way curtains hang from a rail, draping over absence rather than presence. His face was pale, the golden-brown complexion of his race faded to a sickly yellow that made him look like a figure carved from old wax, his cheekbones standing out in sharp relief above hollowed cheeks, his eyes sunken in their sockets like stones at the bottom of a dry well. His crown -- the *makuta* of Mataram, a glorious construction of gold and gems that had been worn by every king since Ken Arok -- sat upon his brow with the precarious balance of something placed there by hands other than his own, slipping slightly to the left, as though even the crown recognized that the head beneath it no longer held the *sakti* to support its weight.

His hands lay upon the arms of the throne like dead things, the fingers twitching occasionally with the involuntary spasms of muscles receiving instructions from a brain that was no longer fully in control of its own functions. His eyes were open but unfocused, staring at a point somewhere in the middle distance of the hall, seeing neither the smoke nor the shadows nor the figures who now stood at the entrance, but something else entirely -- some interior landscape of fog and whispered counsel, the prison that Ki Surasenta had built for him brick by patient brick.

Crouched beside the throne, at the King's right hand, sat -- or rather perched, for the posture was less that of a seated man than of a predatory bird -- **Ki Surasenta**.

The King's advisor was a study in the aesthetics of corruption. He was thin as a reed, his body arranged in angular folds that suggested something not quite human lurking beneath the silk -- as though his bones had been taken apart and reassembled in an order that served a different purpose than the one for which they were designed. His face was long and narrow, dominated by a drooping mustache that hung past his chin like the whiskers of a rat, its ends waxed to points that twitched when he spoke. His eyes were his most arresting feature -- small, dark, and ceaselessly mobile, never resting on any one point for more than a moment, scanning the room with the compulsive vigilance of a creature that knows it is prey as much as predator. They were the eyes of a man who had looked into the fires of Krakatau and had not been purified by the vision but scorched by it, burned into a shape that served the fire rather than the light.

He wore the black and red *dodot* (formal court dress) of a Mataram courtier, but the colors were wrong -- the black too deep, as though it absorbed light rather than reflecting it, and the red too dark, the red of old blood rather than of fresh dye. The *parang* patterns on his sarong -- the broken-blade motif that was the birthright of the Mataram court -- were twisted and distorted, the angular lines that should have been straight rendered crooked, the spaces between them filled with secondary patterns that, if one looked too closely, seemed to move, to writhe, to form shapes that the conscious mind refused to acknowledge.

There were courtiers in the hall. They lined the walls in rows, seated on mats in the formal posture of the Mataram court, their legs folded beneath them, their hands in their laps, their heads bowed. They wore the finery of their rank -- the *dodot* and the *kain* and the keris at the hip -- but the finery was as faded and stale as the hall itself, and the courtiers wore it not with pride but with the mechanical compliance of habit, dressing for a court that no longer functioned, attending a king who no longer ruled, observing a protocol whose meaning had been hollowed out and filled with smoke. Some of them wept silently, tears tracking down faces that did not change expression, as though the weeping were an autonomous function that continued regardless of the mind's condition. Others sat in a stillness so complete that they might have been carved from the same teak as the pillars, their breathing so shallow as to be invisible.

Raden Wijaya stepped into the hall, and the incense smoke parted before him like a curtain drawn aside by an invisible hand. Behind him came the Dwija Putih, and the light of his staff pushed the darkness back a few precious feet, carving a corridor of illumination through the grey murk. Dewi Anjani followed, her pale light diminished but not extinguished, a silver thread woven into the gold. Tumenggung Batu brought up the rear, his hammer held in both hands across his chest, his stance that of a warrior entering enemy territory.

Ki Surasenta's head snapped up. His eyes fixed on the intruders with a reptilian speed, and the muscles of his face rearranged themselves from their customary mask of unctuous servility into something that was, for one unguarded instant, purely feral -- the snarl of a creature whose burrow has been breached.

Then the mask returned, sliding over the feral face like silk over a blade.

"Who are these... intruders?" Jayabaya's voice was a weak, papery rasp that barely carried across the hall. It was the voice of a man speaking through layers of gauze, each word muffled by the accumulation of whispered poisons that had been poured into his ears for years. He did not look at the newcomers directly; his sunken eyes moved in their direction but could not quite focus, as though the visitors were shapes seen through deep water. "Ki Surasenta... why have you allowed these beggars to enter my presence?"

Ki Surasenta leaned closer to the King, and his lips -- thin, mobile, the color of bruised fruit -- curled against the royal ear. His voice was a whisper, but it was a whisper designed to be heard, a performance of intimacy that was in truth a demonstration of control: *See how close I am to the King. See how his ear belongs to me.*

"They are messengers of doom, *Gusti Prabu*," Ki Surasenta murmured, each word a droplet of carefully measured poison. "Rangers and Bidadari who seek to disturb your sacred peace with tales of war and fire. They carry the scent of the Shadow King's strait upon their clothes, seeking to bring the contagion into your sacred halls. They are agents of chaos, *Gusti* -- wolves in the garments of pilgrims, come to shatter the tranquility that *kula* (your humble servant) has worked so diligently to preserve."

The Dwija Putih stepped forward, and his staff struck the marble floor with a sound that was not merely loud but *absolute* -- a percussive impact that shattered the incense-thick silence of the hall the way a stone shatters the surface of a stagnant pool, sending shock-waves through the smoke that rippled outward in visible rings, disturbing the grey plumes of the censers, rattling the bronze keys of the silent *saron*, and reaching, at last, the throne itself, where Prabu Jayabaya flinched as though he had been struck.

"*Mulah!*" the White Sage thundered, his voice filling the hall the way the sun fills the sky at noon -- completely, inescapably, leaving no shadow unexposed. His golden eyes blazed with a light that was not warm but incandescent, the light of sacred fire concentrated into a gaze that could strip away illusion the way flame strips paint from wood. "The contagion is already here, Ki Surasenta. It sits beside the throne, whispering poison into the ear of a king who has forgotten how to lead. It wears silk and calls itself counsel. It breathes *menyan* and calls it meditation. It wraps chains in words and calls them wisdom. *Ingwang* see you, Surasenta. *Ingwang* see what you are."

The courtiers stirred. Along the walls, heads lifted -- some with hope, some with terror, some with the confused, blinking disorientation of sleepers jolted from a dream. The weeping ones wept harder. The still ones shifted on their mats. The hall, which had been a place of absolute stasis, began to move, to breathe, to remember that it was a place where living people gathered.

Ki Surasenta rose from his crouch beside the throne, unfolding himself to his full height, and his thin body cast a shadow on the marble that was disproportionately large, as though the shape that cast it was not entirely contained by the physical form. His rat-whiskers twitched. His dark eyes narrowed to slits.

"You dare," he hissed, and his voice was the voice of oil on cold water, smooth and spreading and leaving a stain wherever it touched. "You dare to enter the *kadaton* of the Prabu with threats and theatrics, you wandering charlatan? You fire-juggler? You decrepit performer of *debus* tricks? *Gusti Prabu*, this man is a fraud -- a *dalang* without a stage, playing with light and shadow to frighten children."

He turned to the King, his posture changing from confrontational to solicitous in the space of a breath, his body bending toward Jayabaya like a vine wrapping itself around a supporting trunk. "You are tired, *Gusti*. You need rest. Let *kula* send these vagrants away, and you may return to the peace that your soul requires."

Jayabaya's hand trembled on the arm of the throne. "I... I have not forgotten," he said, the words coming slow and thick, as though each one had to be dredged from the bottom of a well of fog. "Mataram is safe. The borders are... secure."

"The borders are burning, Prabu Jayabaya!"

Raden Wijaya stepped forward, and his voice was the *Krama Inggil* of a true heir of Mataram -- the highest register of the Javanese court language, the language in which kings address the gods and the gods address the cosmos, a register so formal, so laden with the weight of centuries of royal usage, that its very sound carried an authority independent of the words it shaped. His bearing changed as he spoke, the weary ranger straightening into the posture of the prince, the travel-worn clothes becoming irrelevant as the bearing beneath them asserted itself -- the straight spine, the squared shoulders, the lifted chin, the dark eyes blazing with a fire that was not the Dwija Putih's sacred radiance but something older, something bred into the bone, the fire of Mataram's bloodline.

"*Duh Gusti*, look at me!" His voice rang through the hall, striking the teak beams and rebounding, filling the space with echoes that layered upon each other like the voices of a choir. "*Kula Raden Wijaya, putranipun Prabu Hayam Wuruk, ahli waris dhampar Mataram!* I am Raden Wijaya, son of Hayam Wuruk, heir to the throne of Mataram! The Horse Lords of Kediri are all that stand between your people and the Buta Raksasa. The Shadow King's armies are massing at the gates of Merapi, and *Sira* sits here in a cloud of incense, listening to a snake!"

He pointed at Ki Surasenta, and the gesture was a judgment -- the extended hand of a prince identifying the enemy in the court, the ancient ritual of accusation that, in the old law of the Mataram kingdom, carried the weight of a formal charge.

"This man has stolen your mind, Uncle. He has taken your strength and fed it to the shadow. He has closed your gates and silenced your gamelan and let your fields die and your temples go dark, and he has told you it was peace. It is not peace. It is a coffin, and he is the undertaker, and if you do not wake now, *Gusti Prabu*, there will be nothing left to wake to."

The words fell into the smoke-thick air of the hall like stones into deep water, and the ripples spread outward through the rows of courtiers, touching each one, stirring each one, reaching even the most deeply entranced and loosening, by a fraction, the grip of the whispered *mantra* that held them.

Ki Surasenta laughed. It was a high, skittering sound, the sound of claws on glass, the sound of something small and sharp and venomous expressing its amusement. "A snake?" He spread his hands in a gesture of theatrical innocence, his thin lips stretching into a smile that exposed his teeth -- small, yellowed, numerous, arranged too closely together. "I am the King's only true *priya* (friend). I have kept him safe from the cold winds of the east. I have given him the peace that his ancestors could not find -- the peace of the mind, *Gusti*, the peace that comes from releasing the burden of the world and trusting it to capable hands."

He gestured at the hall, at the courtiers, at the smoke-shrouded instruments and the empty offering trays and the clouded marble. "Look at this court. Does it not rest? Does it not breathe the sacred *menyan* and find solace? The world outside is fire and chaos and the screaming of men who do not know when to surrender. But here, *Gusti* -- here, there is silence. Here, there is peace. Here, the King is protected from the storm."

"You've turned a lion into a house-cat!" Tumenggung Batu roared, and his voice was the voice of the mountain itself -- the deep, grinding bass of stone moving against stone, the sound that precedes a landslide, the sound that warns everything in the valley below to flee. His hammer rose in his fist, the dark iron of its head catching the feeble light and throwing it back as a hard, metallic gleam. "I've seen your kind before, whisperer! In the deep places under the mountains, where the dark things crawl. They wrap their silk around the strong and feed on them, and call it friendship. You are a parasite, Surasenta. A *leech* in silk and a *parang* sarong!"

The Kurcaci's words were blunt as his hammer, unadorned by rhetoric, unpolished by diplomacy -- and for that very reason they struck harder than all the eloquence in the hall. Several courtiers flinched. One, a young woman in a faded batik *kebaya*, let out a sob that she immediately smothered with her hand.

"Enough," Ki Surasenta snapped, and for a moment the oily smoothness of his voice cracked, revealing something beneath it that was not human confidence but something else, something cornered and desperate -- the sound of a creature whose territory is being invaded. "I will not be insulted by a *Kurcaci* digger and a dispossessed prince. *Gusti Prabu*, command these intruders to leave. They disturb your sacred rest."

But Jayabaya was not resting.

Something was happening behind the King's unfocused eyes. The words of Raden Wijaya -- the *Krama Inggil* of the Mataram court, the language of his blood, the register that his own father had used when addressing the sabha, the register that the court poets had used when reciting the *kidung* of the old kings -- had reached past the layers of fog and *menyan* and whispered poison and had struck something deep within, a bedrock of identity that Ki Surasenta's years of patient erosion had not been able to reach.

*Wijaya.*

The name echoed in the dark chambers of his drugged mind like a bell struck in a sunken temple. *Wijaya. The son of my brother. The blood of Hayam Wuruk. The heir.*

He blinked. His fingers, which had lain like dead things upon the arms of the throne, curled slightly, as though grasping for something they could no longer see.

## IV. Breaking the Spell

The Dwija Putih saw the moment. He had been waiting for it -- watching the King's face with the patient, focused attention of a physician watching for the first sign of response in a patient who has been given the antidote to a slow poison. He saw the blink. He saw the fingers curl. He saw the faintest tremor of awareness disturb the flat surface of the King's entranced gaze, like a single ripple breaking the mirror of a dead pond.

He raised his staff.

The golden light at its tip, which had been burning with a steady, contained radiance since he entered the hall, now blazed -- blazed with the full, unrestrained power of the *geni suci* (sacred fire) that had been rekindled in the depths beneath the Jembatan Batu, where the Dwija Putih had fallen as Eyang Guru Batara and risen as something more, something tempered and transformed, a vessel for a light that was older than the mountains and stronger than the darkness that lay beneath them.

"Prabu Jayabaya!" His voice was no longer the voice of a man. It was the voice of fire itself -- not the wild, destructive fire of the eruption, but the deep, sustaining fire at the heart of the earth, the fire that melts rock and forges continents, the fire that is the source of all heat, all light, all life. "Look at me! Look into the light of the *geni suci* and remember who you are!"

The light expanded. It was not merely illumination; it was revelation. It filled the hall the way water fills a vessel, pouring into every corner, every shadow, every hidden space where the *menyan* smoke had coiled and thickened for years. The grey plumes of incense recoiled from it, shredding into wisps that evaporated like fog in sunlight. The bronze *saron* keys in the silent gamelan caught the light and rang -- a single, pure, sympathetic vibration, the ghost of a note, as though the instruments themselves were remembering what it felt like to be played.

Ki Surasenta shrieked. It was not a human shriek but something older, something that came from a place deeper than the human throat, a sound that belonged to the dark hollows and the deep crevices where things lived that had never seen the sun. He threw his arms before his face, and his shadow on the marble floor writhed -- not in the way that shadows writhe when the light that casts them moves, but independently, autonomously, as though the shadow were a separate entity that the light had exposed, a parasite that clung to the man's body and was now being peeled away from it by the sacred radiance.

"No!" Ki Surasenta howled, and his voice was a chorus of voices now, layered one upon another, the oily murmur of the courtier stripped away to reveal the guttural snarl of the thing beneath, the servant of the shadow, the mouthpiece of Empu Gandring's corrupted will. "My Lord, do not look! It is a trick! A Bidadari illusion! A *debus* fraud! Close your eyes, *Gusti*, close your eyes and return to the peace --"

"*Mulah!*" Dewi Anjani's voice rang through the hall, and it was the voice of the Bidadari -- not the gentle wind-chime melody of conversation, but the full harmonic resonance of the celestial warriors, a sound that contained within it the overtones of every sacred chant, every temple bell, every prayer ever offered to the *Dewata* since the first voices rose from the first rice fields. "You will not speak, poisoner! The *Bayu* of the cosmos flows through this hall, and your whispers are chaff before the wind!"

She raised her hand, and the silver light that was the Bidadari's birthright blazed from her palm, joining the golden light of the Dwija Putih's staff in a confluence of radiance that was more than the sum of its parts -- gold and silver weaving together like the threads of the most sacred *songket* (brocade), creating a tapestry of light that spread across the ceiling of the hall and burned away the century of soot, revealing the carved *naga* and *garuda* in their original splendor, their eyes of inlaid gems flashing in the renewed light, their scales and feathers shining as they had shone in the age of Hayam Wuruk.

And Jayabaya looked.

For the first time in years -- in the countless years of smoke and whispers and the slow dissolution of his will -- the King of Mataram raised his head and looked directly into the golden eyes of the Dwija Putih, and the fog began to lift.

What happened next was not visible to the courtiers or the companions. It was not a spectacle of light and sound, not a clash of sorcerous forces, not the dramatic confrontation that the *wayang* storytellers would later embellish with their art. It was something private, something that happened in the interior landscape of Prabu Jayabaya's mind, in the prison that Ki Surasenta had built for him over years of patient, methodical poisoning.

He was drowning.

That was the first thing he knew -- that he was drowning, and had been drowning for a very long time, sinking slowly through dark water that was not water but something thicker, something that clung to the limbs and weighed upon the chest, something that tasted of *menyan* and whispered lies. He had been drowning so long that he had forgotten what air tasted like, had forgotten that there was such a thing as a surface, had accepted the dark water as the totality of his existence, the only reality, the only world.

But now there was light.

It came from above -- from infinitely far above, a golden radiance filtering down through the dark water, dim at first but growing, strengthening, becoming a shaft of brilliance that penetrated the murk and reached him where he hung suspended in the depths. And with the light came a voice -- not the whispered counsel that was his constant companion, the oily murmur that told him to rest, to sleep, to stop struggling -- but something else, something vast and warm and impossibly ancient, a voice that spoke in the language of fire and stone and the deep, slow pulse of the living earth.

*Remember.*

The word was not spoken. It was transmitted through the light itself, carried on the golden shaft like a message carried on a beam of sunlight, and it struck the bedrock of his identity -- the place where Ki Surasenta's whispers could not reach, the place where the blood of Mataram ran in its deepest channel, connecting him through an unbroken river of ancestry to the first kings who had built the first temples and planted the first waringin trees and forged the first keris in the fires of the sacred forge.

*Remember who you are.*

And he remembered.

He remembered the sound of the *gong ageng* at his coronation, the deep, reverberating note that shook the walls of the *Balairung* and set every instrument in the *gamelan Sekaten* singing in sympathetic resonance. He remembered the weight of the *makuta* upon his brow -- not the dead weight that he felt now, but the living weight, the weight of purpose, the weight of *dharma*. He remembered the faces of his people looking up at him from the courtyard of the *kadaton*, their eyes bright with hope and trust, their hands raised in the *sembah*, the gesture of reverence that was also a gesture of expectation -- *you are our king; now rule*. He remembered the sound of his own voice, strong and full, speaking the oath of kingship: *I will protect the land and the people with my body and my blood, as the mountain protects the valley, as the river nourishes the field, as the fire warms the hearth.*

And he remembered Raden Wijaya. His brother's son. The boy he had last seen as a child, bright-eyed and laughing, riding a wooden horse through the corridors of the *kadaton*. The boy who was now a man, standing in his hall, his voice ringing with the *Krama Inggil* of the Mataram court, calling him back from the dark water.

*Uncle.*

The word reached him through the depths, through the layers of fog and poison and whispered counsel, and it was a rope thrown to a drowning man, and he seized it with both hands.

He began to rise.

The dark water resisted. It clung to his limbs with the tenacity of tar, pulling him back down, whispering its ancient lullaby -- *rest, rest, the world is too heavy, the burden is too great, let it go, let it go* -- but he rose. He rose against the current, against the weight, against the voice that had been his only companion for years, and each inch was agony, a tearing away of encrusted layers of passivity and surrender, a rebirth that was as painful as birth itself.

He broke the surface.

---

In the Balairung, the courtiers saw the King's body jerk as though seized by an invisible hand. His back arched against the throne. His hands gripped the obsidian arms with a strength that made the gold fittings creak. His jaw clenched, the muscles of his neck standing out like cables, and a sound escaped his throat -- not a word, not a cry, but a groan of such profound effort that it seemed to come from the earth beneath the hall, from the volcanic bedrock of Jawa itself, as though the mountain were groaning with him.

Ki Surasenta hurled himself at the King, his hands reaching for Jayabaya's temples, his fingers spread like claws. "*Mulah, Gusti!*" he screamed, and his voice was stripped of all pretense, raw and desperate, the voice of a parasite feeling its host pulling free. "Do not listen! They are lying! The world is dark and cold and full of fire, and only *kula* can protect you! Only *kula*! Close your eyes, close your mind, return to the peace --"

The Dwija Putih's staff came down.

Not upon Ki Surasenta's body -- the Sage was not a man of violence, not in this form, not with this fire -- but upon the marble floor between the advisor and the King, and the impact was not a physical impact but a metaphysical one, a detonation of sacred *sakti* that blasted the dark *mantra* in the air around the throne the way a depth charge blasts the water around a submarine, shattering the invisible chains that Ki Surasenta had woven between himself and the King's mind, burning them away in an instant of golden fire that left nothing behind but the faint, acrid smell of burnt shadow.

Ki Surasenta was thrown backward. He flew -- not in the gentle arc of a man pushed by physical force, but in the violent, uncontrolled trajectory of a creature ripped from its anchor, his thin body spinning through the smoke-thick air and striking the marble floor ten paces from the throne with a crack that might have been bone or might have been the sound of something deeper breaking, some connection, some cord of dark *sakti* that had bound him to his master's throne for years.

He lay on the marble, wheezing, his rat-face twisted with pain and fury, and the shadow beneath him writhed like a thing in agony, stretching and contracting, reaching toward the throne with tendrils of darkness that the golden light burned away as fast as they formed.

And Jayabaya opened his eyes.

For a moment -- a long, terrible moment -- the eyes that stared from the ruined face held nothing at all. They were the eyes of a man who has surfaced from deep water and cannot yet remember how to breathe, who sees the world but cannot name it, who knows that he is *someone* but cannot recall *who*. His lips moved without sound. His hands opened and closed on the obsidian arms of the throne, and the movement had the slow, bewildered quality of a sleeper waking in an unfamiliar room -- the groping, the testing, the wordless question: *where am I? what is this place? whose hands are these?*

Then the fog cleared from his eyes, and what remained was not the puppet-king's vacant stare but something fiercer -- dark, blazing with a clarity that was almost terrifying, like looking into the caldera of a volcano and seeing not the dead ash but the living magma beneath, the molten core that had never been extinguished, only buried.

"Wijaya?" the King whispered, and his voice was different -- still thin, still rough from disuse, but clear, *clear*, as though a bell that had been wrapped in wet cloth for years had finally been uncovered and struck. "Is it... truly you? The son of my brother?"

Raden Wijaya crossed the hall in three strides. He knelt before the throne -- not the formal *nembah* of the courtier, but the unstudied, spontaneous gesture of a nephew kneeling before a beloved uncle, one knee upon the cold marble, his head bowed, his eyes bright with the tears he had not shed since the breaking of the Persekutuan.

"It is, Uncle," he said, and his voice, which had rung through the hall with the authority of the *Krama Inggil*, was soft now, intimate, the voice of family speaking to family in the language of the heart. "I have returned. Not to take your throne, but to help you defend it. To restore *Dharma* to this hall."

Jayabaya's hand rose from the arm of the throne -- trembling, uncertain, as though it had forgotten how to move with purpose -- and found Raden Wijaya's face. His fingers touched the prince's cheek, feeling the shape of it, the warmth of it, the reality of it. And in that touch, in the tactile confirmation that the man kneeling before him was flesh and blood and not another phantom of his drugged imagination, the last of the fog burned away.

---

## V. The Lion Awakens

Prabu Jayabaya stood.

It was a slow, painful movement -- the movement of a man rising from a sickbed after a long illness, every muscle protesting, every joint complaining, the body remembering its weakness before it remembers its strength. He placed his hands upon the arms of the throne and pushed himself upward, and the heavy silk of his royal robes fell around him like water, cascading from his gaunt frame in folds that settled and resettled as he straightened, finding their proper drape for the first time in years.

He swayed. For a moment, the courtiers thought he would fall -- several of them started forward, hands reaching -- but he steadied himself, his jaw setting, his spine straightening by degrees, vertebra by vertebra, as though an architect were rebuilding a tower from the inside.

He looked down at Ki Surasenta. The advisor had risen to his hands and knees on the marble, his rat-face twisted into an expression that was part terror and part cunning -- the expression of a cornered animal calculating its options, weighing the cost of flight against the chance of one last desperate attack.

"My Lord," Ki Surasenta began, his voice slipping back into the oily register, the practiced smoothness of the professional whisperer, "you are unwell. The strain of this intrusion has --"

"Get out."

The voice of the Lion of Mataram.

It was not loud. It was not the roar that the *wayang* storytellers would later describe, the thunderous command that shook the pillars and cracked the marble. It was something quieter and far more devastating -- the low, controlled growl of a great cat that has woken from a drugged sleep and found a rat in its den. It was the voice of a king who has been robbed of his will and has recovered it, and who is now looking at the thief with an absolute, crystalline clarity that admits no argument, no appeal, no mitigation.

"Get out of my sight, Surasenta."

Ki Surasenta flinched as though the words were blows.

"You have fed me lies and stolen my strength," Jayabaya continued, and with each word his voice grew, deepened, found its resonance, like a *gong ageng* that has been left unplayed for years and is now being struck, softly at first, then harder, each impact calling forth more of the instrument's true voice. "You have poisoned my ears and clouded my eyes and turned my kingdom into a coffin. You have let my people starve while you whispered of peace. You have let my soldiers become puppets while you whispered of security. You have let the darkness take root in my house while you whispered that the darkness was my friend."

He descended the steps of the throne -- three steps, carved from obsidian, each one inlaid with gold in the pattern of the Garuda -- and with each step he seemed to grow, not in physical stature but in presence, in the weight of his authority, as though the *sakti* of the throne were flowing back into him through the soles of his feet, rising from the stone that his ancestors had laid, filling the emptied vessel of his body with the power of the bloodline.

"If *Sira* is still in this hall by sunset," Jayabaya said, standing now at the base of the throne steps, looking down at the crouching Ki Surasenta with eyes that burned like embers in a face that was still gaunt but no longer dead, "I will feed you to the crocodiles of the Solo river. And I will watch them eat."

Ki Surasenta scrambled to his feet. The mask was gone -- the silk and the smoothness and the oily servility were gone -- and what remained was the raw thing beneath, the creature of shadow and servitude, the instrument of Empu Gandring's corrupted will. His face twisted into a snarl that bared his yellowed teeth, and for one terrible instant, the shadow beneath him swelled and darkened and seemed to take on a form of its own -- the silhouette of something tall and horned and burning, the merest suggestion of the master whose hand had guided him.

"You will regret this, old fool," Ki Surasenta hissed, and the words were not addressed to Jayabaya alone but to the hall, to the Dwija Putih, to the light itself. "The Shadow King's arm is long, and his memory is longer. You have chosen fire. You shall have it."

He turned and fled. He did not walk, did not stride, did not even run in the manner of a man -- he scuttled, his thin body folding and refolding as he moved through the shadows at the edge of the hall with a speed that was not entirely human, his robes fluttering behind him like the wings of a bat, his shadow trailing after him like a dog on a leash, and then he was gone, through a side door, into the corridors, into the darkness that would carry him, ultimately, back to the master he served.

The courtiers stared at the empty doorway through which he had vanished. For a long, trembling moment, no one moved. The incense smoke, thinned by the Dwija Putih's light but not yet dispelled, coiled in lazy spirals above the heads of the assembly, and the silence was the silence of a held breath, the hush that falls between the last note of the old song and the first note of the new.

Then Jayabaya turned to the hall.

He was still thin. He was still pale. The *makuta* still sat at an angle upon his brow, and the royal robes still hung from his wasted frame with more grace than the body beneath them warranted. But the light was back in his eyes -- not the golden light of the Dwija Putih's sacred fire, but the dark, fierce, human light of a king who has been betrayed and has survived the betrayal, who has been humiliated and has risen from the humiliation, who has been imprisoned in his own mind and has broken free, and who is now looking at the ruins of his kingdom with the clear, pitiless vision of a man who intends to rebuild.

"The gamelan," he said. His voice cracked on the second syllable, and he paused, swallowed, and spoke again. "Bring the musicians. Light the torches. Open the windows. Clear this smoke from my hall."

A courtier -- the young woman in the faded *kebaya*, her face still wet with tears -- rose from her mat and moved toward the gamelan niches. Others followed. The movement was tentative at first, uncertain, the cautious exploration of people who have been told for years that movement itself was dangerous and are now rediscovering that their limbs obey their own will -- but it grew, it spread, it became a wave of activity that swept through the hall as the courtiers of Mataram began, one by one, to wake from the long sleep that Ki Surasenta had imposed upon them.

Hands reached for mallets. Fingers touched the bronze keys of the *saron*. Someone opened a window, and the morning sunlight -- real sunlight, golden and warm and carrying the scent of frangipani and rain -- streamed into the hall for the first time in years, cutting through the residual smoke like a blade through silk.

The *bonang* chimed. A single note -- tentative, uncertain, a question asked by metal and wood of the empty air. Then the *saron* answered, a lower register, firmer, more confident. Then the *kendang* drum, its goatskin head struck by a hand that trembled but did not hesitate. And then, rising above them all, the *rebab* -- the single-stringed fiddle, the voice of the Javanese soul -- drew a line of melody so pure and so heartbreaking that several of the courtiers wept openly, not with the autonomous, purposeless weeping of the enchanted, but with the real, hot, human tears of people who have just remembered that they are alive.

It was not a formal composition. It was not the *Srepegan* of the court or the *Dangdanggula* of the sabha. It was something spontaneous, something unscripted -- a melody that the musicians were creating in the moment, note by note, discovering it as they played it, letting the music emerge from the instruments the way water emerges from a spring, following the contour of the earth, finding its own channel.

Jayabaya stood in the center of his hall and listened to the gamelan play, and his eyes closed, and his lips moved, and the tears that tracked down his gaunt cheeks were the tears of a man who has been given back the world.

The Dwija Putih leaned upon his staff and watched, and there was something in his golden eyes that might have been satisfaction, or might have been gratitude, or might have been the vast, encompassing tenderness of a being who has walked through fire and death to stand in this hall at this moment and see this king rise from his chair.

Then Jayabaya opened his eyes, and they were dry, and they were hard, and the grief had been replaced by something that was not its opposite but its complement -- a cold, focused determination that took the raw material of sorrow and forged it, in the furnace of the royal will, into the blade of purpose.

"Raden Wijaya," the King said, and his voice was the voice that the courtiers remembered, the voice that had filled this hall in the years before the shadow -- deep, resonant, carrying the natural authority of a man born to the throne. "Tell me everything. Tell me what has happened while I slept. Tell me about the Shadow King. Tell me about Merapi. Tell me about the Persekutuan, and the keris, and the war that I have been too blind and too drugged to see."

He paused, and his jaw tightened, and his hand moved to his hip, where a keris should have hung but did not -- the absence a rebuke, a reminder of how far he had fallen.

"And then," he said, "bring me my sword."

He looked at the wall behind the throne, where the ancestral sword of Mataram -- the *Pedang Naga Pusaka*, the Dragon Sword of the Royal Heritage, a blade of ancient iron that had been forged in the age of Ken Arok and had hung upon that wall since the first king of the current line had placed it there as a symbol of the kingdom's strength -- gleamed dully in the renewed light, its edge still keen, its hilt still bright, untouched by Ki Surasenta's corruption, as though the sword itself had resisted the darkness, had kept itself sharp and ready for the hand that would, at last, reach up and claim it.

"*Inggih*," Raden Wijaya said, his voice breaking on the syllable, breaking with relief and joy and the fierce, exhausting tenderness of a man who has just watched someone he loves return from the dead. "*Inggih*, Uncle. I will tell you everything."

## VI. The Sabha of War

They gathered in the smaller chamber adjacent to the *Balairung Agung* -- the *sabha* room, the council chamber of the Mataram kings, a space designed not for ceremony but for strategy, its walls hung with maps of the kingdom's territories and its floor marked with the tactical grid of the *papan catur* (chessboard), the ancient war-gaming tool that the generals of Mataram had used since the time of Ken Arok to plan their campaigns.

The room was smaller than the great hall, but it was cleaner -- Ki Surasenta had had little use for a war room, and so the chamber had been left to gather dust rather than corruption. The maps on the walls were outdated by months, but they showed the essential geography: the volcanic spine of Jawa running east to west, the river systems that were the kingdom's arteries, the mountain passes and coastal approaches that were its vulnerabilities. A teak table occupied the center of the room, its surface scarred by the marks of a hundred strategy sessions, the impressions left by the tips of *tombak* spears used as pointers and the rings left by cups of *jamu* drunk during long nights of planning.

Jayabaya sat at the head of the table. He had bathed and changed -- the courtiers had brought him fresh robes, and the young woman in the *kebaya* had prepared a draught of *jamu* that was less medicine than miracle, a concentrate of *temulawak* and *jahe* and *kunyit* that burned through his veins like liquid fire, stripping away the last chemical residue of Ki Surasenta's drugs and replacing it with a fierce, head-clearing warmth. He was still thin, still pale, still bearing the marks of his long captivity, but his eyes were clear and his hands were steady and his voice, when he spoke, carried the full weight of the throne.

The *Pedang Naga Pusaka* lay upon the table before him, unsheathed, its ancient iron catching the torchlight. He had taken it from the wall himself, reaching up with arms that trembled but did not fail, grasping the hilt with hands that remembered, even if the muscles did not, the proper grip -- the warrior's grip, the grip of a king who held his sword not as ornament but as instrument.

Around the table sat the war council. Raden Wijaya to the King's right, his keris Nagasasra upon the table beside a map of the eastern passes. The Dwija Putih to the King's left, his staff leaning against the wall, his golden eyes half-closed in an expression of profound concentration that was either deep thought or communion with forces that the others could not perceive. Dewi Anjani sat opposite the King, her bow across her knees, her amber eyes scanning the maps with the ancient, strategic intelligence of a being who had witnessed a thousand campaigns and understood the mathematics of war in a way that mortal generals could only approximate. Tumenggung Batu sat at the far end of the table, his hammer on the floor beside him, his thick arms folded across his chest, his expression that of a man who has been waiting for this moment and intends to make the most of it.

And Arya Kamandanu, Tumenggung of Kediri, who had been summoned from the courtyard where his horsemen waited, sat with his *tombak* across his lap, his sun-darkened face grave and attentive.

"Begin," Jayabaya said.

Raden Wijaya spoke first. He told the story from the beginning -- not the very beginning, for that belonged to the sabha at Dieng and the forming of the Persekutuan, but from the point of fracture, the moment when the Fellowship had broken at the Selat Sunda and the companions had been scattered across the face of Nusantara like seeds scattered by a typhoon.

He spoke of the crossing of the strait. Of the ghost-ship in the yellow fog. Of the Buta ambush in the mangrove swamps, and the desperate fight at Candi Sewu, and the long pursuit through the forests of Jawa. He spoke of the Horse Lords of Kediri, and the alliance with Arya Kamandanu, and the desperate ride across the plains. He spoke of the Wanantara forest, and the return of the Dwija Putih, and the news that the two Tuyul -- Cepot and Dawala -- were safe in the care of Mbah Jangkung and the *Sanghyang Wana*, the walking forests that had awakened from their long sleep to march upon Merapi.

And he spoke of Kian Santang. The keris-bearer. The small, frightened Tuyul from the highlands who carried the fate of Nusantara in a bundle of batik at his hip, climbing the slopes of the Land of Fire with only Bagus and the treacherous Si Burik for company, moving toward the caldera of Krakatau where the Keris Murka could be destroyed.

Jayabaya listened. His face was stone, his eyes burning coals. He did not interrupt. He did not ask questions. He listened as a king listens to a report of catastrophe -- with the full, focused attention of a mind that is already processing the information, already converting grief into strategy, already turning the raw material of disaster into the framework of a plan.

When Raden Wijaya finished, the silence in the *sabha* room was dense enough to carve.

"How long?" Jayabaya asked. His voice was quiet, but the quietness was the quietness of compressed force, the silence of a bowstring drawn to its full extension. "How long has my kingdom been a puppet of the shadow while I sat on my throne dreaming of peace?"

"Months, *Gusti Prabu*," the Dwija Putih said gently. "Perhaps a year. Ki Surasenta's influence was gradual -- a slow poison, not a sudden strike. He worked through the *menyan*, through the *mantra*, through the whispered counsel that eroded your will as water erodes stone. You are not the first king to fall to such arts. The Shadow King has been perfecting his methods since the First Age."

"That does not excuse me," Jayabaya said, and his voice was the voice of a man speaking a truth that cuts him to the bone. "A king who can be drugged and deceived while his people starve is a king who has failed his *dharma*. I have failed."

"You have woken," the Dwija Putih said. "That is the beginning of the answer. The rest must be earned."

Jayabaya's jaw clenched, and then relaxed, and the grief that had been building behind his eyes was channeled, deliberately, consciously, into the furnace of the royal will. He turned to the maps.

"The strategic picture," he said. "Show me."

Arya Kamandanu rose from his seat and crossed to the largest map -- a detailed rendering of the central Javanese landscape, showing the volcanic chain of Merapi, the river systems of the Solo and the Brantas, the highland forests, and the plains of Kediri. He took a *tombak* from the rack by the wall and used its tip as a pointer.

"The Horse Lords of Kediri hold the western plains," he said, tracing the boundary with the spear-point. "We have perhaps three thousand cavalry, well-mounted, well-armed, but stretched thin across a front that extends from the Brantas river to the foothills of the volcanic range. The Buta raiders have been probing our lines for weeks -- small incursions, testing our responses, mapping our patrol patterns. They are not merely raiding, *Gusti Prabu*. They are preparing."

"For what?" Jayabaya asked, though his eyes, scanning the map, suggested that he already knew.

"For the hammer-stroke," the Dwija Putih said. He did not need to rise; his voice carried from where he sat, and his words fell upon the council with the weight of prophecy. "Empu Gandring is building his army at the forge of Merapi. The *Uruk-hai* -- the great Buta, the iron-men, the creatures of his dark craft -- are being bred in the pits beneath the mountain. They are stronger than the common Buta, faster, more disciplined. They can endure sunlight. They carry weapons of forged iron that the old Buta never possessed. And when they are ready -- and they are very nearly ready -- Gandring will unleash them upon Mataram in a single, overwhelming assault."

He paused, letting the words settle into the silence.

"The target," the Dwija Putih continued, "is Candi Abang."

The name fell into the room like a stone into a well, and the ripples it sent through the council were visible in the tightening of jaws, the shifting of hands, the involuntary straightening of spines. Candi Abang -- the Red Temple, the ancient fortress that guarded the pass between Merapi and the heartland of Mataram -- was the keystone of the kingdom's defenses. If it fell, the pass would be open, and the armies of Gandring would pour through into the central valley like lava through a breached dike, and there would be nothing between them and Giri Mataram but flat rice paddies and the prayers of farmers.

"Candi Abang," Jayabaya repeated. His hand moved to the *Pedang Naga Pusaka*, and his fingers closed around the hilt. The ancient iron was warm beneath his grip, warm with the forge-fire of its making, warm with the memory of the kings who had held it before him. "How long do we have?"

"Days," the Dwija Putih said. "Perhaps less. The scouts report movement in the pits of Merapi. The forges burn day and night. The army of the iron-men is nearly complete."

"Then we ride for Candi Abang," Jayabaya said.

Dewi Anjani spoke. Her voice was the layered harmony of the Bidadari -- the wind-chime register, multiple tones woven together, carrying within its resonance the intelligence of ages.

"The Shadow King's strategy is two-fold, *Manusa*," she said, her amber eyes moving from face to face around the table. "The assault on Candi Abang is the sword-arm. But the shield-arm is the Gerbang Hideung -- the Black Gate of Krakatau, where the Shadow King masses a second army to prevent any approach to the caldera. If Kian Santang is to reach the fire and destroy the keris, the Shadow King's attention must be drawn to Candi Abang. The siege is not merely a defensive action. It is a *distraction*. A gambit. We must hold Candi Abang long enough for the keris-bearer to complete his quest, and we must do so in a manner so conspicuous, so defiant, that the Shadow King cannot look away."

"You ask me to sacrifice my soldiers as bait," Jayabaya said, and his voice was the voice of a king who understands the mathematics of war and hates them.

"I ask you to give them a purpose worthy of their courage," Dewi Anjani replied, and her words were as precise and as beautiful as the facets of a gem. "Every hour that Candi Abang holds is an hour that Kian Santang walks closer to the fire. Every Buta that dies against its walls is a Buta that does not guard the path to Krakatau. The fortress is the fulcrum. Upon it, the fate of Nusantara turns."

Tumenggung Batu spoke from his end of the table, and his voice was the grinding of millstones, the bass-note of the mountain.

"The walls of Candi Abang are good stone," he said. "I've seen them. Andesite and basalt, laid by builders who understood the weight of the world. They can hold, if the defenders hold. The question is not the stone but the steel -- the men behind the walls, the arms in their hands, the fire in their bellies."

He looked at Jayabaya, and there was a challenge in his eyes -- not disrespectful but direct, the challenge of a craftsman speaking to a craftsman, a man of stone addressing a man of iron.

"Your soldiers have been asleep, *Gusti Prabu*. Ki Surasenta drugged them as he drugged you. Can they be woken in time? Can you put the fire back in the forge? Because if you cannot, the walls are just rock, and rock without fire is just a tomb."

Jayabaya met the Kurcaci's gaze, and held it, and the light in his eyes was the answer before his words confirmed it.

"They will wake," Jayabaya said. "As I have woken. I will stand before them, and I will speak, and the words of the Lion of Mataram will burn the sleep from their eyes as the *geni suci* burned it from mine. And if there are any who will not wake --" His jaw set, and the muscles of his gaunt face hardened into the expression of a king who has made his decision and will not be moved from it. "Then I will carry them on my back to the walls of Candi Abang, and I will hold a sword in their hands, and I will fight beside them until the fire returns or until we are all dead. There is no other way. There is only *dharma*."

He turned to Arya Kamandanu. "How soon can your Horse Lords be ready to ride?"

"They are ready now, *Gusti Prabu*," Arya Kamandanu said, and for the first time since entering the *kadaton*, the Tumenggung of Kediri smiled -- a hard, fierce, wolfish smile that had nothing to do with humor and everything to do with the grim satisfaction of a soldier who has finally found a king worth following. "We have been ready for months. We were only waiting for someone to give the order."

"Then the order is given," Jayabaya said. He stood, and the *Pedang Naga Pusaka* rose with him, its ancient blade catching the torchlight and splitting it into a constellation of reflected points that danced across the ceiling of the *sabha* room. "We ride for Candi Abang at dawn. Every soldier in Mataram who can hold a blade. Every *bayangkara* who can be woken from Surasenta's sleep. Every farmer who can wield a *clurit*, every smith who can lift a hammer, every man and woman of Mataram who will not kneel to the shadow."

He looked around the table, and his gaze rested on each member of the council in turn -- the Dwija Putih with his golden eyes and his staff of sacred fire, Raden Wijaya with his ranger's face and his prince's blood, Dewi Anjani with her ancient beauty and her warrior's bow, Tumenggung Batu with his mountain-born strength and his forge-hardened will, Arya Kamandanu with his horseman's bearing and his wolf's smile.

"*Priya*," Jayabaya said, and the word -- *friends* -- was spoken in the old language, the Sunda Kuna that was the root of all the tongues of Nusantara, and it carried a weight that no modern word could bear. "The shadow has had its turn. The smoke has cleared. The gamelan plays again in the halls of Mataram, and the Lion is awake. Let the Shadow King hear it. Let Empu Gandring hear it. Let the darkness hear it and tremble."

He raised the *Pedang Naga Pusaka* above his head, and the ancient blade sang -- a high, clear, ringing note, the voice of iron that has been silent too long and is hungry to speak again.

"*Mataram Jaya!*" Jayabaya thundered. "*Mataram rises!*"

"*Mataram Jaya!*" The response came from every throat in the room -- the Kurcaci bass and the Bidadari soprano and the deep, steady baritone of the Kediri horseman and the ringing tenor of the Mataram prince and the resonant, golden voice of the White Sage -- and it echoed through the walls of the *kadaton*, carrying outward through the corridors and the terraces and the gates and the streets of the city, reaching the ears of soldiers and courtiers and farmers and children, reaching the ears of a kingdom that was waking from a nightmare and discovering, with the shocked, grateful relief of the reprieved, that the dawn had come, and the Lion was roaring, and the world had not ended after all.

---

The *kadaton* of Giri Mataram, which had been a place of silence and shadow and stagnant smoke, became a place of movement and noise and controlled chaos. The preparations for war began.

In the armory, soldiers who had stood guard with empty eyes now blinked in the torchlight and reached for weapons they had forgotten how to hold, their hands guided by *bayangkara* officers who had been among the first to wake, who had wept with shame and then dried their tears and begun the work of rebuilding an army from the ashes of an enchantment. The forge-fires were lit for the first time in months, and the sound of hammers on iron rang through the *kadaton* like the heartbeat of a giant coming back to life.

In the stables, the *kuda turangga* of Mataram -- lean, powerful horses bred in the volcanic highlands, their bloodlines as ancient as the kingdom itself -- stamped and whinnied as grooms who had been sleepwalking through their duties suddenly remembered that horses need more than silence and shadows, that they need hay and water and the firm hand of a rider who knows where he is going.

In the kitchens, cooks who had been preparing the minimal, drugged meals of Ki Surasenta's regime now threw open the pantry doors and began to prepare the food of warriors -- dense, nourishing provisions of rice and dried fish and *sambal* and the thick, black *kopi* of the highland farms, strong enough to wake the dead and certainly strong enough to fuel a forced march to Candi Abang.

And in the great courtyard of the *kadaton*, where the waringin tree stood in its declining majesty, Jayabaya assembled his soldiers. They came by the dozens, then by the hundreds -- *bayangkara* in their bronze armor, foot soldiers in leather jerkins, archers with their composite bows of bamboo and horn. They were thin and pale from the months of enchantment, their eyes still bearing the traces of the vacant stare that had been their constant expression, but they came, and they stood, and they looked at their King.

Jayabaya stood before them in the armor of the Mataram kings -- a cuirass of hardened leather plated with bronze, its surface worked with the emblem of the Garuda, the *makuta* replaced by the war-helm of the line, a conical helmet of iron crowned with a crest of horsehair dyed in the red and gold of Mataram. The *Pedang Naga Pusaka* hung at his hip. He was still thin, still bearing the marks of his captivity, but in the armor, in the war-helm, with the sword at his side, he looked like what he was -- a king going to war. Not the ghost upon the throne. Not the puppet of the whisperer. A king.

Raden Wijaya stood at his right hand. The Dwija Putih stood at his left, his white robes blazing in the afternoon sun that had burned through the residual haze, his staff casting a shadow that pointed due east, toward Merapi, toward the battle that waited.

Jayabaya spoke to his soldiers. He did not shout. He did not declaim. He spoke in the *Krama Inggil* of the court, the register that connected him to every king who had stood in this courtyard before him, and his words were simple, and they were true, and they were enough.

He told them that they had been enchanted. He told them that the enchantment was broken. He told them that the enemy was at the gates, and that the kingdom would fall if they did not ride. He told them that he was sorry -- sorry for the months of drugged sleep, sorry for the neglected fields and the silenced gamelan and the children who had grown old-eyed in the shadow of a court that had forgotten how to live. And he told them that the time for sorrow was over, and the time for *dharma* had come.

"I do not ask you to forgive me," Jayabaya said. "I ask you to follow me. Not because I am your king, but because the road to Candi Abang is the road to redemption -- for me, and for Mataram, and for every village and every *sawah* and every child who deserves to grow up under the sun rather than in the shadow."

The soldiers looked at him. And in their eyes, the last traces of the vacant stare guttered and went out, replaced by something that was not merely awareness but conviction -- the fierce, collective determination of an army that has found its purpose.

A soldier in the front rank drew his sword. "*Mataram Jaya!*" he shouted, and his voice cracked with emotion, and he did not care.

"*Mataram Jaya!*" The cry rose from the courtyard -- a thousand voices, then two thousand, then more, the sound swelling and reverberating from the stone walls of the *kadaton* and rolling outward through the streets of the city, reaching the outer walls, reaching the fields beyond, reaching the mountains themselves, where the *karuhun* of the land heard the voice of Mataram and stirred in their ancient sleep.

The waringin tree in the courtyard rustled. A single green leaf -- impossibly, improbably, defiantly green in the midst of the yellowed and dying canopy -- uncurled from a branch, and then another, and then another, as though the tree, too, were waking.

---

Raden Wijaya found a moment of solitude in the small *pura* (shrine) behind the *kadaton*, where the ancestral altar of the Mataram kings stood in a grove of frangipani trees. The shrine was ancient, its stones worn smooth by centuries of offerings and prayers, its altar blackened by the smoke of a thousand sacred fires. It was the quietest place in the *kadaton* -- a place where the noise of preparation and the clamor of an army preparing to march could not reach, where the only sounds were the rustle of the frangipani leaves and the distant, almost subliminal hum of the gamelan that had begun to play again in the *Balairung Agung*, tentatively, as though the musicians were relearning how to breathe.

He knelt before the altar, his forehead touching the cold stone in the full *sujud* of the Javanese prayer, the posture of absolute submission before the divine, the posture that strips away rank and title and leaves only the naked soul.

"*Duh Gusti*," he whispered, and the words were not the formal invocation of the court but the raw, unpolished prayer of a man speaking to his God in the small hours. "I am afraid. I carry the blood of kings and the duty of *dharma*, and I do not know if I am strong enough for what is coming. Candi Abang will burn. Men will die. The shadow will fall upon us with all its weight, and I do not know if we can hold."

He pressed his palms together in the *sembah* and felt the calluses on his fingers, the rough evidence of years of wandering and fighting and surviving, and he thought of Kian Santang -- small, frightened Kian Santang, somewhere on the slopes of the Land of Fire, carrying the fate of the world in a bundle of batik -- and he felt, for a moment, the kinship of the afraid, the brotherhood of those who walk toward the fire not because they are brave but because they cannot bring themselves to turn away.

"But I will go," he said to the silent altar, to the ancestors whose faces he could feel watching him from the stone, from the earth, from the frangipani-scented air. "I will go, and I will fight, and if I fall, I will fall facing the enemy, with the keris of my ancestors in my hand and the name of Mataram on my lips. *Inggih*. I will go."

He rose. The frangipani blossoms fell around him like fragrant snow, and the gamelan played in the distance, and the army of Mataram was gathering in the streets, and the dawn was coming, and the road to Candi Abang stretched before him like a line drawn on a map by the hand of fate.

He walked back into the light, and the *kadaton* received him, and the preparations continued, and the Lion of Mataram roared in the courtyard, and the world turned, and the war that would determine the fate of Nusantara had begun.
