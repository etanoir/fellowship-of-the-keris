# Bab 9: Pasukan Sembilan
## (The Nine Companions)

---

Three days Prabu Siliwangi gave them. Three days to prepare for a journey from which they might not return.

It was not enough. It was also, Kian Santang thought, far too much -- too much time to sit with the knowledge of what lay ahead, to turn it over in his mind like a stone he could not put down. The Great Council had spoken. The Keris Murka would be carried south, through the volcanic highlands and the cave-riddled karst of Gunung Sewu, south to the coast and west across the sea to the Sunda Strait, and there -- in the molten throat of Krakatau, where the blade had first been forged -- it would be unmade. He had volunteered. The word had left his mouth before his mind had fully formed it, rising from some part of him deeper than reason, deeper than courage, from that place where a thing is simply known to be necessary and the body obeys.

Nine companions. *Persekutuan Keris*, Prabu Siliwangi had named them -- the Fellowship of the Keris. Nine against the nine Leak, nine against the shadow, nine very different souls bound together by a single purpose and a slender blade that whispered in the dark.

Three days. Kian Santang used them badly and well, in equal measure, wandering the ancient temple compound of Dieng in a state of heightened attention that made every detail vivid and permanent -- the texture of moss on stone, the taste of mountain air, the way the dawn mist coiled between the candi like the breath of sleeping gods. He was memorizing, he realized. He was saying goodbye to beauty, in case he should not see it again.

But around him, the others prepared. And it was in watching them prepare that he began to understand who they truly were.

---

Eyang Guru Batara had claimed a corner of the great library of Dieng -- a dim, cool chamber in the oldest candi, its walls lined with rack upon rack of lontar manuscripts, palm-leaf pages bound between carved teak boards, their edges brown with age. The library smelled of dust and camphor and something older still, the accumulated breath of centuries of ink and thought. Eyang Guru sat cross-legged on the stone floor, surrounded by unrolled manuscripts like a man at the center of a paper ocean, his white hair loose upon his shoulders, his teak staff resting against the wall behind him.

He was mapping their route.

"The old roads are watched," he murmured, tracing a line across the yellowed surface of a lontar with one long finger. His nail was stained with ink. His eyes moved with the fierce, consuming attention of a mind that had spent centuries reading and had not yet tired of it. "The Leak will expect us to go west, directly toward the coast. We cannot oblige them. We go south first -- through the volcanic highlands, where the terrain is broken and the forests are thick. Then west through the Gunung Sewu caves."

He glanced up at Kian Santang, who stood in the doorway, not wanting to intrude. "Come in, boy. You should see the path you will walk."

Kian Santang knelt beside him. "*Nembah, Eyang,*" he said softly -- the Sundanese *basa lemes* of a young man addressing an elder with deepest respect -- "*ingwang hoyong diajar tina pangersa Eyang.*" *I bow to you, Elder -- I wish to learn from your wisdom.*

Eyang Guru regarded him with eyes that held the warmth of old embers. "Good. A student who asks with humility receives more than one who demands with pride."

The lontar was a map -- not drawn in ink but pressed into the palm leaf with a stylus, the lines darkened with soot, showing the highlands of central Jawa in extraordinary detail. Mountain ranges rippled across the surface like the spine of a sleeping dragon. Rivers threaded between them in blue-black threads. And the caves of Gunung Sewu were marked as a vast lattice of interconnected tunnels beneath the limestone karst, a hidden highway through the earth.

"The caves are dangerous," Eyang Guru said, as though commenting on the weather. "The Kurcaci know them, but even they do not walk certain passages lightly. There are things in the deep places that have not seen light since the old wars." He rolled the lontar shut and reached for another. "But the Leak cannot follow us underground. Their Harimau Siluman are creatures of open darkness, not the enclosed dark of stone. It is a trade -- one danger for another."

He had been at this for hours. Beside him, a small bronze bowl held the remains of a meal he had not finished -- cold rice, a few leaves of lalab, forgotten. Around him, the manuscripts spoke of routes and risks, of water sources and shelter points, of the tides of the Sunda Strait and the volcanic moods of Krakatau. He absorbed it all with the quiet voracity of a man who understood that knowledge, in the end, was the most reliable form of armor.

And in the intervals between study, he forged jimat.

Kian Santang watched, fascinated, as Eyang Guru took small blanks of bronze -- flat, thumb-sized ovals, smooth and warm -- and inscribed them with a fine-pointed stylus, cutting Sanskrit mantras into the metal with a precision that belied his ancient hands. Each character was minute and perfect. Each jimat was different, tailored to its intended bearer: protection against fire for one, against deception for another, against despair for a third. He worked with the concentration of an empu at the forge, though his forge was silence and his hammer was a whisper. Before inscribing each jimat, he murmured a Weda mantra -- an invocation of the Tri Murti, of Brahma the creator and Wisnu the preserver and Siwa the transformer -- his voice a thread of ancient Sanskrit weaving through the chamber's dust-laden air, charging each bronze blank with the sacred resonance of the oldest prayers.

"One for each of you," he said, threading the finished jimat onto cords of braided cotton. "They will not stop a blade or turn a spell. But they will remind you of who you are, when the darkness tries to tell you otherwise. That is worth more than you know."

---

Kian Santang found Raden Wijaya at dawn.

The exiled prince of Mataram had chosen a clearing on the eastern slope of the plateau, where the land dropped away toward the lowland forests and the mist gathered in a pale lake below the grass line. He stood alone in the blue-grey light, barefoot on the wet earth, moving through the forms of Aliran Mataram -- the silat school of the old Javanese kings -- with a fluidity that made Kian Santang stop at the clearing's edge and simply watch.

Raden Wijaya was tall -- taller than any man Kian Santang had met, lean and long-limbed, his dark skin sheened with mist and exertion. His movements were water given purpose. Each stance flowed into the next without pause or visible effort -- *kuda-kuda rendah*, the deep horse stance, sinking until his thighs were parallel with the earth, then rising into *sikap pasang*, the fighting ready position, hands open and turning, palms tracing slow circles in the air as though drawing invisible patterns in the mist. His strikes, when they came, were sudden and exact: a palm heel to an invisible throat, an elbow driven into empty air with a force that would have shattered bone, a low sweeping kick that cut the grass and scattered dew. Then stillness again. The cycle repeating.

There was a sadness in it. Kian Santang could see it now, in the way Raden Wijaya's eyes went distant between sequences -- a man practicing the art of his fathers in exile, keeping alive a tradition that might die with him if he fell on this journey. The last prince of Mataram, drilling his forms in the mist of a foreign plateau, for an audience of clouds.

"You move well," Kian Santang said, stepping into the clearing. Then, embarrassed by the inadequacy: "I mean -- I have never seen anything like it."

Raden Wijaya turned. His expression was guarded -- it was always guarded, the face of a man who had learned early that trust could be fatal -- but something in it softened when he looked down at the small Tuyul standing in the wet grass with his hands clasped behind his back like a student approaching a teacher.

"You practice silat," Raden Wijaya said. Not a question. He had seen Kian Santang's build, the way he distributed his weight, the calluses on his feet. A warrior reads another warrior's body the way a farmer reads the soil.

"Sundanese style. Cimande. My uncle taught me a little. But nothing like -- nothing like what you do."

"Cimande is good. Practical. Built for smaller fighters." Raden Wijaya paused, then inclined his head toward the clearing. "Come. I will teach you something."

They trained together for the better part of an hour, the towering Javanese prince and the small Sundanese Tuyul, and it was both comical and strangely beautiful. Raden Wijaya adapted each movement for Kian Santang's stature with a patience that surprised them both. He showed him *elakan*, the evasive movements -- how to drop beneath a strike meant for a larger target, how to use his low center of gravity as an advantage rather than a limitation. He corrected his *kuda-kuda* with a touch to the hip, a nudge to the knee.

"Lower," he said. "You are small. Be lower. Let them reach for you. When they reach, they overextend. When they overextend, they are yours."

Kian Santang sank deeper into the stance, his thighs burning.

"You have been carrying something heavy," Raden Wijaya said quietly, not looking at him, adjusting his own stance in mirror. "And not only on your back."

Kian Santang said nothing. The keris was in its wrappings, left in his quarters, but its weight followed him everywhere.

"I know that weight," Raden Wijaya said. "The weight of a thing you did not choose but cannot set down." His eyes were fixed on the horizon, where the mist was beginning to thin and the first orange thread of sunrise burned along the edge of the world. "I was born to a throne that no longer exists. I carry it still. Some weights are not meant to be set down. Only carried well."

He returned to the form, and Kian Santang followed, and for a time the weight was lighter.

---

At Telaga Warna, the sacred lake at the plateau's heart, Dewi Anjani sat in perfect stillness on a stone that jutted from the water's edge like the prow of a sunken ship.

The lake was extraordinary. Its surface shifted color through the day -- jade at dawn, sapphire at noon, amber at dusk -- as mineral deposits and the angle of light conspired to paint it in impossible hues. This morning it was the pale green of young rice, and in its mirror Dewi Anjani's reflection was doubled in perfect detail: the Bidadari warrior-princess, cross-legged, her enchanted bamboo bow across her knees, her eyes closed, her long dark hair unbound and falling past her waist like a curtain of silk.

She was communing. With the Dewata -- the divine spirits of water and mountain and sky, the unseen forces that the Bidadari navigated as easily as mortals navigated the visible world. Kian Santang watched from a distance, not daring to approach, and saw the air around her shimmer faintly, as though heat rose from her skin despite the morning cold. He could hear, at the edge of perception, the faintest whisper of a mantra in the ancient tongue -- an invocation of Sri, the goddess of life and abundance, whose blessing flowed through sacred waters.

When she opened her eyes, they were luminous -- not with ordinary light but with something deeper, an inner illumination that made her dark irises glow like embers seen through amber glass. It faded quickly, but Kian Santang saw it, and he understood: the Bidadari were not merely long-lived and beautiful. They were connected to the fabric of the world in ways that other beings were not. They heard the hum of creation itself.

Before her, on the stone, lay a row of bamboo arrow shafts, freshly cut, their surfaces still green and smelling of sap. She took them up one by one and ran her fingers along their length, and where her fingers passed the bamboo changed -- its color deepened, its surface hardened, and a faint luminescence settled into the grain, a light that was not reflection but emanation. Bidadari-blessed arrows. Each one would fly straighter, strike harder, and carry within it a trace of the light that the dark things feared.

She worked in silence, and the lake held her reflection, and Kian Santang left her to her communion.

---

Tumenggung Batu, by contrast, was impossible to ignore.

The Kurcaci chieftain had commandeered the smithy at the edge of the temple compound -- a modest affair by Kurcaci standards, he had announced loudly, barely fit for mending a plow -- and from it came, all day and much of the night, the sounds of hammering, grinding, and increasingly colorful profanity in the guttural Kurcaci tongue.

He was inspecting everyone's weapons. And he was dissatisfied with all of them.

"This," he said, holding up Bagus's golok between thumb and forefinger as though it were a dead rat, "is not a weapon. This is an agricultural insult. The edge has more waves than the Sagara Jawa. The balance -- the balance, surface-dweller! -- is an abomination." He tossed it onto his workbench with a clang that made Bagus wince. "I will fix it. Under protest."

He had already re-edged Cepot's short knife ("Adequate steel, incompetent tempering"), re-wrapped the grip on Dawala's staff ("Who taught you woodwork, a blind monkey?"), and spent twenty minutes examining Raden Wijaya's sword with an expression of grudging, almost pained respect ("Acceptable. For human work."). His own war hammer hung at his belt -- a thing of dark stone and darker iron, heavy enough to crack granite, yet wielded by the stocky Kurcaci with casual ease.

The Kurcaci of Gunung Sewu traced their lineage to the diamond-miners of Kalimantan, the ancient smiths who had followed veins of precious stone from the great rivers of Borneo to the limestone caves of Java. Tumenggung Batu wore that heritage in the patterns etched into his armor -- diamond shapes within diamond shapes, the geometric memory of the Kalimantan gemfields encoded in obsidian and iron. His smithing techniques bore the hallmarks of Dayak metalwork: the triple-fold quenching, the use of river-sand from Mahakam as a tempering medium, methods passed down through generations of underground craftsmen who had carried the fire-knowledge of Borneo's interior across the sea to the caves of the south.

But his masterwork was not a weapon.

On the second morning, he called Kian Santang to the smithy with a gruffness that Kian Santang was beginning to recognize as the Kurcaci version of kindness.

"Come here, Tuyul. I have something for you. Stand still."

From a leather pack, Tumenggung Batu drew a shirt of mail. Kian Santang stared. It was not metal as he understood metal -- not iron, not bronze, not the silvery steel of the Bidadari blades. It was dark, almost black, with a faint iridescence that shifted purple and green in the light, like the wing of a beetle. It was woven in rings so fine they appeared to be fabric, and when Tumenggung Batu held it up it hung from his fists like silk, flowing and supple, making a sound like rain on leaves.

"Volcanic glass," Tumenggung Batu said. "Obsidian, drawn into fibers finer than spider silk and woven on Kurcaci looms a league beneath Gunung Sewu. It will turn a blade. It will blunt an arrow. And it weighs less than that ridiculous shirt you are wearing." He thrust it toward Kian Santang. "Put it on. Under your clothes. No one needs to know."

Kian Santang took it. It was cool to the touch, impossibly light, and when he held it against the sunlight the weave seemed to contain captured darkness, a distillation of the deep places where the Kurcaci made their home.

"Why?" he asked.

Tumenggung Batu's face, craggy and hard as the stone he was named for, betrayed nothing. "Because you carry the blade that will either save us or doom us, and you have the defensive instincts of a rice farmer." He turned back to his anvil. "Even a Tuyul deserves proper armor."

---

Gajahmada trained alone.

He had found a space behind the northern candi, out of sight of the compound, and there he worked with his keris and his Majapahit long spear in combinations that were beautiful and brutal -- the spear sweeping in wide arcs to control distance, the keris drawn in close for the killing strikes, the transitions between the two weapons seamless and savage. His style was nothing like Raden Wijaya's flowing grace. It was direct, aggressive, built for the battlefield, for the press of bodies and the chaos of melee. It was the style of a man who had commanded armies.

He was, Kian Santang thought, watching from the shadow of the candi wall, magnificent. And frightening.

On the second evening, Gajahmada sought Kian Santang out. The general found him sitting on the steps of the western candi, watching the sunset paint the plateau in shades of gold and blood. He sat beside him -- not close, not familiar, but not distant either. The careful distance of a man who was accustomed to formality but attempting something less structured.

"You have not seen Majapahit," Gajahmada said. It was not a question.

"No."

"It is..." He paused, and for the first time Kian Santang saw something other than pride or calculation in the general's face. He saw love. The fierce, aching love of a man for his homeland. "The city sits where the river meets the sea. The palaces are red brick and gold leaf, and in the morning the sun strikes the spires and the whole city blazes like a lantern. The markets stretch for a thousand paces in every direction -- spices from Maluku, silk from Champa, porcelain from the northern kingdoms, sandalwood from Sumba and Timor, cloves from Ternate and Tidore, beeswax from the forests of Kalimantan, turtle shell from the shores of Sulawesi. You can hear twelve languages spoken before you have finished your morning rice.

"And the people." His voice deepened. "Farmers, soldiers, artisans, priests. Fishermen who read the tides by the color of the water. Weavers who put stories into cloth. Children who play in the temple courtyards while their grandmothers sell jasmine garlands at the gates." He looked at Kian Santang directly. "That is what I protect. Not a throne, not an empire -- them. Every sacrifice I make, every oath I swear, is for my people -- from the rice paddies of Jawa to the spice groves of Maluku, from the gold mines of Sumatera to the pearl beds of Nusa Tenggara. All the peoples of the seven great islands and the thousand lesser ones -- they are the Nusantara I serve."

There was genuine nobility in his voice, a grandeur of purpose that Kian Santang found himself drawn to. He could see why armies had followed this man. He could see the fire, and the fire was real.

"*Kula badhe ngreksa sedaya tiyang Nusantara,*" Gajahmada said then, shifting into the formal *krama* register of Javanese court speech, each syllable weighted with the solemnity of an oath. "*Punika sumpah kula.*" *I shall protect all the peoples of Nusantara. This is my vow.*

But the keris on Kian Santang's back stirred.

It was subtle -- a pulse of warmth, a faint vibration, like a sleeping thing turning in its sleep at the sound of a familiar voice. The keris *noticed* Gajahmada. It always noticed him. In the Great Council, when the general had argued that the blade should be used as a weapon against its maker, the keris had hummed so loudly that Kian Santang had pressed his hand against the wrapping to muffle it. And now, in this quiet moment, with Gajahmada speaking of love and sacrifice and the protection of his people, the blade whispered with renewed interest.

Not because the general was evil. Because he was strong. Because his desire to protect was so fierce, so consuming, that it bordered on the desperate -- and desperation was a door that the keris knew how to open.

"I believe you," Kian Santang said carefully. "I believe that you love your people."

Gajahmada looked at him. In the general's dark eyes there was a question that he did not yet know how to ask, and an answer that Kian Santang could not yet give. The keris pulsed between them like a third heart.

"Then we understand each other," Gajahmada said. He rose, offered a nod that was almost a bow -- the concession of a proud man to someone he did not fully comprehend but had chosen to follow -- and walked away into the dusk.

Kian Santang pressed his hand against the keris wrapping until the whispering stopped. It took a long time.

---

Before the fellowship could be sealed, there was the matter of *sesajen* -- the sacred offerings that must precede any great undertaking, lest the spirits of earth and sky withhold their blessing.

On the eve of the second day, Eyang Guru laid out the offerings in the courtyard of the central candi, arranging them on a square of white cloth with the meticulous care of a priest preparing a homa fire. Rice -- white for the sky-spirits, yellow for the earth-spirits, black for the spirits of the deep. Frangipani blossoms, their petals white and waxy, fragrant with the scent the Dewata were said to favor. Incense of kemenyan, its smoke rising in blue-grey threads toward the carved spires of the candi. A coconut, halved, its water poured onto the earth as a libation. Seven betel leaves arranged in a circle, their green faces turned upward. And at the center, a small flame burning in a clay lamp filled with coconut oil -- the *pelita*, the eternal light, the earthly echo of the divine fire that Brahma kindled at the world's beginning.

"We ask the blessing of the Dewata," Eyang Guru intoned, his voice carrying the cadence of the ancient Weda. "Of Siwa Mahadewa who guards the mountains. Of Wisnu who sustains the rivers. Of the karuhun -- the ancestors who walked these paths before us and whose spirits dwell still in stone and stream and the roots of ancient trees. We ask their permission. We ask their protection. We ask that the road receive our feet with kindness."

The smoke of the kemenyan rose, and the flame of the pelita flickered, and something in the air of the courtyard shifted -- a settling, a quieting, as though the temple compound itself had drawn a breath and held it, listening.

True character, Eyang Guru murmured, studying each face in the circle of firelight, reveals itself as in the tale of the two sisters -- Bawang Merah and Bawang Putih -- where the generous heart receives gold and the selfish heart receives serpents. "Our journey will test each of you," he said. "Not with the tests of the battlefield alone, but with the subtler tests of the spirit. The keris will whisper. The road will wear. And in the wearing and the whispering, you will discover whether your heart is Bawang Putih -- pure, giving, steadfast -- or whether it bends toward the darkness of Bawang Merah. This is the test that matters. This is the one you cannot cheat."

---

Meanwhile, Bagus had found his purpose.

While the warriors trained and the sage studied and the Bidadari communed with the unseen, Bagus did what he had always done: he made sure everyone would eat.

He had installed himself in the compound's kitchen -- a long, open-sided structure with a row of tungku clay stoves and a pantry stocked with the provisions of the Bidadari -- and there he held court with Dieng's cooks, a pair of elderly Bidadari women who regarded the stocky Tuyul with first suspicion, then amusement, and finally something approaching maternal affection.

"Dendeng," he said, pointing to strips of spiced beef drying on bamboo racks above the fire. "Two weeks' worth. Thick cut, heavy on the coriander. And rempeyek -- peanut, not anchovy, they travel better." He consulted a list scratched into a scrap of palm leaf. "Gula aren, eight blocks. Dried fish, salted, wrapped in pandanus. Rice, as much as we can carry without breaking our backs."

The Bidadari cooks exchanged a glance. "He is very particular," one murmured.

"He is very good," the other replied, watching Bagus test the firmness of a gula aren block by rapping it with his knuckle. "He understands provisions."

Bagus packed with the methodical care of a quartermaster who had never held the title but had always possessed the temperament. Each item was weighed, wrapped, positioned in the packs for balance and accessibility. Dry goods on the outside. Dense, heavy items low and close to the back. Fragile things cushioned with rolled cloth. He divided the provisions across multiple packs, so that the loss of any one would not leave them starving.

"*Dika mah riweuh teuing,*" Cepot observed in casual Sundanese *basa loma*, leaning against the kitchen doorpost with his arms crossed. *You fuss too much.*

"*Dika mah teu puguh gawe,*" Bagus replied in the same easy register, without looking up. *And you are completely useless.* "And yet here we are."

---

Cepot and Dawala, for their part, had found their own way to prepare: by exploring every corner of Dieng that was not expressly forbidden to them, and several corners that were.

They charmed the temple attendants with Cepot's quick wit and Dawala's guileless smile. They sampled foods that Bidadari hospitality had not specifically offered them. Dawala discovered a hot spring behind the eastern candi and spent an entire afternoon soaking in it until his skin pruned. Cepot, drawn to conversation the way a moth is drawn to flame, insinuated himself into a dozen discussions with Dieng's residents and emerged from each one with some scrap of useful information.

"The southern passes are blocked by rockfall," he reported to Eyang Guru on the second day, with the casual authority of a general delivering intelligence. "Happened three weeks ago. Landslide took out the main trail. But there is a shepherds' path that goes around -- through the caldera rim of Gunung Prau. The temple gardener told me. His cousin uses it to move goats."

Eyang Guru looked at him for a long moment, then adjusted his map.

"Also," Cepot added, "the Bidadari who tends the northern shrine says that the forest south of Gunung Sundoro has been... quiet. Too quiet. The birds have left. The monkeys have gone. She said it as though it were gossip, but she was not gossiping."

Eyang Guru's eyes sharpened. "No," he said quietly. "She was not. Thank you, Cepot."

Cepot gave a half-bow that managed to be both respectful and irreverent. "I live to serve, Eyang. Preferably from a safe distance."

---

On the second night, they gathered around a fire in the compound's central pendopo -- the open-sided pavilion where, in gentler times, the Bidadari held their musical gatherings and the temple scholars debated philosophy. The night was cold, as highland nights always are, and the fire sent its warmth outward in waves, painting the faces of the nine companions in shifting shades of gold and shadow.

They had eaten well. Bagus had seen to that -- a meal of nasi liwet, the rice cooked in coconut milk and spiced broth, served on banana leaves with fried tempe and sambal and fresh lalab greens. Tumenggung Batu had contributed a flask of Kurcaci arak that tasted of plums and volcanic earth and made Dawala cough until his eyes watered. Even Dewi Anjani had taken a small cup, and a trace of color had risen in her pale cheeks.

It was Cepot who began the wayang show.

He did not announce it. He simply positioned himself between the fire and the nearest wall so that his shadow was thrown large and sharp against the stone, and he raised his hands, and the shadows became something else.

A figure appeared -- angular, exaggerated, instantly recognizable: Semar, the great clown-god of the wayang, corpulent and wise, his round belly a dark bubble against the stone.

"Aduh!" Cepot's voice shifted, becoming deeper, rounder, imbued with the self-important wheeze of the cosmic buffoon. "What is this? Nine heroes setting out to throw away a perfectly good keris? In my day, we kept our magic blades! We polished them! We displayed them on special racks and showed them to visitors!"

A second shadow-figure appeared, thinner, sharper -- Petruk, Semar's lanky son. "Bapak, the keris is evil."

"Evil is a strong word, my boy. I once knew a keris that was merely rude. It would rattle in its sheath whenever someone told a bad joke. Very useful at court functions."

Dawala, who had been watching with growing delight, began providing sound effects -- a rattling noise for the sheath, a low boom for dramatic moments, a high-pitched whistle that was presumably meant to represent wind but sounded more like a distressed kettle. His timing was atrocious. His enthusiasm was total.

Cepot continued undeterred. His shadow-play became an abbreviated, wildly inaccurate retelling of the Ramayana -- Rama portrayed as a bewildered farmer who kept losing his wife ("Has anyone seen Sinta? I put her down right here, I swear"), Hanoman as an enthusiastic but dim-witted monkey who solved every problem by hitting it with a tree ("It worked on the rock. It worked on the gate. Surely it will work on feelings?"), and Rahwana as a fussy bureaucrat more concerned with the organizational logistics of kidnapping than the kidnapping itself ("Form 17-B, application for bride theft, submitted in triplicate to the Department of Villainous Schemes, subdivision Lanka").

Even Raden Wijaya cracked a smile. It was a small thing -- a slight upward movement at the corner of his mouth, gone almost as soon as it appeared -- but Kian Santang saw it, and it changed the prince's face entirely, revealing for an instant the young man beneath the exile's armor.

Dewi Anjani's laughter was like water over stones -- clear, bright, surprising. Tumenggung Batu pounded his knee and shouted corrections to the mythology in a voice that shook the pendopo pillars. Even Gajahmada, who had been sitting apart with the careful stillness of a man who was not certain he belonged, let out a bark of laughter at Rahwana's administrative complaints that seemed to surprise him as much as anyone.

"You have a gift," Eyang Guru said to Cepot when the performance ended and the applause -- Dawala's the loudest, and most prolonged -- had faded.

"I have many gifts, Eyang," Cepot said. "Modesty is not among them."

"No. I mean it. The dalang's art -- the ability to make people laugh at darkness -- that is not a small thing. On the road ahead, there will be nights when laughter is the only warmth we have. Remember that."

Cepot's sharp face went still for a moment. The wit dropped away, and beneath it was a young Tuyul who understood, perhaps for the first time, that his jokes were not merely jokes.

"I will remember, Eyang," he said quietly.

And it was then -- in the warmth and the firelight and the lingering echo of laughter -- that Kian Santang realized: this fellowship might work. Not because they were strong enough, or wise enough, or brave enough. But because together they were something that none of them was alone. The scholar, the prince, the warrior-princess, the chieftain, the general, the quartermaster, the jester, the heart, and the bearer -- nine instruments in an orchestra that had never played together, tuning to a shared key.

It was also, he realized, the last easy night. They all knew it. No one said so.

---

Meanwhile, Tumenggung Batu and Dewi Anjani had managed to have an argument.

It had begun over the enchantment of weapons -- a technical matter that should have been dry as chalk but which, between a Kurcaci smith and a Bidadari mystic, became a philosophical battle as fierce as any fought with steel.

"Enchantment," Tumenggung Batu declared, his thick arms crossed over his obsidian breastplate, "is a function of the metal. The alloy, the tempering, the fold count, the quenching medium. You do not pour spirit into a blade. You forge a blade so pure, so perfectly structured, that it *attracts* spirit. The enchantment is a consequence of craft, not its cause."

"*Eta mah ukur wadahna,*" Dewi Anjani replied in the highest register of Sundanese -- *basa luhur*, the language of the divine court, each syllable carrying the crystalline precision of one who spoke as the Dewata spoke. "*Sanes eusina. Hiji keris anu mulah gaduh niat ruhaniah mung saukur beusi seukeut.*" *That is merely the vessel. Not the substance. A keris without spiritual intention is merely sharp iron.* "It is the *niat* -- the will, the purpose -- that the empu pours into the metal during forging that gives a keris its tangguh, its soul. Without niat, your perfect alloy is a letter without words."

"Words without a letter have no way to travel."

"A letter without words has nothing to say."

They glared at each other -- the short, broad Kurcaci in his dark armor, and the tall, luminous Bidadari with her unbound hair and her eyes like deep water. Two traditions. Two truths. Two stubborn certainties colliding.

It was Eyang Guru who intervened, not with words but by placing between them, on the flagstone floor, the jimat he had been forging -- the small bronze amulet, inscribed with mantras, threaded on its cotton cord.

"Bronze," he said mildly. "And words. Craft and intention. Neither one without the other."

Tumenggung Batu stared at the jimat. Dewi Anjani looked at the old sage, then at the Kurcaci. Something shifted between them -- not agreement, precisely, but recognition. The understanding that their argument was not a disagreement but a conversation, and that the conversation might require the entire journey to complete.

"Your metalwork is acceptable," Dewi Anjani said, after a pause.

"Your arrows fly straight," Tumenggung Batu allowed.

It was, by the standards of Kurcaci and Bidadari diplomacy, practically an embrace.

---

On the eve of departure, Prabu Siliwangi summoned them to the great pendopo.

The Tohaan of Dieng stood at the center of the pavilion, his *kancana* robes catching the torchlight, his bearing as still and certain as the candi that surrounded him. Behind him, a row of Bidadari attendants held gifts wrapped in cloth of white and green -- the colors of Pasundan, the colors of hope and living things.

He gave them to each companion in turn, with words that were both blessing and farewell.

To Kian Santang, he gave a phial.

It was small -- no larger than his thumb -- carved from a single piece of mountain crystal and stoppered with a seal of silver filigree. Within it, a liquid glowed with a soft, shifting radiance -- water from Telaga Warna, the sacred lake, blessed by the Bidadari with their deepest light. In the torchlit pendopo it pulsed gently, casting prismatic reflections across Kian Santang's fingers, and its warmth was the warmth of the lake at dawn, clean and boundless.

"A light when all other lights go out," Prabu Siliwangi said. "Carry it close."

To Raden Wijaya, he gave a keris.

Kian Santang heard the prince's sharp intake of breath. The blade was slender, its seven luk curves catching the light like frozen lightning, and its pamor -- the pattern in the metal -- was extraordinary: a sinuous, coiling form that resolved, unmistakably, into a naga, a serpent-dragon, its body winding through the steel as though the creature had been caught mid-strike and turned to metal. The hilt was gold, cast in the shape of a naga's head with ruby eyes, and the sheath was dark wood inlaid with gold wire.

"Keris Nagasasra," Prabu Siliwangi said. "The ancestral blade of the Mataram kings. It was broken when Mataram fell. The Kurcaci reforged it, and the Bidadari blessed it. It has waited here for the hand that would carry it home."

Raden Wijaya's hand closed around the hilt, and his eyes -- those guarded, wary eyes -- glistened. He bowed, deeply, and said nothing. He did not need to.

To each companion, Prabu Siliwangi gave a cloak.

They were woven from highland wool -- the fine, dense fleece of the sheep that grazed the upper slopes of Pasundan -- and dyed the deep green of the *wanadri*, a green so rich and so true that it seemed to shift with the light, lighter in sun, darker in shadow, blending with leaf and moss and bark until the wearer became part of the landscape. Each cloak was clasped at the throat with a small brooch of silver shaped like a salam leaf.

"These will not make you invisible," Prabu Siliwangi said. "But they will make you difficult to see. The dye is drawn from the forests themselves, and it remembers its origin. Wear them, and the land will shelter you."

There were other gifts. Rope woven from enchanted rattan, strong enough to bear the weight of Tumenggung Batu in his full armor, light enough to coil at a belt. Lembaran -- maps drawn on lontar leaf, showing the passes and the cave systems and the coastal approaches in exquisite detail. A pouch of healing herbs, prepared by the Bidadari healers, for wounds and fever and the exhaustion of long marches. Waterskins of cured leather, each one charmed to keep its contents cool.

The companions received them in silence, with the gravity appropriate to gifts that might mean the difference between life and death.

---

The farewell began at the hour before dawn.

Uncle Kian was there. He had come to Dieng weeks ago, thin and trembling, freed from the keris's long torment but still bearing its scars. He stood at the edge of the courtyard as the companions assembled, wrapped in a shawl against the highland cold, and when Kian Santang came to him, the old Tuyul's eyes were bright with a grief he could not fully conceal.

He embraced his nephew. His arms were thin, his grip fierce.

"Come back," he said, his voice rough. "Come back and finish my book for me. I have left the last chapter unwritten. Thirty years of travel and I cannot find the ending. Perhaps you will find it for me, on the road."

"I will try, Uncle."

"No. Do not try. Come back." He held Kian Santang at arm's length and studied his face -- the face he had watched grow from infancy to youth, the face that had inherited his sister's eyes and his father's jaw and something else, something that was entirely and only Kian Santang. "You are braver than you believe. Remember that when the road is dark."

They held each other for a moment longer, and then Uncle Kian released him and turned away, because there are partings that cannot survive the weight of further words.

Nearby, in the shadow of a frangipani tree, Dyah Pitaloka -- Prabu Siliwangi's niece, who had barely spoken to Raden Wijaya during his time in Dieng and yet had been noticed, always, at the edge of every gathering where the prince was present -- pressed something into Raden Wijaya's hand. A pendant. Small, delicate. A jasmine flower preserved in amber, its petals still white, suspended in golden stone as though the moment of its blooming had been caught and held forever.

She said nothing. He said nothing. Their hands touched, and parted, and she stepped back into the shadow of the tree, and Raden Wijaya tucked the pendant into his shirt, close to his heart, with the careful deliberation of a man placing something exactly where it needed to be.

Then Prabu Siliwangi raised his hands, and the courtyard fell silent.

His voice, when it came, was not loud. It did not need to be. It carried the authority of a Tohaan who had held his nagara against darkness for centuries, and the words he spoke were old -- older than Dieng, older than the temples, older perhaps than the giri themselves. An ancient Sundanese invocation, a blessing of the road, spoken in the *basa luhur* that was reserved for communion with the Dewata:

*"Ka nu ngalengkah, mugia dijaga ku tanah.*
*Ka nu ngajajah, mugia dituntun ku bintang.*
*Ka nu mulang, mugia ditampi ku imah."*

To those who walk, may the earth protect you.
To those who journey, may the stars guide you.
To those who return, may home welcome you.

The words settled over them like a second cloak, invisible but felt, a warmth that had nothing to do with fire. Eyang Guru bowed his head and murmured a responding mantra -- an invocation of the Dewata Sanghyang Widi, the supreme divine principle, asking that the spirits of mountain and river and forest walk beside them. The kemenyan incense that burned at the pendopo's four corners sent its smoke *sumebar* in spirals that seemed, for a moment, to form the shapes of unseen guardians -- karuhun, the ancestral spirits, gathering to witness the departure of those who carried the fate of their world.

And then the Bidadari sang.

It began with a single voice -- a woman's voice, high and pure, rising from the colonnaded gallery above the courtyard like a bird taking flight. A *Pangkur* -- the *kawih bwatuha* of determination and resolve, its martial rhythm tempered here with feminine grace, each stanza built on the ancient syllabic structure: eight-eleven-eight-seven-twelve-eight-eight, the numbers themselves a kind of incantation. The melody was in the Sundanese pelog scale, each note bending and shimmering with ornaments that a human throat should not have been able to produce.

*Mangkat ti gunung nu luhung,*
*sapuluh suku napak kana batu,*
*salapan hate ngahiji,*
*sumpah na pageuh,*
*lampah ka kidul ngajugjug lautan sora,*
*keris digenggem pageuh,*
*nyawa dipasrahkeun ka Batara.*

*Departing from the sacred mountain,*
*ten feet upon the stone,*
*nine hearts made one,*
*the oath holds fast,*
*the journey south seeks the thundering sea,*
*the keris gripped firm,*
*our souls surrendered to the Divine.*

Other voices joined -- two, then four, then more, weaving around each other in harmonies that were not fixed but fluid, shifting like water, like light on water, like memory. The Pangkur's driving rhythm -- that insistent, forward-leaning pulse of resolve -- carried beneath the melody like the heartbeat of the fellowship itself, urging them onward, telling them: *You are ready. You are enough. Go.*

The song had no words that Kian Santang could fully understand in its oldest verses. It was older than the language he knew, older than the language anyone knew. But he understood it nonetheless. It was a song of parting. A song that said: *We release you to the road, but we do not release you from our hearts.* A song that said: *Go, and carry our light with you, and know that it will be burning here when you return.*

Dawala wept openly, his round face crumpled with emotion. Bagus stood rigid, his jaw clenched, fighting the tears with the same stubbornness with which he fought everything. Cepot was still for once, his sharp face soft, his wit silenced by something it could not deflect. Even Tumenggung Batu looked away, and his hand went to his eyes, and if anyone noticed, no one spoke of it, because even Kurcaci chieftains are allowed their tears when the Bidadari sing farewell.

The song faded. The silence it left was sacred.

---

They departed Dieng at dawn.

Nine companions, walking south into the mist. The ancient temples rose behind them, their stone spires emerging from the clouds like the fingers of buried gods reaching toward a sky they could no longer touch. The path descended through wild highland grass that brushed their shoulders -- their waists, in the case of the Tuyul -- and the dew soaked through their new cloaks and the cold air burned in their lungs.

Eyang Guru led, his teak staff striking the earth in a steady rhythm, his white hair streaming behind him in the morning wind. Raden Wijaya walked beside him, the Keris Nagasasra at his hip, his long stride shortened to match the old sage's pace. Behind them, Dewi Anjani moved with the silent grace of a creature born to forests, her bow across her back, her eyes scanning the tree line with the habitual vigilance of one who had hunted things far more dangerous than deer. Tumenggung Batu stumped along with the dogged, ground-eating pace of the Kurcaci, his war hammer swinging from his belt, his obsidian armor clinking faintly with each step. Gajahmada walked alone, slightly apart, his spear across his shoulders, his gaze fixed ahead.

And the four Tuyul -- Kian Santang, Bagus, Cepot, Dawala -- walked together in the center, surrounded by their larger companions like saplings sheltered by ancient trees.

Kian Santang looked at them. His eight companions. A sage who had lived since before the oldest candi was built. A prince without a kingdom. A warrior-princess of the Bidadari. A Kurcaci chieftain who believed the surface world was fundamentally poorly constructed. A Majapahit general who burned with a pride that could save or destroy him. A farmer who packed provisions like poems. A jester who made shadows tell the truth. And a boy with wide eyes and a brave heart who wept at beautiful songs and would follow his friends into fire.

So different from each other. So improbable as allies. United by nothing but a task that none of them could accomplish alone, and a slender, whispering blade wrapped in batik cloth on the back of the smallest among them.

Kian Santang was terrified. He could feel the keris against his spine -- warm, alert, aware of every step that carried it closer to its origin. It whispered. It always whispered. It would whisper all the way to Krakatau, he knew, and the whispers would grow louder, and the warmth would grow hotter, and there would come a time when the whisper became a roar and the warmth became a fire and he would have to choose, alone, what he would do.

But that was ahead. That was the future, the unknown, the darkness beyond the next ridge.

Here, now, in this moment, he was not alone.

He listened to the sound of eight other pairs of feet on the mountain path -- the heavy tread of the Kurcaci, the near-silent step of the Bidadari, the measured stride of the prince, the steady plod of the sage, the martial rhythm of the general, the solid tramp of Bagus, the quick patter of Cepot, the stumbling but determined shuffle of Dawala -- and the sound was louder than the whisper. Not by much. But enough.

Behind them, the temples of Dieng disappeared into the cloud. Ahead, the volcanic highlands of Jawa stretched southward under a sky of pewter and pearl, range after range of green and grey and blue, folding into distance and mist and uncertainty.

Nine companions. One road. One purpose.

The Fellowship of the Keris walked south, and the mountains watched them go.
