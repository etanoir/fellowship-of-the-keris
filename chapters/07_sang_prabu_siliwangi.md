# Bab 7: Sang Prabu Siliwangi
## (The Wise King)

---

The temple galleries of Dieng ran deep into the mountain, and they were older than memory.

Prabu Siliwangi walked ahead of Kian Santang through corridors carved from living basalt, the stone still warm with the volcanic heart that beat far beneath the plateau. Torches set in iron brackets threw a flickering amber light across the walls, and upon those walls -- every surface, every span, from floor to vaulted ceiling -- the history of Nusantara was carved in stone. At the gallery's entrance, a stone *lingga-yoni* stood sentinel — the sacred pillar rising from its base, Siwa and Parvati, the masculine and feminine principles of creation joined in eternal balance. Kian Santang pressed his palms together and bowed his head as he passed, the way Eyang Guru had taught him — one did not enter a *kabuyutan*, a place of ancestral power, without acknowledging the forces that dwelled there.

The reliefs were extraordinary. They were not the stiff, formal panels of the lowland candi, where figures stood in orderly rows with their hands folded in devotion. These were alive. The stone figures moved -- or seemed to move -- in the torchlight, their bodies caught in attitudes of such fierce dynamism that Kian Santang half-expected to hear the clash of their weapons, the cries of their battles, the songs of their celebrations. Centuries of master carvers had worked this stone, generation after generation, adding their chapters to a chronicle that began at the very dawn of the world and stretched forward through the long, tangled, magnificent, and terrible history of the archipelago.

Prabu Siliwangi paused before the first panel and lifted his torch so that the light fell full upon it.

"Here," he said. "Here is where it begins."

His voice was quiet, but it carried the particular resonance of a man accustomed to being heard -- not because he spoke loudly, but because when he spoke, silence arranged itself around his words like water around a stone. He was tall for a Sundanese king -- though any human was tall by Tuyul reckoning -- and he carried himself with the upright, unhurried grace of one who had worn authority so long it had become indistinguishable from his posture. His hair was silver-white, pulled back and bound in the simple iket of a Pasundan nobleman, and his face was the kind of face that age had not so much weathered as refined -- each line an inscription, each crease a record of grief endured and wisdom gained. His eyes were dark and still and deep, the eyes of a man who had spent a lifetime looking at the world and had somehow not despaired of it.

He had healed Kian Santang three days ago -- the wound on the young Tuyul's shoulder where the Leak's shadow-blade had bitten deep, leaving a cold that no ordinary medicine could reach. Prabu Siliwangi had worked through the night with poultices of turmeric and galangal and *tirta* — sacred water drawn from the sulfurous springs and blessed with the fourteen mantras of purification. His hands moved with the unhurried precision of a master healer, his lips murmuring invocations in old Sundanese — '*Om Swasti Astu, Sang Hyang Wisnu, nu sinembah, nu ngajaga hirup, nu ngajaga napas*' (*Om, may all be well, Lord Wisnu, the honored one, who guards life, who guards breath*) — syllables that sounded less like words than like the earth itself breathing. By morning, the cold had retreated to a dull ache, a phantom chill that surfaced only at night, and Kian Santang had slept without nightmares for the first time since leaving Padalarang.

Now the king stood before the carved stone and began to tell the story of the world.

---

"In the beginning," Prabu Siliwangi said, "there was only fire and water."

The first panel showed it: a vast ocean, its waves rendered in stone with such delicacy that the crests seemed to curl and foam, and beneath the ocean, the volcanic fire -- great plumes of molten rock rising from the deep, meeting the water in explosions of steam that the carvers had captured as spiraling clouds of stone.

"Sang Hyang Widhi -- the Supreme Being, the breath behind all breathing -- shaped the islands from the meeting of these two forces. Fire rising. Water falling. Where they met, stone was born. And from that stone, the first land emerged."

He moved to the next panel, his torch painting shadows across the carved figures.

"The peoples came after. Each born from a different aspect of creation." His long fingers traced the stone. "The Tuyul -- your people, Kian -- emerged from the earth itself. From the rich volcanic soil of the highlands, shaped by the same patient forces that grow the rice and the teak and the banyan. That is why your kind are so rooted, so connected to the land. You are the earth's own children."

Kian Santang looked at the carved figures -- small, broad-shouldered, working the terraced fields with tools in their hands, their faces turned toward the sky with expressions of quiet contentment. He recognized them. They looked like his neighbors in Padalarang. They looked like Bagus.

"The Bidadari," Siliwangi continued, moving along the wall, "descended from the celestial realm — the *Swarloka*, the highest of the Triloka. Sang Hyang Widhi breathed a portion of the divine light into mortal form, and they walked upon the earth as beings of grace and luminance. They are the longest-lived of all the peoples -- nearly immortal, some say, though I have known Bidadari who would dispute that claim." A faint smile. "They are keepers of ancient knowledge, guardians of the spiritual arts. Their memory stretches back to the first dawn."

The Bidadari in the carvings were tall and slender, their features carved with an unearthly delicacy, robed in garments that the stone-carvers had rendered so finely they seemed to ripple like silk. They stood among mountain temples, their hands raised in gestures of blessing or of power.

"The Kurcaci were born from the volcanic stone itself -- from the deepest fires of the earth, where pressure and heat forge diamond from carbon and iron from ore. They are smiths and miners, builders and delvers. They carved the first tunnels through the mountains of Gunung Sewu. They forged the first iron tools, the first bronze gongs, the first blades. They are proud, stubborn, and as enduring as the stone that birthed them."

The Kurcaci in the relief were squat and enormously muscled, their bodies wide as barrels, their beards long and elaborately braided. They worked at great forges deep within mountain halls, their hammers raised above anvils that glowed with carved flame. Despite their small stature -- even smaller than the Tuyul figures beside them -- they radiated a density, a concentrated power that the carvers had rendered with remarkable skill.

"And the humans," Siliwangi said, "arose from the meeting of earth and sky. They are the youngest of the peoples, and the most restless. They build kingdoms and tear them down. They wage wars and compose poetry. They are capable of great beauty and great cruelty, often in the same breath." He paused. "I say this as one of them."

The human figures in the relief were various -- warriors and farmers, kings and fishermen, their forms taller and more varied than the other peoples, spreading across the carved landscape in every direction, building, planting, fighting, praying.

"For a long age, the peoples lived in a kind of harmony. Not perfect harmony -- the Kurcaci and the Bidadari have never entirely trusted one another, and the Tuyul preferred to be left alone, and humans, being humans, quarreled among themselves with great enthusiasm. But there was balance. The peoples traded, and sometimes married across their borders, and the spiritual energy of Nusantara -- the *bayu*, the sakti that flows through every island, every stone, every drop of water -- sustained them all."

He moved further into the gallery. The torchlight swayed, and the stone figures seemed to shift with it, their carved shadows deepening.

"Then came the darkness."

---

The panel that showed the coming of Raja Iblis was carved in a different style -- sharper, more angular, the figures rendered with an urgency that bordered on violence. A figure stood at the center, taller than any other in the relief, robed in shadow, crowned with a diadem of interlocking serpents. Its face was beautiful and terrible -- the face of a being that had chosen cruelty not from necessity but from preference, the way an artist chooses a medium.

"He came from beyond the world," Prabu Siliwangi said, and his voice dropped lower, as though the stone itself might be listening. "From the spaces between realities, drawn by the sakti of the archipelago. He desired it. He desired all of it -- not to nurture, not to share, but to possess. To bend every living thing to his will. He was the antithesis of everything the peoples of Nusantara had built — the very negation of *dharma*, the sacred order that holds the worlds in balance."

The subsequent panels told the story in grim sequence. Raja Iblis establishing his throne in the caldera of Krakatau. The forging of the Keris Murka -- depicted as a scene of terrible industry, dark figures laboring over a forge that blazed with unnatural fire, the blade taking shape amid smoke and shadow. The creation of the nine lesser keris. Nine kings, carved with faces of pride and hunger, each accepting a shadow blade from the outstretched hand of the Shadow King.

"They were great rulers once," Siliwangi said softly, studying the carved faces of the nine. "Kings of Nusantara -- wise, powerful, beloved by their people. But the shadow keris whispered to them, promised them empire, promised them immortality — a perversion of *moksha*, the true liberation of the soul. What the keris offered was not freedom from the cycle of death and rebirth, but imprisonment within it — an eternity of bondage disguised as eternal life. And one by one, they fell. Their bodies changed. Their souls burned to ash. They became the Leak -- the nine terrors of the night, shape-shifters bound forever to the Shadow King's will."

The carving of the Leak was the most disturbing work Kian Santang had ever seen rendered in stone. The figures were caught mid-transformation -- half-human, half-something else, their forms stretching and contorting, their mouths open in silent screams that might have been agony or ecstasy. Their eyes, inlaid with chips of dark obsidian by some long-dead craftsman, seemed to follow Kian Santang as he passed.

"And the Buta," Siliwangi continued, moving to the next section. "They did not arise naturally. They were made." The word carried a weight of disgust. "Raja Iblis captured peoples of every kind -- Tuyul, Kurcaci, humans, even Bidadari -- and in the furnaces of Krakatau, he twisted them. Corrupted their forms with dark sakti. Broke their wills and reshaped their bodies into instruments of war. The Buta are not a people. They are a crime."

The Buta in the carvings were monstrous -- hulking, tusked, their skin rendered in rough chisel-strokes that suggested the grey-green of rotting stone. They marched in vast columns across the relief, an army of the ruined, carrying weapons of black iron.

"Nusantara burned," Siliwangi said. His voice was level, but his hand tightened on the torch. "For an age, the darkness spread. The kingdoms fell one by one. The great forests were put to the torch. The sea routes were blocked by Naga Laut -- sea serpents bred in the deep trenches and bent to the Shadow King's purpose. It seemed that all was lost."

He stopped before the largest panel in the gallery -- a composition so vast it covered the entire wall from floor to ceiling, the figures carved in deep relief, some of them projecting a full hand's breadth from the stone.

"But the free peoples found their courage," Siliwangi said, and now something shifted in his voice -- a warmth, a fiercer light. "They came together. Not because they trusted one another perfectly, not because they had forgotten their rivalries, but because they understood that divided, they would be devoured. Gotong royong, Kian. The oldest principle of Nusantara. Mutual aid. The understanding that the strength of one is the strength of all."

The great alliance panel was magnificent. Bidadari warriors stood beside Kurcaci smiths, their forms so different -- the Bidadari tall and luminous, the Kurcaci broad and granite-hard -- yet united in purpose. Tuyul *yoda*, small but fierce, held the flanks in the *kidang sumeka* formation. Human kings led the charge on horseback in the sweeping *garudabihwa* — the eagle formation — their keris drawn, their batik standards flying. Above them all, a constellation of symbols -- the rice sheaf of the Tuyul, the mountain hammer of the Kurcaci, the celestial lotus of the Bidadari, the garuda eagle of the human kingdoms -- woven together in a single emblem.

"The last battle was fought on the slopes of Krakatau itself," Siliwangi said. "The alliance broke through the Buta hordes. They scaled the volcanic slopes through rivers of fire and clouds of poison. They entered the caldera. And there, in the heart of the Shadow King's domain, a prince of Mataram -- a young man, bold and proud, the greatest silat master of his age -- faced Raja Iblis in single combat."

The carved scene showed it: a lone figure standing before the towering form of the Shadow King, a keris raised in his hand, the volcano erupting behind them in a cascade of carved stone flame.

"He cut the Keris Murka from the Shadow King's hand. Without the blade, Raja Iblis's physical form shattered. His spirit was driven down, deep into the caldera, a thing of smoke and fury, too weak to take shape but too bound to the keris to be truly destroyed."

Prabu Siliwangi fell silent for a long moment. The torchlight flickered. In the dancing shadows, the carved figures seemed to breathe.

"The prince's name was Raden Kartawijaya," he said at last. "He was Raden Wijaya's ancestor -- thirteen generations removed."

Kian Santang looked up sharply. "Raden Wijaya? The ranger? The man who brought us here?"

"The same." Siliwangi's expression was unreadable. "The blood of *adiwira* runs in him, Kian. But so does the blood of hubris. For Kartawijaya did not destroy the keris. The alliance begged him to cast it into the caldera, to unmake it in the fire where it was forged. But he could not. The blade had touched him. Its whisper had found the ambition in his heart -- the desire to be not just a prince, but a king above kings. He kept it. Claimed it as a war trophy. And within a generation, the throne of Mataram began to rot from within."

"And the keris was lost," Kian Santang said quietly.

"Lost. Stolen, recovered, stolen again, carried across the archipelago by a succession of fools and madmen and thieves, each one believing they could master what had mastered the Shadow King himself. Until it vanished into the caves of Gunung Sewu, where it slept for centuries."

Siliwangi turned to Kian Santang, and in the amber torchlight his face was kind and grave and very old.

"Until your uncle found it. Until you inherited it. Until you carried it here, to this place where the old stories live in stone."

He placed a hand on Kian Santang's shoulder -- the unhurt one -- and the touch was warm and steady.

"You have done well to bring it this far, Kian Santang. But the road ahead is longer than the road behind, and far more dangerous. Tomorrow we will hold council. Tonight, rest. Let the stones tell their stories. They have been waiting a long time for someone to listen."

He spoke then in the ancient cadence of the *Sinom* — the pupuh of wisdom and reflection, the song of the sage who has seen much and weighs each word like gold upon a scale. '*Sumewaka kaula ka sadaya rahayat,*' he said, '*jeung tanah anu ngalahirkeun kami.*' (*I devote service to all the people, and the land that gave us birth.*) It was the old oath of Sundanese kingship — the *dharma* of the ruler, not dominion but service, not command but stewardship.

---

They emerged from the temple galleries into the pale afternoon light of the Dieng plateau, and Kian Santang found that the world above had changed while he walked below.

Delegations had arrived.

The first he noticed was the sound -- a deep, rhythmic clanking, like hammers striking an anvil in steady time. It came from the eastern path, where the trail wound up through the sulfurous mist from the lower valleys, and as Kian Santang watched, a procession emerged from the vapor like figures materializing from a dream.

The Kurcaci of Gunung Sewu had come.

There were three of them, and they were extraordinary. They were short -- shorter even than the Tuyul, which Kian Santang had not believed possible. The tallest of them barely reached his chest, and Kian Santang was not a tall man. But what they lacked in height they compensated for in breadth with a thoroughness that bordered on the aggressive. They were built like boulders -- enormously wide through the shoulders, thick through the chest, their arms and legs corded with muscle that seemed carved from the same granite as their homeland. Their skin was the grey of volcanic stone, weathered and rough-textured, and their features were blunt and strong -- heavy brows, broad noses, jaws that could have been chiseled from cliff faces.

The one who led them was the most imposing figure Kian Santang had ever seen at any height. His beard was magnificent -- a great cascade of iron-grey hair, braided into seven thick ropes, each braid adorned with small gemstones that caught the pale light and threw it back in sparks of red and blue and green. Rubies, sapphires, emeralds -- a fortune in stones woven into his facial hair with the casual extravagance of a people who mined such things the way Tuyul grew rice. His eyes, beneath the heavy brow ridge, were the grey of flint, and they carried the same quality: hard, sharp, and capable of striking sparks.

He wore armor of volcanic glass -- obsidian plates, carved and fitted with extraordinary precision, overlapping like the scales of a great fish. The armor was black as a starless night, but where the light caught its edges, it gleamed with an oily iridescence, purple and green and dark gold. Across his broad back, strapped with leather thongs as thick as Kian Santang's wrist, he carried a war hammer. It was enormous -- its stone head the size of a man's torso, its iron shaft wrapped in treated sharkskin for grip. No human could have lifted it. The Kurcaci carried it as easily as Kian Santang carried his waterskin.

"Tumenggung Batu," Prabu Siliwangi said, with the careful neutrality of a diplomat introducing a potential problem. "Chieftain of the Gunung Sewu caves. Master smith. Warrior. And a man of... strong opinions."

Tumenggung Batu climbed the last steps to the temple plateau, planted his iron-shod boots on the ancient stone, and looked around with the expression of a man surveying property he suspected of being inferior. His gaze swept across the temple spires, the colored lakes, the mist-wreathed peaks -- and then it found the Bidadari.

Dewi Anjani stood on the terrace of the northern temple, her hand resting on the hilt of her moonstone blade. She had been watching the Kurcaci arrive with an expression of studied composure that did not entirely conceal the wariness beneath. The Bidadari warrior-princess was tall and pale as starlight, her features sharp and fine as carved ivory, and she wore the light silk armor of her people with the ease of long practice. When her gaze met Tumenggung Batu's, the air between them seemed to crystallize.

The Kurcaci chieftain's nostrils flared. "Bidadari," he said, in the way another man might say *mosquitos*.

'*Kurcaci,*' Dewi Anjani replied, her voice carrying the cool precision of *basa luhur*. '*Rahayu, Ki Tumenggung.*' (*Peace, Lord Tumenggung.*) The greeting was formally correct, but the warmth of an icicle.

Prabu Siliwangi stepped smoothly between them. '*Sugeng rawuh ing Dieng, Tumenggung,*' he said, offering the Javanese greeting of welcome in the refined *krama* register — an acknowledgment that the Kurcaci, though they had their own tongue, conducted formal affairs in the Javanese of the old courts. (*Welcome to Dieng, Tumenggung.*) "The council is grateful for the long journey you have undertaken."

"Grateful." Tumenggung Batu unstrapped his war hammer and set it down on the stone with a thud that Kian Santang felt in his feet. "We did not come for gratitude, Prabu. We came because our scouts report Buta Raksasa massing in the eastern tunnels -- creatures so large they crack the stone as they walk. Something is breeding them, and something is driving them outward. The deep roads are no longer safe." He glanced again at Dewi Anjani. "I trust we will be discussing this in council, and not wasting time on celestial pleasantries."

Before anyone could respond, a horn sounded from the southern path.

---

The Majapahit delegation arrived with the kind of entrance that suggested careful planning.

They came up the southern trail in formation -- eight soldiers in the red and gold batik of Majapahit's royal guard, their keris at their belts, their bearing parade-ground perfect. They moved with the synchronized precision of men who trained together daily and fought together regularly, and their eyes, alert and scanning, marked every vantage point and every shadow with the automatic vigilance of soldiers on hostile ground.

At their center walked a man who did not need a formation to command attention, but clearly did not object to having one.

Gajahmada was tall -- tall even among humans, broad across the shoulders, narrow at the waist, built with the powerful, proportioned frame of a man who had trained for combat since boyhood and had never found a reason to stop. His skin was the rich brown of teak heartwood, weathered by sun and campaign. His hair was black and thick, pulled back in a warrior's knot, and his face was the kind of face that would have been handsome had it been capable of relaxing -- strong jaw, high cheekbones, a mouth that seemed permanently set in an expression of controlled intensity. His eyes were his most striking feature: dark, intelligent, and lit with an inner fire that burned steadily and without rest, the eyes of a man who saw every conversation as a negotiation, every room as a battlefield, every moment as an opportunity to advance a cause he believed in with his entire being.

He wore the distinctive batik of Majapahit nobility -- a sarong of deep crimson patterned with gold thread in the parang motif, draped in the martial style that left the right arm free for drawing a weapon. At his belt hung a keris -- not the Keris Murka, not a shadow blade, but a weapon of extraordinary craftsmanship nonetheless. Its sheath was inlaid with gold filigree, and its hilt was carved from dark sandalwood in the shape of a garuda with outspread wings. An heirloom. A statement. The keris of a general who had never known defeat and did not intend to make the acquaintance.

He mounted the temple steps three at a time and stood before Prabu Siliwangi with the confidence of a man accustomed to being the most important person in any room he entered. His vision for Nusantara was as vast as the archipelago itself — he dreamed of unity from the seafaring Bugis of Sulawesi to the mountain warriors of Papua, from the rice terraces of Bali to the pepper forests of Sumatra. Every island, every people, every shore bound together under one banner. It was a dream of breathtaking ambition, and its power lay in the fact that Gajahmada believed in it absolutely.

"Prabu Siliwangi." His voice was deep, resonant, pitched to carry. He gave the traditional salute -- right fist to left palm -- with a crispness that made it feel less like courtesy and more like an opening move. '*Kula nyuwun pangapunten, Prabu, bilih kula késah kasar.*' (*I ask forgiveness, Prabu, if my manner is blunt.*) The *krama* Javanese was spoken with the clipped precision of a man who had learned court language as a tool rather than as a native tongue. "Majapahit sends its regards, and its general. The times are too grave for envoys."

"General Gajahmada." Siliwangi returned the salute with the unhurried grace that was his natural armor. "You are welcome. Your journey was long."

"Long, and troubled. Buta war-bands on the eastern roads. Three skirmishes between Surabaya and Semarang. My scouts report armies massing beyond the Brantas River -- not raiding parties, Prabu, not bandits. Armies. With siege equipment and supply lines and something directing them with a strategy that no Buta mind could conceive." His jaw tightened. "Majapahit is the strongest kingdom remaining in Nusantara, and we are being tested. If we fall, the eastern half of Jawa falls with us."

His gaze swept the gathering and came to rest on Kian Santang. The general studied the young Tuyul with the open, appraising look of a man unaccustomed to concealing his judgments.

"This is the keris-bearer?" he said. "The one who carries the weapon of the Shadow King himself?"

Kian Santang felt the weight of the man's attention like a physical pressure. He straightened, though the top of his head barely reached the general's elbow.

"It is not a weapon," Kian Santang said quietly. "It is a curse."

Something flickered in Gajahmada's dark eyes -- not quite dismissal, but not far from it. "In Majapahit, we have a saying: the blade that cuts your enemy is a weapon, and the blade that sits in a chest is a waste. The Keris Murka could turn the tide of this war, Tuyul. We could use its power to break the Buta armies, to drive them back into the wastelands, to end this shadow before it swallows the archipelago."

"Or it could swallow you," said a voice from the northern terrace.

Raden Wijaya descended the temple steps with the quiet, unhurried stride of a man who moved through the world like water through stone -- finding every gap, occupying every space, without seeming to displace anything at all. He was lean where Gajahmada was broad, dressed in the simple traveling clothes of a ranger rather than the finery of a court, his dark hair unkempt, his face half-hidden by the shadow of his hood. He carried no keris at his belt, but his hands -- scarred, calloused, the hands of a silat master who had spent decades perfecting the art of making the body itself a weapon -- hung loose at his sides with a readiness that needed no display.

Gajahmada turned to face him, and recognition crossed the general's features like a cloud crossing the sun.

"You," he said. The single word carried layers -- surprise, calculation, something that might have been respect and might have been resentment. His eyes narrowed. "I know your face. You look like the old kings of Mataram. The bloodline shows." A pause, weighted and deliberate. "But Mataram's throne sits empty. It has sat empty for three generations."

Raden Wijaya said nothing. His expression did not change. He stood in the afternoon light of Dieng like a shadow that had chosen to be still, and his silence was more eloquent than any reply.

Gajahmada held his gaze for a long moment, then turned back to Prabu Siliwangi. "We have much to discuss in council, Prabu. I trust the discussions will be... practical."

He strode away toward the guest quarters, his guard falling in around him like armor closing, and the space he left behind seemed somehow larger for his absence, as though his presence had compressed the air.

---

The day continued to bring arrivals. As the sun traced its arc above the mist-wreathed plateau, Dieng filled with the representatives of a world under siege.

A Sriwijayan envoy arrived in the early afternoon -- a slim, weathered man in the indigo sarong of the maritime empire, his arms tattooed with the wave-and-serpent patterns of the Sumatran seafarers. He had sailed from Palembang and then ridden overland for two weeks, and the news he carried was written in the exhaustion on his face before he spoke a word of it. The sea routes were failing. Naga Laut -- great serpents of the deep, creatures that had slept in the ocean trenches since the first age -- had been sighted in the Java Sea. Three Sriwijayan trading vessels had been lost in the past month alone, dragged beneath the waves by coils as thick as temple pillars. The spice trade from Maluku had slowed to a trickle. The naval patrols could not hold the shipping lanes.

"The sea serpents do not act on their own," the envoy said, his voice cracked with salt and weariness. "Something is waking them. Something is commanding them. The seas that have connected our peoples since the first boats were carved from logs are becoming walls."

A Kediri horseman arrived on a mount lathered with sweat and wild about the eyes -- a young rider, barely more than a boy, who had ridden for four days without proper rest. Kediri, the kingdom of the horse-lords on the eastern plains of Jawa, was under attack. Buta raiding parties had been striking the eastern border villages, burning granaries, slaughtering livestock, disappearing into the forests before the cavalry could respond. The raids were not random. They were probing attacks -- testing the defenses, mapping the response times, searching for weakness with a methodical patience that no Buta horde had ever displayed before.

"They are being led," the horseman said, his young face tight with the effort of holding his composure before so many elders and lords. "There is intelligence behind the attacks. A strategy. Our commanders have never seen Buta fight like this."

Word had also come — carried by relays of message-runners and signal fires across the islands — from peoples beyond Jawa. Bugis seafarers of Sulawesi, those fearless navigators of the eastern waters, had sent word through the Sriwijayan channels: shadow movements in the deep ocean, Naga Laut seen further east than ever before, the trade winds themselves seeming to shift as though something vast were disturbing the currents. And from the eastern reaches of the archipelago, from the mountainous forests of Papua, runners had arrived at Sriwijayan ports bearing pledges of support from the warrior clans — peoples who had never before involved themselves in the affairs of the western kingdoms, but who understood, with the intuition of those who live close to the earth, that what threatened Jawa threatened all.

Kian Santang stood at the edge of the gathering, listening to report after report, and felt the shape of the world contracting around him. From every direction, the same story: darkness massing, borders crumbling, ancient terrors stirring in the deep places. The free peoples of Nusantara were being encircled, slowly and methodically, by a will that had spent centuries preparing for this moment.

And at his hip, wrapped in batik cloth and hidden beneath his sash, the Keris Murka pulsed with its slow, patient heartbeat, and said nothing, and waited.

---

That evening, as the sulfurous mists rose from the colored lakes and the temperature dropped to the highland chill that made Dieng feel like a place suspended between earth and sky, Prabu Siliwangi gathered three others in the small inner chamber behind the main temple.

The room was lit by a single oil lamp. Its walls were bare stone, uncarved, austere. A tikar mat covered the floor, and on it sat four figures: Prabu Siliwangi, cross-legged, his hands resting on his knees; Eyang Guru Batara, his staff across his lap, his white beard luminous in the lamplight; Raden Wijaya, leaning against the far wall with the coiled stillness of a man who had learned to be comfortable in discomfort; and Kian Santang, small among them, the youngest by centuries, feeling the weight of the keris at his hip like an anchor dragging him toward a depth he could not see.

"The council will convene tomorrow," Prabu Siliwangi said. "All the delegations will speak. All will have their opinions. Some will be wise. Some will be foolish. And some -- " he glanced, almost imperceptibly, in the direction of the guest quarters where Gajahmada had retired " -- will be dangerously compelling."

"He wants to use the keris," Raden Wijaya said. His voice was low, flat, stating a fact rather than making an accusation.

"He does." Siliwangi nodded. "And you cannot blame him entirely. He is a general. His kingdom is dying. He sees the most powerful weapon in Nusantara sitting in the sash of a young Tuyul, and his soldier's instinct tells him to use it. It is a logic that will appeal to many in the council."

"It is the logic that destroyed Mataram," Raden Wijaya said, and there was something in his voice -- quiet, bitter, old -- that silenced the room.

Siliwangi let the silence hold for a moment. Then he spoke again, and his voice carried the cadence of a man who had spent a lifetime thinking about what he was about to say.

"Let me tell you what I understand about power," he said. "It is a thing I have studied -- not as a warrior studies it, not as a general or a king, though I have been all of these things. I have studied it the way an empu studies the metal in his forge: its nature, its properties, its tendencies. And what I have learned is this."

He leaned forward, and the lamplight carved his face into planes of gold and shadow.

"The Keris Murka was forged on a single principle: that power flows from the one to the many. One blade, one will, one master -- and all others bend. That is Raja Iblis's vision. A single throne. A single hand. All of Nusantara gathered beneath one shadow."

He paused.

"But that is not our way. The peoples of Nusantara -- all of them, Tuyul and Kurcaci and Bidadari and human alike -- have understood since the first age that true power flows in the opposite direction. From the many to the one. We call it gotong royong."

The word hung in the air of the chamber like a gong still ringing.

"Gotong royong," Siliwangi repeated. "Mutual aid. Community. The understanding that no single person, no single kingdom, no single people can stand alone -- that our strength lies in the web of connections between us, in the willingness to carry one another's burdens, to share the harvest and the labor, to build the house together that no one could build alone. This is not weakness, Kian Santang. It is the deepest power in Nusantara. It is the power that defeated the Shadow King in the first age, and it is the only power that can defeat him again. It is the truest expression of *dharma* — the cosmic order, the righteous path — and against it, no blade forged in malice can prevail."

He looked at each of them in turn.

"The keris is the antithesis of gotong royong. It makes one ruler of all. It concentrates power into a single point and burns everything around it. To use it -- even with the best of intentions, even in defense of the things we love -- is to accept the Shadow King's premise. It is to say that domination is the answer. And the moment you say that, you have already lost. You have become what you sought to destroy."

The lamp flame guttered and steadied. Somewhere outside, the night birds of Dieng began their calls -- strange, plaintive sounds that echoed across the plateau like the voices of ghosts.

"There is more," Eyang Guru said. He had been silent until now, his expression grave, his eyes fixed on some middle distance. "I had hoped to keep this for the council, but you should hear it first." He looked at Siliwangi, who nodded.

"Empu Gandring," the sage said, and the name fell into the room like a stone into still water. "My old friend. My brother in craft. He has built a fortress on Gunung Merapi."

Raden Wijaya's head came up. Even his composure cracked at this.

"In the caldera halls of Merapi," Eyang Guru continued, "he is breeding Buta Raksasa -- giant demon warriors, larger and more cunning than any the world has seen since the first age. An army is being forged there, Raden. Not just the raiding parties that strike the borders of Kediri and Majapahit. A true army, with siege engines and war beasts and a commander who understands the craft of war as well as any general in Nusantara."

"War on two fronts," Raden Wijaya said quietly. '*Gusti Ingkang Maha Kuwaos*,' he murmured — *God Almighty* — the Javanese invocation spoken in *krama inggil*, the highest register of the language, reserved for moments when the mortal mind confronts the enormity of what it faces. "Merapi in the east. Krakatau in the west."

"And the sea routes cut between them," Siliwangi said. "The Naga Laut ensure that Sriwijaya cannot bring its navy to bear. We are being isolated. Separated. The opposite of gotong royong."

The four of them sat in the small stone room, and the truth of their situation settled over them like the sulfurous mist of the plateau -- heavy, acrid, inescapable.

"Then the keris must be destroyed," Kian Santang said. "That much is clear. It must be taken to Krakatau and unmade."

Prabu Siliwangi looked at him for a long moment, and in the old king's eyes there was something that might have been pride, or sorrow, or both.

"Yes," he said. "But who will carry it? And who will walk beside them? These are the questions the council must answer. And the answers will not come easily."

---

Kian Santang could not sleep.

He lay on the stone sleeping shelf of his small chamber in the temple complex, staring at the ceiling where the lamplight played across the old carvings. The mist had thickened outside, pressing against the narrow windows like something alive, and the plateau was silent except for the distant bubbling of the sulfurous springs and the occasional cry of a night bird.

The keris lay on the shelf beside him, wrapped in its batik cloth, tucked into its warangka of ancient sandalwood. It was quiet tonight. It had been quiet since they arrived at Dieng, as though the sacred ground of the plateau suppressed its voice the way heavy cloth suppresses fire -- muffling it, containing it, but not extinguishing it entirely.

But Kian Santang could not silence his own thoughts, and they drove him from his bed and into the corridor and out through the temple gate, seeking air, seeking space, seeking the cold clarity of the highland night.

He found himself on the terrace above Telaga Warna, the Lake of Colors. The sulfurous waters shimmered in the moonlight -- pale greens and blues and milky whites, shifting and blending as the underground vents fed their mineral streams into the basin. The moon was three-quarters full, riding high above the mist, and its light fell on the water like scattered silver.

He sat on the stone balustrade and unwrapped the keris.

He did not mean to. Or rather, he meant to without deciding to, which was how the keris worked -- it did not compel, not exactly. It suggested. It created conditions in which the thing it wanted felt like the thing you wanted, until the boundary between its will and yours grew so thin that the act of reaching for the blade felt as natural and inevitable as breathing.

The warangka slid off with a whisper of sandalwood against metal, and the keris lay bare in his hands.

In the moonlight, the pamor patterns came alive.

Kian Santang had seen the pamor move before -- that slow, oily shifting of the nickel patterns in the iron matrix, the serpentine coiling that made the blade seem to breathe. But this was different. In the direct light of the moon, the seven pamor patterns blazed. They writhed and rearranged themselves with a speed and clarity that was unmistakable, forming images as sharp as the reliefs in Siliwangi's temple gallery -- but these images moved.

He saw armies marching. Vast columns of Buta pouring across burning plains, their formations stretching to every horizon. He saw kingdoms in flame -- the great halls of Majapahit crumbling, the temple spires of Prambanan toppling, the sawah terraces of his own Pasundan highlands blackened and dead, the water in the paddies boiled to steam. He saw a throne of obsidian rising from the caldera of Krakatau, immense, impossible, carved with the faces of the enslaved and the conquered, and upon it --

The pamor shifted again, and the images dissolved, and in their place there were eyes.

Two eyes. Red as forge-fire. Older than the stone beneath him, older than the mountains, older than the memory of any living thing on any island of Nusantara. They looked out at him from the metal of the blade with an intelligence that was vast and intimate and utterly, terribly personal.

The Shadow King spoke.

Not in sound. Not in words that traveled through air and struck the ear. The voice came from the blade itself, rising through the metal, through Kian Santang's hands, through his bones, arriving in his mind like a stone dropped into still water, sending ripples through every thought.

*You cannot destroy me, little one.*

The voice was quiet. Conversational. Almost gentle. And that gentleness was the most frightening thing Kian Santang had ever experienced -- worse than the Leak on the ridge above Padalarang, worse than the nightmare visions of Krakatau, worse than the cold of the shadow-wound in his shoulder. Because the voice was not threatening. It was explaining. With the patience of something that had eternity to wait and knew that patience itself was the most devastating weapon.

*I am in the metal. I am in the fire. I am in you now.*

The wound on Kian Santang's shoulder blazed with phantom cold -- a spike of ice that drove through his flesh and into the bone, and for one terrible instant he felt the connection: the blade in his hand and the wound in his shoulder and the red eyes in the pamor, all linked, all part of a single circuit of dark sakti that ran through him like a wire through a puppet.

*You carry me. You feed me. Every step you take, every breath, every beat of your frightened little heart -- it all flows into the metal. Into me. You think you are bringing me to my destruction? You are bringing me home.*

Kian Santang slammed the keris into its warangka.

The motion was violent, graceless, the desperate act of a man snatching his hand from a flame. The sandalwood sheath swallowed the blade, and the red eyes vanished, and the voice cut off mid-syllable like a door closing on a scream. He wrapped the batik cloth around the sheathed blade with hands that shook so badly he could barely grip the fabric, folding it once, twice, three times, as though layers of old cotton could contain what layers of ancient stone could not.

He was shaking. His whole body trembled, fine and rapid, the shaking of a man in the grip of a cold that comes from inside rather than out. The wound on his shoulder throbbed -- a dull, sick pulse, rhythmic and wrong, as though something beneath the healed skin was keeping time with a heartbeat that was not his own.

He sat on the stone balustrade above Telaga Warna, and he breathed, and he tried to remember who he was. Kian Santang. A Tuyul of Padalarang. A farmer's nephew. A young man who loved the smell of jasmine in the morning and the sound of rain on a thatched roof and the taste of his mother's nasi timbel, which he would never eat again because his mother was gone and his lembur was behind him and the world was larger and darker and more terrible than he had ever imagined.

*I am in you now.*

He pressed his hands to his face and did not weep, but it was a near thing.

---

Footsteps on the stone. Soft, familiar, unhurried.

Kian Santang did not look up. He did not need to. He knew those footsteps the way he knew his own breathing -- the slightly heavier tread of the left foot, the particular rhythm of a stocky Tuyul walking at his natural pace, neither fast nor slow, solid as the earth he came from.

Bagus sat down beside him on the stone balustrade. He did not speak. He did not ask what was wrong. He did not offer comfort or wisdom or solutions, because Bagus understood -- had always understood, with the deep, wordless intelligence of the truly good -- that there were moments when a person needed not to be helped but simply to be accompanied. To not be alone.

They sat together in the moonlight, and the mist drifted across the plateau, and Telaga Warna shifted its colors beneath them -- green to blue to pale gold, the mineral waters catching the moon's light and transforming it into something strange and beautiful.

After a while -- it might have been minutes, it might have been an hour -- Bagus reached into his traveling pouch and produced a small clay cup and a bamboo thermos stoppered with a carved wooden plug. He poured carefully, and the steam rose in a thin, fragrant curl. The scent hit Kian Santang before the warmth did: ginger, fresh and fierce, sweetened with palm sugar, warmed with a hint of lemongrass. Wedang jahe. The drink of home. The drink that Tuyul grandmothers pressed into cold hands on rainy evenings, that farmers shared at the edge of the sawah after a long day's planting, that mothers gave their children when the night was dark and the world felt too large.

"I heated it on the kitchen coals," Bagus said. "It is still warm. Drink."

Kian Santang took the cup. The clay was warm against his palms, and the heat seeped into his shaking hands, and the ginger burned gently at the back of his throat, and the simple, absolute goodness of the gesture -- a friend, a cup of tea, the willingness to sit in the dark with someone who is afraid -- broke something open in his chest that was not grief but its opposite. Gratitude. The bone-deep, wordless gratitude of a man who has looked into the abyss and turned to find that he is not standing at its edge alone.

"Thank you," Kian Santang said.

"Drink your tea," Bagus said.

They sat together on the terrace above the Lake of Colors, and the mist moved around them, and the stars turned slowly overhead in their ancient patterns — the same constellations that the old *Saka* calendar mapped, the same stars that Bidadari navigators had followed since before the first kingdom set stone upon stone. Tomorrow was the council. Tomorrow, the fate of Nusantara would be debated by kings and generals and sages, and the arguments would be fierce and the stakes would be absolute, and every word spoken would carry the weight of nations.

But tonight, the wedang jahe was warm, and a friend sat close, and the colored waters of Telaga Warna held the stars in their shifting depths like scattered *sesajen* offerings, like prayers laid on sacred water, like the lights of a thousand lembur reflected in the oldest mirror in the world.

And that was enough. For tonight, that was enough.
