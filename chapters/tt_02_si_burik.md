# Bab 2: Si Burik di Rawa Bakau

## (Si Burik in the Mangrove Swamps)

---

*Wanda anyar deui.*
*Dina layar, aya dua Tuyul leutik leumpang dina taneuh hideung.*
*Sisi basisir nu panas, dimana seuneu papanggih jeung cai.*
*Saha nu ngadagoan di kalangkang? Saha nu ngambekan dina lumpur?*
*Dalang nyekel wayang kulit nu ruksak -- awakna bengkok, hulu ageung, panon bodas kawas bulan nu geus paeh.*
*Ieu wayang teh lain pahlawan, lain raksasa. Ieu wayang teh naon anu jadi pahlawan lamun cinta paeh sarta harepan geus leungit.*
*Regepkeun, dulur-dulur. Ieu carita teu bisa ditunda.*

*Another act.*
*On the screen, two small Tuyul walk on black earth.*
*At the edge of a hot shore, where fire meets water.*
*Who waits in the shadow? Who breathes in the mud?*
*The Dalang holds aloft a damaged wayang puppet -- its body twisted, its head too large, its eyes white as a dead moon.*
*This puppet is neither hero nor demon. This puppet is what a hero becomes when love dies and hope is forgotten.*
*Listen, brothers and sisters. This story cannot be postponed.*

## A. The Volcanic Wasteland

Grey.

The western coast of Jawa was a desolate liturgy of ash and stone, a landscape where the earth had been flayed raw by the fires of the deep and left to heal in silence, and the healing had not come. Here, the green mercy of the archipelago ended -- ended not gradually, as twilight ends the day, with soft gradations and the gentle courtesy of fading, but abruptly, violently, the way a sentence ends when the speaker is struck dead mid-word. One step: the last stubborn fern, scorched brown but still reaching for light. The next step: nothing. Only the grey. Only the black. Only the colourless, lifeless expanse of a world that had been unmade by its own innards and had not yet gathered the courage to begin again.

The ground was a mosaic of cooled basalt, fissured and sharp, the colour of old charcoal and dried blood. It stretched in every direction like the floor of a ruined cathedral -- a cathedral whose vaulted ceiling was the ash-choked sky, whose pillars were the obsidian ridges that thrust upward from the plain in jagged, irregular rows, like the broken teeth of a jaw too vast to comprehend. Between the ridges, the basalt fractured into hexagonal columns -- the geometry of cooling lava, the mathematics of a world being born from fire -- and these columns formed a pavement so regular, so precisely tessellated, that it seemed almost designed, as though some divine architect had laid a floor for a hall that no mortal foot was meant to walk upon.

Nothing grew here but silence. And the silence was not peace. It was the held breath of a landscape that remembered fire and expected fire again -- the way a beaten child remembers the fist and flinches at every shadow.

The ash fields began where the basalt ended, a transitional zone where the solid earth gave way to powder. Volcanic ash, fine as ground bone, lay in drifts a hand's depth thick, grey and featureless, and the wind -- what wind there was, a sluggish, superheated exhalation from the south -- sculpted the ash into low ridges and ripples that mimicked the waves of the sea, so that walking through them was like wading through a frozen ocean, an ocean that had been drained of water and colour and life and left with only its shape, its empty, mocking geometry. The ash rose at every footfall in small, silent clouds that hung in the motionless air before settling again, coating everything -- skin, cloth, the insides of nostrils, the corners of eyes -- in a fine grey film that tasted of iron and sulphur and the deep, mineral bitterness of the earth's interior.

Above it all, the air shimmered. Heat distortions rippled across the landscape like the surface of a lake disturbed by stones dropped from a great height, and through these ripples the obsidian ridges seemed to move -- to sway, to breathe, to lean toward the two small figures that picked their way across the dead plain like insects crossing the face of a cooling forge.

Kian Santang sat on a ridge of razor-sharp obsidian, his knees drawn tight to his chest, his arms wrapped around his shins as though he were trying to compress himself into the smallest possible space, to reduce the surface area of his being so that the thing on his back would have less of him to touch. The air was thin and hot, tasting of metal and rotten eggs -- the breath of the volcano that loomed behind them, its dark cone blocking out the southern stars. The Keris Murka was heavy against his back, wrapped in the batik cloth that Eyang Guru had blessed in another age, in another world, when the old sage was alive and the path ahead was merely terrifying rather than impossible. The blade was a physical weight that seemed to increase with every step they took away from the sea, as though distance from water -- from the cleansing, the purifying, the *holy* element that the karuhun had always used to wash away corruption -- made the blade heavier, denser, more present. A stone tied to a drowning man.

It was silent now, the keris. But its silence was not peace. It was the holding of breath before a scream. The gathering of a wave before it breaks. The moment between the lightning and the thunder, when the air itself is pregnant with violence and the only question is not *whether* but *when*.

He looked at his hands.

They were trembling.

Not with fear, though fear was a constant, cold companion in his belly -- a rat that gnawed ceaselessly at the walls of his resolve, that whispered in the voice of reason that he should turn back, should throw the blade into the sea, should bury it in the ash and walk away and let someone else -- someone stronger, someone older, someone who was not a Tuyul of the highlands, a child of the *sawah* and the swimming hole -- carry this burden to the fire. The fear was there. It was always there. But the trembling was not fear.

The trembling was hunger.

A hunger to touch the hilt. To unwrap the batik cloth and feel the warmth of the gold against his palm. To close his fingers around the grip and feel the blade's *bayu* -- its life force, its terrible, seductive vitality -- flow into his hand and up his arm and into the hollow, aching chambers of his heart, filling them with a warmth that was not warmth but a simulation of warmth, a counterfeit fire that burned without illuminating, that consumed without nourishing, that gave the sensation of fullness while deepening the void.

The keris whispered. Not in words. Not yet. It whispered in sensations -- a tingling in his fingertips, a tightness in his throat, a sudden, vivid flash of memory: the weight of the hilt in his hand when he had held it in the Gua Sewu, the way the blade had seemed to *fit* him, to recognize him, to welcome him home the way a parasite welcomes its host, with an intimacy more terrible than love because love requires two beings and the keris did not recognize Kian Santang as a being at all but only as a vessel, a container, a shape to be filled with darkness and then discarded.

*Touch me,* the keris did not say, because it did not need to say it. The desire was already there, planted in Kian Santang's blood like a seed in fertile soil, and it grew with every hour, every step, every breath of the sulfurous air. *Touch me, and the pain will stop. Touch me, and the fear will quiet. Touch me, and you will be strong enough to face what waits ahead. You are too small for this task. Too weak. But I can make you more. I have always been able to make you more.*

Kian Santang clenched his fists until the nails bit into his palms. He focused on the pain -- the simple, honest, physical pain of nails breaking skin -- and used it to drown the whisper, the way a man caught in a riptide uses a rope to pull himself back to shore. The pain was real. The whisper was not. The pain was *his*. The whisper belonged to something else.

But the distinction was growing harder to maintain.

He remembered Eyang Guru's words -- spoken in the firelight at Dieng, in another lifetime, before the caves, before the bridge, before the fall. *The keris does not corrupt by force, Kian. It corrupts by invitation. It offers you what you most desire, and the price is everything you are.* The old sage's eyes had been kind. His voice had been gentle. And Kian Santang, who was then a boy with a boy's confidence in his own goodness, had nodded and believed he understood.

He had not understood. Understanding came now, on this ridge of broken glass, with the ash wind scouring his skin and the blade on his back humming its silent, patient song. Understanding came with the knowledge that the keris did not need to offer him power, or glory, or dominion over others. Those were the offers it made to kings and warriors, to the proud and the ambitious, to the men who wore their desires on their faces like banners. To Kian Santang -- to a tired, frightened, homesick Tuyul who wanted nothing more than to sit in the shade of a banana tree and eat his mother's cooking and never, never, never again feel the cold weight of destiny on his back -- the keris offered something far more insidious.

It offered relief.

*Put me down,* it whispered without whispering. *Lay your burden aside. You have carried enough. You have suffered enough. Let someone else take the next step. Rest. Sleep. Be small again.*

And the terrible truth -- the truth that Kian Santang could share with no one, not even Bagus, not even the ghost of Eyang Guru that walked beside him in his dreams -- was that he *wanted* to. Every fibre of his being wanted to lay the keris down and walk away. And the wanting was not weakness. The wanting was wisdom. The wanting was the honest assessment of a mind that knew its own limits and had measured them against the task ahead and found them wanting. He was too small. The task was too great. The distance too far, the enemy too strong, the darkness too deep. These were not fears. These were facts. And the keris, with the cunning of all cursed things, whispered the facts to him and let the facts do its work.

The only thing that kept him on the ridge, his knees to his chest, his nails in his palms, the blade on his back -- the only thing that prevented him from unwrapping the batik and touching the hilt and letting the false warmth flood through him and drown the knowledge of his own inadequacy -- was Bagus.

"Eat," a voice said.

Kian Santang started. The world snapped back into focus -- the ash, the heat, the obsidian ridge, the vast and indifferent sky. Bagus was there, crouching beside him, offering a strip of dried meat on a broad leaf. The leaf was a *jati* leaf, teak, and Kian Santang wondered distantly where Bagus had found it in this wasteland, and the wondering was itself a kind of salvation, because it meant that some part of his mind was still engaged with the world, still curious, still tethered to the ordinary, miraculous business of living.

Bagus's face was gaunt. The flesh had melted from it over the past weeks the way wax melts from a candle, leaving the underlying architecture exposed -- the broad cheekbones, the strong jaw, the wide, slightly crooked nose that Kian Santang had broken with a wayward elbow during a wrestling match when they were seven. His skin was smeared with ash and sweat, dark rivulets cutting channels through the grey film like rivers through a delta. He had lost weight. They both had. The journey from the sea had cost them flesh and sleep and something less tangible but more essential -- a certain lightness, a certain ease with the world, the unthinking confidence of the young and the loved.

But his eyes were the same. Steady. Brown. Warm as the earth of Padalarang after a morning rain. Anchored in a reality that Kian Santang felt slipping away from him like sand through open fingers. Those eyes said: *I am here. I am not going anywhere. You are not alone, and you will not be alone, not while I have breath and the strength to stand beside you.*

"I'm not hungry," Kian Santang whispered.

"Eat," Bagus repeated, pressing the food into Kian's hand. His fingers were rough and warm, and the touch -- the simple, human touch of skin on skin -- was like a rope thrown to a drowning man. "We have miles to walk before we find water. You need strength, *Priya*."

The word -- *Priya*, friend, spoken in the easy *basa loma* of their village, the language of the *sawah* and the swimming hole and every shared secret since boyhood -- cut through the keris's whisper like a blade through fog. Kian Santang forced himself to chew. The meat was tough and tasteless, like leather left in the rain, but he swallowed it. For Bagus. Because Bagus had carried this meat from the last village, had rationed it, had given Kian Santang the larger portion every time, and to refuse it would be to refuse the love that offered it, and that was a sin that Kian Santang -- despite the blade on his back, despite the darkness creeping in at the edges of his soul -- was not yet capable of committing.

"The mangroves are ahead," Bagus said, pointing north. A dark line of vegetation broke the monotony of the ash fields, a tangle of shadows rising from the grey mud like a wall erected between this dead land and whatever lay beyond. It was the first green they had seen in three days, and the green was not reassuring -- it was the dark, oily green of things that grew where nothing should grow, that fed on rot and silence and the slow decomposition of the world. "We might find a spring there. Or at least shade."

"Or Leak," Kian Santang murmured.

"Or Leak," Bagus agreed. His hand found the hilt of his gardening knife -- the short, broad-bladed *kujang* that he had carried from Padalarang, sharpened on a stone every evening with a patience that was both ritual and meditation. It was not a warrior's weapon. It was a tool for cutting roots and trimming hedges and harvesting the small, stubborn vegetables that grew in the rocky soil behind his family's house. But Bagus held it the way a warrior holds a keris: with certainty, with purpose, with the quiet understanding that the value of a blade lies not in its forging but in the hand that wields it. "But we have to move. The sun is coming up, and out here, we are just bugs on a plate."

They stood, their legs protesting -- muscles cramped from the cold of the night and the heat of the morning and the particular, grinding exhaustion of walking through ash, which was like walking through deep sand except that sand did not taste of death and the deep sand did not whisper promises in the dark. They shouldered their meagre packs and began the descent toward the swamp.

But the descent was not direct. The basalt field between the obsidian ridges and the mangrove line was bisected by a depression -- a shallow, elongated basin that Kian Santang first took for a dry riverbed. As they drew closer, he realized it was something else entirely.

The pools began at the edge of the basin: shallow depressions in the basalt, each no larger than a cooking pot, filled with water that was not water. The liquid was thick, viscous, the colour of spoiled egg yolk -- a sulphurous, pale yellow-green that shimmered with an oily iridescence in the low light. Steam curled from each pool in thin, constant wisps, and the smell was a physical assault: hydrogen sulphide, the stench of the earth's bowels, so concentrated that it burned the inside of the nostrils and brought tears to the eyes.

"*Mulah* breathe deep," Bagus warned, pulling his shirt over his nose. "The gas can kill."

But it was not the gas that stopped Kian Santang in his tracks.

It was the faces.

In the largest pool -- a kidney-shaped depression perhaps ten paces across and knee-deep, its surface trembling with the convection of internal heat -- he saw them. Not reflections. Not tricks of the light. *Faces.* Pale, elongated, frozen in expressions of agony that had outlasted the flesh, visible beneath the sulphuric surface like images preserved in amber. They were the faces of warriors -- Kian Santang could see the outlines of helmets, the suggestion of armour, the open mouths that had been screaming when the volcanic heat had taken them, had boiled the water and the blood and the hope from them in a single, cataclysmic instant and left them here, suspended between death and dissolution, their features preserved by the sulphur and the mineral-rich water in a state that was neither burial nor rest but something far more terrible: a permanent, geological witness to the violence that had unmade them.

"*Karuhun,*" Kian Santang breathed. Ancestors. Ancient warriors, perhaps from the wars that preceded the Shadow King's rise, perhaps older still -- soldiers from an age when the volcanoes were worshipped as gods and men went willingly into the fire, believing they would be reborn from the ash. The sulphuric pools had preserved them the way a temple preserves its reliefs: as images, as memories carved in a medium more durable than stone.

The keris pulsed.

It was the first time the blade had made itself known through physical sensation rather than whisper, and the feeling was unmistakable: a warm, rhythmic throbbing against his spine, like a second heartbeat pressed against his own, syncopated, out of phase, the heartbeat of something that lived on a different frequency than flesh. The keris recognized the dead. Not as individuals -- they were beyond individuality, their names and their stories long since dissolved into the mineral broth that preserved their shapes -- but as *souls*. Trapped souls. Souls that had not been released into the *samsara* cycle, that had not been permitted to die and be reborn and die again in the eternal, turning wheel that the *karuhun* believed governed all existence. They were stuck. Caught between worlds, between states, between the living and the dead, preserved by the same volcanic chemistry that preserved their faces, and the keris -- the Keris Murka, the Blade of Wrath, the instrument of the Shadow King's malice -- *resonated* with them, because the keris, too, was a thing caught between states, a thing that was neither alive nor dead, neither weapon nor soul, but something in the liminal space between, something that fed on the trapped and the transitional and the unfinished.

Kian Santang took a step toward the pool. Then another. His feet moved without his permission, drawn forward by the keris's resonance the way iron filings are drawn to a magnet -- not by choice but by the fundamental alignment of forces that operate below the threshold of will. The faces in the pool seemed to turn toward him. The open mouths seemed to speak, though no sound came -- only a vibration, a sub-audible hum that he felt in his teeth and his sternum and the marrow of his bones.

*Join us,* the faces said without saying. *Lay down your burden. Rest here, in the warm dark, where the wheel does not turn and the fire does not burn and the weight of the world is borne by the water and the stone.*

The keris throbbed. His foot found the edge of the pool. The yellow-green water lapped at the toe of his boot, and where it touched, it *warmed* -- not with the hostile heat of the sulphuric reaction but with something more intimate, more welcoming, the warmth of a bath drawn by loving hands, the warmth of a bed after a long day's work, the warmth of everything that Kian Santang had lost and would never have again.

He leaned forward. The pool waited. The faces waited. The keris sang its silent, patient song of release.

A hand seized his collar and hauled him backward with a force that nearly snapped his neck.

"*MULAH!*"

Bagus's voice was a thunderclap. The word -- *don't* -- was spoken with such violence, such raw, unfiltered terror, that it shattered the keris's enchantment the way a stone shatters a mirror, in an instant, completely, leaving Kian Santang standing on the edge of the pool with his heart hammering and his vision clearing and the sudden, sickening realization of how close he had come flooding through him like ice water.

He stumbled backward. Bagus caught him, his arms wrapping around Kian Santang's chest from behind, pulling him away from the pool, away from the faces, away from the edge. They fell together in a tangle of limbs and ash, and Bagus held on -- held on with the desperate, crushing strength of a man who has just watched his friend walk calmly toward his own destruction and has no words for the horror of it, only hands, only arms, only the brute physical insistence of a body that refuses to let go.

"Kian," Bagus whispered, and his voice was broken, his breath ragged. "Kian, *Priya*, stay with me. Stay *here*. Stay on this side."

Kian Santang lay in the ash, staring at the grey sky, feeling Bagus's arms around him, feeling his friend's heartbeat through their pressed-together backs. The keris was silent again. The faces in the pool were still. The moment had passed.

But something had changed. Something in the quality of the silence, in the texture of the air, in the relationship between Kian Santang and the blade on his back. The keris had learned something in that moment -- had tested its bearer and measured his defenses and found the crack, the weakness, the point of entry. The hunger for relief. The longing for rest. The bone-deep, soul-deep exhaustion of a creature carrying a weight it was not built to bear.

The keris would remember. The keris would come back to this moment, to this weakness, again and again, the way water returns to a crack in stone, and one day -- one day soon, if Kian Santang did not find some deeper strength, some more fundamental anchor than his own depleted will -- the stone would break.

"I'm all right," Kian Santang said, though he was not all right, had not been all right since the Gua Sewu, might never be all right again. "I'm here. *Aing* di dieu." He sat up, gently disengaging from Bagus's grip. His hands were shaking. His whole body was shaking. He looked at the pools -- at the dead marshes, the volcanic dead, the preserved and imprisoned warriors who would never ride the wheel of *samsara*, who would never be born again as farmer or fisherman or the seed of a fruit tree in the highlands -- and he felt a grief so vast that it seemed to originate not from his own heart but from the earth itself, from the wounded, fire-scarred, endlessly suffering earth that bore them all.

"We need to move," he said. "Before I -- before it --"

He did not finish the sentence. He did not need to. Bagus understood. Bagus always understood.

They skirted the dead marshes in a wide arc, giving the pools a berth of fifty paces, and Bagus walked on the side nearest the water, his body between Kian Santang and the sulphuric glow, a living shield against the pull. Neither spoke. The silence between them was not the comfortable silence of old friends but the charged, wary silence of a man and his keeper, of a patient and his nurse, and Kian Santang felt the shift in their dynamic like a blade between the ribs.

He thought of the fellowship. Of the nine who had set out from Padalarang in a different age, in a different story, when the world was still green and the task ahead was daunting but not impossible and the company of friends made the impossible seem merely difficult. He thought of Raden Wijaya's oath at the council -- *I will go with you to the end* -- and the prince's steady, unshakeable eyes. He thought of Eyang Guru's hand on his shoulder, the old sage's voice saying *You were chosen because you are small, Kian, and the great despise the small, and the small endure where the great are broken.* He thought of Cepot's laughter and Dawala's appetite and Tumenggung Batu's gruff, iron-throated affection and Dewi Anjani's luminous, ancient, terrifying beauty.

Gone. All of them, gone. Scattered across the burning archipelago like leaves in a storm, each pursuing a path that might lead to salvation or to death, and Kian Santang -- who was the reason for all of it, whose burden had broken the fellowship and sent its members into the wilderness -- walked on, through the ash and the heat and the silence, with only Bagus beside him and the keris on his back and the growing, inescapable knowledge that he was not enough. Had never been enough. Would never be enough.

The mangrove line drew closer. The dark green wall rose from the grey mud like the rampart of a fortress, and as they approached, the ground changed beneath their feet -- the basalt giving way to sediment, the sediment softening to mud, the mud blackening and thickening until each step required a conscious, deliberate effort to extract the foot and place it again, and the smell changed too, the clean, mineral harshness of the ash field yielding to something organic and ancient and faintly, unmistakably alive.

The swamp was breathing.

## B. Cathedral of Rot

Black.

The mangrove forest was a cathedral of rot.

Not a metaphor. A cathedral -- with pillars and arches and vaulted ceilings and the dim, filtered light of stained glass, except that the pillars were the trunk-roots of ancient *bakau* trees, rising from the black mud in twisted columns that resembled nothing so much as the limbs of tortured men frozen in the act of trying to pull themselves free from the earth. And the arches were the interlocking branches above, woven into a canopy so dense and so continuous that it formed a ceiling of living wood, a vault of leaves and vines and parasitic orchids that blocked the daylight with the thoroughness of stone and admitted only a sickly, greenish gloom -- the colour of old copper, of stagnant ponds, of things that live in the permanent twilight between the water and the air. And the stained glass was the occasional gap in the canopy where a tree had died and fallen and the sky showed through like a wound in the forest's skin, and the light that entered through these gaps was not the golden light of the tropical sun but something filtered and diminished and strange, as though the canopy did not merely block the light but *transformed* it, corrupted it, turned it from something wholesome into something complicit with the darkness below.

The *bakau* trees here were ancient. Kian Santang had seen mangroves before -- in the tidal flats near Padalarang, where the fishermen set their traps and the children played in the shallow water among the prop roots -- but those were young trees, slender and orderly, their roots neat as the legs of a wading bird. These were something else. These trees were old beyond reckoning, their trunks as thick as the stone columns of a *candi*, their bark black and fissured and encrusted with lichens and mosses and small, pale fungi that glowed faintly in the gloom with a cold, bioluminescent light that was not reassuring but spectral, the light of decay, the light of matter returning to its constituent elements in the slow, patient chemistry of rot. Their roots were not merely tangled but *tortuous* -- they coiled and twisted and looped back upon themselves in configurations that defied the logic of growth, as though the trees had been in agony, as though the soil they grew from had caused them pain, and they had contorted themselves in a futile attempt to escape their own foundations.

Between the roots, the mud. Black, oily, fathomless mud that swallowed the foot to the ankle and held it with the insistent, gelid grip of something that did not want to let go. The mud had a texture that was more animal than mineral -- it was not the honest dirt of the highlands, the clean, red earth of Padalarang that smelled of rain and growth, but something older and more complex, a slurry of decomposed leaves and rotted wood and the accumulated sediment of centuries of tidal action, laced through with the pale threads of fungal mycelium and the dark, sinuous shapes of roots that had died and been swallowed and were now part of the substrate, part of the flesh of the swamp itself. Walking through it was like walking through the gut of a living thing -- the squelch and suck of each footstep an obscene parody of digestion, as though the swamp were tasting them, assessing them, deciding whether to let them pass or to close around them and add their bones to the geological record.

And from the mud, rising in irregular lines like the ribs of some buried leviathan -- some creature of the deep past that had crawled ashore and died here and been consumed by the swamp and incorporated into its structure -- the great prop roots arched overhead, forming passages and corridors and chambers that had the feel of architecture, of spaces designed for habitation, though the only things that inhabited them were the insects and the crabs and the small, scuttling things that fled from the vibration of approaching feet.

The air hung heavy and motionless. Saturated. Every breath was a mouthful of steam -- warm, wet, thick with the smell of decay. Decomposing leaves, stagnant water, and the underlying, sharp tang of volcanic sulphur that seeped up through the mud from the geological vents below, a reminder that beneath this forest of rot and silence, the earth was still burning, still molten, still possessed by the fires that had created the wasteland they had just crossed. The sulphur tang mixed with the organic stench of the swamp to produce a smell that was unique and unmistakable: the smell of a world caught between creation and destruction, between fire and water, between the volcanic forces that built the land and the tidal forces that eroded it, and neither was winning, and the result was this -- this liminal, in-between place, this threshold, this no-man's-land of rot and silence and the slow, patient work of dissolution.

They moved slowly. Each step was a negotiation -- with the mud, which did not want to release the foot; with the roots, which were slick with algae and treacherous underfoot; with the pools of standing water that appeared without warning between the root structures, their surfaces black and still and depthless, reflecting nothing. Kian Santang climbed over a root the thickness of his body, his fingers slipping on the algae-slick bark, his boots finding purchase in a crevice between two prop roots that formed a narrow, V-shaped channel filled with water the colour of old tea. He lowered himself into the channel and sank immediately to mid-thigh, the mud closing around his legs with a cold, intimate pressure that made his skin crawl.

"Step where I step," Bagus said from ahead, navigating the root system with the instinctive competence of a gardener, a creature who understood growing things even when the growing things were hostile and strange. He found the firmer patches -- the places where the mud overlay stone or where the root network formed a mat dense enough to support weight -- and Kian Santang followed his footprints, and for a time there was no sound but the squelch and suck of their passage and the dripping of water from the canopy and the distant, subsonic rumble of the earth, a sound so low and so constant that it registered not as sound but as vibration, as a tremor in the bones, as though the world itself were humming a note too deep for ears.

Something moved in the water beside him. A ripple, a disturbance in the black surface that spread outward in concentric rings from a point three paces to his left. Kian Santang froze. The ripple subsided. The water was still. Nothing emerged. But the sense of presence remained -- the feeling of being watched by something that lived below the surface, something that observed without eyes, that assessed without intelligence, that simply *existed* in the dark water with the patience of geology and the hunger of the living.

"What was that?" he whispered.

"Mudskipper, maybe," Bagus said, not turning. "Or a monitor. Keep moving."

But Kian Santang did not think it was a mudskipper. The ripple had been too large, too deliberate, and the water where it had originated was too deep -- a pool that extended under the roots and into the darkness, a passage that connected this pool to others, a network of subterranean channels through which things could move unseen, following the two Tuyul the way a hunter follows a trail, keeping pace, staying just out of sight, *waiting*.

They pressed on. The swamp deepened. The roots grew thicker, more tortuous, their configurations more elaborate and more disturbing. In one passage, the roots had grown into a shape that so perfectly resembled a human figure -- arms outstretched, head thrown back, mouth open in a silent scream -- that Kian Santang stopped and stared at it for a full minute before Bagus pulled him away. In another, the roots formed a cage -- a lattice of interlocking growths that enclosed a small, circular space in which the remains of some animal lay decomposing, its bones half-swallowed by the mud, its skull crowned with a cap of pale mushrooms that glowed with the same cold, spectral light as the fungi on the bark.

Insects clouded the air. Most were the familiar mosquitoes and gnats of the lowlands, but others were strange -- bioluminescent beetles that drifted through the gloom like sparks from a cold fire, their abdomens pulsing with a blue-green light that was beautiful and wrong, beautiful and predatory, beautiful the way a *lure* is beautiful, designed to attract, to fascinate, to draw the eye and the attention away from the real danger that waited in the dark. The beetles orbited Kian Santang's head in slow, elliptical paths, and he swatted at them, and they dispersed and reformed, patient, persistent, drawn to the warmth of his body or the *bayu* of the keris or something else entirely, something that the beetles sensed and the Tuyul could not, an emanation, a signal, a frequency.

"It watches us," Kian Santang whispered.

Bagus stopped. He turned. His face in the green gloom was a mask of controlled anxiety -- the expression of a man who is fighting hard to maintain the appearance of calm because he knows that if he shows his fear, the person he is protecting will shatter. "What watches us?"

"The swamp. The mud. The roots. All of it." Kian Santang's voice was hoarse, thin, stripped of its usual warmth. "It feels... hungry. Like it's waiting for us to stop moving. Like it's waiting for us to fall."

"Swamps don't watch, *Priya*," Bagus said, but his hand tightened on the hilt of his *kujang*, and his eyes swept the corridor of roots ahead and behind with the quick, darting assessment of a prey animal scanning for predators. "Swamps are just... swamps. Mud and water and trees. Nothing more."

But even as he said it, they both knew it was not true. This was not an ordinary swamp. This was the borderland between the living world and the domain of the Shadow King, the transitional zone where the natural order began to fray and the rules that governed the green, sane world of the highlands -- the rules of growth and death and the cycling of seasons, of the *samsara* wheel turning in its appointed course -- began to bend and warp and break down. Here, things grew that should not grow. Here, the dead did not decompose but *persisted*, their shapes preserved by the chemistry of the volcanic soil, their spirits trapped in the liminal space between the wheel's turnings. Here, the earth remembered violence the way a scar remembers a wound, and the memory was not passive but *active*, a force that shaped the landscape and the things that lived in it and the things that passed through it, leaving its mark on everything it touched.

The swamp watched them. Kian Santang was certain of it. Not with intelligence -- not with the focused, intentional malice of the Shadow King or his servants -- but with something older and less specific, the awareness of a vast, slow organism that sensed disturbance the way a spider senses vibration in its web, without curiosity, without analysis, with only the patient, persistent attention of a thing that has learned that disturbance often means food.

They moved deeper. The canopy thickened above them until the green gloom became something closer to true darkness -- a murky, submarine twilight in which shapes lost their edges and distances became uncertain and the boundary between solid and liquid, between root and water, between the seen and the imagined, blurred and dissolved. The bioluminescent fungi provided the only light -- cold points of blue and green that clung to the bark and the mud and the surface of the standing pools, mapping the contours of the swamp in a spectral cartography that was more confusing than illuminating, because the fungi grew on everything, and their light made it impossible to tell where the roots ended and the water began, where the floor was solid and where it would swallow a foot to the knee.

A splash. Distant. Behind them.

They both stopped. Turned. Listened.

Silence. The dripping of water. The hum of insects. The sub-bass vibration of the earth.

Then -- another splash. Closer. Deliberate.

"Something's following us," Bagus said, and his voice was flat, stripped of inflection, the voice of a man who has moved beyond fear into the cold, clear space of tactical assessment. He drew his *kujang* and held it low, the blade angled downward, the way the fishermen of Padalarang held their knives when they waded into the river to gut the catch. "Moving through the water. Using the root channels."

A third splash. Closer still. And with it, a sound that was not a splash but something else entirely -- a soft, wet, rhythmic sound, like skin slapping against mud. Like bare palms on wet stone. Like the hands of a small creature pulling itself along the underside of a root, grip by grip, moving through the canopy the way a spider moves through its web, inverted, silent, patient.

*Pat. Squelch. Pat.*

"Behind us," Bagus breathed.

They turned. The shadows of the mangroves were deep and shifting, and the bioluminescent light played tricks -- shapes formed and dissolved in the gloom, suggestions of movement that might have been the swaying of roots in a current or might have been something else, something with limbs and intention, something that was learning their pace and choosing its moment.

The sound stopped. The swamp was silent. Even the insects had gone quiet -- the bioluminescent beetles extinguishing their lures simultaneously, as though responding to some signal that the Tuyul could not perceive, and the sudden darkness was more frightening than the darkness they had been walking through, because this darkness was *new*, was *chosen*, was the darkness of a predator that has just closed its eyes.

## C. Si Burik Stalking and Encounter

Green.

The green of the bioluminescent fungi crept back, slowly, reluctantly, like witnesses returning to the scene of a crime. And in their cold, spectral light, the swamp reformed itself around the two Tuyul -- the roots, the mud, the black water, all exactly as before, all exactly the same, and yet somehow *different*, as though the swamp had rearranged itself while the lights were out, had shifted its architecture by subtle degrees, moving a root here, deepening a pool there, creating a new passage where there had been none and closing one that had been open, so that the landscape they stood in was the landscape they had entered but was also, impossibly, a different landscape, a mirror of the original that had been reversed in ways too subtle to identify but too significant to ignore.

"We keep moving," Bagus said. "North. Stay close."

They moved. And the sound followed.

*Pat. Squelch. Pat.*

It was not always behind them. Sometimes it came from the left, from the tangle of roots that formed the western wall of their passage. Sometimes from the right, from the standing pools that bordered the path to the east. Once -- and this was the worst, this was the moment that made Kian Santang's heart seize like a fist clenching around a stone -- the sound came from *above*, from the canopy directly overhead, and he looked up and saw nothing, only the interlocking branches and the hanging roots and the pale, spectral glow of the fungi, but he *felt* it, a presence that hovered just beyond the threshold of sight, a weight in the air above him, a disturbance in the pattern of the darkness.

Something was stalking them. Not following -- *stalking*. There was a difference. Following implied curiosity, or coincidence, or the blind, directionless movement of a creature sharing the same path. Stalking implied purpose. Stalking implied assessment. Stalking implied a predator that was studying its prey, learning its movements, identifying its weaknesses, choosing its moment with the careful, calculated patience of a hunter who knows that the difference between a meal and a missed opportunity is the difference between perfect timing and everything else.

The sound grew bolder. The creature -- whatever it was -- was closing the distance, tightening the circle, moving from the periphery of their awareness toward the center. The splashes became louder. The *pat-squelch-pat* of skin on mud became more frequent, more insistent, as though the creature were growing impatient, or excited, or unable to contain itself any longer. Kian Santang caught flashes of movement in the corner of his vision -- a shadow detaching from a root and reattaching to another, a shape that was there for an instant and then was not, a blur of pale limbs and dark, empty space that his brain struggled to resolve into a coherent image and failed.

It was small. That much he could determine. Roughly the size of a child -- a child of perhaps ten or twelve, though the proportions were wrong in ways that nagged at the brain's pattern-recognition centers, like a puzzle piece that almost fits but doesn't, that slides into the space with a millimetre of gap on every side. The limbs were too long. The head was too large. The torso was too narrow, too compressed, as though the creature had been taken apart and reassembled by someone who understood the general principle of a body but not the specific ratios -- the golden section of bone and muscle and sinew that makes a living thing look *right*, look *whole*, look like something that belongs in the world rather than something that the world has rejected and forgotten.

"There," Kian Santang pointed.

A shape detached itself from the roots of a massive *bakau*. It moved with a fluid, spider-like grace that was profoundly disturbing -- not the scuttling, insectile movement of a creature that lacked coordination but the smooth, deliberate, *practiced* movement of something that had spent lifetimes learning to navigate this terrain, that knew every root and every pool and every shadow the way a musician knows the keys of his instrument, by touch, by memory, by the deep, embodied knowledge that comes from repetition carried beyond mastery into something indistinguishable from instinct.

It crawled toward them on all fours, and the crawling was not the crawling of a thing that could not walk but the crawling of a thing that had chosen to crawl, that had found, over centuries of inhabiting this swamp, that four points of contact were better than two, that closeness to the mud was safety, that the low, the prone, the abased posture was the posture of survival, and it had adopted this posture so completely, for so long, that its body had reshaped itself around the choice -- the spine curving, the shoulders broadening, the hips narrowing, the fingers lengthening and spreading to distribute weight across the mud's surface.

It stopped at the edge of the fungi's glow. Its eyes -- huge, bulging orbs set in a skull too large for the spindly neck that supported it -- caught the bioluminescent light and reflected it back with a hungry, milky luminescence, the way a cat's eyes reflect torchlight in the dark but without the cat's feline beauty, without the cat's predatory dignity, with only a raw, desperate, naked need that was more human than animal and more terrible for it.

"Jimat-ku..." the creature hissed.

The voice was a wet rattle, like stones shaken in a flooded jar, like the last breath of a drowning man bubbling up through water, like the sound a throat makes when it has screamed so long and so hard that the vocal cords have frayed to strings and the words they produce are no longer sounds but textures -- rough, wet, broken. It was speaking *basa loma*, the low Sundanese of the village, the language that Kian Santang and Bagus spoke to each other in private, the language of home and hearth and the ordinary, sacred business of daily life. But the words were twisted, chewed up and spat out by a mouth that had forgotten how to form them, by a tongue that had spent centuries speaking only to itself, in the dark, in the mud, rehearsing the same words over and over until they had worn grooves in the creature's mind the way water wears grooves in stone.

"Milik aing... milik aing... jimat kancana..." -- *Mine... mine... the golden amulet...*

"*Gust!*" Bagus whispered, retreating a step, his *kujang* raised between himself and the creature. The word was pure Padalarang -- an exclamation of shock and disgust that belonged in the market square, in the gossip of old women, in the vocabulary of a world where the most disturbing thing one might encounter was a fish that had gone off in the heat. Here, in this swamp, facing this thing that crawled toward them with its white eyes and its rattling voice and its long, long fingers reaching through the gloom, the word was both inadequate and heartbreaking -- a reminder of the world they had left behind, the world of small, comprehensible horrors, the world where *Gust!* was a sufficient response to the worst the day could offer. "What is it?"

Kian Santang stared at the creature. It crept closer, and as it entered the fuller glow of the fungi that encrusted the nearest root, he saw its skin -- and the seeing was a kind of violence, an assault on the eyes and the mind that processed what the eyes reported, because the skin was *wrong* in the way that a disease is wrong, not in one particular but in everything, in the aggregate, in the total effect of a thousand small corruptions that combined into a single, overwhelming impression of ruin.

It was pale. Not the warm, brown paleness of a Tuyul who has spent too long indoors, but a sickly, greyish-white, the colour of something that has been submerged in water for too long, the colour of a fish's belly, of a drowned man's face, of the underside of a stone in a stagnant pool. Over this base colour, patches of green slime grew -- not on the skin but *in* it, as though the skin had become porous, had lost its integrity as a barrier between the creature and its environment, and the environment had begun to colonize it, to send exploratory tendrils of algae and fungus and bacterial film into the tissue itself, so that the green was not a coating but an *integration*, a merging of the creature with the swamp that had sheltered it and that was, slowly, consuming it. Between the green patches, grey scales had formed -- not the scales of a fish or a reptile but something cruder, more pathological, raised plaques of hardened skin that had the texture of dried mud and the colour of old ash, as though the volcanic landscape the creature inhabited had left its mark on its flesh the way a branding iron leaves its mark on cattle.

Its head was large and hairless -- completely, absolutely hairless, not a single follicle, not a single brow or lash, the skull exposed beneath the translucent skin like a geography viewed through clouded glass, the sutures visible, the fontanels long since closed but the shape of the head still carrying the suggestion of infancy, of incompleteness, as though the creature had grown old without ever fully finishing the process of becoming adult. The nose was nearly flat, pressed against the face, the nostrils wide and dark and perpetually flared, pulling in air with a rapid, shallow rhythm that spoke of chronic oxygen deprivation, of lungs that had adapted to the thick, sulphurous air of the swamp by breathing faster and shallower, sacrificing depth for frequency.

Its mouth was a ruin. The lips were thin and cracked and pulled back from gums that were dark and swollen and almost entirely empty of teeth -- a few remained, discoloured pegs jutting at odd angles from the inflamed gum line, more painful to look at than the gaps, because the remaining teeth were a reminder of what had been, a memorial to the mouth that had once been whole, that had once smiled and spoken and eaten and kissed, before the centuries and the corruption and the slow, grinding entropy of the keris's influence had reduced it to this -- this wreckage, this ruin, this archaeological site of a face.

And the eyes. The eyes were the worst. Huge, bulging, protruding from the sockets like eggs too large for their nests, their surfaces milky and white -- not the white of blindness, for the creature could clearly see, tracking their movements with a precision that was unsettling, but the white of a film, a membrane, a secondary lid that had grown over the original eye and that gathered the dim light of the swamp and reflected it back with that hungry luminescence, so that the creature's gaze was not merely observation but *appetite*, each glance a statement of desire, each movement of those pale, bulging orbs a confession of need.

It was a ruin. A wreck of a living thing. A body that had outlived its purpose and its dignity and its species and was now merely *continuing*, not living but persisting, not surviving but enduring, trapped in the same liminal space as the warriors in the sulphuric pools -- between death and dissolution, between the wheel's turnings, preserved by the same terrible force that had twisted it beyond recognition.

But it was familiar.

Something in the shape of the face -- beneath the corruption, beneath the scales and the slime and the lipless ruin of the mouth. Something in the proportions of the body -- the short torso, the broad feet, the hands that were wrong, so wrong, but not *entirely* wrong. Something in the eyes -- those terrible, milky, bulging eyes -- that reminded Kian Santang of eyes he had known, of eyes that had looked at him across a kitchen table, across a sawah, across the small, green, comprehensible distances of a life that now seemed as remote and as mythical as the wayang tales.

The recognition stirred, deep in his gut, like a fish turning in dark water.

"Jimat-ku!" the creature shrieked, and the shriek was a high, thin wail of longing so pure and so desperate that it cut through the air like a keris through silk, a sound that was not anger but grief, not rage but loss, the sound of a soul crying out for the thing that had destroyed it with the voice of a lover calling a name in the dark. It lunged.

It moved faster than anything that wasted should be able to move. The spider-grace that Kian Santang had observed during its approach was nothing -- a walking pace, a Sunday stroll -- compared to the explosive, convulsive speed of its attack. It went from crouching stillness to full, airborne velocity in a fraction of a heartbeat, crossing the four paces between them in a single, lunging leap that was more insect than mammal, the long limbs extending, the fingers spreading, the mouth opening to reveal those few remaining teeth in a rictus of desperate, single-minded intent. It was a blur of pale limbs and snapping jaws and the stench -- *Karuhun*, the stench -- that preceded it like a wave, a physical front of odour so concentrated and so foul that it struck Kian Santang before the creature did, a stench of raw fish and old graves and the deep, organic rot of a body that was decomposing while it was still alive, a body that the keris had kept from death but not from decay, that the dark *sakti* had preserved the way formaldehyde preserves a specimen, maintaining the shape while the substance rotted within.

It hit Kian Santang in the chest. The impact drove him backward, his boots losing their grip on the algae-slick root, and he fell -- fell backward into the mud, the black water closing over his shoulders and the back of his head, and the creature was on top of him, crouching on his chest, its weight negligible -- it weighed nothing, less than nothing, it was a hollow thing, a shell, all bone and sinew and desperation -- but its grip was iron, its fingers digging into his shirt, clawing at the fabric, reaching over his shoulder for the batik wrapping on his back.

Cold, clammy hands found the cloth. The fingers -- those long, wrong fingers -- scrabbled at the wrapping with a frantic, obsessive intensity, and the creature's face was inches from Kian Santang's own, the milky eyes enormous, the lipless mouth working, and the voice -- that wet, rattling, broken voice -- poured words into Kian Santang's face like venom.

"Give it! Give it to Si Burik! *Gollum... gollum!* Thief! Thief! Nasty thief! It belongs to Si Burik! Si Burik found it! Si Burik carried it! Si Burik loved it and the nasty thieves *stole* it! Give it back! Give it back to Si Burik! *Gollum!*"

The words were *basa loma*, twisted and degraded but unmistakable -- the language of home, the language of the village, the language in which mothers sang lullabies and children argued over games and old men debated the weather and the harvest. To hear it from this mouth, this ruin, this corruption of everything the language represented, was an obscenity that went beyond the physical assault, beyond the stench and the cold hands and the milky eyes -- it was a desecration of the sacred, an act of violence against the language itself, against the culture and the people and the world that had produced it.

Kian Santang struggled, gagging on the creature's breath. He kicked out, but the creature clung to him like a leech -- its legs wrapping around his hips, its arms locked around his neck, its whole body constricting, contracting, trying to merge with him, to reach through him to the blade on his back, and its touch was cold, so cold, the cold of the grave and the deep water, the cold of a body that had forgotten what warmth was and could only remember the shape of it, the location of it, the place on the back where the blade rested, where the gold was warm, where the dark *sakti* pulsed like a second heart.

"Get off him!" Bagus roared. "*Mulah!*"

Bagus threw himself onto the creature's back with the full weight of his body -- a weight that was not great, for Bagus was a Tuyul of the highlands, small and compact, built for the sawah rather than the battlefield -- but it was solid, and it was driven by a force more powerful than muscle, the force of love pushed past patience into fury, the rage of a gentle soul confronted with something that threatened the person it existed to protect. Bagus wrapped his thick arm around the creature's neck -- his left arm, the stronger one, the arm he used to turn the soil and haul the water and carry the baskets of vegetables from the garden to the kitchen -- and hauled backward.

The creature hissed. A sound like steam escaping from a crack in the earth, like a serpent in the undergrowth, like something that has been pressed and compressed and heated past the point of containment and is now venting its fury in a sustained, high-pitched exhalation of rage. Its limbs flailed -- the long arms windmilling, the clawed fingers raking the air inches from Bagus's face -- but Bagus held on, his arm locked around the creature's throat, his legs braced against a root, his jaw set in the expression that Kian Santang knew best: the immovable, unreasonable, absolutely non-negotiable determination of a friend who has decided that this thing will not happen, not today, not ever, not while he has an arm to hold and a voice to shout and the stubborn, inextinguishable will to stand between the darkness and the light.

The creature was strong. Impossibly, terrifyingly strong, given its wasted, hollow, half-decomposed body -- strong with the strength of desperation, the strength of obsession, the strength of a need so old and so total that it had become the creature's only fuel, the engine that drove the ruined machinery of its body through the centuries, through the mud, through the darkness, in perpetual, exhausted, unending pursuit of the thing it had lost. Bagus felt the strength in the thrashing limbs, felt the sinews like steel cables beneath the ruined skin, felt the creature's body vibrate with a force that was not muscular but *spiritual*, the dark *sakti* of the keris's corruption flowing through its veins like a toxic current, animating the dead tissue, overriding the body's natural surrender.

"Stop!" Bagus shouted, tightening his grip, pressing his forearm into the creature's windpipe with a force that would have collapsed a normal throat. "Stop or *Aing* will snap your neck!"

The creature went limp. The transition was instantaneous -- from full, frantic resistance to complete, boneless collapse, like a puppet whose strings have been cut, like a creature that has practiced surrender so many times that it can do it without transition, without the gradual deflation of resistance that a normal body undergoes, without the stages of exhaustion and acceptance that mark the arc of a normal fight. It simply *stopped*. It collapsed into the mud, wheezing, its large eyes rolling in its head, its thin chest heaving with rapid, shallow breaths.

"Aksama... aksama... ampun! *Gollum... gollum...*" it whimpered. The words were barely audible, a wet whisper, a prayer spoken into the mud. "Mulah paehan... mulah paehan Si Burik! *Gollum...*" -- *Forgive... forgive... mercy! Do not kill... do not kill Si Burik!*

The creature pressed its face into the black mud, its body curling into a foetal position, its long arms wrapped around its head in a posture of total, abject, annihilating submission. It was the posture of a thing that had been beaten so many times, by so many forces -- by the keris, by the centuries, by the loneliness and the darkness and the slow erosion of everything that had once made it a person -- that submission had become its default state, the resting position to which it returned when all other options were exhausted.

Kian Santang scrambled to his feet, wiping the black slime from his face. The taste of the swamp was in his mouth -- bitter, mineral, organic, a taste that was both ancient and alive, the taste of time itself decomposing. He drew his short blade, his hand shaking. Not the keris -- never the keris, not here, not now, not with the blade's hunger already awakened by the creature's presence and the dead marshes and the long, grinding hours of walking through the dark. The short blade. The *bedog*, the machete, the tool of the farmer and the trail-cutter, the honest, uncorrupted steel.

He looked down at the creature -- at the pathetic, shivering heap of bones and skin and scales and slime that lay in the mud at his feet, whimpering, weeping, its milky eyes leaking a fluid that was not tears but something thicker, more viscous, something that left trails on the grey skin like the tracks of slugs on stone.

And he felt a shock of recognition that went through him like an ice pick driven into the base of the skull.

## D. Capture, Backstory, and Recognition

White.

White as the creature's eyes, white as the fungi on the bark, white as the shock that drained the colour from Kian Santang's face and left him standing in the black mud with his blade raised and his hand shaking and the world tilting on its axis like a *gasing* -- a spinning top -- that has reached the end of its momentum and is beginning to wobble, to list, to fall.

"Si Burik?" Kian Santang asked, and his voice trembled -- not with fear this time, not with the keris's hunger or the swamp's oppression, but with something older and deeper, the tremor of recognition, the vibration that runs through a body when it encounters something that the mind rejects but the blood confirms. "Is that... your name?"

The creature looked up. Its milky eyes fixed on Kian Santang's face with a searching, assessing intensity that was disconcertingly intelligent -- the intelligence of an animal, perhaps, or the intelligence of a madman, the remnant of a mind that had been brilliant once and was brilliant still but whose brilliance now served only one purpose, one obsession, one need. The eyes moved across Kian Santang's features the way a scholar's eyes move across a manuscript, reading, interpreting, looking for the passage that confirms the thesis.

"Burik... Burik... yes..." The creature's voice was different now -- softer, more considered, almost reflective, as though the act of being named had awakened something in it, some buried stratum of selfhood that the centuries of corruption had covered but not entirely erased. It uncurled slightly, raising its head from the mud, and the movement had the tentative, fragile quality of a flower opening -- or of a wound. "Si Burik found the amulet... Si Burik carried the amulet... to the cave... but the amulet was stolen! Stolen by the nasty Tuyul! Stolen by Kian Perkasa!"

Kian Santang froze. The name -- his uncle's name -- detonated in his chest like a volcanic bomb, scattering the fragments of his composure in every direction. Kian Perkasa. His uncle. His father's brother. The quiet, gentle Tuyul who had lived alone in the hills above Padalarang, who had kept to himself, who had been regarded by the village as eccentric but harmless, a hermit with odd habits and a tendency to mutter to himself and a collection of old stones and bones and artefacts that he kept in a locked room and showed to no one. Kian Perkasa, who had found the keris in the Gua Sewu -- who had brought it home and kept it hidden and been consumed by it, slowly, over years, until the day he had given it to his nephew and died, released at last from the burden that had eaten him alive.

"Kian Perkasa," he breathed. "My uncle."

"Uncle!" Si Burik spat the word like poison, and the transformation was instantaneous -- the soft, reflective quality vanishing, replaced by a venomous, hissing fury that contorted the creature's features into something that was barely recognizable as a face. "Thief! He took it! He took my precious! My birthday present!"

*Birthday present.* The phrase was so incongruent, so impossibly domestic, so perfectly *Tuyul*, that Kian Santang felt his throat constrict. Tuyul celebrated birthdays with great enthusiasm -- with feasts of *ketupat* and *rendang* and sticky rice cakes, with the exchange of small, carefully chosen gifts, with the singing of old songs in the *basa loma* of the village. A birthday present. The keris -- the Keris Murka, the instrument of the Shadow King's will, the blade that had destroyed kingdoms and corrupted souls and sent a dragon of fire against the fellowship in the deep dark of the mountain -- had been, to this creature, a birthday present. Something found and treasured and held close, the way a child holds a favourite toy, the way a lonely heart holds a letter, the way the desperate hold the one thing that makes the darkness bearable.

"He found it in the cave," Kian Santang said, the pieces of the puzzle falling into place with a terrifying click -- the sound of tumblers in a lock, the sound of a mechanism completing its design, the sound of a story that had been scattered across centuries and continents coming together in a single, sickening moment of coherence. "You... you lost it there. And he found it."

"Lost?" Si Burik wailed, and the wail was the loneliest sound that Kian Santang had ever heard -- lonelier than the wind on the obsidian ridges, lonelier than the silence of the dead marshes, lonelier than the empty space beside a grave. The creature rocked back and forth in the mud, its arms wrapped around itself, its body a parenthesis of grief. "Not lost! Hiding! Resting! Si Burik was resting in the cave, in the dark, in the nice cool dark, and Si Burik put the *jimat* down -- just for a moment! Just for a rest! And then... and then..." The rocking intensified, the creature's whole body convulsing with the rhythm of an ancient, rehearsed, endlessly repeated agony. "Gone! Gone! And Si Burik has wandered... wandered in the dark... seeking... seeking..."

The creature's voice trailed off into a keening, a sound without words, a sound that occupied the space between speech and music and grief, and in that keening Kian Santang heard the echo of every lost thing that had ever been sought, every door that had been knocked on in the dark, every voice that had called a name into the emptiness and heard only the emptiness call back.

"How long?" Kian Santang asked. His voice was barely a whisper. "How long have you been looking?"

Si Burik's rocking slowed. The milky eyes fixed on a point somewhere beyond Kian Santang, beyond the swamp, beyond the present -- a point in the deep past where the creature's story had its origin, its first cause, the moment when everything had begun to go wrong.

"Long..." Si Burik said. The word was not a measurement but a texture -- the texture of centuries, the feel of time experienced not as a sequence of events but as a weight, as a pressure, as a force that accumulated and compressed and transformed, the way the weight of stone transforms organic matter into coal and coal into diamond and diamond into the hardest, most impenetrable substance in the natural world. "Long and long and long. Si Burik does not count the days. The days count Si Burik. They count Si Burik's fingers and Si Burik's teeth and Si Burik's hairs, and each day they take one more, and one day there will be nothing left to count."

Kian Santang looked at Bagus. Bagus was staring at the creature with a mixture of horror and revulsion and -- somewhere beneath both, visible only to someone who knew his face as well as Kian Santang did -- a dim, reluctant, deeply uncomfortable pity.

"He's a Tuyul," Kian Santang said quietly. The words fell into the swamp's silence like stones into dark water, sinking immediately, leaving only ripples. "Bagus... look at him. He was like us. Once."

Bagus looked. He looked at Si Burik's hands -- those terrible, ruined hands with their too-long fingers and their blackened nails and their webbing of translucent skin that stretched between the fingers like the membrane of a bat's wing. But the hands had five fingers. Five. Not six, not four, not the three-fingered claws of the Buta Raksasa or the seven-jointed appendages of the Leak. Five fingers, arranged in the familiar pattern of a Tuyul hand -- thumb, forefinger, middle, ring, little -- though each finger was distorted, elongated, the joints swollen and calcified, the knuckles protruding like knots in a vine. Bagus looked at the ears. Beneath the scales and the slime, beneath the grey crust and the fungal growths, the ears were pointed. Small. Set high on the skull. The ears of a Tuyul.

"*Karuhun...*" Bagus whispered. The word was an invocation, a prayer, a curse. Ancestors. He invoked them not as witnesses but as accusers -- as the embodiment of everything that had been done to this creature, everything that had been taken from it, everything that the keris had consumed in its centuries-long feast on Si Burik's flesh and soul. "The keris did this."

He looked at Kian Santang. His eyes were wide, and in them Kian Santang saw not just horror but *understanding* -- the sudden, terrible, unwanted understanding of what the keris was, what the keris did, what the keris would do to its current bearer if it were given enough time.

"It ate him," Bagus whispered. "It kept him alive. And it ate him."

"It kept him alive," Kian Santang said. His voice was steady, but the steadiness was a mask, a veneer of calm laid over a chaos of emotion so intense that it threatened to crack him open the way the volcanic heat had cracked the basalt on the plain. "Beyond his time. Beyond his natural span. It fed on him and preserved him and twisted him, and it kept him alive because a dead host is no use to a parasite, and a consumed vessel is no use to the thing that lives inside it."

He looked at the creature, and he saw his own reflection in a dark mirror.

Not a metaphor. A literal reflection -- his own features, distorted and degraded and stretched across centuries of corruption, but still *his*, still recognizable, still bearing the marks of the species and the culture and the world that had produced them both. This was the end of the road. This was the destination that the keris was carrying him toward with every pulse, every whisper, every offer of relief. This hunched, wretched, keening thing, half-submerged in the mud of a swamp at the edge of the world, its body a ruin and its mind a maze and its soul a fading spark in a hurricane of darkness -- this was what waited for the bearer who did not let go.

This was what he would become.

The knowledge settled over Kian Santang like a second skin -- cold, tight, inescapable. He had known, in theory, that the keris was destroying him. Eyang Guru had told him. The signs were there -- the trembling hands, the hunger, the whispers, the growing difficulty of distinguishing his own thoughts from the blade's suggestions. But theory is not knowledge. Theory is the map; knowledge is the territory. And the territory, the actual, physical, undeniable territory of the keris's destruction, was crouching in the mud before him, rocking and keening and weeping its milky tears, and it had a name, and the name was Si Burik, and Si Burik had once been a Tuyul of the highlands, a child of the green earth, a person with a birthday and a home and a life that was, in all the ways that mattered, indistinguishable from Kian Santang's own.

The *samsara* wheel turned, and the *samsara* wheel was supposed to be the great leveller, the great recycler, the mechanism by which all things were broken down and remade and given another chance. Death was not an end; it was a transition, a turning, a passage from one form of existence to another. The *karuhun* taught this. The temples carved it into their reliefs. The Dalang spoke it in the wayang tales. But the keris had broken the wheel for Si Burik. It had jammed the mechanism, had held the creature in the space between turnings, between lives, between deaths, in a perpetual state of suspension that was neither life nor death nor anything in between but only endurance -- raw, purposeless, unrelieved endurance, the continuation of consciousness without the consolation of meaning.

This was what the Shadow King's *sakti* did. Not merely corruption. Not merely destruction. *Interruption.* The interruption of the natural cycle, the jamming of the wheel, the trapping of souls in the liminal space where they could neither advance nor retreat, neither die nor be reborn, neither suffer the full weight of their *karma* nor be released from it. It was a cruelty more profound than simple destruction, because destruction at least had the decency to be final. The keris did not destroy. It *preserved*. It preserved suffering. It preserved need. It preserved the ghost of the person within the ruin of the body and forced that ghost to watch as its home was dismantled, piece by piece, tooth by tooth, hair by hair, across centuries that had no end.

"We should kill him," Bagus said.

His voice was hard. Harder than Kian Santang had ever heard it -- harder than the *kujang*'s edge, harder than the basalt under their feet, harder than the decision itself, which was, for Bagus, the hardest thing he had ever contemplated, because Bagus was a gardener, and gardeners do not kill, do not uproot, do not destroy what grows, but tend and nurture and protect, and the hardness in his voice was the hardness of a man who has been pushed past his nature into a territory where his nature cannot help him, where the rules of the garden do not apply and the only rule is survival.

"He's dangerous. He'll betray us the moment we turn our backs. He'll lead us into a trap, or wait until we sleep and take the keris, or signal the Buta Raksasa, or -- " Bagus stopped. Drew a breath. The hardness wavered, and beneath it, Kian Santang saw what it had cost him -- the effort of being hard, the violence of a gentle soul forced to contemplate violence. "He's too far gone, *Priya*. There's nothing left in there to save. The keris ate him. And what's left is not a person. It's... it's an appetite. A hunger in the shape of a person."

It was a reasonable argument. A *correct* argument, perhaps. The tactician in Kian Santang -- the small, cold voice of strategic assessment that the journey had developed in him the way a callus develops over a blister, through repetition and pain -- agreed with Bagus. Si Burik was a risk. Si Burik was unpredictable, obsessive, driven by a need that superseded loyalty and reason and self-preservation. Si Burik would, given the opportunity, take the keris and disappear into the swamp and spend the next five hundred years crouching in a cave, speaking to the blade in the dark, while the Shadow King's power grew and the world burned. This was not speculation. This was *certainty*, the mathematical certainty of a creature whose entire being had been reduced to a single variable: proximity to the *jimat*.

And yet.

"No! *Gollum! Gollum!*" Si Burik shrieked, cowering in the mud, his long arms over his head, his body convulsing with a terror that was absolute, animal, beyond the reach of reason or dignity. "No kill! Si Burik helps! Si Burik knows the way! Knows the paths! *Gollum...* The master wants to go to the Fire, yes? To the Big Fire?"

The words tumbled out in a rush -- a desperate, frantic outpouring of usefulness, the way a condemned man might recite his skills to a judge, each word a plea, each phrase a bargain, the currency of survival exchanged at a rate of desperation.

"Yes! Yes! *Gollum... gollum!*" Si Burik nodded frantically, the head bobbing on the spindly neck like a lantern in a wind. "Si Burik has been there! Si Burik knows the secret stairs! The dark tunnels! *Gollum...* The Buta do not see Si Burik. Si Burik is small. Si Burik is quiet. Si Burik knows the ways that the big ones do not know, the cracks in the stone, the paths under the mountain, the passages that go down, down, down to the fire! *Gollum...*"

"He's lying," Bagus warned. His grip on his *kujang* had not relaxed. His eyes had not left the creature's face. He stood between Si Burik and Kian Santang with the automatic, unconscious positioning of a bodyguard -- a posture he had adopted without deciding to adopt it, a stance that his body assumed the way a compass needle assumes north, by nature, by design, by the fundamental orientation of his being.

"I don't think so," Kian Santang said.

He lowered his blade. The movement was slow, deliberate, the conscious unclenching of a fist, and it cost him -- not because he wanted to kill Si Burik but because lowering the blade meant making a choice, and the choice was not between mercy and justice or between compassion and pragmatism but between two versions of himself: the version that could kill a helpless creature and the version that could not, and neither version was wrong, and neither was right, and the choice between them would define not just this moment but every moment that followed, every step of the path that led to the Mountain of Fire.

He looked into the creature's pale eyes. He saw the hunger there, yes. The obsession. The single-minded, all-consuming need for the *jimat* that had become the creature's only reason for existing. But he also saw fear. Fear of death, fear of the dark, fear of the loneliness that had been its only companion for centuries. And beneath the fear -- so deep that it might have been an illusion, a trick of the fungi's light, a projection of Kian Santang's own desperate need to find something worth saving in this ruin -- he saw a desperate, pathetic, heartbreaking loneliness. The loneliness of a creature that had lost everything -- its name, its face, its species, its place in the world -- and had only the memory of loss to prove that it had ever possessed anything at all.

He thought of Kian Perkasa. His uncle. The quiet man in the hills who had found the keris and been consumed by it and had made, in the end, the choice to give it away rather than let it consume him completely. Kian Perkasa had shown Si Burik mercy -- had taken the keris but had not killed the creature that bore it. Had he known? Had he understood, as Kian Santang now understood, that the creature was a mirror, a warning, a prophecy? Had he looked into Si Burik's milky eyes and seen his own future and been afraid? And had the mercy been genuine mercy -- the compassion of a soul that could not bring itself to destroy another soul, however corrupted -- or had it been something else, something more selfish, something closer to self-preservation? Had Kian Perkasa spared Si Burik not because killing was wrong but because killing the creature would have meant acknowledging what he himself was becoming, and that acknowledgment would have been more destructive than the keris itself?

The questions turned in Kian Santang's mind like the *samsara* wheel, like the patterns in the keris's *pamor*, like the slow, geological cycles of the volcanic earth beneath his feet. Questions without answers. Choices without clarity. The territory of the moral life, where the map is always incomplete and the compass always uncertain and the only reliable guide is the thing that trembles in the chest when the blade is raised and the helpless thing cowers in the mud.

"He wants the keris," Kian Santang said. "He wants to be near it. He will lead us to the fire because that is where the keris is going. His need is our compass."

"His need is our *danger*," Bagus countered. "A compass that wants to steal itself from your hand is not a compass. It's a trap."

"Perhaps," Kian Santang said. "But we have no other guide. We have no map. We have no knowledge of the paths between here and the caldera. We are two Tuyul of the highlands, lost in a swamp at the edge of a volcanic wasteland, and we are trying to walk into the heart of the Shadow King's domain without being seen, without being captured, without being killed. We cannot do it alone, Bagus. We have been trying, and we are failing. We are lost. We are running out of food. And the keris is --"

He stopped. The sentence hung in the air, unfinished, and the unfinished part was louder than anything he could have said, because the unfinished part was the truth: *the keris is winning*. The blade was gaining ground. Every day, every hour, every step through the ash and the mud and the dark, the boundary between Kian Santang and the Keris Murka grew thinner, more porous, more permeable, and the whispers grew louder, and the hunger grew sharper, and the moments of clarity -- the moments when Kian Santang could say with certainty *this thought is mine, this desire is mine, this fear is mine and not the blade's* -- grew fewer and further apart.

He needed a guide. He needed a path. He needed *speed*, because time was the keris's ally, and delay was the keris's weapon, and every day that Kian Santang spent wandering in this wasteland was a day the blade used to dig deeper, to spread further, to convert more of his soul's territory from his own dominion to its own.

"We have to trust him," Kian Santang said. "Not because he is trustworthy. Because we have no other choice."

## E. Oath-Binding and Departure

Gold.

Gold as the keris's hilt, gold as the light of the *geni suci* that Eyang Guru had wielded on the bridge, gold as the colour of the *jimat* that Si Burik had carried and lost and mourned across the centuries. Gold: the colour of power and corruption and the thin, fragile, luminous line between the two.

Kian Santang stepped closer to Si Burik. The creature flinched -- a full-body recoil, every muscle contracting, the spine curling tighter, the arms wrapping more closely around the head, as though it expected a blow. The flinch was reflexive, automatic, the response of a creature that had learned through long and bitter experience that approach meant violence, that proximity meant pain, that the closing of distance between itself and another being was invariably the prelude to being struck, or kicked, or driven away.

"Si Burik," Kian Santang said.

He spoke in a voice that was not entirely his own. The words came from his mouth, shaped by his lips and tongue and teeth, propelled by the air from his lungs -- but the *tone*, the register, the quality of authority that the words carried, these were not his. They belonged to the keris. Not the keris's corruption, not the dark whisper that promised relief and oblivion, but something else -- something older and more ambiguous, a residue of the *sakti* that had been poured into the blade at its forging, the power of command, the power to bind and compel and hold, the power of a thing that had been made to rule and that communicated its rulership through the bearer, using the bearer's voice as a channel, the bearer's words as a medium, the bearer's authority as a vessel for its own, vaster, more terrible authority.

It was the command of the keris-bearer. Not the command of Kian Santang the Tuyul, the homesick boy from Padalarang who wanted nothing more than to put this blade down and go home. It was the command of the *Bearer* -- the role, the function, the archetypal position in the cosmic drama that had been playing out since the Shadow King first forged the blade from the fire of Krakatau and poured his malice into the *pamor* and set it loose in the world to corrupt and consume and return, always return, to the hand that made it. Kian Santang felt the authority flow through him and was simultaneously empowered and diminished by it -- empowered because the authority was real, it carried weight, it had the power to bind and compel; diminished because the authority was not *his*, it was borrowed, it was the keris's gift, and every gift from the keris came with a price, and the price was always a piece of the bearer's self, a fragment of autonomy surrendered, a boundary erased, a wall breached in the fortress of identity.

"You will guide us, Si Burik." The voice was calm. Measured. It did not threaten. It did not plead. It stated, with the unarguable certainty of a natural law, what would happen next. "You will take us to the Black Gate. You will show us the paths through the wasteland, the tunnels under the mountain, the secret stairs that the Buta do not know. And you will not touch the *jimat*."

He paused. The swamp was silent. The insects had stopped their humming. The water was still. Even the sub-bass vibration of the earth seemed to have quieted, as though the world itself were listening, were holding its breath, were waiting for the words that would bind a broken creature to a doomed quest with the chains of an oath that could not be broken because the power that forged those chains was the same power that had broken the creature in the first place.

"Do you swear it?"

Si Burik trembled. The trembling began in the creature's extremities -- the long fingers, the bare toes, the pointed tips of the ruined ears -- and spread inward, like a wave converging on a centre, until the creature's whole body was vibrating with a frequency that was not fear, not exactly, but something more complex: a mixture of fear and desire and the agonized, impossible tension between the two. Fear of the oath. Desire for the *jimat*. Fear of the binding. Desire for the proximity. Fear of the master. Desire for the blade.

Si Burik looked at the wrapped blade on Kian Santang's back. The milky eyes fixed on the batik wrapping with an intensity that was almost physical, a gaze so concentrated that it seemed to exert pressure, to push against the fabric, to reach through the cloth and the layers of protection and touch the gold beneath. The creature's lipless mouth worked, the remnant teeth grinding against each other with a sound like chalk on slate, and the tongue -- long, thin, grey, furred with the same algal growth that colonized the rest of its body -- emerged and licked the cracked lips in a gesture of hunger so naked, so unguarded, so absolutely devoid of shame or pretence that it was more revealing than any confession, any testimony, any exposure of the soul.

The hunger was everything. The hunger was Si Burik's entire being, concentrated into a single, involuntary gesture. The licking of the lips said: *I want. I want with a wanting that has consumed everything else I was and left only the wanting itself. I want with a wanting that has outlasted my name and my face and my teeth and my humanity. I want, and the wanting is all I am, and I will do anything -- swear anything, serve anything, endure anything -- to be near the thing I want.*

"Swear... yes..." Si Burik's voice was a whisper, barely audible, spoken not to Kian Santang but to the blade, to the wrapped shape on the Tuyul's back, to the golden hilt that the creature could sense through the cloth the way a needle senses north through stone. "Swear by the *jimat*..."

"Swear by the *jimat*," Kian Santang repeated. And as he spoke the words, the keris responded.

It was not a movement. The blade did not shift in its wrapping, did not unsheathe itself, did not perform any of the overt, dramatic manifestations that the wayang tales attributed to enchanted weapons. It was subtler than that. Warmer. The keris generated heat -- not the aggressive, threatening heat of its anger but a different kind, a deep, steady, pulsing warmth that spread outward from the blade through the batik wrapping and into Kian Santang's back and from his back into the air around him, filling the space between the three of them -- the bearer, the servant, the gardener -- with a warmth that was almost pleasant, almost comforting, almost like the warmth of a hearth fire on a cold night, the warmth that says *home* and *safety* and *rest*.

Almost. But not quite. Because beneath the warmth there was a pulse -- a rhythmic throb, like a heartbeat, like the ticking of a clock, like the turning of a great wheel -- and the pulse was not comfort but *acknowledgment*. The keris was listening. The keris was *present*. It heard the oath. It witnessed the binding. And it accepted the terms -- not because the terms served Kian Santang's purposes but because the terms served *its* purposes, because the oath brought Si Burik into the keris's orbit, into its sphere of influence, into the circle of souls that the blade could touch and taste and feed upon, and one more soul in the circle was one more source of sustenance, one more tributary feeding the dark river of its power.

The warmth pulsed. The oath was heard.

"Si Burik swears!" the creature hissed, pressing his face into the mud. The prostration was total -- forehead, nose, chin, all pressed into the black earth, the body flattened, the limbs extended, the posture of a worshipper before an altar, of a supplicant before a king, of a creature before the thing that had created it and destroyed it and that it loved with a love more absolute and more annihilating than any human passion. "Si Burik serves the master of the precious! We go... we go to the Mountain of Fire!"

The words were not spoken to Kian Santang. They were spoken to the blade. The oath was not given to the bearer but to the *jimat*, and the distinction was important, and terrible, because it meant that Si Burik's loyalty -- such as it was, such as it could be, given the ruin of the creature's moral architecture -- was not to Kian Santang but to the keris itself, and the keris's interests and Kian Santang's interests were not the same, had never been the same, and would diverge more sharply with every step toward the Mountain of Fire, and when they diverged, Si Burik would follow the blade, not the bearer.

Kian Santang knew this. He saw the oath for what it was: a chain forged from need and desire, a binding whose strength depended entirely on the proximity of the thing that Si Burik needed, a leash that would hold only as long as the *jimat* was present and that would snap the instant the *jimat* was removed. It was not trust. It was not loyalty. It was the dark mathematics of addiction -- the addict's contract with the dealer, the prisoner's contract with the warden, the drowning man's contract with the hand that holds him under.

But it would serve. For now. For the distance between this swamp and the Mountain of Fire, it would serve. And that distance was all that mattered, because beyond that distance, beyond the fire, there was nothing -- no future, no plan, no vision of what came after -- only the fire itself, the forge, the crucible, the place where the keris had been made and where the keris would be unmade, and if Kian Santang survived the unmaking, he would deal with what came next then, and if he did not survive, the question would be moot.

"Let him up," Kian Santang said.

Bagus looked at him. A long look, heavy with all the things that Bagus could not say and would not say and did not need to say because the look said them all: *I do not trust this creature. I do not believe this oath. I think you are making a mistake, and the mistake will cost us, and the cost may be everything. But you are my friend, and I trust you, and if this is your decision, I will follow it, not because I agree but because following you is what I do, is what I have always done, is the axis around which my life turns, and I will not abandon that axis even when it points into the dark.*

Bagus released his grip. He stepped back. His hand remained on his *kujang*, and his eyes remained on Si Burik, and the vigilance in those eyes was not the temporary, aroused vigilance of a man responding to a threat but the permanent, structural vigilance of a man who has accepted that the threat will not end, that it has become a feature of the landscape, a constant, and who has adjusted his entire being -- his posture, his attention, his sleep, his waking, every moment of every day -- to accommodate it.

He would watch Si Burik. He would watch the creature with the tireless, unblinking attention of a gardener watching a weed that has been allowed to remain in the garden for tactical reasons, and at the first sign of treachery -- the first reaching hand, the first furtive glance, the first step toward the wrapped blade on Kian Santang's back -- Bagus would act. Without hesitation. Without mercy. Without the moral ambiguity that had stayed Kian Santang's blade. Because Bagus was not the bearer. Bagus did not carry the keris's burden or hear the keris's whisper or feel the keris's pull. Bagus was free -- free to love and free to kill and free to make the cold, hard, necessary decisions that the bearer could not make, because the bearer was compromised, and they both knew it.

Si Burik scrambled away. The creature moved in a scuttling, crab-like motion -- sideways, keeping its face toward Kian Santang, keeping the milky eyes on the wrapped blade, never turning its back on the *jimat*, never breaking the line of sight between itself and the thing it had sworn to serve. It reached a safe distance -- four paces, five -- and crouched there, rubbing its neck where Bagus's arm had pressed, the grey skin marked with a red welt that was already fading as the creature's unnatural vitality -- the dark *sakti* of the keris's residual influence -- healed the damage with a speed that was wrong, that was too fast, that spoke of a body that no longer healed in the natural way but was *repaired*, maintained, kept in functional condition the way a tool is maintained, not for the tool's sake but for the user's.

Si Burik grinned. The grin was a terrible thing -- a rictus of broken gums and remaining teeth and the dark, swollen flesh of a mouth that had forgotten the mechanics of smiling and was attempting to reconstruct the expression from memory, like a musician trying to play a melody he heard once, decades ago, in a language he has since forgotten. The grin was both pathetic and cunning -- pathetic because it was a ruin's attempt at a human expression, cunning because behind the ruin, behind the pathos, behind the milky eyes and the shaking limbs, a mind was working. A damaged mind, a degraded mind, a mind that had been through the fire of the keris's corruption and come out the other side reduced and distorted and *changed* -- but a mind nonetheless, and a cunning one, and a mind that was already calculating, already assessing, already playing the long game that had kept it alive through centuries of darkness and loneliness and the unending, excruciating proximity of its own ruined selfhood.

"We go now?" Si Burik asked. The voice was different again -- lighter, almost cheerful, the voice of a creature that has gotten what it wanted and is willing, for the moment, to be pleasant about it. "Nice master. Good master. Si Burik knows the way. Si Burik will show the way. Come, come. The path goes north. Through the mud. Through the roots. Si Burik knows where the mud is firm and where the mud is deep. Si Burik has walked these paths many, many times. In the dark. In the wet. Looking. Always looking."

The creature turned and began to move -- that fluid, spider-like crawl, four points of contact, the body low, the head up, the milky eyes scanning the path ahead with a competence that was, despite everything, impressive. It moved through the root system with the easy familiarity of a creature in its native environment, choosing paths that were invisible to the Tuyul, finding firm ground where the mud looked deepest, navigating the maze of roots and pools and standing water with an instinct that was half intelligence and half animal knowledge, the deep, bodily understanding of a terrain that had been home for so long that the body itself had become an extension of it.

"We go," Kian Santang said.

He walked past the creature, his head high, though his heart was cold. Cold as the mud. Cold as the sulphuric pools. Cold as the space between the stars where the *karuhun* said the gods lived, in palaces of ice and silence, looking down on the turning world with eyes that saw everything and intervened in nothing.

He was leading them into the dark. Leading them with a monster for a guide, and a cursed blade for a compass, and a gardener for a bodyguard, and the only light he had was the terrible, burning fire on his back -- the fire that did not illuminate but consumed, that did not warm but corrupted, that promised salvation and delivered damnation and would not, could not, would never stop until it reached the forge that had made it and was unmade in its turn, and the wheel turned, and the cycle completed, and the *samsara* of the Shadow King's greatest weapon came, at last, to its end.

Behind him, Bagus walked. Silent. Watchful. His hand on his *kujang*, his eyes on Si Burik, his heart full of a love so fierce and so uncomplicated and so absolutely, stubbornly, unreasonably *present* that it shone in the darkness of the swamp like a single candle in a cathedral -- not bright enough to dispel the shadows, not strong enough to push back the night, but bright enough to see by. Bright enough to take the next step. And the next. And the next.

Between them -- behind Kian Santang, ahead of Bagus, occupying the space that had once been empty and was now filled with the scuttling, sniffing, muttering presence of the creature that the keris had broken -- Si Burik crawled. The creature moved with a pathetic, eager energy, its body low, its head bobbing, its lipless mouth murmuring a constant, half-audible stream of words that were part navigation and part conversation and part the ceaseless, compulsive self-narration of a mind that had been alone for so long that it had learned to be its own audience, its own companion, its own interlocutor in a dialogue that had neither beginning nor end.

"This way, this way... nice path... firm mud here... Si Burik knows... Si Burik always knows... the master follows Si Burik, yes... the master is kind... the master lets Si Burik stay near the precious... near the beautiful *jimat kancana*... so warm... so warm and golden and good..."

The murmuring was both reassuring and disturbing -- reassuring because it confirmed that Si Burik was, for the moment, cooperative; disturbing because the content of the murmur was the creature's inner life laid bare, the running commentary of an obsession so total that it had replaced thought itself, so that every perception, every sensation, every experience was filtered through the lens of the *jimat* and assessed according to a single criterion: *does this bring me closer to the precious or further from it?*

They moved north. The swamp thickened around them, the roots growing denser, the canopy lower, the mud deeper. The bioluminescent fungi multiplied, their cold light painting the passage in shades of blue and green and the occasional, startling flash of purple -- a rare fungus that Kian Santang had never seen, that clung to the underside of a root like a bruise, pulsing with a light that was almost black, almost absent, as though the fungus were not producing light but consuming it, drawing the surrounding illumination into itself and converting it into something darker.

Si Burik led them through passages that would have been invisible without the creature's guidance -- narrow gaps between roots, submerged paths beneath the surface of the standing pools, tunnels through the undergrowth that required them to crawl on their hands and knees, their faces inches from the mud, the stench of decomposition filling their nostrils with every breath. The creature moved through these passages with an ease that spoke of intimate familiarity, and its constant murmuring took on a navigational quality -- "Left here, left... the right goes to the deep pool... Si Burik fell in once... cold, cold water... and things in the water... things with teeth... left is safe... Si Burik knows..."

Kian Santang followed. He did not look back. He did not look at the keris. He looked at the path -- at the mud and the roots and the cold light and the small, hunched, scuttling shape of the creature that was leading them deeper into the darkness, and he thought about *samsara*.

The wheel turned. This was the fundamental truth of the *karuhun*'s teaching -- the truth that the temples proclaimed and the Dalang enacted and the sages repeated in the firelit circles of the highland villages. The wheel turned, and all things that lived were upon it, rising and falling in the eternal cycle of birth and death and rebirth, each life a revolution, each death a turning, each birth a new beginning in a story that had no beginning and no end. The wheel was justice. The wheel was mercy. The wheel was the mechanism by which *karma* -- the accumulated weight of all actions, all choices, all moments of compassion and cruelty and courage and cowardice -- was distributed and balanced and resolved, across lifetimes, across ages, across the vast, patient mathematics of cosmic time.

Si Burik was on the wheel. Had been on the wheel. Had been pulled *off* the wheel by the keris's dark *sakti* and suspended in the space between turnings, where the wheel's justice could not reach and the wheel's mercy could not comfort, and the only law was the law of endurance, and endurance was not a virtue but a curse.

Was this Kian Santang's *karma*? To encounter Si Burik, to see his own future in the creature's ruin, to be forced to choose between mercy and survival, between the teachings of the *karuhun* and the demands of the mission? Was the wheel -- the turning, tireless, infinitely patient wheel -- presenting him with this test not as punishment but as *teaching*, the way the Dalang presents the wayang tales not as entertainment but as instruction, as *moral education*, as the dramatization of choices that every soul must face, sooner or later, in one life or another?

He did not know. He could not know. The wheel did not explain itself. The wheel turned, and the turnings were inscrutable, and the only response available to the souls upon it was to act -- to choose, to decide, to move through the moral landscape with whatever light they had, however dim, however flickering, however close to extinction.

Kian Santang had chosen mercy. He had chosen to spare Si Burik, to bind the creature with an oath, to use its knowledge and its hunger as a compass in the dark. The choice might save them. The choice might destroy them. The wheel would reveal which, in its own time, at its own turning.

But as he walked -- through the mud and the roots and the cold, spectral light of the fungi, with the keris on his back and the monster at his feet and the gardener at his back and the fire ahead and the darkness on every side -- he knew one thing with a certainty that went beyond hope and fear, beyond the keris's whisper and the swamp's oppression, beyond the *samsara* cycle and the *karma* ledger and the inscrutable, turning, tireless wheel.

He knew that Si Burik, the broken thing, the ruin, the wreck, the creature that the keris had eaten and preserved and twisted beyond recognition -- Si Burik, the cautionary tale, the dark mirror, the prophecy in flesh -- was not merely his guide.

Si Burik was his debt. The debt that the bearer owed to every soul that the blade had consumed, every life that the Shadow King's malice had interrupted, every revolution of the wheel that the dark *sakti* had prevented. Si Burik was the face of that debt -- specific, individual, undeniable -- and the bearing of the debt was part of the bearing of the blade, and neither could be laid down without laying down the other, and both would be carried to the fire or not at all.

The swamp closed around them. The canopy sealed overhead. The light of the sky -- the last thin thread of connection to the world above, to the sun and the stars and the open air -- vanished behind the interlocking branches, and they walked on in the darkness, the three of them, the bearer and the gardener and the broken thing, moving north, moving toward the fire, moving deeper into the shadow's country with every step.

And the keris, warm and patient on Kian Santang's back, said nothing.

It did not need to speak. It had already won this round. Not the war -- the war would be decided at the fire, at the forge, at the place of unmaking where the Shadow King's *sakti* had given the blade its terrible life and where that life would be taken back. But this round -- this small, local, tactical engagement in the long campaign -- the keris had won. It had brought Si Burik into the circle. It had added another soul to its orbit. It had extended its reach, deepened its influence, strengthened the web of need and desire and corruption that it wove around its bearer the way a spider weaves its web around its prey -- strand by strand, layer by layer, with a patience that was not patience but program, not strategy but nature.

The keris had won this round. And it was patient. And the fire was far away.

*"But this is only one thread of the story, and the loom holds many. Now, cast your eyes to the east -- to the ancient trees of Wanantara, where the earth remembers what the axe forgets. *"Wanda anyar deui. Layar diputer. Carita tuluy lumaku."*

*"Another act. The screen turns. The story walks on."*
