# Bab 19: Godaan Gajahmada
## (The Temptation of Gajahmada)

---

The islands of the Sunda Strait were the bones of a dead world.

They rose from water the color of rust -- small, jagged, treeless humps of black volcanic rock arranged in a loose chain across the strait like the vertebrae of some vast creature that had crawled from the deep and died there, its spine arching above the surface, its flesh long since dissolved into the poisoned sea. The air above them shimmered with heat that had nothing to do with the sun, which hung behind a permanent veil of ash-grey haze, its disc reduced to a pale smear of light no brighter than a tallow candle seen through muslin. The light it gave was thin, exhausted, the light of a world from which color had been slowly drained.

And at the end of the chain, Krakatau.

The mountain filled the southern horizon like a wall. It was enormous -- not in the way that Gunung Merapi had been enormous, a peak among peaks, part of a landscape. Krakatau stood alone, rising from the sea in a single brutal mass of black stone, its flanks scarred with ancient lava flows that had hardened into ridges and channels of vitreous rock, gleaming dully in the haze like the scales of a serpent. Its summit was lost. A column of dark smoke, dense and slow-moving, rose from the caldera in a pillar that broadened as it climbed, spreading outward into a canopy that occluded the sky for leagues in every direction, turning day into a perpetual dusk. Lightning flickered within the smoke -- red flashes, irregular, silent at this distance, like the pulse of a diseased heart.

The water around the islands was warm. Not pleasantly warm, not the warmth of tropical shallows where coral grows and fish glitter in the sunlight, but an uncomfortable, mineral-laden heat, the temperature of bathwater that has sat too long, carrying with it a faint, persistent stench of sulfur and something else -- something organic, something rotting, as though the sea itself were ill. Bubbles rose in clusters from vents in the shallow seabed, breaking the surface with soft gaseous pops that released wisps of vapor smelling of rotten eggs and hot metal. The water was discolored -- yellowish near the vents, rust-brown in the shallows, and in places a deep, unsettling red, as though blood were seeping up from the ocean floor.

Nothing lived here. No birds circled above the islands. No fish broke the surface of the poisoned water. No insects hummed in the stagnant air. The only vegetation was dead -- skeletal trees, bleached white as bone, their branches clawing at the haze like the fingers of buried men reaching for a sky they could no longer see. Between the dead trees, the rock was bare and sharp, covered in a fine layer of grey ash that coated everything with a funeral dust, soft underfoot, tasting of chalk and fire.

This was Raja Iblis's domain. The edge of it. The threshold.

And the Keris Murka knew it was home.

It had not stopped vibrating since they crossed the strait. The dark hum that Kian Santang had felt in his bones for weeks -- the constant, low-frequency resonance of the blade against its wrappings, against its bamboo case, against the sacred batik that Eyang Guru had drawn with his own hands in another age -- had intensified as they approached the volcanic islands until it was no longer a hum but a drone, a sustained note of power that rattled Kian Santang's teeth and blurred his vision and pressed against the inside of his skull like a fist. The batik still contained it -- the sacred patterns held, the mantras of the resi priests woven into the indigo and turmeric dye pushed back against the blade's dark emanation -- but the containment was failing. He could feel it. The way one feels a dam beginning to crack: not the catastrophic breach, not yet, but the small seepages, the hairline fractures, the slow, inevitable yielding of one force to another.

The keris whispered to him constantly now. Not in words -- it had moved beyond words, beyond the seductive murmurings of power and empire that had characterized its earlier attempts at corruption. Now it communicated in images, in feelings, in raw bursts of sensation that erupted behind his eyes without warning: the throne of black stone in Krakatau's caldera, empty, waiting; the crown of volcanic fire that would make its wearer lord of all Nusantara; the ecstasy of absolute power, the pleasure of domination so pure it was indistinguishable from pain. And beneath it all, louder than the promises, louder than the visions, a single imperative that the blade drove into his mind like a nail driven into wood:

*Come home. Bring me home.*

Kian Santang walked, and did not fall, and did not yield. But the effort of not yielding had carved him hollow. He had lost weight that he could not afford to lose -- his ribs were visible beneath the obsidian mail that Tumenggung Batu had given him, his face was gaunt, his eyes sunken in dark hollows that aged him by decades. He ate only when Bagus pressed food into his hands and watched until he swallowed. He slept only when exhaustion dragged him under, and even then the keris followed him into his dreams, turning them black, filling them with fire.

He was, he knew, being consumed. Not quickly. Not dramatically. But steadily, the way a candle is consumed -- the flame eating the wax, the wax feeding the flame, the whole diminishing by degrees until there was nothing left to burn. The path of adharma -- the unrighteous way, the way of the keris's hunger -- pulled at him with every step, and only the thin thread of dharma, the righteous duty laid upon him by Eyang Guru, kept his feet on the true road.

---

Raden Wijaya called the halt on the largest of the chain islands, a flat expanse of black rock perhaps two hundred paces across, distinguished from its neighbors only by the remnants of a stone jetty that jutted from its southern shore -- evidence that someone, long ago, had used this place as a waypoint for the crossing to Krakatau. The jetty was broken, its stones tumbled into the warm water, but the flat area behind it offered enough shelter among the larger rock formations for the fellowship to rest.

They had been traveling for three days since crossing the strait, hopping from island to island in the outrigger that Nyai Roro Kidul's sea spirits had provided -- a narrow, swift craft of dark teak that the waves seemed to part for, as though the ocean remembered the Sea Queen's authority even here, at the edge of the Shadow King's domain. The crossing had been harrowing. The water grew rougher and hotter as they moved south. The haze thickened. Twice, Dewi Anjani had raised her hand and the company had fallen silent, pressing themselves flat in the hull, as shapes moved in the murk above them -- dark, winged, circling -- Leak scouts, perhaps, or worse things, patrolling the approaches to their master's stronghold.

They had not been seen. Or so they hoped.

"We rest here," Raden Wijaya said. His voice was flat with fatigue -- even the prince's extraordinary endurance had been tested by the strait crossing, and his dark skin was grey with ash dust, his lean face drawn tight. He looked south, toward Krakatau, and his eyes narrowed with the calculating attention of a general surveying a battlefield. "We need to scout the approach. There may be landing points on the northern shore, but we need to know where the Buta patrols are thickest and where the terrain offers cover."

He turned to Dewi Anjani. "Will you come with me?"

The Bidadari inclined her head. She alone among the company seemed undiminished by the poisoned landscape -- her luminous skin remained pale and clear, her golden eyes undimmed, though even she had grown quieter as they approached Krakatau, as though the spiritual corruption of the place pressed against her Bidadari senses with a weight the others could not perceive.

"The rest of you -- stay here. Keep low. No fires. We return before the light fails." Raden Wijaya took up the Keris Nagasasra and checked the blade -- a habitual gesture, a warrior's reflex -- and the naga-patterned steel caught what little light filtered through the haze and threw it back in a cold, serpentine gleam. "Gajahmada. You have command until we return."

The general nodded. His face was composed, martial, the face of a man accepting orders with the discipline of long service. But his eyes -- his eyes flicked, for just an instant, toward Kian Santang, toward the bamboo case on the young Tuyul's back, and in that instant something moved behind them that was not discipline and not composure.

Raden Wijaya saw it. His gaze lingered on Gajahmada for a heartbeat longer than necessary, and a crease appeared between his brows -- a question he did not ask, a concern he filed away for later. Then he turned, and he and Dewi Anjani moved south across the black rock, and within minutes the haze had swallowed them, and the fellowship was six.

---

The hours crawled.

Without the prince and the Bidadari, the camp felt exposed -- a small huddle of exhausted bodies on a shelf of volcanic rock in the middle of a poisoned sea, overlooked by the vast, smoking bulk of a mountain that wanted them dead. Tumenggung Batu positioned himself at the southern edge of the camp, his war hammer across his knees, his small dark eyes scanning the murk with the grim patience of a sentinel who expected trouble and intended to meet it with blunt force. Cepot and Dawala sat together in the lee of a large boulder, speaking in low voices -- Cepot's wit muted, for once, by the oppressive atmosphere, his jokes coming slower and with less conviction, as though even his irrepressible spirit could feel the weight of the place pressing down upon it.

Bagus stayed close to Kian Santang. He always stayed close to Kian Santang. The loyal Tuyul had positioned himself between the keris bearer and the rest of the camp, not obviously, not ostentatiously, but with the quiet deliberation of a man who had identified the point of greatest vulnerability and placed himself at it. He sharpened his golok with slow, methodical strokes of a whetstone, the sound rhythmic and grounding -- *shik, shik, shik* -- the most ordinary sound in the world, an anchor of normalcy in a landscape that had abandoned all pretense of the normal.

And Gajahmada paced.

He walked the perimeter of the camp in long, restless circuits, his hand on his keris, his stride betraying an agitation that his face worked hard to conceal. His eyes moved constantly -- scanning the horizon, checking the approaches, performing the habitual duties of a commander securing a position. But they always came back. Always, inevitably, inexorably, they came back to the bamboo case on Kian Santang's back.

The Keris Murka was singing to him.

It had been singing to him since Dieng, but Gajahmada had not recognized the song for what it was. He had mistaken it for his own thoughts -- his own ambition, his own concern for Majapahit, his own burning, consuming desire to protect his people. The keris was clever. It did not announce itself. It did not arrive as an alien presence, a foreign voice in his head. It came disguised as *him* -- as his own deepest convictions, his own most noble impulses, amplified and distorted until they became something else, something that wore the mask of virtue but moved with the logic of corruption.

He thought of Si Pahit Lidah, the man whose bitter tongue could make any word reality -- and understood now that oaths were not merely promises but weapons, that the power to bind others with words was the most dangerous sorcery of all. His Sumpah Palapa had been such a word: a binding, a shaping of the world through the force of will. And now the keris was turning that binding against him, twisting his own sworn purpose into a door through which the darkness walked.

*You are the strongest here,* it whispered, and the whisper sounded like Gajahmada's own inner voice, the voice of the strategist, the tactician, the man who had sworn the Sumpah Palapa and meant every word. *The boy is failing. Look at him -- skin and bone, barely able to stand. He will not make it to the mountain. The keris will fall into the enemy's hands, or worse, it will be lost in these barren rocks, and everything -- the quest, the fellowship, the hope of Nusantara -- will die here on this wretched island because a child was given a burden meant for a warrior.*

*You could save them. You could save them all. You have the strength. You have the will. Take the keris. Carry it to Krakatau yourself. Not to wield it -- no, never that -- only to bear it, to protect it, to ensure that it reaches the fire. The boy cannot. You can. Is that not what a leader does? Is that not what a general does? He takes the burden that the weak cannot carry.*

The voice was so reasonable. So measured. So perfectly calibrated to the frequencies of Gajahmada's own moral architecture that he could not tell where his thoughts ended and the keris's influence began. It spoke to him in the language of duty, of sacrifice, of love for his people, and every word was true, and every word was a lie, and the difference between truth and lie had become a line so thin that a man could cross it without knowing he had moved. This was the keris's deepest art: the art of karma twisted, of a man's own accumulated deeds and desires turned into chains. Every righteous act Gajahmada had performed, every sacrifice he had made for Majapahit, the keris now presented as evidence that he deserved the blade -- that his karma demanded it.

He paced. He watched. The hours passed. The keris sang.

---

Kian Santang went to find water.

There was a spring -- if it could be called that -- near the eastern edge of the island, where a crack in the volcanic rock released a thin trickle of liquid that was not quite water and not quite anything else. It was warm, slightly sulfurous, faintly yellow, and it tasted of minerals and old stone. But it was wet, and they needed it. The provisions from the mainland were running low. Bagus had rationed the clean water carefully, but even his careful management could not stretch finite supplies indefinitely, and the poisoned sea that surrounded them offered nothing drinkable.

Kian Santang knelt beside the spring with a waterskin, tilting the mouth of the skin beneath the trickle, waiting for the slow drip to fill it. The keris hummed against his back. The ash-grey haze pressed down. The silence of the dead island was absolute -- no wind, no waves, no living sound. Only the faint drip of the spring and the distant, almost subliminal rumble of Krakatau, a sound felt more than heard, the mountain's voice vibrating through the rock beneath his knees like the growl of a vast and sleeping beast.

He did not hear Gajahmada approach.

The general moved quietly for so large a man -- a skill learned in the forests of eastern Jawa, where the Gajah Mada regiment had hunted rebels and bandits through terrain that punished noise. He came up the slope of black rock with careful steps, his boots finding the solid surfaces, avoiding the loose scree, and when he spoke, his voice was gentle.

"Kian."

Kian Santang looked up. Gajahmada stood above him, silhouetted against the haze, his broad frame blocking what little light reached through the volcanic murk. His face was in shadow. His hands were at his sides, open, unthreatening. His posture was that of a man approaching a frightened animal -- slow, careful, deliberately nonthreatening.

"You should not wander alone," Gajahmada said. He descended the slope and crouched beside the spring, and his proximity was a physical thing, the warmth and mass of a large body displacing the dead air. He picked up a loose stone, turned it in his fingers, set it down. "Not here. Not this close to the mountain."

"I am only getting water."

"I know." Gajahmada was quiet for a moment. He looked at the trickle of sulfurous liquid filling the waterskin with a slowness that seemed to mock their urgency. Then he looked at Kian Santang, and his eyes were dark and earnest and full of something that was almost tenderness.

"You are exhausted, Kian. Do not deny it -- I can see it. We all can. The keris is draining you. Every day you carry it, it takes more. Your weight. Your sleep. Your strength." He paused. "Soon it will take your will. And then it will take everything."

Kian Santang said nothing. The truth of the words was a blade sharper than any keris.

"Let me carry it."

The words fell between them like stones dropped into still water. Gajahmada's voice was calm. Reasonable. The voice of a senior officer presenting a tactical assessment to a subordinate who lacked the experience to see what he saw.

"Only to carry," Gajahmada continued. "Not to wield. I would never draw it. You know that. I swore the Sumpah Suci at the lingga -- the same oath you swore. I am bound. But the oath does not say that *you* must carry the blade. Only that *we* must see it destroyed. Let me bear the weight. I am stronger. My body can endure what yours cannot. I am a warrior, Kian. I was built for burdens."

"Gajahmada." Kian Santang's voice was quiet but steady, steadier than his trembling hands, steadier than the hammering of his heart. "The keris was given to me. Eyang Guru chose me to carry it. Not because I am strong, but because --"

"Because you are *weak*!" The word burst from Gajahmada before he could catch it, and he heard it, and flinched, and tried to pull it back. "No. No, I did not mean -- forgive me. I meant only that -- Kian, please." His voice cracked. The composure fractured. Beneath it, something raw and desperate surged up like magma through a fissure, and his eyes -- his dark, intelligent, tormented eyes -- blazed with a light that was not entirely his own.

"My people are *dying*."

He rose to his feet. His hands were no longer at his sides. They were clenched, the knuckles white, the tendons standing out like cords of rope. His breathing had quickened. The words came faster now, tumbling over each other, propelled by a force that had been building for weeks, for months, since the council at Dieng where they had decided to destroy the very weapon that Gajahmada knew -- *knew*, with every fiber of his warrior's heart -- could save Majapahit.

"While we crawl across these cursed rocks, the Buta are marching on Trowulan. My soldiers are fighting and dying without their general. The court is in chaos. The kingdom I swore to protect is *burning*, and I am HERE, on this dead island, watching a boy -- a *child* -- stagger under a weight that would break a man twice his size, and I am supposed to do NOTHING?"

He stepped closer. Kian Santang scrambled backward, his feet slipping on the wet rock, the waterskin forgotten, its contents spilling into the ash.

"With the keris, I could end this war TODAY." Gajahmada's voice rose, filling the dead air, echoing off the black rock. "Not in darkness -- in GLORY. My Sumpah Palapa -- do you know what that means? I swore to unite all of Nusantara. Every island, every kingdom, every people -- one nation, one purpose, one will. I have fought for that dream my entire life. I have bled for it. I have buried friends for it. And now the power to achieve it -- the power to make it REAL -- is right HERE, and you want me to watch it be thrown into a volcano?"

He was close now. Too close. Kian Santang could see the veins in his temples, the flare of his nostrils, the way his pupils had dilated until his dark irises were nearly swallowed by black. And in that black -- in the depth of his eyes, in the furthest recesses of his gaze -- something *flickered*. A red light. Faint, brief, like the reflection of a distant fire in deep water. The keris's influence, made visible.

"Give it to me, Kian."

"No."

"GIVE IT TO ME!"

Gajahmada lunged.

It was not a warrior's attack -- not precise, not calculated, not the disciplined violence of the Majapahit general who had won a dozen campaigns with tactical brilliance. It was a desperate, graceless grab, the lunge of a man in the grip of a compulsion stronger than his training, stronger than his honor, stronger than the oath he had sworn on the sacred lingga in the temple of the ancient candi. His hand closed on the strap of the bamboo case. He pulled.

The Keris Murka *blazed*.

Dark fire erupted from the bamboo case -- not flame but its opposite, a darkness that burned, a black radiance that consumed light rather than producing it, that poured upward from the wrapped blade in a column of shadow-fire that lit nothing and illuminated everything, throwing the two figures into terrible relief against the volcanic haze. The dark fire wrapped around Gajahmada's hand, his arm, climbed his shoulder like a living vine of shadow, and where it touched him his skin darkened and his eyes -- his eyes went red. Both of them. The full, deep, glowing crimson of heated iron, of volcanic eruption, of the fires at the heart of Krakatau where the keris had been born.

For one instant -- one eternal, annihilating instant -- Gajahmada was no longer Gajahmada. He was a vessel. A host. The keris poured into him, filled him, showed him everything it could give: the throne, the crown, the armies kneeling, the archipelago united under one will, his will, the will of the greatest general Nusantara had ever known, wielding the greatest weapon Nusantara had ever forged, and the world -- the whole shining, terrified, submissive world -- laid at his feet like an offering.

Kian Santang screamed.

He tore the bamboo case from his back, tore it from Gajahmada's grasping hand, and in his terror -- in the blind, animal panic of prey cornered by a predator it cannot fight -- he did the one thing he had been warned never to do.

He drew the keris.

The blade came free of its wrappings and its sheath in a single motion, and the Keris Murka appeared in his hand -- slender, impossibly beautiful, its seven luk curves gleaming with the oily iridescence of its pamor, each fold of metal containing a darkness that was deeper than the darkness around it. It was warm. It was alive. It screamed with power -- a sound beyond sound, a vibration that passed through bone and blood and thought, that shook the rock beneath their feet and sent cracks spiderwebbing across the volcanic stone.

And Kian Santang vanished.

---

One moment he was there -- a small, terrified Tuyul standing on black rock with a keris blazing in his fist. The next, he was gone. The keris's power wrapped around him like a cloak of absolute darkness, bending light, bending perception, removing him from the visible world as completely as if he had never existed. To mortal eyes, the space where Kian Santang had stood was empty. Only a faint distortion in the air -- a shimmer, like heat haze, visible only if one knew exactly where to look -- betrayed the presence of something unseen.

Gajahmada staggered.

The dark fire died the instant Kian Santang vanished, snuffing out like a candle in a gale, and the red light in Gajahmada's eyes went out with it, and the visions -- the throne, the crown, the kneeling armies -- shattered like glass struck by a hammer, and what was left was a man. Only a man. A man standing on dead rock with his hand outstretched toward nothing, his fingers grasping empty air, and the expression on his face -- the expression that replaced the red-eyed hunger of the keris's possession -- was the most terrible thing Kian Santang had ever seen on a human face.

It was comprehension.

Gajahmada understood what he had done. The knowledge struck him with physical force -- he doubled over as though punched in the stomach, his hands dropping to his knees, a sound escaping his throat that was not a word and not a cry but something between them, the sound of a man watching his own honor die in front of him.

"Kian." His voice was a rasp. "KIAN!"

He spun, searching the empty air, reaching for what was no longer there. His hands found nothing. The ash beneath his feet showed no footprints -- the keris's invisibility was total, perfect, a power designed by the Shadow King to make his instrument unfindable by any enemy.

"What have I done?" The words came in a whisper. Gajahmada looked at his own hands -- the hands that had reached for the keris, the hands that had tried to take by force what had been entrusted to the smallest and most vulnerable among them -- and the disgust that crossed his face was absolute. He folded. His knees struck the rock. His head bowed. "What have I *done*?"

Running footsteps. Bagus arrived at the spring, golok drawn, his broad face tight with alarm. He had heard the scream. He had felt the pulse of dark energy. He saw Gajahmada on his knees in the ash, and he saw the empty space where Kian Santang should have been, and he understood -- not everything, not yet, but enough. Enough to know that something had broken.

"Where is he?" Bagus's voice was quiet. Quiet and cold and stripped of every warmth that ordinarily lived there, stripped down to the bare iron of a man who had one purpose in life and that purpose had just been violated. "*Dimana siya?*" he repeated in the basa loma of his anger, the familiar Sundanese erupting from him before he could think. "Gajahmada. *Where is he*?"

Gajahmada did not look up. "I tried to take it." The words were flat, dead, the confession of a man who had passed beyond shame into a territory where shame was insufficient. "The keris. I tried to take it. He drew the blade and -- vanished."

Cepot and Dawala arrived, breathless, their silver daggers gleaming in their hands. Behind them came Tumenggung Batu, his war hammer raised, his stone-grey face set in a mask of battle readiness that crumbled into confusion when he saw no enemy -- only Gajahmada kneeling in the ash and Bagus standing over him with murder in his eyes.

"Where is the bearer?" Tumenggung Batu demanded, his gravelled voice sharp.

"Gone," Bagus said. He did not sheathe his golok. "The general tried to take the keris. Kian drew the blade and disappeared."

The silence that followed was worse than any accusation. Cepot's hand tightened on his dagger. Dawala looked from face to face with wide, uncomprehending eyes. Tumenggung Batu's hammer came up to his shoulder, and his small dark eyes locked on Gajahmada with a stare that could have cracked stone.

"You swore an *oath*," the Kurcaci chieftain said. Each word was a granite block laid in a wall of condemnation. "On the lingga. On the sacred stone. You *swore*."

"I know what I swore." Gajahmada raised his head. His face was ashen, his eyes bloodshot, the red light entirely gone, replaced by something worse -- the absolute, unsparing clarity of a man who has seen his own capacity for betrayal and cannot look away from it. "The keris... it showed me... it used my oath. My Sumpah Palapa. It turned my own purpose against me. It made my love for Majapahit into a door, and it walked through."

"That is not an excuse," Bagus said.

"No." Gajahmada met his gaze. "It is not."

---

Invisible, Kian Santang ran.

He did not know where he was running. The keris was unsheathed in his hand and the world was transformed -- the volcanic landscape, already alien, had become something else entirely, a place of layered realities, as though the keris's power had peeled back the visible surface of the world and revealed the spiritual architecture beneath. He could see the dark energy that permeated the islands, flowing through the rock in veins of shadow like the circulatory system of a vast and malevolent organism. He could see the corruption radiating from Krakatau -- waves of it, pulsing outward in concentric rings, each pulse a heartbeat of the thing that dwelled within the mountain. The air was thick with presences he could feel but not identify, spiritual entities drawn to the keris's unveiled power like sharks drawn to blood in water.

He ran across black rock, leaping crevices, scrambling over ridges, his bare feet finding purchase on surfaces that should have been too sharp, too hot, too unstable to traverse at speed. The keris guided him -- or controlled him; the distinction had collapsed. It pulled him south, always south, toward the mountain, toward the fire, toward the place it called home with a yearning so intense it was indistinguishable from agony.

And then the eye found him.

It came without warning. One moment he was running through the dead landscape, his mind a chaos of terror and instinct, and the next -- a *presence* crashed into his awareness with the force of a tsunami striking a shore. It was vast. It was ancient. It was aware, with an awareness that had nothing to do with the limited, sequential consciousness of mortal minds -- this was an intelligence that perceived everything simultaneously, that existed in multiple dimensions at once, that had been waiting, searching, scanning the spiritual frequencies of Nusantara for the signature of its lost blade for centuries.

And it had found it.

**THERE you are, little bearer.**

The voice was not a voice. It was a geological event. It was the sound of a mountain splitting open, of an ocean draining into the earth, of the fundamental bedrock of reality being wrenched apart by a will that predated the concept of will. It filled Kian Santang's mind -- not entering through his ears but materializing inside his consciousness, occupying it, displacing his own thoughts the way a flood displaces air.

**Come to me.**

He could see it. Through the keris -- through the blade's connection to its maker -- he could *see* the Raja Iblis, and the sight was annihilation. Not a figure. Not a form. An *intensity* -- a concentration of malice and power so absolute that it had burned through the physical world and existed now as pure will, pure hunger, a darkness that was not the absence of light but the opposite of light, the thing that light was invented to resist. It sat at the center of Krakatau like a spider at the center of its web, and its attention -- the full, unbearable, crushing weight of its attention -- was focused on a single point.

On him.

On the keris in his hand.

**Bring my blade HOME.**

The word *home* was a command woven into the fabric of the keris itself, a summons encoded in the seven pamor patterns, in every fold of the metal, in the fundamental spiritual DNA of the weapon. The blade responded -- it *vibrated* in Kian Santang's hand with a frequency that shattered the rock beneath his feet, that sent cracks racing through the volcanic stone in a web of fractures, and the pull toward the south -- toward Krakatau, toward the fire, toward the throne of obsidian -- became a physical force, a tidal pull, a gravity that grabbed his body and dragged it forward.

Kian Santang screamed.

He screamed with his body and his mind and his spirit, screamed against the thing that was trying to swallow him, and in the scream he found a final reserve of will -- not strength, not power, but the simple, stubborn refusal of a small creature to be consumed by a large one, the refusal of a Tuyul rice farmer's nephew from Padalarang to kneel before the lord of all darkness. He seized the scream. He shaped it into an act. And he slammed the keris back into its sheath.

The connection severed.

The vision of Raja Iblis collapsed -- imploding inward, sucked back through the keris and into the blade's dormant state, the spiritual channels closing like wounds healing, the terrible awareness withdrawing with a howl of frustrated rage that shook the island to its foundations. Kian Santang felt the eye release him, felt the crushing weight of that impossible attention lift from his mind, felt his own consciousness flood back into the spaces it had been displaced from.

He was visible again. The invisibility died with the keris's sheathing. He stood -- no, he lay, he had collapsed without knowing it -- on a flat shelf of black rock behind a tumble of boulders on the island's eastern edge, curled around the sheathed keris like a child curled around a wound. His face was wet. He was weeping. His body shook with tremors that he could not control, the aftershocks of a spiritual violation so profound that his physical form could not process it.

The Shadow King knew. He knew where they were. He knew the keris was close. The element of surprise -- the fragile, precious advantage that had been their only shield -- was gone.

And it was gone because Gajahmada had reached for the keris, and Kian Santang had drawn it, and the act of drawing had been a beacon, a flare launched into the spiritual sky, visible to every dark power in Nusantara.

Kian Santang lay behind the rocks and wept, and the tears cut channels through the ash on his face, and Krakatau smoked above him, enormous, terrible, patient, and close. So close.

He sat up. His hands were still shaking but his mind was clearing, the way a sky clears after a storm, not into blue but into the grey, hard clarity of a landscape stripped of all illusion. He looked south, toward the mountain. He looked north, toward the camp, toward his friends.

He could not go back.

The thought arrived with the force of revelation, but it was not new -- it had been forming for days, weeks, growing in the silence beneath the keris's noise, in the spaces between the whispers. The keris had corrupted Gajahmada -- the strongest, the most disciplined, the most committed among them. If it could corrupt Gajahmada, it could corrupt anyone. Raden Wijaya, with his hunger for his stolen throne. Tumenggung Batu, with his fierce Kurcaci pride. Even Bagus, loyal Bagus, if the keris found the right lever, the right desire, the right vulnerability.

He could not risk it. He could not risk *them*.

He had to go to Krakatau alone.

Or -- almost alone.

Because even as the thought formed, even as the logic of solitary sacrifice crystallized in his mind, another thought arrived, quieter, warmer, carrying with it the memory of a steady voice and a steady hand and the smell of nasi timbel and dried fish and the patient *shik, shik, shik* of a golok being sharpened.

*Where Kian Santang goes, I go.*

Bagus. Bagus would never let him go alone. Bagus would follow him into the fire, not because of oaths or duty or the grand purposes of kingdoms, but because that was who Bagus was -- a man whose loyalty was not a principle but a reflex, as natural and as unshakeable as breathing.

Kian Santang looked toward the camp. He looked toward the mountain. He pressed his shaking hands against his face, against the tears and the ash, and he breathed, and he made his choice.

He would find Bagus. He would tell him the truth. And together -- just the two of them, a Tuyul and his friend -- they would go to Krakatau.

The others would have to find their own road. Their own purpose. And perhaps -- perhaps that was the keris's final lesson, the one truth hidden among all its lies: that this burden could not be shared. That the road to the fire was a road for small feet and small hearts, for creatures too humble to be corrupted and too stubborn to be broken.

Kian Santang rose. He wrapped the keris in its batik, in its bamboo case, and slung it across his back. The weight was terrible. The whispers resumed.

He walked north, toward the camp, toward Bagus, toward the last conversation he would have with all of his friends together.

Behind him, Krakatau rumbled, and the dark smoke thickened, and the Shadow King's eye -- blinded by the keris's sheathing but not deceived, never deceived -- turned its attention to the chain of volcanic islands and began to summon its armies.

# Bab 20: Pertempuran di Ratu Boko
## (The Battle at Ratu Boko)

---

The horns sounded across the water like the voices of drowned gods.

They came from the south -- low, resonant, shaking the air with a vibration that was felt in the chest before it was heard by the ears. Three blasts. Long, sustained, each one deeper than the last, as though the instruments producing them were descending into some register below the range of human hearing, into frequencies that belonged to the earth itself, to tectonic movement, to the groaning of stone under unimaginable pressure. The sound rolled across the warm, discolored water of the strait and struck the chain of volcanic islands like a wave, and every living thing that heard it -- the six companions scattered across the black rock, the keris bearer hiding among the boulders, the invisible creatures of the deep water -- understood what it meant.

War had come to find them.

Tumenggung Batu was the first to see the boats. He stood at the southern edge of the camp, his war hammer raised to his shoulder, his stone-grey face turned toward the source of the horns, and his deep-set eyes -- Kurcaci eyes, designed for the lightless depths of the earth, preternaturally sharp in dim conditions -- picked out the shapes before anyone else could resolve them from the haze.

"Boats," he said. His voice was flat, stripped of everything but information. "Twenty. Perhaps more. Coming fast from the southwest."

Bagus was beside him in an instant, squinting into the murk. "What kind of boats?"

"The wrong kind."

They emerged from the volcanic haze like a nightmare materializing from smoke. Twenty dark vessels -- long, low-slung war galleys of black timber, their hulls sealed with pitch that gleamed wetly in the dim light, their prows carved into the snarling faces of demonic figures whose open mouths seemed to vomit the vessels forward through the water. They moved with unnatural speed, driven not by sail or oar but by some darker propulsion -- the water around their hulls roiled and steamed, and in their wake the sea turned black, as though the passage of each vessel poisoned the water it touched.

And standing in the boats, packed shoulder to shoulder in ranks of dark iron and brutal flesh, were the Buta Raksasa.

These were not the shambling, disorganized brutes that the fellowship had encountered in earlier skirmishes -- the Buta patrols of the highland passes, the raiders that harried the edges of settled lands. These were something else entirely. Empu Gandring's elite. Bred in the volcanic forges of Merapi, armored in black iron smelted from the ores of cursed earth, disciplined with a savagery that had hammered animal intelligence into something approaching military order. They stood eight feet tall, their bodies massive with slabs of muscle overlaid with dark, rough-textured skin the color of cooled lava. Their faces were broad and flat, dominated by heavy jaws lined with teeth like broken obsidian, their small eyes burning with a dull red light that was not intelligence but its terrible imitation -- obedience, hunger, the programmed fury of weapons made flesh.

They carried weapons that matched their scale -- massive cleaving blades of black iron, war-axes with heads the size of millstones, spears thick as saplings tipped with barbed points designed to punch through armor and anchor in flesh. Their armor was volcanic iron, shaped to their enormous frames in overlapping plates that covered chest and shoulder and thigh, leaving their arms bare to allow the full range of their devastating strength. The armor was crude but effective -- thick enough to turn a sword, dark enough to drink the light, decorated with nothing, because these creatures had no concept of decoration. They were instruments. Tools of annihilation. And they had been sent here with a single purpose.

At their head, standing in the prow of the lead vessel, was their captain.

He was enormous even by Buta Raksasa standards -- nine feet of armored malice, his body encased in a suit of spiked black iron that added another dimension of lethality to his already devastating frame. The spikes protruded from shoulder, elbow, knee, and knuckle, each one a weapon in itself, and the armor's surface was etched with patterns that glowed faintly red, as though the metal remembered the forge-fire that had shaped it. His face was a ruin of scars and fury, his jaw undershot, his eyes -- unlike the dull red of his troops -- burning with a brighter, more focused malevolence that suggested something closer to actual intelligence. He carried a spear -- a weapon the height of two men, its shaft of black wood, its head a cruel, tri-pointed thing of dark iron that looked less forged than grown, like the fang of some mineral predator.

He opened his mouth and roared, and the sound was the sound of an avalanche in a canyon, and the Buta Raksasa in the boats behind him took up the cry, and the water shook, and the volcanic haze trembled, and the war fleet surged toward the island chain.

---

"The ruins," Tumenggung Batu said. "On the next island. There are walls. Choke points. We cannot fight them on open ground."

Bagus saw it. Thirty paces across a shallow channel of warm water, the adjacent island -- larger than theirs, higher, with a flatter summit -- bore the remains of what must once have been a temple complex. Ancient stone walls, broken but still standing in places to the height of a man, formed a rough labyrinth of corridors and chambers across the island's upper surface. Carved blocks of andesite lay tumbled among the black volcanic rock, their surfaces worn by centuries of salt wind and volcanic ash but still bearing traces of relief sculpture -- the geometric patterns of a civilization that had built here long ago, when these islands were perhaps less dead, less poisoned, less close to the domain of the Shadow King.

Ratu Boko. The name surfaced in Kian Santang's memory from the lontar manuscripts he had read in Padalarang, a lifetime ago. A ruined palace-temple, said to have been built by a giant king, abandoned when the old wars began, left to the slow reclamation of wind and fire and time.

"Move," Bagus commanded, and in that instant the quiet farmer from Padalarang became something else -- a field commander, a man whose instinct for tactical necessity was as natural as his instinct for provisioning. "*Geura indit!*" he barked in Sundanese, the urgency beyond formality. "Cepot, Dawala -- across the channel, now. Tumenggung Batu -- hold the crossing until everyone is over. Gajahmada --"

He turned to the general, and his eyes were hard, but the hardness was not personal. It was the hardness of a man who needed every weapon at his disposal and could not afford the luxury of grudges.

"Fight," Bagus said. "Redeem yourself, or don't. But *fight*."

Gajahmada met his eyes. Something passed between them -- not forgiveness, not yet, but the recognition that forgiveness was a currency that could only be minted in battle, and the battle was here.

"I will fight," Gajahmada said. And he drew his keris -- his own keris, the Majapahit blade, the weapon of a tumenggung of the Gajah Mada regiment -- and the steel caught no light in the ashen air, but it gleamed nonetheless with the dull, honest gleam of a warrior's tool, and it was enough.

They ran.

The channel between the islands was shallow -- waist-deep for the Kurcaci, chest-deep for the Tuyul, thigh-deep for Gajahmada -- and the water was warm and foul, tasting of sulfur and rust, but they waded through it with the desperate speed of creatures fleeing a forest fire, and behind them the dark boats drew closer, and the horns sounded again, and the Buta Raksasa leaped from their vessels into the shallows with splashes that sent columns of discolored water ten feet into the air.

They reached the ruins of Ratu Boko as the first Buta Raksasa set foot on the island.

---

Before the first blow fell, Kian Santang pressed his palms together and whispered an invocation that Eyang Guru had taught him -- an ancient call to the Tri Murti, the three faces of the divine: "Sang Hyang Brahma, bringer of sacred fire -- purify this ground. Sang Hyang Wisnu, preserver of dharma -- shield those who walk the righteous path. Sang Hyang Siwa, destroyer of darkness -- let the adharma be unmade." The words were older than the ruins around them, older than the kingdoms of Jawa, and whether the gods heard them in this poisoned place Kian Santang could not know, but the speaking steadied his heart.

The battle erupted like a volcanic event -- sudden, total, devastating.

The Buta Raksasa came up the slopes of the island in a wave of dark iron and brute strength, their massive feet finding purchase on the black rock with a surety that belied their size, their weapons raised, their mouths open in the continuous roar that served as both battle cry and psychological weapon. Dozens of them. Fifty, sixty, more pouring from the boats in an unending stream, spreading across the island's lower slopes in a *Babah buhaya* -- a crocodile jaw formation -- that tightened as they climbed, driving the fellowship upward into the ruins.

Tumenggung Batu held the gap.

There was a passage between two standing walls of ancient stone -- a corridor perhaps six feet wide, the remains of what might once have been a processional approach to the temple's inner sanctum. The walls rose to twice the Kurcaci's height on either side, carved with faded reliefs of dancers and warriors, and the floor was fitted stone blocks that centuries of ash had not entirely buried. It was the only approach to the upper ruins from the south, and Tumenggung Batu planted himself in it like a boulder that had rolled into a ravine and intended to stay.

"Come, then," the Kurcaci chieftain growled, and his war hammer swung.

The first Buta Raksasa to enter the passage was eight feet of armored fury, carrying a cleaving blade that could have halved a horse. It charged the narrow gap with the confidence of a creature that had never met anything it could not overpower. Tumenggung Batu let it come. He waited -- patient as the stone he was named for, his deep-set eyes tracking the Buta's approach with a calm that bordered on contempt -- and at the last instant he moved.

The hammer struck the Buta's knee with a sound like a temple bell rung with a battering ram.

The creature's leg buckled. It pitched forward, its momentum carrying it into the wall, and Tumenggung Batu stepped inside its reach and brought the hammer up in a devastating rising arc that caught the Buta beneath the jaw. The impact lifted the eight-foot monster off its feet and sent it crashing backward into the Buta behind it, and both went down in a tangle of limbs and dark iron, and Tumenggung Batu stepped back to his position and waited for the next.

"Is that all?" he roared. "Is THIS what the Shadow King sends against a son of Gunung Sewu? Send me something WORTHY!"

The narrow passage forced the Buta to come at him one at a time, sometimes two abreast, and in that confined space the Kurcaci's compact power and low center of gravity were devastating advantages. He fought with the precise, economical brutality of a creature designed for close-quarters combat -- no wasted movement, no unnecessary flourish, every swing of the hammer a calculated exercise in applied force. The walls funneled his enemies into his killing zone. The fitted stone floor gave his planted feet absolute purchase. The hammer rose and fell and rose again, and each time it fell, something large and dark and armored crashed to the ground and did not get up.

Above him, on the ruined walls, Dewi Anjani arrived.

The Bidadari and Raden Wijaya had heard the horns from the southern islands and returned at a dead sprint, covering in minutes ground that had taken them an hour to cross on the outward scout. They arrived at the channel just as the last of the fellowship waded across, and Raden Wijaya had not broken stride -- he hit the water, crossed it in four enormous strides, gained the far shore, and charged up the slope toward the ruins with the Keris Nagasasra drawn and blazing in his hand, the naga-patterned blade throwing cold silver light across the volcanic haze.

Dewi Anjani did not cross the channel. She flew.

It was not flight as birds fly -- it was a leap, a surge, a moment of Bidadari grace that carried her from one island to the next in a single arc, her cloak streaming behind her, her bow already in her hand. She landed on the highest point of the ruined walls and nocked her first arrow before her feet had fully settled, and when she released, the Bidadari-blessed bamboo shaft crossed the space between wall and shore in a streak of luminous green and took a Buta Raksasa through the eye.

The creature fell. The arrow's light spread through its body like fire through dry grass, burning away the dark enchantment that animated it, and the Buta collapsed into a heap of dead flesh and dark iron that did not move again.

Dewi Anjani nocked another arrow. And another. And another.

She fired with a speed and accuracy that transcended the mechanical limitations of bow and arm and eye. Each arrow found its mark -- throat, eye, the gap between breastplate and shoulder guard where the dark skin was exposed. Each arrow carried the Bidadari's light, and each Buta it struck fell and did not rise. She moved along the wall like a spirit of war, repositioning between shots, her golden eyes blazing with a fierce, otherworldly concentration that transformed her from a figure of ethereal beauty into something older and more terrible -- a Bidadari warrior-princess of the ancient lineage, heir to the beings who had stood against the Shadow King at the dawn of the world and who remembered, in their immortal blood, how to fight the dark.

Raden Wijaya hit the Buta Raksasa flank like a typhoon making landfall.

The prince of Mataram fought with a fury that Kian Santang had never seen in him -- not the cold, controlled precision of his morning training sessions, but something rawer, something that drew on reserves of anger and grief and desperate determination that his composed exterior had always concealed. The Aliran Mataram style was designed for exactly this scenario -- a single warrior against multiple opponents, using speed and evasion and devastating counter-strikes to dismantle a numerically superior force.

Raden Wijaya was a hurricane in human form. The Keris Nagasasra cut through Buta Raksasa armor as though the dark iron were paper, the ancient blade's naga-pattern flaring with silver light each time it bit, the serpent-dragon in the steel coming alive with the joy of a weapon reunited with its purpose. Raden Wijaya flowed between the Buta like water through stones -- ducking beneath their massive swings, rolling under their guard, rising inside their reach to deliver strikes that opened throats and hamstrung legs and sent fountains of dark ichor spraying across the volcanic rock. His footwork was poetry. His blade work was butchery. Together, they were the most devastating display of pencak silat the world had seen since the old wars.

In the lower ruins, among tumbled stone blocks and broken walls, Cepot and Dawala fought back to back.

The two Tuyul had been cornered by a squad of Buta Raksasa -- three of the monsters, each one four times their height, closing in with the heavy-footed confidence of predators that had identified easy prey. The Buta were wrong. Cepot and Dawala drew their silver daggers -- the gifts from Nyai Roro Kidul, the Sea Queen's enchanted blades -- and the silver blazed with a cold, marine light that made the Buta flinch, their dark eyes narrowing against a radiance that struck at the spiritual foundation of their corrupted existence.

"You know," Cepot said conversationally, adjusting his grip on his dagger as the first Buta lunged, "I always imagined I would die in bed. Comfortable bed. Clean sheets. Perhaps a bowl of kolak on the nightstand."

He dodged the Buta's swing -- not with the formal elegance of trained silat but with the quick, instinctive agility of a small creature that had spent its life dodging things larger than itself -- and slashed upward with the silver dagger. The blade opened a line across the Buta's forearm and the creature *screamed*, a sound of genuine agony, because the Sea Queen's silver burned Buta flesh the way fire burns paper, the enchanted metal searing through dark hide and dark enchantment alike.

"Instead," Cepot continued, ducking under a second swing and driving his dagger into the Buta's thigh, "I am going to die on a volcanic island, fighting giants, with no kolak in sight. *Dasar nasib teh teu ngarti sopan santun!*" -- *Fate has no manners whatsoever!* -- the complaint delivered in basa loma, the everyday Sundanese of a Tuyul whose earthly wisdom expressed itself even in mortal combat.

Dawala, behind him, fought in silence. The young Tuyul had no quips, no commentary -- only a fierce, frightened determination that manifested as a whirlwind of small, precise strikes aimed at the Buta's legs, their hands, any body part that descended within his reach. His silver dagger left trails of cold light in the air, and every cut burned, and the Buta that faced him learned -- quickly, painfully -- that size was not the only factor in a fight, and that a small creature with an enchanted blade and nothing left to lose was a creature to be feared.

The three Buta fell, one by one, cut and burned and brought down to a level where even Tuyul could finish them.

Cepot wiped dark ichor from his face with the back of his hand. "We are going to need a very long bath after this."

"And food," Dawala said.

"Dawala, if we survive this, I will personally cook you the largest meal in the history of Nusantara."

---

Gajahmada fought alone.

He had broken away from the others when the battle began, not from cowardice or self-interest but from a purpose that burned in him brighter than the keris's corruption had ever burned -- a purpose that was entirely his own, uncorrupted, unmanipulated, forged not in the dark fires of Krakatau but in the clear, terrible light of a man who had betrayed his honor and wanted, more than he had ever wanted anything, to reclaim it.

He fought toward the eastern edge of the ruins, where Kian Santang had disappeared. The Buta Raksasa between him and his destination were obstacles, not opponents -- things to be removed, cleared away, disposed of with the efficient brutality of a man who had spent thirty years removing obstacles from the path of Majapahit's expansion.

His style was nothing like Raden Wijaya's elegant flow. It was direct, aggressive, overwhelming -- the pencak silat of the battlefield, designed not for duels but for the chaos of massed combat, for the press of bodies and the thunder of charging formations. He used his Majapahit keris in his right hand and a captured Buta cleaving blade in his left -- the massive weapon, too heavy for any ordinary man, wielded by the Majapahit general with a sakti, an inner power, that came not from enchantment but from decades of martial discipline and the absolute, crystalline focus of a man who had found, in the wreckage of his disgrace, a reason to fight that was purer than any he had known before.

He cut through the Buta like a ship through heavy seas -- plowing forward, always forward, the keris striking with surgical precision, the captured blade sweeping in wide arcs that cleared space around him, his body absorbing blows that would have felled lesser men and continuing to move, because stopping was not an option, because somewhere ahead a small Tuyul was alone and afraid, and the last thing that small Tuyul had seen from the man sworn to protect him was a monster with red eyes reaching for his burden.

*I will make it right,* Gajahmada thought, and the thought was a lance of light in the dark chaos of the battle, and he followed it forward.

---

The Buta Raksasa captain found Kian Santang first.

The keris bearer was hiding among the easternmost ruins -- a cluster of fallen pillars and shattered walls that formed a natural enclosure, open to the sky, carpeted with ash and fragments of carved stone. He had heard the horns. He had heard the battle. He had drawn his small sword -- the ordinary blade, not the Keris Murka, never the Keris Murka again -- and pressed himself into the deepest shadow of the ruined walls, making himself as small as a Tuyul could be, which was very small indeed.

But the Buta Raksasa did not need to see him. They could *feel* the keris. The blade pulsed through its wrappings with a dark resonance that called to the corruption in their own flesh, and they followed that call the way iron filings follow a lodestone, converging on the eastern ruins with a purpose that overrode the general chaos of the battle.

The captain came with a squad of five -- his largest, most brutal warriors, each one armored in the heaviest black iron, each one carrying weapons designed for the single task of overwhelming any defense and seizing the prize within. They entered the enclosure from three sides, their massive forms blocking the openings between the fallen pillars, and the captain's red eyes locked on the small figure pressed against the far wall with a keris-shaped bundle on his back, and his scarred face split in a grin that was all teeth and no humanity.

Kian Santang raised his sword.

The blade trembled in his hand. He was exhausted -- hollowed out by the keris's drain, shattered by the encounter with Raja Iblis's awareness, weakened by days of insufficient food and sleep. The sword felt like an iron bar in his grip. The Buta Raksasa captain was nine feet of spiked armor and killing intent. The math was not complicated.

He fought anyway.

He fought because there was nothing else to do, because surrender was not a concept that a Tuyul from Padalarang could parse, because Uncle Kian had told him he was braver than he believed and he was, in this moment, testing the absolute outer limit of that belief. He fought with the Cimande silat that Raden Wijaya had refined -- low stances, evasive movement, using his size as an advantage, making the Buta reach for him, making them overextend. He dodged the first spear thrust. He ducked beneath a cleaving blade. He drove his sword into the gap between a Buta's greave and boot and felt the blade bite, and the Buta staggered, and for one wild, improbable instant Kian Santang thought he might actually survive this.

Then the captain swatted him aside.

It was not even a proper attack -- a casual backhand from one enormous spiked gauntlet that caught Kian Santang across the shoulder and sent him tumbling across the ash-covered stone like a doll struck by a child. He hit the base of a fallen pillar and the breath exploded from his lungs and his sword flew from his grip and the Buta Raksasa closed in, their shadows falling across him like the shadow of a closing tomb.

"YOU SHALL NOT HAVE HIM."

Gajahmada erupted through the gap between two fallen pillars with the force of a siege engine.

He hit the nearest Buta Raksasa at full speed, driving his keris through the creature's throat in a strike of such precision and ferocity that the Buta was dead before it understood it was under attack. He tore the blade free, seized the falling creature's cleaving sword, and pivoted to face the remaining four and their captain with his feet planted between them and Kian Santang, and his eyes were clear, and his face was set, and the expression he wore was not the expression of a man fighting for redemption.

It was the expression of a man who had found it.

"Get up, Kian," he said, without turning. "Get behind me."

The Buta Raksasa captain studied the man before him. Its red eyes moved from Gajahmada's face to the Majapahit keris in his right hand, to the captured cleaving blade in his left, to the planted feet and the set jaw and the absolute refusal to move that radiated from every line of the general's body. It assessed the threat. It calculated the cost.

Then it attacked.

The duel that followed was the duel of an age.

The Buta Raksasa captain was the most dangerous single combatant the Shadow King's forces had produced -- a creature of immense physical power augmented by the dark enchantments of Empu Gandring's forges, fast for its size, armored against all but the most perfectly placed strikes, wielding its tri-pointed spear with a skill that transcended the brute capability of its kind. It fought with a controlled savagery that was almost beautiful in its efficiency -- the spear sweeping in arcs that covered the full width of the enclosure, thrusting in combinations that tested every angle of Gajahmada's defense, the spiked armor itself a weapon in close quarters.

Gajahmada was outmatched. He knew it. The knowledge did not slow him by a fraction of a heartbeat.

He fought with everything he had -- every technique of thirty years of martial training, every lesson of a hundred battles, every ounce of the sakti that flowed through his body like electricity through copper. His Majapahit keris was a serpent in his right hand, striking at the gaps in the captain's spiked armor -- throat, wrist, the inside of the elbow, the back of the knee. His captured cleaving blade was a shield in his left, parrying the captain's spear thrusts with jarring impacts that numbed his arm to the shoulder. He moved with a speed he had not possessed since his youth, as though the burden of his guilt and the fire of his redemption had burned away every limitation, every hesitation, every moment of the decades between the young warrior who had sworn the Sumpah Palapa and the aging general who was, at last, beginning to understand what that oath truly meant.

And as the *Pangkur* of moral determination pulsed in his heart -- that ancient pupuh form of resolve and struggle, its rhythm driving his limbs -- Gajahmada fought not for conquest but for the dharma he had nearly abandoned.

He killed two of the remaining four Buta with strikes so fast they seemed simultaneous -- keris through one throat, cleaving blade through another's face, a spinning transition between the kills that left both creatures falling as Gajahmada was already engaging the third. The third he brought down with a combination that used the Buta's own momentum against it -- a sidestep, a redirecting palm to the creature's elbow, and the keris driven upward through the gap beneath the breastplate into the heart.

But the fourth Buta drove a spear through Gajahmada's side.

The point punched through the gap between his ribs -- a gap that armor would have covered, but Gajahmada wore no armor, had never worn armor, had always trusted his speed and his skill to be his shield. The iron head entered beneath his left arm and exited through his back, and the pain was a white explosion that blanked his vision and buckled his knees.

He did not fall.

He locked his legs. He clenched his jaw until his teeth cracked. He seized the spear shaft with his left hand and held it -- held the weapon in his own body, preventing the Buta from withdrawing it for a second strike -- and with his right hand he drove his keris into the Buta's eye, and the creature died, and Gajahmada released the spear shaft and straightened and turned back to the captain, and the blood -- his blood, bright red against the black volcanic stone -- poured down his side in a steady stream.

The captain's eyes widened. Not with fear. With something that might, in a creature capable of such emotions, have been respect.

They clashed again. The captain's spear thrust for Gajahmada's throat. The general deflected it -- barely, the tri-pointed head scoring a deep furrow across his shoulder -- and countered with a strike at the captain's wrist that forced it to adjust its grip. The cleaving blade was gone, abandoned, too heavy now for his weakening left arm. He fought with the keris alone, the slender Majapahit blade against the captain's massive spear, and the disparity in reach and power was overwhelming.

The captain's spear butt caught Gajahmada across the ribs -- the wounded ribs -- and this time he did fall. One knee struck the stone. The pain was a living thing, a creature of fire and ice that sank its teeth into his consciousness and tried to drag him into the dark.

He rose.

The captain struck him again -- a glancing blow from the spear shaft that opened a cut above his eye and filled his vision with blood. Gajahmada wiped his eyes with his forearm and reset his stance, the keris raised, his weight on his back foot, the Majapahit guard position from which a thousand battles had been fought and won.

The captain drove its spear through his thigh.

Gajahmada fell to both knees. The pain was beyond pain now -- it had crossed some threshold into a territory where sensation becomes abstraction, where the body's screaming is reduced to information, relevant but not determinative. He was dying. He knew it. The wounds were mortal -- the one in his side, certainly; the others accelerating what the first had begun. His blood pooled on the black stone around him, dark as the rock itself, steaming faintly in the volcanic heat.

The captain loomed above him. It raised its spear for the killing blow, the tri-pointed head silhouetted against the haze-choked sky like a dark trident, and its scarred face was expressionless with the finality of a creature that had won and knew it.

Gajahmada looked up at the descending spear.

And he moved.

Not away. Forward. Into the blow. Under the spear's arc, inside the captain's reach, so close that the spiked armor pressed against his chest and the creature's foul breath was hot on his face. He drove his keris upward -- upward through the gap beneath the captain's jaw, through the soft tissue of the throat, through the root of the tongue, into the brain.

The Majapahit blade went in to the hilt.

The captain's eyes went wide. Its mouth opened. No sound came out. It swayed -- a mountain of dark iron and corrupted flesh, suddenly purposeless, suddenly empty -- and then it fell, backward, like a tower collapsing, and the impact of its armored body striking the stone shook the island to its roots.

Gajahmada's hand slipped from the hilt of his keris, buried in the captain's throat. He swayed on his knees. The world was dimming at its edges, collapsing inward toward a single point of light that was -- he realized, with a clarity that surprised him -- the face of a small Tuyul, tear-streaked and ash-covered, kneeling in front of him with shaking hands reaching out to catch him as he fell.

---

"No. No, no, no --"

Kian Santang caught Gajahmada as the general collapsed. The weight was enormous -- even diminished by wounds and blood loss, the Majapahit warrior was twice Kian Santang's size, and the young Tuyul staggered under him, lowering him to the ground with a care that bordered on reverence. Gajahmada's blood soaked into Kian Santang's clothes, hot and wet, and the general's face -- grey now, drained, the dark skin turned ashen -- tilted upward, and his eyes found Kian Santang's face, and they were clear.

Clear. No red light. No dark fire. No corruption, no madness, no hunger for power. Only the eyes of a man -- a tired, wounded, dying man -- looking at someone he had wronged with an expression of such unguarded sorrow and such absolute peace that Kian Santang felt something inside his own chest crack and give way.

"Forgive me." Gajahmada's voice was a thread. His hand -- the hand that had reached for the keris, that had grasped at a power not meant for him -- rose from the bloody stone and found Kian Santang's arm, and his grip was gentle. "Little one. Forgive me. I would have taken it. I would have --" A cough. Blood on his lips. "The keris showed me... my weakness. My strength. The same thing. I did not know they were the same."

"Do not speak," Kian Santang said, and the tears fell on Gajahmada's face and mixed with the blood and the ash, and the general smiled.

"I must speak. There is... not much time."

Around them, the battle continued. The clash of weapons, the roar of Buta Raksasa, Tumenggung Batu's war cries echoing from the passage -- all of it reached them as distant sound, muffled by the walls of their small enclosure, separated from this moment by a gulf wider than volume or proximity. This was a private space. A space between two people and one death.

"My Sumpah Palapa." Gajahmada's eyes drifted -- not unfocused, but searching, looking for something beyond the grey sky, beyond the volcanic haze, beyond the borders of the visible world. "I swore to unite Nusantara. I thought... I believed it meant conquest. Empire. One throne above all thrones. My people, ruling. My flag, flying. My..." He coughed again, and the cough was wet, and his hand tightened on Kian Santang's arm. "My pride. Dressed as duty. Dressed so well I could not see the pride beneath."

He turned his head. Beyond the fallen pillars, through the gaps in the ruined walls, the fellowship fought. Raden Wijaya and his blazing keris. Dewi Anjani on the wall with her luminous arrows. Tumenggung Batu in his passage, immovable, indomitable. Cepot and Dawala, small and fierce and fighting back to back with their silver daggers burning. Different peoples. Different races. Different traditions. United.

"But perhaps..." Gajahmada's voice was barely audible now. His grip weakened. His eyes came back to Kian Santang, and in them was a light that had nothing to do with the keris and everything to do with understanding. "Perhaps unity is not one throne. Perhaps unity is... *this*." He gestured -- weakly, the movement of a hand that was running out of strength -- toward the sounds of battle, toward the fellowship. "Gotong royong. Working together. Carrying the weight together. A Tuyul and a Bidadari and a Kurcaci and a prince with no kingdom and a general with too much pride, all choosing -- *choosing* -- to walk the same road."

He drew a shuddering breath, and when he spoke again, the words carried the weight of a krama inggil -- the highest register of his Javanese tongue, the register of sacred oaths and royal pronouncements, words meant to be carved in stone:

"*Tan amukti palapa... saderengipun Nusantara manunggal -- sanes dening pedhang, nanging dening gotong royong saklawase.* Not Nusantara conquered, but Nusantara united -- from the highlands of Sunda to the rice terraces of Bali, from the diamond rivers of Kalimantan to the spice groves of Maluku, from the seafaring coasts of Sulawesi to the sacred valleys of Papua -- all bound not by the sword but by gotong royong, forever."

His hand fell.

"That is unity," he whispered. "That is the Sumpah Palapa."

Footsteps on stone. Raden Wijaya emerged through a gap in the ruins, the Keris Nagasasra dark with Buta ichor, his lean body streaked with blood that was not his own. He saw Gajahmada on the ground. He saw the blood. He saw Kian Santang's face.

He knelt.

"Raden." Gajahmada's eyes found the prince's face, and something passed between the two warriors -- old, complicated, layered with the tensions and the trust that had defined their relationship since the council at Dieng. Warrior and prince. Soldier and sovereign. Two men who had disagreed on almost everything and who had, in the end, been fighting for the same thing.

"Mataram needs its king, Raden," Gajahmada said. His voice was sand on stone, fading, fading. "Do not wait too long. The people need... they need someone to follow. Someone who leads not by conquest but by..." He searched for the word. "By example. You have always known that. I am... slow to learn."

He gripped Raden Wijaya's hand. The prince's long fingers closed around the general's thick, calloused fist, and held.

"Gajahmada --" Raden Wijaya began.

"Let me finish." The faintest shadow of the old iron crossed the general's face -- the stubbornness, the refusal to be interrupted, the immovable will that had built an empire and nearly destroyed a fellowship. Then it softened, and he turned to Kian Santang, and his eyes -- his clear, human, uncorrupted eyes -- were bright with a faith that cost him everything he had left.

"Destroy it," he said. "I believe in you now, little one. I believe in you."

His hand relaxed in Raden Wijaya's grip. His chest rose, and fell, and rose again. His eyes stayed on Kian Santang's face -- fixed there, held there, as though the face of the small Tuyul he had wronged and defended and died for was the last sight he wanted to carry into whatever came next.

Then the light in his eyes went still.

Raden Wijaya released the general's hand. He placed it on Gajahmada's chest, crossed with the other, and closed the unseeing eyes with a touch as gentle as anything his warrior's hands had ever done.

"*Mugi-mugi atmanipun pikantuk katentreman, wangsul dhateng Sang Hyang Widhi.*"

The ancient Javanese prayer for the departed, spoken in krama -- the high register of mourning and reverence. Not a prayer to any singular deity of later ages, but the oldest form: a commendation of the atma, the eternal self, back to the divine source.

*May his atma find peace, returning to the Supreme.*

Kian Santang wept. He wept over the body of the man who had tried to take the keris. Who had fought an army to protect it. Who had died standing between the darkness and the light, not because he was uncorrupted but because he had *chosen*, in the end, to resist the corruption, and the choice had cost him everything, and he had paid the price without hesitation.

The battle raged on beyond the walls. But here, in this small space among the ruins of Ratu Boko, there was only silence, and grief, and the quiet, devastating sound of a hero's last breath settling into the ash.

---

The tide of battle turned.

Word of the captain's death spread through the Buta Raksasa ranks with a speed that suggested some dark, hive-like connection between the creatures and their leader. The organized assault -- the disciplined formations, the coordinated thrusts from multiple angles -- dissolved into confusion, then into rage, then into something approaching fear as the Buta realized that the thing that had directed them was gone and the enemies they faced were very much alive and very, very angry.

Raden Wijaya emerged from the eastern ruins with a cold fury that transformed his fighting from art into annihilation. The Keris Nagasasra was a storm of silver light in his hands, and the Buta Raksasa that faced him died not because they were outmatched in skill -- though they were -- but because they were outmatched in will, because the prince of Mataram fought now with the weight of a fallen comrade on his heart and the rage of a man who had just watched a brother die and intended that the death should *mean something*.

He cut through the Buta lines like a ship's prow through water, driving them back from the upper ruins, opening space for Tumenggung Batu to advance from his held passage and take them in the flank. The Kurcaci chieftain, tireless, his war hammer streaming with dark ichor, smashed into the Buta's side with a roar that shook the ruins, and between the prince and the chieftain the enemy was caught, compressed, broken.

Above them, Dewi Anjani nocked her last arrow.

It was different from the others. Shorter. Heavier. Its head was not bamboo but stone -- a fragment of meteorite, dark and dense, that the Bidadari had carried since Dieng, saved for a moment of extremity. She held it against her cheek, her golden eyes blazing with a light that was not reflection but emanation, and she spoke a word in the old tongue -- a word that no mortal ear should have heard, a word from the language that the Bidadari used to speak to the fabric of reality itself.

She released.

The meteorite arrow struck the largest remaining concentration of Buta Raksasa -- a knot of twenty or more massed on the southern slope, preparing for a final charge -- and detonated.

Not with fire. With *light*. A burst of radiance so intense, so pure, so fundamentally antithetical to the dark enchantment that animated the Buta, that it unmade them where they stood. The light expanded outward in a sphere of blazing white-gold brilliance that burned through the volcanic haze like a sun erupting from the earth, and within its radius the Buta Raksasa did not merely die -- they *dissolved*, their corrupted flesh burning away, their dark armor collapsing into dust, their red eyes extinguished like candle flames in a hurricane.

The sphere faded. Where twenty Buta had stood, there was only ash.

The remaining Buta broke.

They fled -- scrambling down the slopes, plunging into the warm water, clawing toward their dark boats with the panicked desperation of creatures that had encountered something their programming could not process. Terror. They had been built to inspire it, not to feel it, and the experience unmade their discipline as thoroughly as Dewi Anjani's arrow had unmade their comrades.

Tumenggung Batu and Raden Wijaya pursued them to the water's edge, cutting down the slowest, and stood on the black rock and watched the dark fleet retreat into the haze, and the horns that had announced the attack sounded again -- this time distant, diminishing, the sound of withdrawal rather than advance.

The battle of Ratu Boko was over.

---

They gathered around Gajahmada's body in the long, grey dusk.

Kian Santang and Bagus had carried him -- with difficulty, with tenderness, with the painstaking care of people moving something infinitely precious -- to a stone platform at the center of the upper ruins. It was a flat slab of carved andesite, raised above the surrounding floor by a step of fitted stone, its surface bearing the faded relief of what might once have been a ceremonial scene -- dancers, musicians, figures making offerings to a figure on a throne. A king's dais, perhaps. Or a priest's altar. It would serve as a warrior's bier.

They laid him on the stone with his hands crossed on his chest. Bagus had cleaned the wounds -- closed the eyes, wiped the blood from the face, straightened the limbs with the quiet, practical compassion of a man who had tended to the bodies of fallen friends before and who understood that the last kindness one could offer the dead was the dignity of arrangement.

Raden Wijaya unclasped Gajahmada's cloak -- the Majapahit batik, dark red and gold, the patterns of the Trowulan court woven into the fabric in a design that spoke of rice terraces and naga serpents and the stylized mountains of eastern Jawa. He spread it over the general's body, covering him from chest to feet, and the batik's colors were vivid against the grey stone, a banner of the kingdom Gajahmada had loved and served and almost destroyed and, in the end, honored more than he had ever honored it in life.

They placed his keris on his chest, atop the batik cloak. The Majapahit blade -- not the Keris Murka, never the Keris Murka, but his own weapon, the tumenggung's keris, the blade he had carried through a hundred campaigns -- lay on his still breast with its hilt at his shoulder and its tip at his waist, and the pamor patterns in the metal caught what little light filtered through the haze and held it, a last, dim gleam.

Seven companions stood around the body. The volcanic haze pressed down. Krakatau smoked in the distance, enormous, indifferent, patient. The ruins of Ratu Boko, ancient and broken, held the scene in their stone embrace like a theater built for exactly this performance.

Raden Wijaya spoke.

His voice was quiet, but it carried -- carried across the ruins, carried through the haze, carried with the authority of a man who had been a prince and was becoming, in this moment, something more.

"He was a warrior of Majapahit. He served his kingdom with a devotion that few could match and fewer still could understand. He was proud." A pause. "Perhaps too proud. The keris found that pride and turned it against him, and for a moment he was less than he should have been."

He looked down at the still face, and his own face -- the face of the exile, the wanderer, the man who had lost everything and learned to carry the loss -- held an expression of grief that was also understanding, and understanding that was also forgiveness.

"But in the end, he was *more* than he should have been. He stood between the darkness and the light, and he did not move. He gave his life for the one he wronged, and in the giving he found what he had been searching for all along." Raden Wijaya's voice deepened. "His oath is fulfilled. Not the oath of conquest. Not the oath of empire. The oath of *gotong royong*. The oath of unity. The oath that says: we are stronger together than apart, and the measure of a man is not how much he can take but how much he can give."

He paused. The silence was the silence of sacred things, of temples and lingga and the spaces between mantras.

"He was Gajahmada. He was our brother. He fell defending those he loved."

Tumenggung Batu stepped forward and laid his broad, stone-grey hand on the body's shoulder -- a Kurcaci gesture of farewell, the acknowledgment of a warrior's worth by a warrior's peer. His craggy face was unreadable, but his small dark eyes glistened. "He held the line," the chieftain said. "That is enough. That is always enough."

Dewi Anjani sang. Not a mere fragment of melody -- she lifted her voice in a *Durma*, the pupuh of battle and valor, the form that honors warriors who fall with honor on the field. The Durma's fierce, staccato rhythms softened in her throat into something both martial and tender, and the notes hung in the volcanic air like threads of gold in grey cloth, and for a moment -- a brief, shining, impossible moment -- the haze thinned, and a shaft of actual sunlight broke through the smoke and touched the stone platform, and Gajahmada's batik cloak blazed with color, red and gold, the colors of his kingdom, the colors of his heart.

Then the clouds closed, and the light was gone.

Cepot said nothing. For the first time since anyone had known him, the witty Tuyul with the sharp tongue and the sharper mind had no words. He stood with his arm around Dawala's shoulders, and Dawala wept openly and without shame, his round face streaming with tears that cut channels through the ash, and Cepot held him and stared at the body on the stone and his silence said more than all his words had ever said.

Kian Santang knelt beside the platform. He placed his marked palm -- the palm scarred by the lingga, the palm that bore the oath -- on Gajahmada's crossed hands, and he whispered, "I forgive you. I forgive you, and I will finish what we started. I promise."

The keris on his back hummed. He ignored it. This moment was not the keris's to claim.

Bagus placed his hand on Kian Santang's shoulder. Steady. Warm. Present. The hand that had always been there, that would always be there, the hand of the friend who did not need oaths or sacred stones to know his place.

Seven companions. One fallen.

Raden Wijaya turned toward Krakatau. The mountain filled the sky -- vast, dark, smoking, patient. Closer than ever. Close enough, now, that the rumble of its volcanic heart was a constant tremor in the stone beneath their feet, a pulse that matched the pulse of the Keris Murka, two dark hearts beating in synchrony across the poisoned water.

"We cannot stay," Raden Wijaya said. "The Shadow King knows where we are. More will come."

"The Buta sensed the keris," Tumenggung Batu said. "They tracked it here. As long as the bearer carries the blade, every dark creature in the strait will be drawn to him."

Kian Santang stood. He looked at his companions -- his friends, his fellowship, the six remaining souls who had walked with him from Dieng through caves and forests and over seas to this dead place at the edge of the world. He loved them. He loved them with a fierceness that surprised him, a fierceness that the keris's whispers could not diminish and the Shadow King's terror could not extinguish.

And he knew what he had to do.

But not yet. Not in this moment. This moment belonged to the dead.

He looked at Gajahmada one last time -- the warrior on his stone bier, wrapped in his kingdom's colors, his keris on his chest, his face at peace. A good man who had been broken by a dark power and who had put himself back together in the only way that mattered -- not perfectly, not without scars, but completely, and at the end.

The fellowship turned away. They left Gajahmada on his stone platform in the ruins of Ratu Boko, with the ancient carvings watching over him and the volcanic ash settling on his batik cloak like grey snow, and the light fading, and Krakatau smoking in the distance, and the war -- the real war, the war that Gajahmada had died to keep alive -- still ahead of them, still waiting, still demanding everything they had left to give.

Seven companions walked north through the ruins, toward the channel, toward the next island, toward whatever came next. Behind them, the stone platform held its silent burden. Above them, the sky was the color of ash and iron and grief.

The battle was won. The fellowship was broken. Krakatau waited.

And somewhere in the smoke and the dark water, the Shadow King's eye burned, patient and terrible, watching them come.
