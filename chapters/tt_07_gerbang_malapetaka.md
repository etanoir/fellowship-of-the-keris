## I. The Desolation of Krakatau

Cold charcoal and crushed bone.

Those were the first colors the world offered, and for three days they were the only ones. Not the warm charcoal of a village hearth after the evening meal, not the clean white of cattle bone ground for fertilizer in the *sawah* paddies of home -- but a charcoal that had never known warmth, a bone that had never belonged to anything living, pigments ground from the vocabulary of annihilation and smeared across the earth in every direction until the eye surrendered and the mind accepted that color, as a concept, had been revoked. The land before the caldera of Krakatau was a canvas of endings: grey upon grey upon grey, punctuated only by the occasional vein of obsidian black where ancient lava flows had cooled into glass, or the sickly, sulfurous yellow of fumarole deposits that clung to rock faces like the dried bile of a dying world.

Kian Santang walked through this desolation and felt it enter him.

Not through the eyes alone -- though the eyes had suffered enough, squinting against an ashen haze that turned the sun into a pale smear behind veils of volcanic dust, a disc without warmth, without definition, a ghost of itself. The desolation entered through the skin, which cracked and split in the dry, mineral-laden air. It entered through the lungs, which ached with every breath drawn through the cloth he had tied across his mouth and nose -- a strip torn from Bagus's spare shirt, already grey with particulate, already tasting of sulfur and powdered stone. It entered through the soles of his feet, where the hardened lava fields transmitted a low, persistent vibration into his bones, as though the earth itself were shivering with a fever that would not break. And it entered through the ears, through the silence -- that vast, planetary silence that was not the absence of sound but the presence of emptiness, a silence so total that his own heartbeat seemed like an intrusion, an impertinence, the arrogance of a living thing daring to persist where life had been so thoroughly erased.

On the first day out of the mangrove swamps, they had still seen signs of the living world. A stunted *alang-alang* grass, grey as the ash it grew from, clinging to the lee side of a basalt ridge with the grim tenacity of a creature that did not know it was already dead. A single *elang* -- eagle -- riding the thermals high above, its silhouette dark against the smoke-filmed sky, circling once, twice, and then banking west toward the sea, toward air it could breathe, away from the poisoned updrafts of the caldera's outskirts. A lizard, grey-brown, flat as a leaf, pressed against a warm rock with its throat pulsing -- a *kadal* of the high desert, evolved to live at the edge of nothing, watching them with eyes like polished seeds as they passed. Kian Santang had paused to look at it. The lizard had looked back. And then the wind had shifted, carrying a gust of sulfur dioxide from a nearby vent, and the lizard had flicked its tongue once and retreated into a crack in the basalt, and they had not seen another living thing since.

That was two days ago.

Now they walked on a surface that was not soil but *tephra* -- the accumulated fallout of centuries of eruptions, compressed by rain and time into a brittle, grey crust that crumbled under their weight, plunging their feet ankle-deep into fine ash with every step. Walking on it was like walking on a field of ground glass. The ash invaded boots, filled seams, worked its way into the folds of cloth and the creases of skin, a grey powder so fine and so pervasive that it seemed less a substance than a state of being. Within an hour of entering the ash fields, they were grey. Within half a day, they were indistinguishable from the landscape. Only the eyes remained -- Kian Santang's dark and haunted, Bagus's brown and watchful, Si Burik's pale and luminous -- three pairs of eyes moving through a grey world like lanterns in fog.

"How much farther?" Bagus asked, and even his voice was grey, stripped of its usual warmth by the dryness in his throat, the grit on his tongue.

Si Burik, who moved ahead of them with the low, scuttling gait of a creature born to this landscape, paused and lifted his head. He sniffed the air the way a dog sniffs for game -- short, sharp inhalations through wide nostrils, his head turning left and right, sampling the wind's chemistry with a precision that was almost mechanical.

"Two days... maybe three... to the ridge," Si Burik rasped. "To the seeing-place. Then... then master sees." He shivered, his bony shoulders drawing together as though against a chill, though the air was warm -- a constant, airless warmth that emanated from the ground itself, the residual heat of a geology that had not finished burning. "*Gollum... gollum.* The Black Gate is beyond the ridge. Big. Black. Shut."

"You've been there before," Kian Santang said. It was not a question.

Si Burik's head bobbed in a rapid, birdlike nod. "Many times... many times Si Burik creeps close. Watching. Waiting. Hoping the *jimat* will call him back. But the Gate does not open for Si Burik. The Gate opens only for the Master's servants. And Si Burik is nobody's servant." A pause. A flicker in those pale eyes -- something sly, something calculating, quickly suppressed. "*Gollum.* Nobody's servant. Only the *jimat's* servant."

Kian Santang felt the keris shift on his back. The blade was wrapped in three layers of batik cloth -- the innermost layer Eyang Guru's own, consecrated with *mantra*, now stained and fraying at the edges -- but even through the wrapping, through the cloth and the leather harness and the thickness of his own sweat-soaked shirt, he could feel it. Not its weight, though the weight was considerable and growing, a gravitational pull that bent his spine and carved furrows into the flesh of his shoulders. Not its temperature, though it ran hot now, warmer each day, as though proximity to its place of forging was rekindling the fires of its creation. What he felt was its *attention*. The Keris Murka was aware of him. It pressed against his consciousness the way a sleeping cat presses against a leg -- warm, heavy, possessive, claiming ownership through contact. And when the wind died and the silence deepened and the grey world closed around them like a fist, the keris spoke.

Not in words. Not exactly. Words implied grammar, syntax, the civilized architecture of thought. What the keris offered was more primitive: images, impulses, sensations that rose unbidden from the blade's dark intelligence and bloomed behind Kian Santang's eyes like flowers opening in a poisoned garden. He saw the caldera from above -- the great bowl of fire where the keris had been forged, where the Shadow King had poured his *sakti* into molten metal and hammered it into a shape that could hold rage the way a cup holds water. He saw the gate opening -- the immense obsidian wall parting like lips, revealing the road beyond, the road *home*. He felt the pull -- a magnetic, visceral tug in his solar plexus, drawing him forward, drawing him *down*, into the heat, into the fire, into the embrace of a power that would swallow him whole and call it love.

*Come,* the keris whispered. Not a word. A feeling. A direction. *Come home.*

*Aing has no home in fire,* Kian Santang thought, and the thought was a wall -- crude, crumbling, but a wall -- between himself and the blade's seduction. *Aing comes from Padalarang. From the sawah and the swimming hole and the mango trees. From Ma's cooking and Uncle Kian's stories and the sound of the gamelan on festival nights. That is home. Not this.*

But even as he built the wall, he felt it crack. Because the truth -- the terrible, eroding, undeniable truth that the keris knew and he was only beginning to understand -- was that Padalarang was far away. Not merely in distance, though the distance was immense -- the whole width of Jawa and the poisoned strait of Sunda lay between him and the green hills of his childhood. It was far away in *kind*. The boy who had climbed mango trees and swum in the river and helped Bagus in the garden was a creature from a different world, a world of color and warmth and laughter, and that world had no jurisdiction here. Here, in the ash fields of Krakatau's outer desolation, the only reality was grey, and the only warmth came from the blade on his back, and the only voice that spoke was the voice that promised an end to this walking, this suffering, this slow immolation of hope -- if only he would give in.

He would not give in. He said this to himself as he walked, a mantra of his own, a *japa* without *sakti* but with the desperate, gritted-teeth conviction of a young man who has nothing left but stubbornness and a promise made to the dead.

*Aing will not give in. Aing will carry it to the fire. Aing will throw it in. And then aing will go home.*

If there was still a home to go to. If the Shadow King's armies had not already swallowed the green world. If the war he could not see -- the war that raged beyond the smoke-shrouded horizon, where friends he loved were fighting and perhaps dying -- had not already been lost while he trudged through this grey purgatory with a monster and a gardener for company.

He shook the thought away. He could not afford it. Doubt was a luxury, and he had spent the last of his luxuries somewhere in the mangrove swamps, along with his spare boots and his last packet of dried fish and the final, threadbare remnants of his certainty that the world was a place where good prevailed.

"Kian." Bagus's voice, closer now, at his shoulder. A hand on his arm -- broad, calloused, warm even through the layer of ash. "You're drifting."

"*Aing* know."

"You were stroking the hilt again."

Kian Santang looked down. His right hand had migrated, without his consent, to the leather-wrapped handle of the keris harness. His fingers were curled around it with a tenderness that made his stomach turn. He released it as though burned.

"*Hatur nuhun,*" he whispered. *Thank you.*

Bagus held his gaze for a moment -- a long, searching, worried look that communicated more than any speech could. Then the stocky Tuyul nodded, squeezed Kian Santang's arm, and fell back into his position two steps behind, where he could watch both the path ahead and the keris-bearer's hands.

That was Bagus. That had always been Bagus. Not the keris-bearer, not the hero, not the one the songs would be about -- if there were songs, if anyone survived to sing them. Bagus was the watcher. The guarder. The one who carried the spare water and the dried food and the weight of another's madness on his broad, unbreaking back. In the mangrove swamps, he had fed Kian Santang when the younger Tuyul forgot to eat. In the ash fields, he had woken him from nightmares that left him clawing at the wrapping on his back. On the ridge above the sulfur vents, where the fumes had thickened to a choking fog, it was Bagus who had torn his own shirt for masks, Bagus who had found the windward path, Bagus who had pulled Kian Santang bodily away from a fumarole that the keris's whisper had steered him toward with a gentle, lethal insistence that it was *a shortcut, a faster path, step closer, breathe deep.*

Bagus did not understand the keris. He could not hear its voice. He did not feel its pull. But he understood Kian Santang -- understood the boy behind the burden, the friend inside the bearer -- and that understanding was, in its way, a *sakti* more powerful than any blade.

*Karuhun,* Bagus thought as he walked, invoking the ancestors whose guidance he had sought every morning since childhood, pressing his palms together in the grey dawn while Kian Santang slept and Si Burik skulked and the ash fell like the dandruff of a dying sky. *Karuhun, give him strength. Give me strength. Let us reach the mountain. Let us throw this cursed thing into the fire and go home to the sawah and the mango trees and the sound of the river and the world we remember.*

The ancestors did not answer. They rarely did. But Bagus believed they listened, and that was enough.

Si Burik led them through a narrow defile between two ridges of solidified lava -- a passage so tight that they had to turn sideways to squeeze through, the sharp rock walls scraping skin and cloth. The creature moved through the gap with the boneless ease of a snake, his thin body contorting around projections that would have stopped a normal Tuyul cold. On the other side, the ash field opened into a vast, flat expanse -- a plain of grey desolation stretching toward the northern horizon, where the bulk of the caldera complex rose against the sky like a wall between this world and the next.

It was here, on this plain, that the landscape achieved its fullest expression of despair. The ground was not merely barren -- it was *anti-fertile*, a surface so saturated with volcanic chemicals that it actively repelled life, a substrate of powdered stone and crystallized sulfur and heavy metals leached from the deep earth by centuries of geothermal activity. The air above it shimmered with heat mirages -- phantom pools of water that appeared and dissolved, the cruelest joke the physics of light could play on thirsty travelers. And everywhere, rising from cracks and vents in the tortured ground, columns of steam and sulfurous gas drifted upward like the ghosts of trees, a spectral forest of vapor that the wind shaped and reshaped and scattered and reformed in an endless, meaningless choreography.

Kian Santang looked at this landscape and thought of Padalarang.

Not the thought he wanted -- not the deliberate, fortress-building invocation of green hills and clean water that he used to ward off the keris's seductions. This was the other kind of memory, the kind that ambushed: sudden, specific, devastating in its vividness. He saw his mother's kitchen. The clay stove with its crown of blue flames. The brass *wajan* in which she fried *tempe* on Sunday mornings, the oil snapping and popping, the smell of fermented soy and garlic filling the small room like a benediction. He saw her hands -- his mother's hands, brown and quick and scarred from a lifetime of cooking and gardening and sewing and holding -- lifting the golden slices from the oil and laying them on banana leaf to drain. He heard her voice: *Kian, makan dulu. Eat first. The world can wait.*

The memory was so vivid that it hurt physically -- a contraction in his chest, a tightening behind his eyes. He stumbled. His foot caught on a spur of basalt, and he went down on one knee, the keris shifting on his back with a weight that felt, in that moment, like the weight of every mile between him and that kitchen, every step away from the person he had been before the blade chose him.

"Kian!" Bagus was at his side instantly. "Are you --"

"*Aing* am fine." He pushed himself up. His knee bled sluggishly through torn cloth. The ash immediately found the wound, grey grit in red flesh, stinging. "Fine."

He was not fine. He was twenty years old, a hundred miles from home, carrying a weapon that was slowly eating his mind, walking through a landscape that looked like the world after the world ended. The sulfurous vents hissed and spat in a rhythm that was almost musical -- a dark, inverted *gamelan*, as though the mountain were playing a death-score on instruments of stone and poison, and the only thing between him and its melody was a loyal friend and a treacherous guide and a stubbornness so deep it had become indistinguishable from despair.

But he walked. He kept walking. Because that was all he had left: the walking. The putting of one foot in front of the other on the grey earth, the slow, stubborn accumulation of distance, the refusal -- grim, teeth-gritted, wordless -- to stop.

They walked.

## II. The Dead Coral Marshes

On the evening of the third day, the landscape changed.

The ash fields did not end so much as *descend* -- the flat grey plain tilting downward into a shallow depression, a bowl scooped from the earth by some ancient geological event, and at the bottom of this bowl lay something that was neither land nor water but a hideous compromise between the two.

Si Burik stopped at the rim and pointed.

"The dead sea," he said. His voice was different here -- lower, thinner, stripped of its usual wheedling slyness. He was afraid. "The place where the *sagara* used to be. Before the Master raised the mountain and pushed the water away."

Kian Santang looked down. In the failing light -- the sun a dull orange smear behind the perpetual haze, sinking toward a horizon that was indistinguishable from the ash-colored sky -- the depression below was a study in wrongness. It was white. Not the white of snow or salt or sand, but the bleached, brittle white of dead coral -- thousands upon thousands of coral formations, exposed by the retreat of an ancient sea, standing upright in the grey mud like a forest of bone. They were everywhere: branching *acropora* the size of small trees, their delicate fingers reaching for a sky that had replaced the water they needed to survive; massive brain corals, their convoluted surfaces cracked and dry, the labyrinths of their architecture visible in cross-section like the folds of a fossil brain; staghorn corals in thickets so dense that they formed walls and corridors and chambers, a labyrinth of calcified death that extended across the depression's floor for a mile or more.

Between the coral formations, the ground was a grey, viscous mud -- not liquid enough to be called water, not solid enough to support weight, a gelatinous slurry of volcanic ash and ancient seabed sediment that gave off a thin, persistent stench of decay. Things were embedded in this mud. Kian Santang could see them from the rim: the shells of ancient creatures -- ammonites, nautili, the massive spirals of *keong* snails as large as a man's torso -- their calcium carbonate surfaces bleached and pitted, half-swallowed by the grey ooze. The ribcages of whales. The skulls of creatures he could not name, with jaws hinged for swallowing and eye sockets the size of dinner plates, staring upward through the mud at a sky they had never meant to see.

This was the seabed. The actual, physical seabed of the Sunda Strait as it had been before the Shadow King's *sakti* raised the caldera complex from the depths and pushed the water back, creating the ring of volcanic islands that now blocked the channel between Jawa and Sumatra. The sea had been here -- the warm, teeming, coral-rich tropical sea -- and the Shadow King had evicted it, had lifted the earth's bones through a mile of ocean and set them down in the air to dry and die, and the reef had perished in the sunlight, and the creatures had rotted in their shells, and the mud had sealed them in, and now this place was a cemetery of an ocean that no longer existed, a monument to the power that had unmade it.

"We must cross it," Si Burik said. "The ridge... the seeing-place... is on the other side. The only way around is through the Buta camps. *Gollum.* Through the dead coral, through the mud, we are hidden. The Buta do not come here."

"Why not?" Bagus asked, staring at the white expanse with undisguised horror.

Si Burik shivered. He pulled at his own ears -- a nervous habit that surfaced when the creature was genuinely frightened, not merely performing fear for sympathy.

"Because of the lights," he whispered. "The dead lights. *Mulah* look at them. *Mulah* follow them. Si Burik is telling, Si Burik is warning. Do not follow the lights."

"What lights?" Bagus pressed, his practical mind demanding particulars even as his body recoiled from the rim. "Phosphorescence? Swamp fire? What are we dealing with?"

Si Burik shook his head violently, his ears flapping against his gaunt skull. "Not fire. Not glow-worms. Not the light of the living world. *Gollum... gollum.* The dead lights are the *bayu* of the fallen -- the life-breath of warriors who died here when the Master first raised the mountain. Warriors who fought him. Warriors who *served* him. All the same now. All trapped. The Master's *sakti* keeps them in the mud, keeps their *bayu* burning, keeps their eyes open. They cannot go to the *alam barzah*. They cannot rest. They wait, master. They wait and they watch and they *call*." His voice dropped to a vibration barely distinguishable from the hum of the earth itself. "Si Burik has seen them rise... seen the lights move toward the surface when a living thing passes... seen the mud bubble and shift as the dead turn their faces upward. *Gollum.* They are hungry, master. Not for flesh. For *bayu*. For living breath. They would pull a living soul down into the mud and hold it there, not to kill it but to *keep* it, to have company in the long dark, to hear the sound of a heartbeat that is not a memory."

"And you've crossed this before," Kian Santang said. "Alone."

"Si Burik is small," the creature whispered. "Small *bayu*. Small life. The dead lights barely stir for Si Burik. But master..." He turned those pale, luminous eyes toward the wrapped keris on Kian Santang's back, and the hunger and the terror in them were indistinguishable. "Master carries the *jimat*. The *jimat* has more *sakti* than a thousand living warriors. The dead will *feel* it. They will rise for it. They will reach." He swallowed, his throat clicking in the dry air. "*Mulah* let them touch you, master. If the dead touch the *jimat*... *gollum*... Si Burik does not know what happens. Si Burik does not want to know."

They descended.

The footing was treacherous from the first step. The slope was covered in loose fragments of coral -- sharp, brittle shards that snapped under their weight with sounds like breaking teeth. Below the coral, the ground softened, transitioning from hard ash to a spongy, uncertain surface that gave way without warning, plunging a foot or a leg into grey mud that gripped like a cold hand. Si Burik navigated this terrain with an uncanny precision, his splayed feet finding the solid patches -- the buried coral heads, the submerged boulders, the compressed ridges of ancient sediment -- that would support weight, and he moved between them with a dancer's intuition, rarely hesitating, never sinking deeper than his shins.

Kian Santang and Bagus were not so fortunate. Within minutes of entering the coral marshes, both were coated to the waist in grey slime, their boots filled, their clothes clinging, the cold mud seeping through fabric to find skin with an intimacy that was almost obscene. The stench was appalling -- a miasma of anaerobic decay, of organic matter that had been sealed beneath mud for centuries and was now disturbed by their passage, releasing gases that burned the eyes and made the throat close. Hydrogen sulfide. Methane. The exhalations of deep rot, the breath of the planet's buried dead.

"This is foul," Bagus gasped, pulling his leg from a sucking pool of mud with a sound that was itself an indecency. "*Karuhun*, this is the worst place in all Nusantara."

"Not the worst," Si Burik corrected from ahead, his voice floating back through the gathering dark. "The worst is where we are going. *Gollum... gollum.* This is only the entrance hall."

They wound through corridors of dead coral, the white formations rising on either side like the walls of a roofless cathedral. In the last light of the failing day, the coral glowed faintly -- a phosphorescent luminescence that might have been beautiful in another context, the residual bioluminescence of organisms that had been dead for centuries, still emitting their chemical light in a slow, terminal discharge that would eventually fade to nothing. The light gave the coral forest a spectral quality, as though they were walking through a landscape built of ghosts.

It was full dark when Kian Santang first saw the other lights.

They appeared in the mud between the coral formations -- soft, pale glows deep beneath the surface, like lanterns submerged in grey water. They were steady at first, unmoving, fixed points of cold luminescence that seemed to emanate from specific locations in the muck, as though something beneath the surface were generating them. But as the darkness deepened and the three travelers moved deeper into the marshes, the lights began to shift. They drifted, slowly, through the mud -- sliding laterally, rising toward the surface, sinking away -- with a purposeful, almost intelligent movement that had nothing to do with the physics of phosphorescence and everything to do with something older, something darker.

"The dead lights," Si Burik hissed from ahead, and the fear in his voice was genuine, raw, a child's fear with no slyness behind it. "*Mulah* look, master. *Mulah* follow. They are not lights. They are *eyes*."

Kian Santang looked.

He should not have looked. He knew, even as his gaze was drawn downward to the nearest glow, that looking was a mistake, that the creature's warning was not a guide's routine caution but a survival imperative born of experience and terror. But the keris *wanted* him to look. The blade shifted on his back -- a subtle, insistent pressure that angled his body, tilted his head, directed his gaze downward toward the pale light in the mud -- and before his will could intervene, he saw.

Beneath the surface of the grey ooze, in the cold light of the dead glow, a face looked up at him.

It was not a skull. It was a *face* -- complete, intact, preserved by the anaerobic mud with a fidelity that was beyond nature, a face that looked as though it had been submerged hours ago rather than centuries. The skin was grey-white, the color of drowned flesh, and the features were those of a young man -- Javanese, broad-nosed, strong-jawed, with the high cheekbones of a warrior caste. The eyes were open. They stared upward through the mud with a fixed, glassy intensity that was not sight but its memory, the afterimage of a consciousness that had been trapped at the moment of death and sealed beneath the earth like a letter in an envelope that no one would ever open.

The warrior was armored. Kian Santang could see, through the mud's translucent upper layer, the outline of bronze plate and chainmail, the style of Srivijaya or early Majapahit, ancient beyond reckoning. A keris was at his hip -- a real keris, not the cursed blade Kian Santang carried, but a weapon of war, its *dapur* still visible through the centuries of submersion, the flame-shaped blade dulled but intact. The warrior's hands were crossed on his chest in the posture of burial, but his mouth was open -- open in a scream or a prayer or a final, unfinished word -- and the dead light emanated from within him, from his chest, from the place where the *bayu* -- the life force -- had been when he died, still burning, still burning, centuries after the flesh should have returned to earth and the spirit should have departed for the *alam barzah*, the world between worlds.

There were more. As Kian Santang's eyes adjusted to the darkness and the dead light, he saw them -- dozens, hundreds, *thousands* of faces beneath the mud, stretching in every direction as far as the glow could illuminate. Warriors of every age and allegiance: Srivijayan marines in their distinctive crested helms; Majapahit infantry in banded iron; Sundanese lancers with their long, leaf-bladed spears still clutched in grey hands; soldiers of kingdoms whose names had been forgotten before the first line of the *Negarakertagama* was written. And among them -- interspersed, entangled, their limbs sometimes crossing, their armor sometimes locked together in frozen combat -- were others. Buta. Raksasa. The Shadow King's soldiers, grey-green flesh preserved in grey mud, their tusked faces contorted in the same eternal, unfinished expression of death. Both sides. All sides. Every army that had ever fought on this ground, every warrior who had fallen in the wars of the caldera, sealed together in the mud of the dead sea, enemies and allies alike, stripped of allegiance by death and unified in the democracy of decay.

The dead lights were their *bayu*. The life force of the fallen, unable to depart, trapped by the Shadow King's *sakti* in the soil of his domain, burning in the bodies of warriors who could not die fully and could not live again.

Not all the lights burned with the same colour or the same intensity, and Kian Santang understood this in the way the keris permitted him to understand things -- not through reasoning but through a dark, intuitive communion with the blade's own knowledge of *sakti* and its operations. The warriors who had died fighting the Shadow King burned with a cold, blue-white luminance, the residue of righteous *bayu* corrupted by its imprisonment, turned inward, consuming itself century upon century in a slow autophagic agony. These were the *Karuhun* -- the ancestors of the free peoples, the heroes of the old wars -- and their trapped radiance held something that was not rage but its ghost: a purposeless, undirected fury at the injustice of their entombment, a fury that had no outlet and no target and so had become a gravitational phenomenon, pulling at anything living that passed, seeking to draw it down not from malice but from a blind, desperate need for communion with the warmth of the living world they had been denied.

But the others -- the Buta, the Raksasa, the Shadow King's own fallen -- these burned with a different light entirely: a sickly, greenish phosphorescence, thick and viscous, like light that had spoiled, light that had curdled in the dark the way milk curdles in heat. These dead had no *bayu* in the natural sense. What burned in them was the Shadow King's *sakti*, the borrowed power he had poured into his soldiers at their making, and it did not consume itself but *persisted*, stubborn and malevolent, a dark energy that the mud could not quench and the centuries could not erode. These lights did not merely pull. They *reached*. Tendrils of green luminescence extended upward through the mud like the fingers of drowned hands, questing blindly toward the surface, toward warmth, toward life, and when Kian Santang stepped near one -- too near, his foot sliding on a patch of wet coral -- the tendril lunged upward with a speed that was not physical but *sakti*-driven, a whip of corrupted light that lashed toward his ankle and missed by inches, recoiling back into the mud with a sucking, disappointed sound that was not a sound at all but a sensation, a tremor of thwarted appetite transmitted through the ground and up through the soles of his feet.

The keris *sang* in response. Not the usual whisper -- a full-throated resonance that vibrated through the wrapping and the harness and Kian Santang's spine and filled his skull with a harmony so beautiful and so terrible that he staggered, his vision doubling, the coral corridor splitting into two overlapping images: the real and the *sakti*-seen. In the *sakti*-seen version, the dead lights were not beneath the mud but standing *upon* it -- an army of spectral figures, translucent, luminous, their ancient armour flowing like water on their insubstantial frames, their weapons raised, their mouths open in a battle cry that had been sustained for centuries without breath and without sound, a silent scream that the keris could hear and was now broadcasting into Kian Santang's consciousness with the indiscriminate enthusiasm of a herald announcing a homecoming.

*Your brothers,* the keris whispered. *Your inheritance. The army that waits for the bearer. Take me out. Hold me up. They will follow. They will rise from the mud and follow the blade and you will have a host, a legion, an army of the deathless dead, and with them you can march to the gate and--*

"*BERHENTI!*" Kian Santang cried aloud -- *Stop!* -- and the word burst from him in a voice that was not entirely his own but was layered, doubled, the human cry riding atop a deeper register that belonged to the blade itself, as though the keris had tried to shape his shout into a command and he had wrested it back, twisting the sound away from dark authority and toward simple, human anguish. The dead lights flared in response -- all of them, simultaneously, a pulse of luminescence that lit the coral marshes from below like lightning frozen in the ground -- and then subsided, dimming back to their individual glows, and the spectral army in the *sakti*-seen version flickered and fragmented and dissolved, and the corridor was just a corridor again, white coral and grey mud and the stink of decay and the dark.

Kian Santang found himself on his knees. He did not remember falling. His hands were buried wrist-deep in the mud on either side of the coral path, and the cold ooze gripped his forearms with a pressure that was more than suction, more than physics -- a deliberate, possessive embrace that tightened as he tried to pull free, as though the mud itself were alive and hungry and had tasted the *sakti* bleeding through the keris's wrapping and wanted *more*.

"*Bantuan!*" he gasped -- *Help!* -- and Bagus was there, always there, the gardener's thick hands closing around Kian Santang's wrists and pulling, heaving, his boots braced on the coral ridge, his back arched against the mud's grip, and with a wet, obscene *pop* the ooze released its hold and Kian Santang came free, sprawling backward onto the path, his arms streaked with grey slime that steamed faintly in the cool air as though the mud had been heated by contact with the keris's *sakti*.

Si Burik was crouched three paces ahead, his entire body vibrating with terror, his pale eyes enormous in his skull-thin face. He had pressed himself into a crevice between two coral formations, making himself as small as possible, and his lips were moving in a rapid, voiceless recitation that might have been a prayer or might have been a catalogue of regrets or might have been the creature's own version of a survival mantra: *small, small, be small, too small to see, too small to want, small as nothing, small as the shadow of nothing...*

"*Gollum... gollum... gollum...*" The verbal tic came in rapid, choking clusters, as though the creature were trying to cough up something lodged in his throat -- the physical residue of a terror so acute it had become a blockage, an obstruction in the airway of his courage. "Si Burik *told* master! Si Burik *warned*! *Mulah* look, Si Burik said! *Mulah* touch the mud! The *jimat* calls them, master -- the *jimat* is a beacon, a *torch* in their dark, and they will *reach* for it, they will *always* reach for it! We must go faster. Faster, master. *Gollum.* Before the lights remember the shape of wanting and learn to climb."

*Come,* the lights whispered. Not in words. In *pull*. A gravitational tug, gentle but insistent, drawing Kian Santang's feet toward the edge of the solid coral path, toward the mud, toward the lights. *Come down. Come join us. You carry a blade of the same fire that traps us. You belong here. Come down.*

The keris flared on his back -- a pulse of heat that raced down his spine and spread through his limbs like the rush of *arak* on an empty stomach. His vision blurred. The world tilted. The mud was no longer mud but water -- warm, inviting, the swimming hole in Padalarang where he and Bagus had spent every summer afternoon of their boyhood, and the lights were not dead warriors but fireflies, the *kunang-kunang* of the riverbank, and if he just stepped off the path, just one step, he would be home, he would be swimming in the warm water with fireflies above him and the stars beyond them and no weight on his back and no voice in his head and no war and no death and no keris and no--

"*KIAN!*"

Bagus's hand closed on his collar. The thick, gardener's fist bunched the fabric at the back of his neck and *hauled*, dragging him backward with a force that was part physical and part spiritual, the pull of a living bond against the pull of the dead. Kian Santang's feet left the edge of the path -- he had been standing on the very lip of the coral ridge, one foot already extended over the mud, his body angled forward in the posture of a diver preparing to enter the water -- and he staggered back, gasping, the vision shattering around him like a pane of glass.

The mud was mud again. The lights were dead lights. The face beneath the surface stared up at him with its empty, preserved eyes, and its mouth was not smiling, had never been smiling, was locked in the rictus of a death scream centuries old.

"*Karuhun!*" Bagus was shouting, his face close to Kian Santang's, his hands on the younger Tuyul's shoulders, shaking him. "You almost went in! You were *leaning* -- *Aing* could see you leaning -- your eyes were open but you were not there, Kian, you were *gone* --"

"The keris," Kian Santang whispered. His throat was dry. His heart hammered against his ribs with the violence of a trapped animal. "It pulled me. It wanted me to go to them."

"The dead do not want company," Bagus said, his voice fierce and frightened. "The dead want to be dead. Whatever is pulling you, it is not them. It is the *blade*."

"Yes," Kian Santang said. He pressed his palms against his eyes. Behind the pressure, he saw afterimages of the dead faces, floating in the dark of his own skull. "Yes. It is always the blade."

Si Burik crouched on a coral head nearby, watching them with his pale, luminous eyes. His expression was complex -- the warring faces of the creature's divided soul both visible in the flickering half-light. Burik, the helpful guide, looked pitying. Burik, the treacherous hunger, looked *calculating*, as though measuring the keris-bearer's weakness and filing the measurement away for future use.

"Si Burik warned," the creature said softly. "*Mulah* look, Si Burik said. But the *jimat* makes him look. The *jimat* always makes them look. *Gollum.* Si Burik knows. Si Burik looked once, too. Long ago. Before the *jimat* was taken."

"What did you see?" Kian Santang asked.

Si Burik's face twisted. For a moment -- just a moment -- something surfaced behind the pale eyes that was neither cunning nor madness but grief, a sorrow so old and so compressed that it had fossilized, like the coral around them, into something hard and permanent and beyond repair.

"Si Burik saw... Si Burik saw his *priya*," the creature whispered. "His friend. In the mud. With the dead lights. His friend who was there when Si Burik found the *jimat*. His friend who Si Burik... *gollum*... *gollum*..." The creature's voice broke. He turned away, his bony shoulders shaking, and pressed his face against the coral. "Not talking about it. Not talking. Go now. Go quickly. The dead sea is big and the night is short and we must be through before the sun finds us on the flat."

They went. Kian Santang walked in the center of the path, his eyes fixed firmly on Si Burik's retreating back, and Bagus walked behind him with one hand on his shoulder, a tether, an anchor, a lifeline connecting the keris-bearer to the world of the living. The dead lights drifted and pulsed in the mud on either side, and the faces looked up -- warrior after warrior after warrior, an army of the unmourned, a host of the unburied, their frozen eyes reflecting a light that came from nowhere and illuminated nothing -- and Kian Santang did not look. He held his gaze forward and his mind closed and he walked, and the keris whispered, and he did not listen, and the dead called, and he did not answer, and the coral corridors wound through the marshes like the passages of a labyrinth built by a god who had forgotten the way out.

But the keris did not relent. Twice more before the crossing was done, the blade found purchase in the unguarded moments -- the half-second between one step and the next when the mind relaxes its vigilance, when the rhythm of walking lulls the will into the microsleep of mechanical motion. The first time, Kian Santang found himself veering toward a cluster of blue-white lights that burned with a particular intensity, a knot of fallen *Karuhun* whose combined *bayu* created a gravitational well in the *sakti*-scape so powerful that even the keris seemed to strain toward it, and it was Bagus's hand on his shoulder -- tightening, redirecting, a wordless course correction applied with the matter-of-fact competence of a helmsman adjusting a tiller -- that pulled him back to the path. The second time was worse. The keris showed him something: a vision, sharp and detailed, of a Sundanese warrior in the mud below whose face was not ancient but *young*, barely older than Kian Santang himself, and whose armour bore the crest of Padalarang -- the leaping deer of his own village's ancient heraldry -- and the warrior's dead eyes looked up at him with an expression that was not entreaty but *recognition*, as though this ghost of a soldier dead for five hundred years knew him, claimed him, was reaching upward through the mud to welcome a kinsman into the fellowship of the honourably fallen, and Kian Santang's foot was in the mud, ankle-deep, sinking, before Bagus's shout and Si Burik's shriek combined to shatter the vision and haul him back.

After that, Bagus took the keris-bearer's arm and did not release it. They walked the rest of the crossing linked like prisoners, Bagus's grip on Kian Santang's forearm so tight that it would leave bruises that would not fade for a week, and Kian Santang submitted to the indignity without protest because the alternative was the mud and the lights and the long, companionable silence of the dead, and he was not ready for that silence. Not yet. Not while there was still a fire to walk toward and a blade to break and a world to save.

Si Burik, ahead of them, had begun to sing -- if the thin, tuneless drone that escaped his lipless mouth could be called singing. It was a fragment of an old Sundanese *tembang*, the melody shattered and reassembled by centuries of solitude into something unrecognizable, the words reduced to phonemes without meaning: *na-na-na, gol-lum, na-na-na*. But it was *sound* -- living sound, breath-made sound, the noise of a creature that was still alive and intended to remain so -- and in the silence of the dead coral marshes, where the only other sounds were the suck of mud and the creak of ancient calcium and the wordless whisper of ten thousand trapped *bayu*, Si Burik's broken song was a defiance, a declaration, a thin thread of the living world strung through the territory of the dead.

They emerged near dawn. The eastern sky was a dull copper smear, the sun still invisible behind the caldera's bulk, its light filtering through layers of ash and smoke to produce a luminescence that was not quite daylight and not quite dark -- a crepuscular limbo, a twilight without origin, as though the sky could not decide whether this place deserved the dignity of a proper sunrise.

Behind them, the dead coral marshes stretched back into the depression like a field of white bone. The dead lights were fading as the light grew -- sinking deeper into the mud, retreating from the approaching day the way all ghosts retreat from morning, pulling their cold luminescence down into the dark substrates where they would wait, patient and eternal, for the next night and the next traveler foolish enough to look.

Kian Santang turned his back on them and did not look again.

## III. First Sight of the Black Gate

The ridge was a spine of obsidian -- raw, unweathered, sharp as the edge of a freshly broken bottle. It rose from the ash fields like the dorsal fin of a vast, buried creature, its surface smooth and black and viciously reflective, catching the dull copper light and throwing it back in distorted flashes that made the eyes flinch.

Si Burik led them to the crest on his belly, crawling the last hundred meters over the obsidian with a silence so complete that Kian Santang could not hear him even from five paces away. The creature's pale, mottled skin slid over the dark glass without friction, without sound, a demonstration of stealth so thorough it was almost supernatural.

"Down," Si Burik whispered when they reached the top. "Flat. No heads above the line. *Gollum.* The Buta have eyes, the Leak have more eyes, and the big Eye... the big Eye never closes."

Kian Santang lay flat on the obsidian, feeling its coolness against his chest and belly -- a welcome respite from the ground-heat of the ash fields. The surface was smooth enough to serve as a mirror, and he caught his own reflection in it as he inched forward: a gaunt, ash-covered face, older than its twenty years, the eyes dark-ringed and haunted, the jaw set in a line of grim determination that he did not feel but had learned to wear. Beside him, Bagus settled with a grunt, his broader frame less comfortable on the narrow ridge, his breath coming in careful, measured sips through his ash-clogged nostrils.

Kian Santang peered over the edge.

And the world fell away.

The far side of the ridge dropped in a sheer cliff to a vast, flat plain of ash and cooled lava, and this plain ran north and east for perhaps three miles before terminating abruptly at the base of a structure so immense that the eye refused to process it as a single object and instead broke it into components, each one massive enough to be a landmark in its own right.

The *Gerbang Hideung*. The Black Gate of Krakatau.

It was not a gate in any architectural sense. There were no doors, no arch, no frame. It was a wall -- a sheer, vertical face of obsidian, three hundred feet high and half a mile wide, carved from the living rock of the caldera rim by the Shadow King's dark *sakti* in the First Age of the world. It filled the only natural pass through the caldera's outer ring -- a narrow defile between two towering cliffs of volcanic basalt, each one rising a thousand feet above the gate's crown, their summits lost in the perpetual smoke that clung to the mountain like a shroud. The gate had no seams. No joints. No mortar lines. It was a single, continuous slab of volcanic glass, polished by dark enchantment to a finish so perfect that it reflected the sky and the smoke and the fire of the distant eruptions in its surface with the fidelity of a mirror, and the reflection was *wrong* -- darker than the reality, more vivid, as though the gate were not reflecting the world but *improving* on it, showing a version of things that was truer to the Shadow King's vision: a world of fire and darkness and absolute, unchallengeable power.

At its base, the gate met the ground in a joint so precise that not even the volcanic ash could find a way through. No wind penetrated. No water seeped. No creature, no matter how small, could have passed between the gate and the ground without the Shadow King's permission. It was sealed by *mantra* -- by a spell of such dark potency that even at this distance, three miles away, Kian Santang could feel it: a pressure in his sinuses, a hum in his teeth, a subsonic vibration that was not sound but *intention*, the concentrated will of a being who had lived for a thousand years and had devoted every moment of that millennium to the perfection of control.

But it was not the gate itself that froze the blood in Kian Santang's veins.

It was the army.

Thousands of fires burned in the ash fields before the Black Gate. Not campfires -- war-fires, great bonfires of scavenged timber and *kerosene* and whatever combustible material the Buta could find in this barren landscape, fed by a supply train that must have stretched back through the caldera's interior to forests Kian Santang could not see. The fires were arranged in a grid pattern, a military encampment of terrifying precision, each fire marking the center of a company of Buta Raksasa, and the companies were arranged in regiments, and the regiments in divisions, and the divisions stretched back from the gate's base toward the ridgeline where Kian Santang lay, covering the plain in a carpet of grey-green flesh and black iron and flickering orange light.

He tried to count. He gave up. The numbers defeated the mind the way the gate defeated the eye -- not because they were beyond comprehension but because comprehension led to conclusions that the mind refused to accept. There were tens of thousands. There might have been a hundred thousand. The Buta Raksasa were there in a concentration that exceeded anything Kian Santang had imagined, a massing of force that made the battles described in the *wayang* stories -- Bharatayuda, the great war of the Pandawa and Korawa, with its million warriors and its eighteen days of slaughter -- seem like village disputes settled with sticks.

They were not all the same. From the ridge, Kian Santang could distinguish at least three kinds of Buta in the firelight. The common foot soldiers -- the grey-green, tusked, armored masses that he had encountered before -- made up the bulk of the force, squatting around their fires in clusters of fifty or a hundred, their weapons stacked beside them in pyramids of iron: curved swords, broad-headed spears, the heavy, hook-bladed *arit* that the Buta favored for close combat. These were large -- each one standing a head taller than a tall man, broad-shouldered, thick-limbed, built for violence the way a *kerbau* is built for the plow, with the same dull, powerful patience.

But among them moved creatures that were larger. Half again the height of the common Buta, with skin the color of dried blood and muscles that stood out beneath their armor like cables beneath leather. These were the *Buta Galak* -- the Fierce Buta, bred or forged or conjured in the caldera's depths for the specific purpose of war, and they moved through the lesser ranks with the casual dominance of wolves among sheep, clearing paths by presence alone, their red eyes surveying the host with an intelligence that the common Buta lacked.

And commanding them all -- scattered through the encampment at intervals that Kian Santang recognized, with sick certainty, as the disposition of a professional army's command structure -- were the Leak.

The Leak commanders sat apart from the Buta, each one occupying a raised platform of piled stone at the center of a cleared space where no fire burned and no Buta willingly stood. They were spectral figures -- tall, impossibly thin, robed in darkness that was not fabric but shadow itself, clinging to their bodies the way smoke clings to a candle, shifting and curling with every movement. Their faces were masks of white bone, elongated and angular, with empty eye sockets from which cold light leaked like starlight through keyholes. Each Leak sat upon a *tungangan* -- a shadow-steed, a creature of pure darkness that had the suggestion of a horse without the substance, a nightmare given just enough form to carry a rider, its hooves striking no sound from the stone, its breath leaving no vapor in the air.

The war drums beat. Not a single drum but thousands, each regiment maintaining its own rhythm, the combined effect a low, tectonic percussion that rose from the plain and filled the air with a vibration Kian Santang could feel in his breastbone, in the roots of his teeth, in the fragile, terrified chambers of his heart. The drums did not keep time. They *were* time -- the Shadow King's time, the heartbeat of his dominion, the metronome that counted the remaining moments of the free world's existence.

"*Karuhun,*" Bagus breathed beside him, and the word was not an exclamation but a prayer. "*Karuhun*... there are too many. Too many fires. Too many eyes. Too many noses."

Si Burik crouched a few paces away, his body pressed flat against the obsidian, his pale eyes reflecting the distant firelight with a cold, phosphorescent glow. He watched the army with the expression of one who has seen it before -- not surprise, not horror, but the resigned, bone-deep terror of a creature that knows exactly what it is looking at and has known for years and has never found a way to stop being afraid.

"Big army," Si Burik whispered. "Bigger than last time Si Burik came. Getting bigger every day. More Buta from the mountain. More Leak from the east. More *everything*. *Gollum.* The Master is calling them. Calling them all. He is getting ready."

"Ready for what?" Kian Santang asked, though he knew the answer.

"War," Si Burik said. "The last war. The war that ends things." He shivered and pulled at his ears. "Si Burik does not want to be in the war. Si Burik wants the *jimat* and a dark cave and *fish*, nice raw fish, and silence. *Gollum... gollum.* But the Master will not give silence. The Master will give fire."

Kian Santang gripped the obsidian ridge until his knuckles whitened. The keris was awake on his back -- fully, urgently, unmistakably awake, humming with a dark, eager resonance that was no longer a whisper but a song, a hymn of recognition, a call to its maker across the miles of ash and fire and stone. It knew where it was. It knew its master was near. The blade's *sakti* -- that terrible, corrupted power that had been poured into it at its forging -- responded to the proximity of the caldera the way iron filings respond to a magnet: aligning, orienting, pulling with a force that was physical and metaphysical at once.

*Home,* the keris sang. *You have brought me home. Now open the gate. Call to him. He will welcome you. He will take the burden. You will rest. You will finally, finally rest.*

The seduction was almost unbearable. Kian Santang felt it in every cell -- the promise of relief, of surrender, of an end to this walking and this carrying and this slow, grinding erosion of self. The keris did not threaten. It did not command. It *offered*, with a generosity that was more dangerous than any threat, the one thing Kian Santang wanted most: rest. An end. The laying down of the burden that was breaking his back and bending his mind and wearing his soul to a thin, grey transparency.

All he had to do was stand up. Walk down the ridge. Walk across the plain, through the fires, through the army that would part before the bearer of the blade the way the sea parts before the bow of a ship. Walk to the gate, which would open at the keris's command -- *his* command, the bearer's command, because bearer and blade were becoming one, merging at the edges, the border between Kian Santang and the Keris Murka growing thinner with every day. Walk through the gate, into the caldera, to the throne of the Shadow King who would take the blade and free him and the world would burn but *he would rest* --

"No." The word came from his mouth like a stone from a sling -- hard, fast, aimed. "No."

He pressed his forehead against the cold obsidian and ground it there, using the sharp pain to cut through the keris's silk-soft persuasion. His breathing was ragged. His hands shook. Sweat and ash mingled on his face in grey streaks that looked like war paint.

*Aing will not call him,* Kian Santang thought, and the thought was not a fortress now but a blade of its own -- smaller, duller, but wielded with a desperate conviction that gave it an edge. *Aing will throw you into his fire. Aing will silence you forever. Aing did not walk through the ash fields and the dead coral and the stinking mud to give you back. Aing did not watch Eyang Guru fall into the dark so that you could go home. You do not have a home. You are a weapon. And aing am going to break you.*

The keris fell silent. Not silenced -- *withdrawn*, retreating into itself with the patience of something that has eternity to work with and can afford to wait. It would try again. It always tried again. But for now, for this moment, Kian Santang's will held, and the song died to a hum, and the hum died to a pulse, and the pulse settled into the steady, background throb of dark *sakti* that had become, over the weeks of carrying, as familiar as his own heartbeat.

"We cannot pass," Kian Santang said, and his voice was steady, and he was proud of that. "Not through the gate."

Bagus let out a breath. Relief, Kian Santang thought. Relief that the bearer had not stood up and walked into the dark. Relief tempered by the understanding that not going through the gate meant finding another way, and there might not be another way, and if there was, it would be worse.

"We find another way," Kian Santang said. He turned to Si Burik. "Is there another way, Si Burik? You said you knew the paths. *Mulah* lie to me now."

## IV. Debate and Decision

Si Burik's eyes shifted.

The movement was barely perceptible -- a flicker of the pale irises, a contraction of the enlarged pupils -- but Kian Santang had been watching the creature for weeks now, studying the mercurial geography of his face the way a navigator studies a coast, and he read the shift as clearly as text: Si Burik was about to reveal something, and the revelation cost him. Something in the creature wanted to speak and something did not, and the war between the two was playing out in the muscles around his eyes and the twitch of his lipless mouth and the rapid, unconscious clenching and unclenching of his long, bony fingers.

The creature looked away, toward the high, jagged peaks that formed the western wall of the caldera. A shadow passed over his face -- a look of cunning warred with a look of genuine terror, and the terror won, briefly, before the cunning rallied and imposed a compromise that was neither helpful nor treacherous but something worse: strategic.

"Aya... aya jalan sejean," he whispered. "There is... another way. A secret way. A dark way."

"Where?" Bagus asked, immediately suspicious. The gardener had not trusted Si Burik from the first moment of their association, and the passage through the dead coral marshes -- during which the creature had several times drifted close to Kian Santang's pack, close to the wrapped keris, with movements that were not quite accidental and excuses that were not quite convincing -- had deepened that suspicion into something approaching certainty. Bagus was sure Si Burik would betray them. The only questions were when and how.

"Up," Si Burik said, pointing with a trembling finger toward a narrow, almost invisible cleft in the rock face, high above the main pass where the Black Gate stood. It was barely visible in the haze -- a dark line in darker stone, a crack in the caldera's western wall that might have been a shadow or a geological feature or nothing at all. "Up the stairs. The secret stairs. *Tangga Kalajengking.* The Stairs of the Scorpion."

The name hung in the sulfurous air like a curse.

"Stairs," Kian Santang repeated. "Stairs to where?"

"Over the wall. Over the caldera rim. Into the tunnel. Into the inside." Si Burik's words came faster now, tumbling over each other in the creature's agitation, his Sundanese and his fractured Indonesian mixing with sounds that might have been words in a language older than either. "The stairs are old. Older than the Gate. Older than the Buta. They were carved by... by the people who were here before. Before the Master came. Before the mountain was his." He paused, licked his lips with a grey, furtive tongue. "The stairs are steep. The stairs are hard. But the stairs are *secret*. The Buta do not go there. The Leak do not fly there."

"Why not?" Kian Santang asked.

Si Burik's shiver this time was violent -- a full-body convulsion that started at his hairless skull and rippled down through his emaciated frame to his splayed, grey feet. He hugged himself, his long arms wrapping around his ribcage as though trying to hold his body together against a centrifugal force of pure dread.

"Sieun... sieun ku *Nyi Blorong*..." he breathed. *Afraid... afraid of Nyi Blorong.*

The name struck the air differently from the first. *Tangga Kalajengking* had been a curse. *Nyi Blorong* was a prayer -- the kind of prayer uttered not to invoke protection but to acknowledge the existence of something so far beyond protection that the only appropriate response was submission. The honorific *Nyi* -- Lady, Mistress -- carried the weight of a title bestowed not out of respect but out of the desperate, self-preserving politeness of creatures who know that the entity they address could destroy them with a thought.

"Nyi Blorong?" Bagus paled. He knew the name. Every child in Sunda knew the name. It was the name mothers whispered when they wanted children to stay indoors at night, the name the *dalang* invoked when the wayang story required a horror beyond the ordinary catalogue of Buta and Raksasa and Leak. "The Serpent Queen? The Weaver of Shadows?"

"She lives there," Si Burik hissed, his voice dropping to a register so low it was barely audible above the distant pulse of the war drums. "She lives in the tunnel above the stairs. Has lived there since before the Master came. She is old... old... older than the mountain, maybe. She was here when the sea was here. She was here when the coral was alive. She watched the Master raise the mountain and push the water away, and she did not stop him, because she is not *against* the Master. She is *apart* from the Master. She is her own thing. Her own hunger. Her own dark."

"And you want us to walk past her," Bagus said flatly.

"She sleeps!" Si Burik protested, his voice rising to a desperate whine. "She sleeps mostly! She is old and she has eaten well and she sleeps in her web for years, for decades, waking only when something strays into her tunnel, something foolish, something lost. If we are quiet... if we are small... if we are *fast*... we can pass. Into the tunnel beyond her. Into the caldera."

"And if she wakes?" Kian Santang asked.

Si Burik said nothing. His silence was answer enough.

Bagus turned to Kian Santang with a look of controlled desperation. "Kian. Listen to me. This creature is leading us into a trap. He is taking us to this... this monster, this Nyi Blorong, because he wants the keris for himself and he knows that if the serpent kills us, he can take it from our bodies."

"No!" Si Burik shrieked, so loudly that Kian Santang hissed and clapped a hand over the creature's mouth. They were still on the ridge, still within possible sight of Buta scouts, and a shriek in the silence of the ash fields would carry for miles.

"Quiet," Kian Santang commanded, and the authority in his voice surprised him -- it was the voice the keris gave him, the bearer's voice, the voice of one who commanded because the blade commanded and the blade was the Shadow King's and the Shadow King's voice had commanded armies. He did not like the voice. He used it anyway. "Quiet, Si Burik. Explain."

The creature whimpered behind Kian Santang's palm. When the hand was removed, he spoke in a whisper that trembled like a plucked string.

"Si Burik does not want master dead. Si Burik wants master *alive*. Master carries the *jimat*. If master is dead, the *jimat* goes into the fire, and then... and then..." His face crumpled. The warring halves of his divided soul collapsed toward each other in a moment of unified anguish. "And then Si Burik has nothing. Nothing! No *jimat*. No master. No hope. Just... dark. And alone. And dying, slowly, because the *jimat* kept Si Burik alive and without it, Si Burik is just... *gollum*... just meat that hasn't stopped walking."

The creature's grief was genuine. Kian Santang was certain of that, as certain as he could be of anything involving a creature whose personality was fractured into warring pieces. Si Burik needed the keris the way a drowning man needs air -- not because it was good for him, not because it would save him, but because his body had forgotten how to exist without it. The keris had sustained him for centuries, stretching his life far beyond its natural span, and the stretching had become structural, load-bearing, woven into the fabric of his biology so thoroughly that removing it would not free him but collapse him.

But the grief did not make him trustworthy. A drowning man will pull his rescuer under without hesitation, without malice, without thought, because drowning is not a moral condition but a physical one, and the body's need to breathe supersedes all other considerations.

Kian Santang looked at the Black Gate. He looked at the army. He looked at the cleft in the western wall, high and dark and barely visible.

A thought rose in him then -- unbidden, unwanted, shaped not by the keris but by the same blunt arithmetic of sacrifice that had driven Eyang Guru to the bridge and the fire and the fall. It arrived with the cold clarity of a conclusion that has been forming beneath consciousness for days and now surfaces, fully formed, like a body rising from the dead coral marshes: inevitable, terrible, and already decaying.

*What if aing goes alone?*

Not to the stairs. To the gate. To the army. Walk down the ridge in plain sight, the keris unwrapped, its *sakti* blazing like a torch in the *sakti*-scape, the Shadow King's own weapon returning to its master. The Buta would part. The Leak would bow. The gate would open. And every eye -- every red, burning, disciplined eye in that hundred-thousand-strong host -- would turn toward the lone figure walking across the plain, and while they watched, while the Shadow King's attention was fixed on the returning blade, Bagus could slip through the unguarded western flank, find another way into the caldera, reach the fires of the forge from the other side--

*Aing could be the distraction,* Kian Santang thought, and the thought was so seductive in its selflessness that for a moment he could not distinguish it from the keris's own whisper. Was this his idea or the blade's? Was the impulse to sacrifice himself a genuine expression of love for his friend and his mission, or was it the keris's latest stratagem -- wrapping surrender in the language of heroism, disguising capitulation as courage? The keris *wanted* to go to the gate. The keris *wanted* to be carried to its master. If Kian Santang walked across the plain, the blade would achieve its objective regardless of the walker's intention, and the nobility of his motives would be a footnote in the history of the Shadow King's victory.

But the thought persisted, because the arithmetic was not wrong. One life traded for the survival of the mission. Eyang Guru had made that calculation on the bridge. Kian Santang's own mathematics pointed to the same sum, and the elegance of it -- the clean, terrible efficiency of sacrifice -- pulled at him with a gravity that was not the keris's but his own weariness, his own desire for the burden to end, dressed in the robes of duty.

"There is another way," Kian Santang said slowly, and his voice was too calm, too measured, and Bagus heard the wrongness in it immediately.

"What way?" The gardener's eyes narrowed. He had the look of a man who has heard the first note of a melody he recognizes and does not like.

"*Aing* walk to the gate. Alone. With the keris uncovered. Every eye in the army turns to *aing*. Every Leak, every Buta, every scout and sentinel. They will all watch. They cannot help it -- the keris is the Shadow King's own weapon, and its return is the event they have been waiting for. While they watch..." He paused, swallowed. The plan was building itself in his mind with a momentum that frightened him, each piece clicking into place with the precision of a *wayang* narrative where the hero's death is foreordained and the audience already weeps. "While they watch, you take the western route. The stairs. The tunnel. Si Burik leads you. You carry the-- no." He stopped. The flaw in the plan was obvious, a crack that ran through the foundation of the logic. He could not separate himself from the keris. The keris was the mission. The distraction and the objective were the same object, bound to his back.

Bagus saw the flaw at the same moment. But the gardener did not address the flaw. He addressed the intention behind it, because the flaw was secondary to the thing that truly frightened him.

"You want to die," Bagus said. Not a question. Not an accusation. A diagnosis, delivered with the flat, unflinching precision of a healer who has identified the disease and will not soften the naming of it. "You want to walk into the army and give yourself up and call it sacrifice. You want to lie down, Kian. The keris has been telling you to lie down since the mangrove swamps, and you have been saying no, and now you are trying to find a way to say *yes* that sounds like *no*."

The words struck Kian Santang like a fist. Not because they were cruel but because they were accurate -- accurate in the way that Bagus had always been accurate about Kian Santang's heart, reading the subtext beneath the text, the fatigue beneath the courage, the despair beneath the stubbornness. Bagus could not hear the keris, but he could hear *Kian Santang*, and what he heard now was a young man rehearsing his own death and calling it strategy.

"*Aing* am not--" Kian Santang began.

"You are," Bagus said. "And *aing* will not let you. Not because the plan is bad -- the plan *is* bad, it does not work, you cannot separate the keris from yourself, and even if you could, walking to the Shadow King is not a distraction, it is a *delivery*. But even if the plan were perfect, *aing* would not let you. Because Eyang Guru did not throw himself from the bridge so that you could throw yourself from a different bridge three weeks later. He did not buy your life so that you could spend it in the first marketplace you found." The gardener's voice was shaking now, not with anger but with the effort of containing something much larger than anger -- a love so fierce and so terrified that it had no room for gentleness. "*Aing* promised him, Kian. On the bridge. Before he fell. *Aing* promised him *aing* would bring you to the fire and bring you home. Both parts. Not one. Both."

The silence that followed was not the ash-field silence or the dead-coral silence or the war-drum silence. It was the silence between two people who have known each other since childhood and have run out of lies and have arrived at the place where only truth will serve, and the truth is a country neither of them knows how to navigate.

Si Burik, who had been watching this exchange with the avid, darting attention of a creature for whom the emotions of others are both incomprehensible and fascinating -- a naturalist observing an unfamiliar species -- chose this moment to interject. His voice was small, thin, nearly inaudible, but it carried the weight of an observation that cut through the human drama with the precision of a creature who has survived for centuries by understanding the mechanics of need.

"The fat one is right," Si Burik whispered. "*Gollum.* The fat one is always right about the thin one. Si Burik sees it. Si Burik knows the wanting-to-stop. Si Burik has felt it... the *jimat* shows you a door and calls it a wall, shows you a surrender and calls it a fight. *Gollum... gollum.* Si Burik fell through that door once. Long ago. Si Burik's friend... Si Burik's *priya*..." He trailed off, his pale eyes going distant, sinking into a memory that was centuries old and still unhealed. "Si Burik's *priya* tried to take the *jimat* from Si Burik. To save Si Burik. And Si Burik... the *jimat* made Si Burik think that fighting was the same as protecting, and Si Burik..." He made a sound that was not a word and not a sob and not a cough but all three at once, a *gollum* that was not a verbal tic but a name for the thing that lived in his throat, the unswallowable lump of ancient guilt that had been his companion longer than the keris itself. "The *jimat* lies, master. It lies with your own voice. It lies with your own wanting. *Mulah* listen to the wanting. Listen to the fat one. The fat one does not lie."

"*Mulah* call me fat," Bagus muttered, but there was no heat in it, only the reflexive maintenance of a dignity that the moment did not require.

Kian Santang closed his eyes. Behind the lids, the keris showed him the gate -- always the gate, the beautiful, terrible, obsidian gate, opening like arms, like a mother's arms, like the end of all walking and all carrying and all pain. He did not push the image away. He held it. He looked at it. He examined it the way Eyang Guru had taught him to examine a *mantra* -- not with acceptance, not with rejection, but with the dispassionate curiosity of a mind that seeks to understand the mechanism of its own deception.

The gate was not rest. The gate was death. Not the clean death of a warrior on a battlefield but the slow, corrosive death of a will consumed by the thing it carried -- the death of *Kian Santang*, the person, dissolved into the Keris Murka, the weapon, until there was no bearer, only blade, and the blade walked to its master on borrowed legs and surrendered on borrowed lips and the boy from Padalarang was gone, was nowhere, was a footnote in the history of a weapon's homecoming.

He opened his eyes.

"You are right," he said to Bagus. The words were simple. They cost everything. "You are right. *Aing* was lying to *aing*self. The keris..." He paused. Drew a breath of the sulfurous air. Let it out. "The keris dressed it up. Made it look like bravery. But it was the same thing it always offers. Surrender with a different name."

Bagus said nothing. He reached out and gripped Kian Santang's forearm -- the *silat* clasp, hand to wrist -- and held it, and the grip said everything the mouth did not, and Kian Santang held it back, and for a moment the keris was silent, genuinely silent, not withdrawn but *outmatched*, its whisper overwhelmed by the louder, warmer, more ancient *sakti* of two friends holding on.

He calculated. Not with the cold precision of a strategist -- he was not Gajahmada, not Raden Wijaya, not trained in the calculus of war. He calculated with the blunt, practical arithmetic of a village boy who has spent his life weighing options and choosing the least bad one: *This fruit is rotten. That fruit is wormy. Which one do I eat?*

The Gate was impassable. An army of a hundred thousand Buta stood between them and the only direct route into the caldera. Even if the gate could be opened -- and it could, the keris could open it, the keris was *begging* to open it -- passing through it meant walking into the Shadow King's hands. That was not a path. That was a surrender.

Going back was not an option. The dead coral marshes behind them were a nightmare he would not repeat, and beyond them lay the ash fields, and beyond the ash fields lay the mangrove swamps, and beyond the swamps lay the Selat Sunda, and beyond the strait lay Jawa, and Jawa was at war, and the war was being lost, and every day he spent retracing his steps was a day the Shadow King used to strengthen his armies and extend his dominion and forge the chains with which he would bind the free world.

The stairs. The dark way. The path past the sleeping serpent-queen whose name made Buta tremble and Leak change course.

But the legend -- the shape of Nyi Blorong in the architecture of fear -- demanded understanding before it could be confronted. Kian Santang turned to Si Burik, and the question he asked was not tactical but mythological, because in this world, in the world of *sakti* and *jimat* and blades that whispered, mythology and tactics were the same discipline.

"Tell *aing* about her," Kian Santang said. "Everything. Not what the *dalang* says. Not the children's version. What you *know*."

Si Burik's face underwent a transformation that Kian Santang had not seen before -- a unification, the warring halves of the creature's soul briefly consolidated by a terror so fundamental that it left no room for internal conflict. Both the helpful Burik and the treacherous Burik agreed on this: Nyi Blorong was to be feared absolutely, and the fear was not a performance but a fact, as immutable as gravity, as inescapable as the need to breathe.

"She is the *Ratu Ular*," Si Burik said, and the Sunda Kuna title -- Queen of Serpents -- came from his lips with the cadence of a liturgy, a phrase repeated so many times across so many centuries that it had been worn smooth, like the stairs in her domain, polished by the friction of terrified repetition. "She was old when the first *Karuhun* walked the earth. She was old when the sea was young. The *dalang* calls her a monster because the *dalang* needs simple shapes for simple stories, but she is not simple. *Gollum.* She is the thing that was here before the story began. The thing the story was built on top of. She does not serve the Master because the Master is *beneath* her -- not in power, maybe, but in *age*, in *being*. He raised a mountain. She *is* a mountain. He forged a weapon. She *is* a weapon. He commands an army. She needs no army. She is sufficient."

"Then why does she not stop him?" Bagus asked. "If she is so ancient, so powerful -- why does she permit the Shadow King to occupy her mountain?"

Si Burik made a sound that might have been a laugh if laughter were not so alien to his ruined vocal cords -- a dry, clicking exhalation that conveyed amusement without joy. "Does the mountain *permit* the ant to build upon its slope? Does the ocean *permit* the fisherman to cast his net? She does not permit or forbid. She *does not notice*. Or she notices and does not care. The Master's wars, the Master's armies, the Master's great plan to swallow the world -- to Nyi Blorong, these are the affairs of insects. She has her tunnel. She has her web. She has her sleep. She has her hunger. And her hunger is older than the Master's ambition, and it will outlast it, because ambition requires a mind, and minds decay, but hunger is a function of the body, and the body of Nyi Blorong has not decayed in ten thousand years."

It was not a good option. It was the only option that was not capitulation.

"We take the stairs," Kian Santang said.

The words settled into the ash-laden air with the weight of a verdict. They were not spoken loudly. They were not spoken with the keris-given authority he had used on Si Burik. They were spoken in Kian Santang's own voice -- quiet, young, afraid, but resolved. The voice of a boy who has looked at all the doors and found them all locked and has decided to climb through the window, because the house is on fire and the window is all that remains.

Bagus looked at him. Fear was plain in his honest eyes -- not fear of the Stairs of the Scorpion, though that was there, nor fear of Nyi Blorong, though that was there too. Fear *for* Kian Santang. Fear that the keris was making this decision, not the boy. Fear that the blade had found a way to steer them toward a danger that would serve its purposes -- though what those purposes might be, on a staircase above an abyss, Bagus could not imagine.

"Kian," Bagus said carefully. "Nyi Blorong is a legend. A monster from the old tales. The *dalang* spoke of her when we were children. If she is real..."

"Then we must be quieter than legends," Kian Santang said. And despite everything -- the fear, the exhaustion, the keris's relentless erosion of his will -- he managed something that was almost a smile. Small. Grim. But human. The smile of a boy who still remembers what humor feels like, even if he can no longer feel it.

Bagus saw the smile. And Bagus -- practical, loyal, terrified Bagus, who had followed his friend into the mangrove swamps and the ash fields and the dead coral marshes and would follow him into the throat of the volcano itself if the path required it -- made his decision. Not a new decision. The same decision he had been making since childhood, since the first time Kian Santang had proposed something dangerous and stupid and Bagus had said *yes* because saying *no* meant letting his friend go alone, and that was not something Bagus knew how to do.

"Then we take the stairs," Bagus said. He reached out and gripped Kian Santang's forearm, the traditional clasp of the *silat* brotherhood, hand to wrist, strong and warm. "*Aing* won't leave you, Priya. Not on the stairs. Not at the top. Not in the dark. You know that."

"*Aing* know," Kian Santang said, and returned the grip, and for a moment -- just a moment -- the keris's whisper faded to nothing, drowned out by the simple, unremarkable, infinitely powerful warmth of a friend's hand on his arm.

He turned to Si Burik. "Lead us. But if you lead us into a trap, Si Burik... the keris will know. And what it knows, *aing* will know. And *aing* will not be kind."

Si Burik cringed, hiding his face in his hands, pressing his forehead into the obsidian with a servility that was half genuine and half performance. "No trap! No trap! Si Burik good... Si Burik helps the master of the *jimat*... *gollum... gollum*... Si Burik swore by the *jimat* and Si Burik keeps his oath... mostly... *gollum*..."

That *mostly* hung in the air like smoke from a smoldering fire. Kian Santang heard it. Bagus heard it. Neither commented. There are truths that cannot be addressed, only endured.

"Then lead," Kian Santang said. "Now. Before the light grows and the Buta scouts find our scent on the ridge."

## V. The Ascent Begins

They left the ridge in the grey hour before dawn, descending the obsidian spine on its western face -- the side away from the army, away from the gate, away from the thousand fires that marked the Shadow King's mustering strength. The descent was treacherous: the obsidian was smooth as glass and sharp as razors, and their hands and knees left blood on its surface as they slid and scrambled and clutched at nonexistent handholds, their bodies small and dark against the black glass like insects on a mirror.

Si Burik moved ahead, finding the route with his uncanny knowledge of this terrain. He led them down into a dry gully -- an ancient watercourse, carved by rains that no longer fell in this blighted region, now filled with ash and the debris of a thousand years of volcanic activity. The gully ran west, parallel to the caldera's outer wall, its banks high enough to conceal them from any observer on the plain above. They moved through it in single file, bent low, their feet crunching softly in the ash, their breathing deliberately slowed and quieted, three grey figures moving through a grey channel in a grey world.

The night was moonless. Kian Santang had lost track of the lunar calendar somewhere in the ash fields -- the sky had been too hazy, too choked with particulate, to see the moon or the stars for days -- but Si Burik navigated the darkness with the ease of a creature whose eyes were designed for lightless caves and sunless tunnels. He moved by scent and sound and a sixth sense that Kian Santang could only attribute to the centuries the creature had spent wandering the desolation of Krakatau's outskirts, mapping its terrain in the darkness with the obsessive thoroughness of a prisoner memorizing the walls of his cell.

They circled wide around the army's western flank, giving the outermost fires a berth of half a mile. Even at that distance, Kian Santang could smell them -- the Buta stench, that nauseating compound of unwashed flesh and rancid fat and the metallic tang of iron and blood that seemed to follow the creatures like a miasma. He could hear them, too: the guttural bark of their speech, a language that was not language but a collection of noises shaped by tusked mouths and flat tongues into approximations of communication, stripped of nuance, stripped of beauty, a vocabulary of commands and threats and the crude, animal satisfactions of eating and sleeping and anticipating violence.

Once, they came close. Too close.

A Buta patrol -- a squad of eight, moving through the ash fields in a loose formation, their crude iron lanterns swinging, their tusked faces scanning the dark -- crossed the gully ahead of them, and Si Burik hissed a warning and pressed himself flat against the gully wall, and Kian Santang and Bagus did the same, and they lay in the ash with their hearts hammering and their breath held and the keris burning on Kian Santang's back like a brand, its excitement palpable -- *they are close, his soldiers, his children, call to them, let them take you to the gate, it will be so easy* -- while the patrol passed within thirty meters, their boots crunching in the tephra, their lanterns casting swaying circles of orange light that licked at the gully's rim without quite reaching down to where the three fugitives lay.

One of the Buta paused. It raised its massive, flat-nosed head and sniffed the air -- a long, wet, questing inhalation that reminded Kian Santang, with a sick lurch, of Si Burik's own scenting technique. The creature's nostrils flared. Its small, red eyes narrowed. It grunted something to its companions -- a question, perhaps, or a suspicion -- and for a frozen, endless moment, it stood at the gully's edge, its shadow falling across them like a shroud, its breath pluming in the cool air in clouds of rank, meaty vapor.

Si Burik did something. Kian Santang did not see what -- the creature's hands moved in the darkness with a speed and precision that left no visual trace -- but a sound came from the ash field to the east, a soft, crunching noise, as though something were moving through the tephra fifty meters away. The Buta's head swiveled. It grunted again, more sharply, and the patrol changed direction, moving east toward the sound, their lanterns bobbing away into the darkness like retreating fireflies.

They waited. One minute. Two. Five. The patrol's lights dwindled and vanished.

"Stone," Si Burik whispered, answering the question Kian Santang had not asked. "Si Burik threw a stone. Old trick. Buta are strong but Buta are stupid. *Gollum.* Throw a stone, they chase the stone. Simple creatures. Not like us."

The *us* included Kian Santang and Bagus in a fellowship of intelligence that neither of them found entirely comfortable.

They moved on. The gully bent north, then west again, following the contour of the caldera's base. The outer wall of the volcanic complex rose above them on their right -- a sheer face of dark basalt, fractured and fissured by the stresses of geological time, its surface veined with pale deposits of sulfur and calcite that glowed faintly in the starless dark like the lines on a map drawn by a lunatic cartographer. The wall was high -- hundreds of meters of vertical stone, culminating in the jagged, smoke-wreathed peaks of the caldera rim -- and somewhere up there, invisible in the darkness, was the entrance to the Tangga Kalajengking.

Si Burik found it just before dawn.

The entrance was not a doorway. It was a wound -- a narrow, vertical cleft in the basalt wall, perhaps three feet wide and twelve feet tall, its edges raw and unworked, as though the rock had simply cracked along a line of internal stress and parted, revealing the passage within. There was no marker, no sign, no indication that this particular crack in this particular wall was anything other than a geological accident. A traveler could have passed it a hundred times and seen nothing but stone.

But Si Burik saw. He led them to it unerringly, moving along the wall's base with the certainty of one following a trail only he could perceive, and when he reached the cleft, he stopped and pointed into the darkness within.

"The stairs," he said. His voice was different here -- hushed, reverent, almost fearful, the voice of a creature approaching something sacred. Or something profane. "The *Tangga Kalajengking* begins here. Inside, the steps go up. Up and up and up. To the high pass. To the tunnel. To..." He trailed off, pulling at his ears.

"To Nyi Blorong," Bagus finished.

"*Gollum.*" Si Burik neither confirmed nor denied. He simply stood at the cleft's entrance, his pale eyes reflecting the faintest possible light from the greying eastern sky, and waited.

Kian Santang looked at the crack in the wall. It exhaled -- a thin, steady stream of air flowing outward from the darkness within, carrying a scent that was different from the sulfur-and-ash smell of the outer desolation. This air was damp. Cool. It smelled of stone and minerals and something else -- something organic, something ancient, a musk or a secretion that Kian Santang could not identify but that raised the hair on his arms and the back of his neck with an atavistic recognition that predated thought.

He stepped forward. The cleft admitted him reluctantly, its walls pressing against his shoulders, the rock cold and damp against his skin. Behind him, Bagus followed, turning sideways to fit his broader frame through the gap. The darkness closed around them.

Inside, the passage widened into a narrow corridor -- perhaps four feet across, high-ceilinged, carved not by tools but by water, the walls smooth and curving with the organic shapes of a natural dissolution channel. The floor was wet, slippery with a thin film of mineral-rich water that seeped from the walls and collected in shallow pools. And cut into the floor, beginning where the natural passage ended, were the stairs.

The steps were carved from the living rock. Each one was narrow -- barely a foot deep -- and high -- the rise between steps was nearly two feet, designed for legs longer than a Tuyul's, for creatures taller than men. They ascended steeply, almost vertically, disappearing upward into a darkness so complete that even Si Burik's cave-adapted eyes could not penetrate it. The steps were old -- ancient beyond any reckoning Kian Santang could apply. Their edges were worn smooth by the passage of countless feet over countless years, the stone polished by friction and water and time into a treacherous, glassy surface that offered no grip.

But it was the carvings that stopped him.

On the walls beside the stairs, cut into the stone with a precision that no modern chisel could match, were images. Reliefs. Narrative panels, similar in form to the temple carvings of Borobudur or Prambanan but older, far older, in a style that predated the Hindu-Buddhist influences that shaped those monuments. The figures depicted were not human. They were serpentine -- long, sinuous bodies coiling through scenes of what might have been worship or might have been sacrifice, their heads crowned with hoods like cobras, their bodies scaled with a detail so fine that individual scales could be counted. Among the serpent-figures, smaller creatures appeared -- beings that were humanoid but not human, with elongated limbs and large, round eyes, tending the serpents or offering them objects that Kian Santang could not identify in the darkness.

The carvings were the history of a world that had existed before the Shadow King, before the kingdoms of men, before the concept of *good* and *evil* had been imposed on a cosmos that operated, originally, under different rules. They told a story that Kian Santang could not read, in a language he could not speak, about a time he could not imagine. But the serpent motif was unmistakable.

This place belonged to Nyi Blorong. Had always belonged to Nyi Blorong. The stairs were not a path *past* her -- they were a path *through* her domain, tolerated perhaps, or overlooked, or simply irrelevant to a creature whose concerns operated on a scale that made the passage of small, warm-blooded intruders a matter of no consequence.

"Up," Si Burik said from the darkness above. He had already begun to climb, his hands and feet finding purchase on the worn steps with an ease that the humans could not match. "Up, master. Quickly. Quietly. The stairs are long. We climb now. We rest at the landing."

Kian Santang put his foot on the first step. The stone was cold through his worn boot. The keris hummed on his back -- a low, dissonant vibration that was neither encouragement nor warning but something between, the blade's response to entering a place of power that was not its master's, a domain of *sakti* that predated and dwarfed the Shadow King's own.

He climbed.

The ascent was a nightmare of physical suffering. Each step demanded a high, knee-lifting stride that burned the thighs and strained the calves. The narrowness of the passage forced them to climb single-file, one hand on the wall for balance, the other gripping whatever projection of stone or root of mineral deposit presented itself. The surface was treacherous -- wet stone, sulfur deposits that crumbled under pressure, patches of a slick, organic film that might have been algae or might have been something less identifiable. The darkness was total. Si Burik moved by touch and memory; Kian Santang and Bagus moved by faith and desperation, each step an act of trust in the step before it, each handhold a prayer.

The keris grew heavier. Kian Santang felt the increase with every step upward, as though the blade were drawing weight from the stone itself, accumulating mass in proportion to altitude. By the hundredth step, his shoulders ached. By the two-hundredth, the harness straps had cut through his shirt and were grinding against raw skin. By the three-hundredth, his spine was bowing under a weight that he would have sworn was twice what the blade had weighed at the bottom, and the whisper in his head had changed tenor -- no longer seductive, no longer promising rest, but *mocking*, a cold, contemptuous amusement at the spectacle of a small creature struggling to carry a burden that was never meant for small creatures, a burden designed for the hands of kings and the backs of gods.

*You are not enough,* the keris whispered. *You were never enough. A village boy. A nobody. A Tuyul from the sawah. Who are you to carry the weapon of the great? Set me down. Set me down and rest. Let stronger hands take me forward.*

*There are no stronger hands,* Kian Santang thought, and the thought was tinged with a bitterness that frightened him because it tasted like truth. *There is only aing. And aing is climbing.*

Behind him, Bagus climbed without complaint. The stocky Tuyul's breathing was labored -- harsh, rhythmic gusts that echoed in the narrow stairway like the breath of a bellows -- but his steps were steady, and his hand found Kian Santang's heel at intervals, a touch that said *I am here, I am behind you, I am not stopping.* Bagus did not speak. Speaking required breath, and breath was currency in this vertical tunnel, to be spent only on the essential commerce of survival.

Time lost meaning. The stairs went up and up and up, turning in slow spirals that followed the contour of the caldera wall's interior, and the darkness remained absolute, and the cold remained constant, and the only measure of progress was the burning in their muscles and the rawness of their hands and the slow, counting rhythm of step after step after step.

At last, after what might have been hours or might have been centuries of climbing, Si Burik's voice came down to them from above.

"The landing," he whispered. "Rest here."

## VI. The Keeper of the Stairs

The landing was a natural chamber -- a widening of the stairway passage into a roughly circular space, perhaps twenty feet across, with a ceiling high enough to stand upright and walls that curved inward like the interior of a skull. The floor was flat, dry, covered in a fine dust that might have been powdered stone or might have been the desiccated remains of something organic. The air was different here -- warmer, more humid, carrying a scent that Kian Santang recognized from the cleft's entrance but stronger now, thicker, a musk that was unmistakably animal: the scent of a large, living creature in an enclosed space, metabolizing and breathing and existing in the close, warm dark.

Nyi Blorong's scent. They were in her territory now, not at its edge but within it, and the air carried her presence the way water carries salt -- dissolved, pervasive, inseparable from the medium.

Kian Santang sank to the floor with a groan he could not suppress. The keris's weight bore him down the last few inches, pressing him flat on the stone, and for a long moment he simply lay there, his cheek against the cool dust, his breath coming in ragged, tearing gasps, his muscles trembling with the aftermath of the climb. Bagus collapsed beside him, his broad back heaving, his thick hands splayed on the stone, his eyes closed.

Si Burik did not rest. The creature crouched at the chamber's edge, at the point where the stairs continued upward into the darkness, and listened. His head was tilted, his pale eyes half-closed, his entire body oriented toward the tunnel above with the focused stillness of a prey animal monitoring the movements of a predator.

"How far to the top?" Kian Santang asked when his breathing allowed speech.

"Long," Si Burik said. "Many more stairs. But first..." He hesitated. He looked at Kian Santang, then away, then back, his face cycling through expressions too rapidly to read. "First... Si Burik must tell master about her. About the keeper of the stairs."

"Nyi Blorong," Bagus said. He had opened his eyes. He lay on his back, staring at the ceiling of the chamber, his chest still heaving but his voice steady. "Tell us. If we are walking into her mouth, we should at least know the shape of her teeth."

Si Burik settled onto his haunches and wrapped his arms around his knees, making himself as small as possible, as though smallness were a form of protection. When he spoke, his voice was a thin, reedy whisper that barely disturbed the silence of the chamber -- the voice of a creature telling a bedtime story that had haunted his own sleep for centuries.

"She is old," Si Burik began. "Older than the Master. Older than the mountain. She was here before the fire. She was here when this place was under the sea, when the coral was alive, when the water was warm and the fish were fat and the world was... different." He paused, pulling at his ears. "Si Burik does not know what she is. Not a Naga, not exactly. Not a Buta. Not a Leak. She is... she is a thing from before the categories. Before the names. She is what was here when the world was new, before the gods decided what things should be called and what shapes they should take."

"She is a serpent," Kian Santang said.

"She is *many* serpents," Si Burik corrected. "She is one body with many heads, or many bodies with one mind, or... *gollum*... Si Burik does not understand. Si Burik has only seen her once, and Si Burik was running, and Si Burik's eyes were not reliable because Si Burik's legs were doing the thinking." He rocked back and forth, his eyes distant. "She weaves. That is what she does. She spins webs in the tunnel -- not spider webs, not silk. Something else. Something thick, something sticky, something that glows in the dark with a sick, green light. She webs the tunnel from wall to wall, and the web catches things. Buta, sometimes. Creatures that wander up from the caldera. Things that try to use the tunnel to cross the rim. She catches them and she wraps them and she... *gollum*... she keeps them. For later."

"Eats them," Bagus said bluntly.

"Eventually," Si Burik whispered. "But not right away. She keeps them alive. In the web. For a long time. Si Burik has heard them... heard them whimpering in the dark... wrapped in the green threads, hanging from the ceiling like fruit..." He shuddered violently. "Si Burik ran. Si Burik ran very fast. Si Burik is small and the web was high and Si Burik went under. But the master is not small. And the *jimat*..." He looked at the wrapped keris on Kian Santang's back with an expression of mingled longing and dread. "The *jimat* has *sakti*. Nyi Blorong can feel *sakti*. She is drawn to it. Like Si Burik is drawn to it. Like the Leak are drawn to it. The *jimat* is... it is a light in the dark, and she is a thing that hunts by the light."

Silence. The musk in the air seemed thicker. The warmth of the chamber seemed more oppressive. Kian Santang felt the keris respond to the silence with a pulse of cold amusement that he chose to interpret as coincidence.

"Can the keris be hidden?" Bagus asked. "Wrapped more thickly? Covered?"

Si Burik shook his head. "The *jimat's* *sakti* cannot be covered. Not by cloth. Not by stone. It bleeds through everything. She will know we are there. The question is... *gollum*... the question is whether she will wake. Whether she will care."

"And if she wakes?" Kian Santang asked again.

Si Burik met his eyes. And for a moment -- one single, clear, unfrightened moment -- the creature's face was not the face of Si Burik the treacherous guide or Si Burik the wretched keris-addict but the face of the Tuyul he had once been, centuries ago, before the blade found him and consumed him: a simple face, honest, terrified, the face of a person who is telling the truth because the truth is all he has.

"Then we die," Si Burik said. "Quickly, if she is kind. Slowly, if she is not. And she is never kind."

The words fell into the silence of the chamber like stones into deep water, and the ripples spread outward through the dark, and the dark absorbed them, and the silence returned, thicker than before.

Kian Santang sat up. His muscles screamed in protest. The keris dragged at his spine like an anchor. But he sat up, and he looked at Bagus, and he looked at Si Burik, and he drew a breath of the warm, musk-laden air, and he spoke.

"Then we are quiet," he said. "We are small. We are fast. And we do not die. Not today. Not here. Not in a tunnel."

It was not bravery. Bravery implies a choice between action and inaction, between courage and cowardice, and Kian Santang felt no such choice. There was only forward. The gate behind them was death. The army behind them was death. The dead coral marshes behind them were a trap that the keris would use to swallow him if he tried to cross them again. Forward was the only direction that still contained the possibility -- thin, threadbare, absurd, but present -- of survival. Of completing the task. Of throwing the blade into the fire and ending its voice and going home.

If there was still a home.

"Then we climb," Bagus said, and his voice carried a conviction that Kian Santang knew was manufactured, assembled from spare parts of courage and loyalty and the stubborn refusal to let fear have the last word. "We climb, and we are quiet, and we pass through, and we destroy the cursed thing. And then we go home and eat *tempe goreng* and sit in the sawah and never, never, *never* go underground again."

Despite everything, Kian Santang almost smiled.

"*Hatur nuhun,* Priya," he said.

"*Mulah* thank me until we are sitting in the sawah," Bagus said. "With *tempe goreng*. And *kopi tubruk*. And no keris."

From the darkness above the landing, a sound came. Not a sound, exactly -- a vibration, a tremor in the air, a displacement of atmosphere that was felt rather than heard. It was the sound of something vast shifting in its sleep, adjusting its coils in the close darkness, resettling its weight on the stone. It was there and then it was gone, and the silence that followed was not the same silence that had preceded it. This silence was *occupied*. This silence had weight and warmth and the slow, rhythmic pulse of respiration.

Nyi Blorong. Sleeping. For now.

Si Burik pressed his face against his knees and whimpered, a thin, keening sound that was barely audible. His body shook. His fingers, laced over the back of his skull, were white at the knuckles.

But the keris did not permit Kian Santang the luxury of shared terror. In the aftermath of the sound from above -- that tidal displacement, that vast and somnolent readjustment of coils -- the blade's whisper changed again, and the change was the most unsettling yet. The keris had been seductive, mocking, frightened, and silent by turns during their journey, cycling through its repertoire of manipulations with the fluency of a *dalang* working through the voices of his puppets. But now, in the proximity of Nyi Blorong's sleeping presence, the keris did something new: it *screamed*.

Not aloud. Not in any register that Bagus or Si Burik could hear. The scream was internal, private, a shriek of recognition and terror that detonated in Kian Santang's skull like a *mercon* firecracker in a clay pot, scattering his thoughts, whiting out his vision, filling the space behind his eyes with a blinding, buzzing, static-filled panic that was not his own. The keris *knew* Nyi Blorong. Not as a legend, not as a name, not as a rumour carried by terrified Buta. The blade knew her the way a child knows the dark -- with a primal, pre-rational terror that bypassed all the sophisticated architecture of its malice and went straight to the foundation, to the bedrock of its being, to the place where even an artefact of supreme *sakti* confronted the limit of its power and found something older and hungrier on the other side.

*She ate one of us,* the keris shrieked. Not words. Images. A blade -- not the Keris Murka but another, one of the lesser weapons the Shadow King had forged in the early centuries of his reign -- carried into the tunnel by a Buta captain, a creature of considerable strength and dim, brutal loyalty. And the web. The green, luminous threads descending from above. The Buta struggling, the lesser keris flaring with dark *sakti*, cutting through one thread, two, three -- and then the threads thickening, multiplying, wrapping the blade and the bearer together in a cocoon of luminous mucus, and the *sakti* of the lesser keris guttering, flickering, dying -- *consumed* by the web, *digested* by the threads, the dark power drained from the metal the way a spider drains a fly, and the blade that emerged from the cocoon months later was *empty*, a husk, a keris-shaped shell of metal without *sakti*, without voice, without the animating fire that had made it a weapon, reduced to a piece of dead iron in the coils of a creature that fed on power the way other creatures fed on flesh.

*She will eat me,* the keris screamed. *Turn back. The gate. The master. Anything but her. TURN BACK.*

Kian Santang pressed his palms against his temples and squeezed, compressing the scream into a manageable volume, forcing the blade's panic down into the lower registers of his consciousness where it could throb and wail without depriving him of thought. His jaw ached from clenching. His eyes watered. A thin line of blood traced from his left nostril, triggered by the internal pressure of the keris's psychic convulsion, and dripped onto the dusty stone between his knees.

"Kian?" Bagus's voice, sharp with concern. "Your nose is bleeding."

"The keris," Kian Santang said through gritted teeth. "It is afraid. It is very, very afraid."

He wiped the blood with the back of his hand and looked at the stain on his knuckles -- red against grey, the only true colour in this colourless world, his own blood, proof that he was still alive, still bleeding, still *human*, not yet absorbed into the weapon's dark consciousness, not yet converted from bearer to extension.

And in that moment, looking at the blood, he felt something he had not expected to feel -- something so counterintuitive, so perverse in its logic, that it took him several seconds to identify it.

Relief.

The keris was afraid. The Keris Murka -- that indomitable, relentless, patient engine of corruption that had been eroding his will for weeks -- was *terrified*. And its terror meant that Nyi Blorong was real, and Nyi Blorong was dangerous, and Nyi Blorong was a power that even the Shadow King's weaponry could not subvert or seduce or overcome. Which meant that the tunnel above was not a trap set by the keris. The blade did not want to go up those stairs. The blade wanted to go *back* -- back to the gate, back to the army, back to the master's welcoming hand. The tunnel was the one direction the keris would never willingly choose, and therefore it was the one direction Kian Santang could trust.

The enemy of my enemy, Eyang Guru might have said, is not necessarily my friend. But she is at least my enemy's *fear*.

Si Burik, who had been watching Kian Santang's nosebleed with the attentive horror of a creature who understood exactly what had caused it, now raised his head and sniffed the air. His nostrils flared. His body went rigid -- not with the performative terror he had displayed on the ridge, not with the genuine but manageable fear he had shown at the entrance to the cleft, but with a new quality of dread, an alertness so acute that it transcended fear and became something purely mechanical: the response of a prey animal that has detected not the predator itself but the *change in air pressure* that precedes the predator's movement.

"She stirs," Si Burik whispered, and the whisper was almost inaudible, shaped more by the movement of his lips than by the passage of air through his throat. "She stirs, master. The blood. She smells the blood. The *jimat's sakti* in the blood." He was backing away from the upper stairway, pressing himself against the chamber wall, his body flattening, his limbs drawing inward, making himself as small as physics and terror would allow. "*Gollum... gollum... gollum...* Wipe it. Wipe the blood. Cover it. She is old and she is slow to wake, but the scent of *sakti*... *gollum*... the scent of *sakti* is her alarm, her summons, her dinner bell."

Kian Santang pressed his sleeve against his nose, stanching the flow. Bagus, acting on an instinct that was neither strategic nor magical but purely practical -- the instinct of a gardener who knows that blood draws predators -- scooped a handful of the dusty floor and pressed it against Kian Santang's face, packing the fine powder against the nostril to absorb the blood and mask the scent. The gesture was rough, ungentle, and effective. The bleeding slowed. The iron-and-*sakti* smell of the blood was buried under the mineral dust.

But the damage, if damage it was, had been done. From the tunnel above came a sound that was different from the first -- not the vast, sleepy displacement of a body adjusting its coils but something sharper, more deliberate: a sliding. Scales on stone. The dry, rasping whisper of a surface designed for friction moving across a surface designed by time, and the sound had a *direction* -- it was moving *downward*, toward the landing, toward them, with the slow, investigatory patience of a creature that has been disturbed but is not yet certain whether the disturbance warrants the full expenditure of waking.

The carvings on the wall -- the ancient reliefs that Kian Santang had noticed on the stairs below -- continued here, in the landing chamber, but their character had changed. The serpent-figures below had been formal, liturgical, depicted in the stylized postures of worship and offering. Here, the carvings were different. They told a *story* -- a sequential narrative carved in panels that ran around the chamber's circumference like the illustrated chapters of a stone book. In the first panel, the sea: waves rendered in flowing, sinuous lines, teeming with fish and coral and the serpentine bodies of creatures that were recognizably Nyi Blorong's kindred -- vast, multi-headed, crowned with hoods, their coils intertwined with the architecture of the reef itself, as though the serpents and the coral were a single organism, a living infrastructure that sustained the ocean's abundance. In the second panel, the cataclysm: fire erupting from below, the seabed splitting, the water boiling away in clouds of steam, and the serpent-figures writhing, their coils unwinding, their many heads turning upward toward a sky that had replaced the ocean they had known, their mouths open in what the carver had rendered, with devastating economy, as a scream of bewilderment. In the third panel, the aftermath: the dead coral, the dried mud, the exposed seabed, and a single serpent-figure -- larger than the rest, her coils filling the panel from edge to edge, her many heads crowned with hoods that bore the patterns of a cobra but the proportions of something far larger -- retreating into a tunnel in the rock, drawing herself inward, away from the sunlight and the ash and the ruined world, into the only space that remained to her: the dark. The deep. The interior of the stone itself.

And in the fourth panel -- the last, the one closest to the upper stairway, the one that Si Burik was now backing away from with the slow, deliberate movements of a creature trying not to attract attention -- the serpent waited. She filled the tunnel with her coils. Her many heads faced outward, toward the entrance, toward the light. Her mouths were open. And in the open mouths, depicted with a precision that made Kian Santang's skin contract, were the shapes of the things she had caught: Buta, warriors, creatures of every kind, held in the jaws of her many heads like offerings held in the hands of a deity, not consumed but *displayed*, trophies of a hunger that was not appetite but *sovereignty*, the absolute dominion of a being over its territory and everything that entered it.

The sliding sound grew louder. Closer. The air temperature in the chamber rose by a degree, then two, as though a vast, warm body were displacing the cool air of the stairway above, pushing it downward, filling the space with its exhalations. The musk thickened to a density that was almost tactile -- a presence in the air that coated the tongue and lined the throat and left a taste that was not foul but *ancient*, the flavour of deep time, of biological processes that had been operating without interruption since before the first human had drawn breath on the islands of Nusantara.

Si Burik made a sound. Not a word. Not a *gollum*. A sound that was older than language, older than the creature's centuries of degraded existence -- a whimper from the bottomless well of a terror that predated his corruption, a sound the Tuyul he had once been would have made, crouching in the dark of a primeval forest, hearing the approach of something that his ancestors had not yet learned to name. His hands were over his ears. His eyes were squeezed shut. His body was pressed so tightly against the chamber wall that the stone seemed to be absorbing him, drawing him into itself, offering the only sanctuary available: the embrace of inert matter, the safety of being *nothing*, of being *stone*, of being *not-alive* and therefore *not-prey*.

The keris screamed again -- a continuous, high-frequency whine that drilled into Kian Santang's inner ear and set his teeth vibrating in their sockets. *BACK,* it shrieked. *GO BACK. THE GATE. THE MASTER. ANYTHING. SHE IS WAKING. SHE IS--*

And then -- the sliding stopped.

The silence that followed was not a silence but a *held breath* -- the pause of a vast organism at the threshold of waking, balanced on the knife-edge between sleep and consciousness, between the deep, slow rhythms of dormancy and the sharp, predatory alertness of the hunt. The warm air pulsed once -- a single, massive exhalation from above, carrying the musk of ten thousand years of solitary existence in the deep stone, the concentrated essence of a being whose life cycle was measured in geological epochs -- and then the sound reversed. The sliding moved upward. Away. Back into the tunnel, back into the depths, back into the coils and the webs and the ancient, patient dark.

She had come to the threshold. She had sniffed the air. She had tasted the *sakti* and the blood and the fear. And she had decided -- for now, for this moment, by whatever calculus governed the decisions of a creature older than the mountain that housed her -- that the disturbance was insufficient to warrant waking.

For now.

No one spoke for a long time. No one moved. The three of them -- the keris-bearer, the gardener, and the ruined creature who had once been a Tuyul -- lay in the chamber of the landing and breathed and did not speak and did not move and waited for the certainty that the thing above had retreated fully, had settled back into its coils, had resumed the slow, tidal respiration of deep sleep.

Kian Santang looked at the carvings on the wall. He looked at the tunnel above. He looked at the keris on his back -- or rather, he felt it, because looking at it meant unwrapping it, and unwrapping it meant seeing the blade, and seeing the blade meant hearing the voice, clear and close and unbearable, the voice that promised and threatened and mocked and pleaded and *wanted*, always wanted, an appetite without limit or satiation.

The keris whispered. It had been whispering since the landing, a constant, low-level murmur that ran beneath his thoughts like water beneath ice. But now, in the proximity of Nyi Blorong's sleeping bulk, the whisper changed. It grew urgent. Insistent. And for the first time, it was not seductive but *frightened*.

*Turn back,* the keris whispered. *Turn back. Go to the gate. Give me to the master. He is safer than she is. He will use me. She will* eat *me. Turn back. Turn back.*

The blade was afraid. The Keris Murka, forged in the Shadow King's caldera, imbued with his darkest *sakti*, the weapon that had corrupted nations and broken heroes and bent the arc of history toward darkness for a thousand years -- was *afraid* of what slept in the tunnel above.

Kian Santang closed his eyes. And in the dark behind his lids, in the silence between the keris's frightened whispers, he found something unexpected: a calm. Not peace -- peace was a luxury he had not felt since Padalarang. Not courage -- courage was a word for songs and stories, not for real people in real tunnels with real monsters above them. But calm. The calm of having reached the bottom, the lowest point, the place where all the fear has been felt and all the options have been exhausted and the only thing left is the doing.

He opened his eyes.

"We rest for one hour," Kian Santang said. His voice was steady. The keris's whisper recoiled from it, momentarily silenced by the authority that was not the blade's authority but his own -- the authority of a young man who has chosen his death and found that the choosing has freed him. "Then we climb. Si Burik -- you will lead. You will be silent. You will find the path under the webs. Bagus -- you will follow me. If *aing* falter... if the keris takes *aing* again... pull me back. As you did in the marshes."

"Always," Bagus said.

"Si Burik will lead," the creature whispered from his huddle. "Si Burik will be small. Si Burik will be quiet. *Gollum.* Like a shadow. Like a mouse. Like nothing."

Kian Santang leaned his back against the chamber wall. The keris settled against the stone with a faint, metallic sigh that might have been resignation. Above him, in the tunnel, the air shifted again -- that vast, sleeping displacement, that tidal respiration of something ancient and enormous and patient.

He closed his eyes. Sleep was impossible, but rest was necessary, and his body took what it could, shutting down the higher functions and conserving the lower, hoarding energy for the climb ahead, for the passage through the tunnel, for the confrontation with whatever waited in the dark above the stairs.

In the last moment before his mind surrendered to exhaustion, Kian Santang thought of Eyang Guru. Not the fall -- not the bridge, not the fire, not the terrible, golden descent into the abyss. He thought of the old man's face. The smile. That small, human, impossibly brave smile at the edge of the chasm, the smile of a man who had made his peace with the price of love.

*Pergi. Selamatkan Nusantara.*

*Go. Save Nusantara.*

*Aing am trying, Eyang,* Kian Santang thought. *Aing am trying.*

The darkness held him. The silence held him. The weight of the keris pressed him into the stone, and the stone was cold, and the musk of the serpent-queen filled the air, and somewhere far above, in the tunnel that led over the rim and into the caldera where the Shadow King waited, Nyi Blorong dreamed her ancient, hungry dreams.

And below, far below, on the plain before the Black Gate, the war drums beat. The Buta army stirred. The Leak commanders lifted their bone-white faces toward the western wall and sniffed the air, and found nothing, and looked away, and the drums beat on.

And in the west -- far to the west, across the poisoned strait and the devastated lowlands and the rice paddies turned to mud by war -- another battle was beginning. The screen of the *wayang* shifts. The *dalang's* hands move among the puppets, and a new shape rises against the light: a fortress. Red stone. Thick walls. *Candi Abang* -- the Red Temple -- standing on its ridge above the river valley, its ancient battlements crowded with the shadows of defenders, its gates barred against a darkness that is coming from the east with the sound of drums and the smell of iron and the inexorable, grinding patience of an army that has been promised victory.

The *dalang* does not look away from the fortress. He holds the puppet high, letting the light catch its crimson silhouette, letting the audience see the shape of what is coming -- the siege, the fire, the last stand of the free peoples of Jawa against the Shadow King's tide.

But he does not strike the chest. Not yet. The hammer is raised. The blow is held.

Between the fortress and the volcano, between the siege and the stairs, between the war of armies and the war of a single soul against a blade -- the story breathes. The story waits. The story holds its many threads in its many hands and does not pull, not yet, because the pulling will come, and when it comes, it will pull everything.
