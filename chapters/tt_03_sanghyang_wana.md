# Bab 3: Sanghyang Wana di Wanantara

## (The Forest Guardians of Wanantara)

---

*Wanda anyar.*
*Gunung Merapi ngageleger di kajauhan.*
*Leuweung kolot nu tara kasaba ku manusa.*
*Dina jero leuweung, aya nu ngadangu -- kolot, lambat, sabar sapertos batu.*

*A new act.*
*Gunung Merapi rumbles in the distance.*
*An ancient forest where man rarely treads.*
*Within the forest, something listens -- old, slow, patient as stone.*

*Sang Dalang nyabut wayang dua rupa leutik tina kotak.*
*Si Cepot jeung si Dawala, leungit di antara tangkal-tangkal raksasa.*
*Leuweung hirup. Leuweung inget. Leuweung ambek.*

*The Dalang draws two small puppets from the chest.*
*Cepot and Dawala, lost among the giant trees.*
*The forest lives. The forest remembers. The forest rages.*

---

## A. Lost in the Wanantara

The Wanantara was not merely a forest; it was a memory of the earth from before the time of men.

It sprawled across the volcanic highlands of central Jawa like the dreaming body of a god who had lain down in the First Age and never risen, whose breath had become mist, whose hair had become the canopy, whose slow, tectonic heartbeat still pulsed through the roots that threaded the soil in networks so vast and so interconnected that no individual tree could be said to exist alone. Here, the separation between plant and animal, between soil and stone, between the living and the dead, was a fiction -- a convenience of language that the forest itself did not recognize. Everything was connected. Everything was in conversation. The *rasamala* spoke to the *damar* through fungal threads finer than silk. The *waringin* -- the sacred banyan, whose aerial roots descended from branch to earth like the pillars of a cathedral built by patience rather than hands -- spoke to the soil through chemistry so ancient and so complex that the greatest *dukun* of the lowlands could only glimpse its edges. The forest did not grow; it thought. Slowly, imperceptibly, with the deep, geological patience of a mind that measured its attention span in centuries, the Wanantara thought -- and what it thought about was the preservation of itself.

Here, the air was cool and green, filtered through a canopy so dense that the sun was no more than a rumor, a scattering of gold coins upon a floor of velvet moss. The light that reached the forest floor had been sieved through so many layers of leaf and branch and lichen that it had ceased to be sunlight in any ordinary sense; it was something else, something transmuted, a living luminescence that seemed to emanate from the air itself rather than from any celestial source. It fell in slanting columns through rare gaps in the canopy, and where it struck the moss it burned gold, and where it touched the hanging curtains of Spanish moss and old-man's-beard lichen it turned them to veils of green fire, and where it found the surface of a still pool it shattered into a thousand shifting coins of amber that danced and flickered with the barely perceptible trembling of the water's surface, responding to vibrations so faint that only the forest could feel them -- the footfall of a tapir half a league away, the landing of a rhinoceros hornbill on a branch overhead, the slow, patient flexing of a root as it worked its way through the substrate of volcanic ash and black humus that was the forest's living skin.

The trees were titans.

The *rasamala* rose in columns of pale, fragrant bark that soared eighty, ninety, a hundred feet before the first branch emerged -- not a branch so much as a road, a highway of wood broad enough for a man to walk upon, extending horizontally from the trunk with a structural confidence that defied comprehension. Their bark was aromatic, carrying a scent that was part camphor, part sandalwood, part something older and wilder that had no name in any human language. Cepot, who had grown up among the modest trees of Padalarang -- the mangos, the jackfruits, the slender bamboo that bent in the wind like dancers -- stared up at the rasamala trunks and felt the vertigo of encountering a scale of life that rendered his own irrelevant. These were not trees as he understood trees. These were monuments. These were the vertebrae of the earth's spine.

The *damar* was darker, heavier, its bark ridged and furrowed like the face of an ancient man who has spent a lifetime frowning at the sky. Its resin wept from wounds in the wood -- amber tears that hardened in the air and caught the scattered light like jewels, and the scent of that resin was the scent of the deep forest itself: dense, resinous, slightly bitter, with an undertone of something sweet that might have been decay or might have been the forest's own peculiar form of sweetness, the sweetness of a system so perfectly balanced that even rot was a kind of generosity.

And the *waringin* -- the banyan. The waringin was not a tree. The waringin was a kingdom.

Cepot and Dawala encountered the first one at the edge of a clearing that was not truly a clearing but a space that the waringin had claimed for itself, pushing back the other trees through the sheer imperial weight of its presence. It rose from a root system that covered an area the size of Padalarang's village square, its trunk -- if the word "trunk" could be applied to a structure so vast and so multiple -- splitting into a dozen main pillars, each one the girth of a full-grown teak, each one sending down its own curtain of aerial roots that descended from the branches like the strings of a harp played by a god. Some of those roots had reached the earth and thickened into subsidiary trunks, so that it was impossible to tell where the original tree ended and its children began. The canopy spread above them like a ceiling, and in that canopy, an entire ecosystem thrived -- orchids clinging to the bark in explosions of white and violet, ferns cascading from the crotches of branches, birds-of-paradise calling to each other in voices that were less sound than liquid gold poured from a height, their plumage catching the rare shafts of sunlight and burning with colors that had no business existing in nature: iridescent blues that shifted to green, greens that deepened to black, blacks that erupted into the impossible, extravagant orange of their tail plumes, which hung from the branches like the pennants of a court so beautiful that it had driven its inhabitants mad.

A rhinoceros hornbill watched them from a branch above -- enormous, prehistoric, its casque a helmet of bone and keratin the color of old ivory, its eyes ancient and unreadable, its massive beak holding a fruit with the casual authority of a raja holding a scepter. It regarded the two Tuyul for a long moment, then turned its great head away, dismissing them as beneath its notice. The wingbeat, when it finally launched itself into the canopy, was a sound like a leather sail unfurling in a sudden gale -- *whump, whump, whump* -- each stroke displacing enough air to stir the hair on Cepot's head as the bird vanished into the cathedral dimness above.

Butterflies moved through the understory in ones and twos and sometimes in clouds -- enormous creatures, their wings the size of Dawala's outstretched hands, patterned in designs so intricate that they seemed to have been painted by an artist who had spent a lifetime studying the geometry of Islamic tilework and the organic curves of Javanese batik and had decided to combine both traditions on a canvas of living silk. A Rajah Brooke's birdwing drifted past Cepot's face, close enough that he could see the individual scales on its wings -- emerald green on black velvet, each scale a tiny tile in a mosaic that shifted and shimmered as the butterfly moved through a shaft of light, and for a moment, despite everything, despite the terror and the exhaustion and the hunger that gnawed at his belly like a rat, Cepot felt a stab of beauty so acute it was almost painful.

On the forest floor, the moss was a living carpet -- deep, soft, intricate. It covered every surface in a layer of green velvet that muffled footsteps and softened the edges of the roots and stones until the whole world seemed upholstered. Fallen logs lay across the floor like the bones of ancient giants, and these logs were not dead but transformed -- their bark peeling away to reveal wood that had been colonized by luminescent fungi, shelf upon shelf of pale, translucent growth that glowed with a faint, blue-green light in the deepest shadows, as though the forest had invented its own form of starlight for the places where the sun could not reach. Beetles moved across the fungi in unhurried processions, their carapaces gleaming with metallic blues and greens that seemed too vivid to be organic. A line of ants -- each one the length of Cepot's thumbnail -- crossed their path in a column so disciplined that it might have been a military formation, carrying fragments of leaf in mandibles that worked with the mechanical precision of a machine designed by a mind that valued efficiency above all else.

In the soil beneath the moss, the mycelium network hummed. It was not a sound that could be heard with the ears; it was a vibration that entered through the soles of the feet and traveled up through the bones, a deep, subsonic pulse that was the forest's heartbeat, its nervous system, its memory. The trees communicated through this network -- passing sugars and minerals and information through fungal highways that connected root to root in a web so vast and so ancient that the oldest waringin in the Wanantara was, in a sense, connected to every other tree in the forest, and what one tree knew, all trees knew, eventually, in the slow, patient way that the forest knew things.

Cepot and Dawala knew none of this.

What they knew was that they were lost, and hurt, and terrified, and alone.

Cepot stumbled over a root, catching himself on a fern frond as large as a roof. His breath came in ragged gasps, each inhalation pulling the cool, damp air of the forest into lungs that burned with exhaustion. His clothes were torn -- the simple cotton shirt that Nini Anteh had stitched with her own hands, the one with the small embroidered manggis fruit on the collar, was now a collection of rags held together by luck and a few surviving threads. His trousers were shredded from the knee down, and the skin beneath was a map of scratches and thorn-marks, some still bleeding sluggishly, the blood mixing with the forest's moisture to form thin, pinkish rivulets that traced their way down his calves. His face was a mask of dirt and exhaustion, the usual brightness of his expression buried beneath layers of fear and fatigue. One eye was swollen from a branch that had whipped across his face during the night. His hands were raw, the palms scraped bloody from the moment -- hours ago? days? -- when the Leak had dropped them.

The Leak.

The memory came in flashes, bright and terrible, like lightning illuminating a landscape of nightmare. The spectral tigers -- *macan gaib* -- had come at dusk, materializing from the shadows at the edge of the camp where the fellowship had been resting, their bodies composed of darkness given weight and form, their eyes burning with a cold green fire that was the signature of Empu Gandring's sorcery. They had moved with the silence of thought, striking before anyone could cry warning, and the last thing Cepot had seen before the great jaws closed around his waist -- not biting, not tearing, but gripping, lifting, the way a mother cat carries a kitten -- was Raden Wijaya's face, white with shock, his keris flaring as he cut at the tiger that held Dawala. But the Leak had been too fast, too many. The spectral tigers had seized them both and leaped into the darkness, carrying them through the night at a speed that blurred the world into a smear of shadow and starlight and the hot, rank breath of something that should not have existed.

They had flown -- or something like flying. The tigers ran on paths that did not exist in the physical world, leaping from ridgeline to ridgeline, their paws striking surfaces that Cepot could not see, that might have been stone or might have been the compressed darkness itself. The wind of their passage was cold and tasted of sulfur and old blood. Cepot had screamed until his throat was raw, then fallen silent, then screamed again. Dawala had not screamed at all. Dawala had gone limp in the tiger's mouth, his round face blank with a terror so total that it had passed beyond expression into a kind of grey, dissociative calm. His eyes had been open but unseeing, fixed on a point beyond the visible world, and Cepot, gripped in the jaws of a nightmare, had been more frightened by his cousin's silence than by the tiger's speed.

They had been carried east. Cepot knew this because the stars, when he could see them between the clouds of ash, had wheeled in patterns that placed their trajectory toward the volcanic heartland of Jawa, toward the great mountains that marched across the island's spine like the vertebrae of a sleeping dragon. The tigers had been taking them to Merapi. To the tower. To whatever dark purpose the Shadow King's servants intended for two small Tuyul who had wandered into a war that was too large for them.

But the sun had saved them.

The first light of dawn had risen over the eastern mountains like a blade, and where it touched the spectral tigers, the darkness of their bodies had begun to fray. The Leak -- the sorcerers who rode within the tigers, who *were* the tigers -- screamed in a language that was not a language but a sound of pure, atavistic rage, the fury of creatures whose power was contingent on darkness being forced to confront the democratic fact of sunrise. The tigers had faltered, stumbled, their spectral forms flickering like candle flames in a wind. And then, with a violent, convulsive motion, they had released their burdens. The jaws had opened, and Cepot and Dawala had fallen -- not from a great height, not lethally, but hard enough to knock the breath from their bodies and leave them lying in a tangle of fern and moss and broken branches on the floor of a forest they had never seen, in a land they did not know, while the last wisps of the spectral tigers dissolved in the strengthening light like smoke in a wind.

That had been hours ago. Or perhaps a day. In the perpetual twilight of the Wanantara, time had lost its edges.

"Dawala," Cepot hissed, peering into the emerald twilight. His voice came out as a croak, his throat still raw from the screaming. "Dawala, if you're hiding, this isn't funny. *Aing* is not in the mood for games!"

"I'm... I'm not... hiding," a small voice quavered from behind a curtain of hanging vines. Dawala emerged, his round face pale as a moon in the daylight, translucent almost, the blood drained from his cheeks until the veins at his temples showed blue beneath the skin. He was shivering, though the air was mild -- a deep, internal trembling that originated not in his muscles but in some more fundamental part of his being, the part that understood, in a way that language could not express, that they were very far from home and very close to something vast and incomprehensible. His clothes were in worse shape than Cepot's -- one sleeve torn completely away, his vest hanging by a single button, his knees visible through rents in his trousers, the skin beneath them scraped raw and embedded with tiny fragments of bark and moss. A scratch ran from his left ear to his chin, crusted with dried blood that had turned the color of old rust.

"*Gust*... I'm lost, Cepot. We're both lost. This forest... it goes on forever. It doesn't have an end."

Cepot leaned against the trunk of a massive teak tree, pressing his back against the ridged bark, trying to steady his racing heart. The bark was warm. Not sun-warm -- the sun barely reached this deep -- but warm with its own internal heat, as though the tree's sapwood ran with blood rather than sap. He could feel a vibration through the bark, a low, rhythmic pulse that might have been the tree's heartbeat or might have been the vibration of the mycelium network beneath his feet or might have been his own pulse, reflected back to him by the wood, so that for a moment he could not tell where he ended and the tree began.

"Every forest has an end, *Dika*," he said. His voice was deliberately light, deliberately casual, the way a man whistles past a graveyard. "Padalarang has an end. The world has an end. Even *Aing*'s patience has an end, which is considerably shorter than the forest's."

"This one doesn't," Dawala insisted, his eyes wide and darting, moving from trunk to trunk to trunk, each one identical in its immensity, each one a pillar holding up a ceiling that could not be seen. "Can't you feel it? *Karuhun*... the trees... they're looking at us. Not like the trees at home. The mangga in Nini Anteh's yard -- that tree knows us. It grew up with us. These ones..." He swallowed, his throat clicking dryly. "These ones are older than anything that knows us. They have eyes, Cepot. I can feel them. Old eyes."

Cepot shivered. He felt it too -- a weight of attention that pressed against his skin like the air before a thunderstorm, heavy with potential, dense with watching. It was not the hostile glare of the Leak, that malevolent focus that sought to pierce and possess. Nor was it the mindless hunger of the Buta Raksasa, that vacant, devouring appetite that recognized nothing but prey. It was something else entirely. A slow, vast, contemplative awareness. A patience that measured time not in the beating of hearts but in the movement of tectonic plates, in the rising and falling of mountain ranges, in the slow, inexorable waltz of continents across the face of the deep. Whatever was watching them was not hostile. But it was not friendly, either. It was simply *there*, in the way that gravity is there, in the way that the rotation of the earth is there -- a fundamental condition of reality that did not require their understanding and would not accommodate their fear.

"*Gust!* It's just the wind," Cepot said, though there was no wind, and he didn't believe it, and he knew Dawala didn't believe it either. "We need to keep moving. The Leak dropped us when the sun rose, but they'll be back when the shadows lengthen. We have to find high ground. Maybe we can see... something. A river. A mountain we recognize. A road. Anything."

He pushed himself off the tree and immediately felt the absence of its warmth, as though he had stepped out of a house into winter.

The sense of being watched deepened.

In the canopy above, something moved -- not a bird, not a monkey, but something larger, something that displaced branches with a slow, deliberate weight that sent tremors through the trunk and shook loose a rain of tiny leaves and fragments of bark and the pale, dusty wings of moths that had been resting in the crevices above. Cepot looked up, but there was nothing to see -- only the canopy, layer upon layer of green, impenetrable, a ceiling that might as well have been the sky itself for all the information it yielded.

Somewhere in the distance -- a long way off, or perhaps very close, it was impossible to judge distance in this place -- a tapir called. The sound was a high, clear whistle, eerily human, eerily musical, as though the forest had decided to speak in a register that the Tuyul could almost understand. The whistle rose, held, and faded, and in the silence that followed it, Cepot heard something else: a deep, subsonic groan, like the settling of a structure so vast that its movements were geological rather than mechanical. The ground trembled, faintly, beneath his feet.

"We should go," Cepot whispered.

They went.

## B. Survival and Memory

They walked for what might have been an hour or a lifetime.

The forest offered no landmarks, no variation, no compass points. Every direction was the same: immense trunks, dripping canopy, the soft carpet of moss and leaf litter that swallowed their footsteps and gave back nothing. They might have been walking in circles. They probably were walking in circles. Cepot navigated by instinct and desperation, keeping the faint, warm glow of the eastern sky to his right, which meant they were heading roughly north, which meant nothing, because he had no idea where they were or where north would take them.

Dawala's stomach growled -- a sound so loud and so startling in the cathedral silence that both of them flinched. Then Cepot's stomach answered, a sympathetic rumble that was less a request for food than a formal complaint filed by an organ that had been ignored for too long.

"I'm hungry," Dawala whispered, sinking onto a mossy root. The root was thick as a man's torso, humped above the ground like the back of a sleeping serpent, and it was warm -- that same strange, internal warmth that all the trees in this forest seemed to possess. Dawala pressed his palms against the bark and closed his eyes, and for a moment, just for a moment, the shivering stopped. "I haven't eaten since... since the boat. Since before the Leak. *Aing* could eat a raw cassava right now. I could eat two. I could eat a whole field of them."

"Don't talk about food," Cepot snapped, his stomach giving a treacherous growl that undermined the authority of the instruction. He sat down beside Dawala, his legs giving out with the sudden, humiliating completeness of a structure whose supports have been removed. "Talk about walking. *Mulah* give up now. We are not giving up. Tuyul of Padalarang do not give up in a forest. We grew up in the forest. *Leuweung* is our home."

"This is not *our* forest," Dawala said. He was right, and they both knew it.

The silence settled around them like water filling a well. In the canopy above, the birds-of-paradise had gone quiet, and the only sound was the slow, steady dripping of moisture from leaf to leaf to leaf, each drop producing a tiny, crystalline note, and the accumulated effect of a thousand such notes falling in a thousand different rhythms was a music so complex and so beautiful that it was almost intolerable -- a gamelan played on instruments of water and leaf, with the forest itself as the *dalang*, the puppeteer, conducting a performance that had been running continuously for ten thousand years and would continue for ten thousand more, with or without an audience.

Dawala buried his face in his hands. "I just want to go home, Cepot. I want Nini Anteh's *kolak*."

The word hit Cepot like a fist in the chest.

*Kolak.* Nini Anteh's kolak -- the dessert she made every Ramadan, and sometimes on ordinary evenings when she decided that the world needed sweetening. Ripe banana and sweet potato and cassava, cut into careful pieces and simmered in coconut milk and palm sugar until the liquid thickened to a golden syrup that tasted of everything good -- warmth, sweetness, home, the particular love of a grandmother who expressed affection not through words, which did not come easily to her, but through the precise and patient alchemy of food. The kolak would be served in earthenware bowls on the veranda, and they would eat it while the rain fell on the banana leaves outside, each drop producing a sound like a small, wet drum, and the light would be amber from the oil lamp, and Nini Anteh would sit in her rattan chair with her betel nut and her old, knowing eyes, and the world would be small and warm and utterly, impossibly safe.

Cepot's eyes burned.

"I want to sit on the veranda and watch the rain," Dawala continued, his voice muffled by his hands. "I want to hear the *gamelan* on Jumat night. I want Mang Eman to play the *suling* -- remember how he plays, Cepot? Slow, like the river? I want to hear Si Otong's chickens fighting under the house. I want to smell the *kemenyan* when Nini does the evening prayers. I want... I want to not be afraid."

"You're not afraid," Cepot said, but the words were hollow. He pulled his knees to his chest and wrapped his arms around them, making himself small, which was saying something for a creature that was already small. "You're just tired. There's a difference."

"I'm afraid," Dawala said, looking up. His eyes were wet but steady. "I'm not ashamed to say it. I'm afraid of this forest. I'm afraid of the Leak coming back. I'm afraid that Kian Santang is dead, or worse. I'm afraid that Raden Wijaya will never find us. I'm afraid that we'll die here and Nini will never know what happened to us."

The honesty of it -- the naked, unadorned truthfulness -- landed in Cepot's chest like a stone in still water. He opened his mouth to say something clever, something deflecting, something wrapped in the bright, brittle armor of wit that he had worn since childhood, the armor that kept the world at a safe distance, that converted fear into humor and pain into punchlines. But the words would not come. The armor had cracked somewhere in the night, somewhere between the jaws of the spectral tiger and the cold floor of this impossible forest, and what was left beneath was just Cepot: small, frightened, sixteen years old, a very long way from home.

"Aing is afraid too," he said.

The words came out quietly, almost a whisper, and saying them was like setting down a weight he had been carrying for so long that he had forgotten it was there. He felt, paradoxically, braver.

Dawala looked at him. Something passed between them -- not a word, not a gesture, but something older and more fundamental, a recognition of shared vulnerability that was also, somehow, a source of shared strength. They were afraid. They were lost. They were alone. But they were afraid and lost and alone together, and that *together* was a kind of rope, thin but unbreakable, that connected them to the world of Padalarang and Nini Anteh and the sound of rain on banana leaves, and as long as they held onto it, they would not drown.

"So," Cepot said, wiping his nose on what remained of his sleeve. "*Gust!* We're afraid. Good. That's settled. Now -- food. *Aing* is not dying in this forest on an empty stomach. That would be embarrassing. Nini would be ashamed."

"She would," Dawala agreed, and despite everything, the ghost of a smile crossed his face. "She would say, '*Mulah maledog, tong waka paeh!*' -- Don't be dramatic, and don't you dare die!"

Cepot laughed -- a small, shaky, genuine laugh that sounded strange in the deep silence of the Wanantara, and several things happened at once. A bird-of-paradise, startled by the sound, erupted from a branch above them in a burst of orange and black. A family of long-tailed macaques, hidden in the canopy, chattered a brief, irritable reply. And somewhere in the deep wood, something groaned -- low, resonant, geological -- as though the forest itself had been disturbed in its sleep by a sound it had not heard in a very long time.

The sound of laughter.

They did not notice. They were busy surviving.

Dawala stood, brushing the moss from his trousers, and looked around with the careful, cataloguing eye of a Tuyul who had spent his childhood in a garden. The Wanantara was alien, vast, incomprehensible -- but it was still a forest, and a forest, however ancient, however strange, was a living system governed by rules that a gardener could recognize.

"Those," he said, pointing to a cluster of green fruits hanging from a low branch. "Those are *salak* -- look, the snake-skin bark? Wild ones. They'll be sour but edible."

Cepot stared. "How do you know that?"

"Because *Aing* spent ten years helping Nini in the garden while *Dika* was busy stealing *dodol* from Mang Eman's kitchen," Dawala said, with a quiet dignity that was more devastating than any retort.

They gathered the wild *salak*, breaking through the scaly, brown husks to find the pale, translucent flesh within. It was sour -- eye-wateringly, tooth-achingly sour, with a tannic bite that puckered the mouth and made the eyes water -- but it was food, and it was energy, and the simple act of eating it -- of biting and chewing and swallowing, of performing a function so mundane, so domestic, so profoundly ordinary -- anchored them to the world of the living in a way that all of Cepot's brave words had not.

Dawala found more. His eye, trained by years of Nini Anteh's patient instruction in the garden, began to decode the forest's larder. He found edible ferns -- the curled fiddleheads of *pakis* -- growing in the damp shade of a fallen trunk, and he picked them carefully, selecting only the tightest curls, the ones that would be tender rather than fibrous. He found wild ginger, its root hidden in the loose soil at the base of a *damar* tree, and when he broke it open, the sharp, clean scent of it -- peppery, medicinal, bright -- cut through the heavy, resinous air of the forest like a knife through silk.

"Mushrooms," he said, crouching beside a fallen log that was furred with growth. He examined them with the careful attention of a pharmacist reading a prescription. "These are good -- *jamur tiram*, oyster mushrooms. And these --" he pointed to a cascade of orange shelf fungi climbing the side of a dead trunk -- "these are *jamur kuping*, wood ears. Nini cooks them in coconut milk."

"Dawala," Cepot said, watching his cousin work, "when we get home -- *when*, not if -- *Aing* is going to tell everyone that you saved my life with mushrooms. And no one will believe me, because it's the least heroic rescue in the history of the *wayang*."

"Heroes eat mushrooms too," Dawala said simply, and handed Cepot a piece of wild ginger to chew.

They ate. The food was rough, uncooked, nothing like the warm meals of Padalarang. But it filled the void in their bellies and returned a measure of clarity to their minds, and with the clarity came thought, and with thought came worry.

"Kian Santang," Cepot said, chewing on a fiddlehead fern. "And Bagus. They were heading toward Krakatau when we last saw them. The *Keris Murka* -- it's eating him, Dawala. I saw his eyes. The way they... flickered. Like a candle in a wind."

"Bagus is strong," Dawala said. "He won't let Kian Santang fall."

"Bagus is one person. The keris is... something else." Cepot looked at his hands, still raw and scraped. "And Raden Wijaya? Dewi Anjani? Tumenggung Batu? Are they looking for us? Do they even know we're alive?"

"They know," Dawala said, with a certainty that had no evidence to support it and therefore drew its strength from something deeper than evidence -- from faith, from hope, from the stubborn Tuyul conviction that the people you love will come for you, because that is what love does. "Raden Wijaya promised. At Dieng. He said, 'Every member of this fellowship will be accounted for.' He doesn't break promises."

"He said that before a spectral tiger stole us out of the camp in the middle of the night," Cepot pointed out. But there was no real argument in his voice. He wanted to believe it too. He *needed* to believe it, the way a drowning man needs the surface of the water to be real and reachable, not an illusion, not a trick of the light.

"So what do we do?" Dawala asked.

Cepot looked around at the forest -- vast, indifferent, incomprehensible. He thought of Nini Anteh, who had survived the death of a husband, the raising of two orphaned grandchildren, the poverty of a village that the world had forgotten, and who had done it all with a quiet, implacable determination that did not announce itself as courage because it did not consider itself remarkable.

"We keep walking," Cepot said. "We eat what we find. We don't die. And when they come for us -- because they *will* come -- we will be alive to be found."

He stood. He reached out a hand.

"Come on, *Priya*. Just a little further."

Dawala reached up.

And the root he was sitting on moved.

## C. The Awakening of Mbah Jangkung

It didn't break. It didn't shift under his weight. It *flexed*. Like a muscle. Like a limb that had been still for so long that the act of moving was itself a kind of memory, a recollection of motion dragged up from a depth so profound that it arrived at the surface changed, made strange, made terrible by the sheer distance it had traveled.

Dawala shrieked and scrambled backward, crab-walking through the leaf litter, his hands and feet churning through the moss and humus until his back struck the base of another trunk. "*Gust!* It moved! It moved! The tree moved!"

"Don't be stupid," Cepot began, but the words died in his throat like a candle flame in a sudden wind.

A deep, groaning vibration shuddered through the earth. It was not a sound in the ordinary sense; it was a frequency, a wavelength of disturbance that bypassed the ears entirely and entered the body through the feet and the spine, traveling upward through the bones to vibrate in the skull and the chest and the soft, vulnerable cavity of the belly. The moss beneath their feet rippled. The leaf litter trembled. The fern fronds, ten feet tall, swayed and shook, dislodging cascades of water droplets that fell like silver rain. And the teak tree that Cepot was standing beside -- the massive, ancient teak whose ridged bark he had pressed his back against moments ago -- trembled.

Not with the wind. There was no wind.

It trembled with something else. Something internal. Something that was waking up.

Slowly, impossibly, the tree began to change.

The transformation did not happen all at once, and it did not happen fast. It happened the way dawn happens -- gradually, then suddenly, a progression so incremental that the eye could not pinpoint the moment when darkness became light, when stillness became motion, when wood became flesh. The bark of the trunk shifted. Plates of grey-brown wood slid against each other with a grating, tectonic sound, like the grinding of millstones in a giant's kitchen. The ridges of bark deepened, became furrows, became features -- the suggestion of a nose, broad and flat, the bridge of it a ridge of hardwood that might have been carved by a master sculptor or might have been shaped by the pressure of centuries of rain and wind and the slow, patient tectonics of growth. The furrows beneath the nose widened, becoming nostrils -- dark, cavernous, bearded with moss, exhaling a breath that smelled of loam and resin and the deep, mineral sweetness of volcanic soil.

And then the eyes opened.

A fissure in the bark, just above the nascent nose, widened. Not cracked -- *opened*, with the deliberate, muscular motion of an eyelid. The fissure revealed a depth of darkness that, for a moment, was simply darkness -- and then, from within that darkness, a light. Not the cold, malicious green of the Leak's fire, nor the warm amber of Eyang Guru's *bayu*. This was a brown light, deep and rich, the color of earth seen through clear water, the color of the forest floor after rain, the color of the eyes of something that has watched the world grow from a seed and has the patience to wait for the harvest. The light resolved into an iris, vast, the size of Cepot's head, ringed with striations of amber and green and the dark, fertile brown of humus. A pupil, black and deep, contracted in the green-gold light of the understory.

It blinked.

Cepot felt his knees give way. He sat down -- not voluntarily, but because his legs had made an executive decision to stop supporting a body that was witnessing something that legs were not designed to support. He sat in the moss and leaf litter and stared upward at the eye that was staring downward at him, and his mind, usually a whirl of jokes and strategies and the bright, sharp machinery of survival, ground to a complete halt. The machinery jammed. The gears locked. There were no jokes for this. There was no strategy for this. There was only the raw, unprocessed fact of a tree opening its eyes and looking at him, and the fact was so large that his mind could not contain it, the way a cup cannot contain the ocean.

A second eye opened, beside the first. This one was slower, more reluctant, crusted with lichen that crumbled and fell in pale, dusty flakes as the lid creased back. A beetle, displaced from its home in the bark-crevice, dropped onto Cepot's shoulder and scuttled away indignantly.

A branch creaked.

Not with the passive creak of wood bending in a wind. With the active, deliberate creak of a limb -- a limb in the anatomical sense, an arm -- straightening after a long sleep. The branch extended from the trunk at what would have been shoulder height, and as it moved, the smaller branches that grew from it folded and articulated, becoming fingers -- seven of them, long, gnarled, each one tipped with a cluster of small leaves that served as fingernails, or perhaps as the sensory organs that allowed this impossible hand to feel the texture of the air. The arm reached outward, stretching, and the sound it made was the sound of a tree growing -- the sound of fiber pulling against fiber, of cambium stretching, of wood remembering that it had once been supple and young and capable of motion.

On the other side of the trunk, a second arm emerged.

At the base, the roots shifted.

This was the most terrible part -- not because it was frightening, though it was, but because it was *wrong* in a way that bypassed fear and went straight to the foundations of reality. Roots do not move. Roots are the most fixed, the most grounded, the most immovable things in nature. They are the definition of stability, the antithesis of motion. And yet, these roots were pulling themselves from the soil with a tearing, ripping sound of snapping earth and breaking stone, lifting vast clods of dark humus and the pale, thread-like fungal networks that had connected them to the rest of the forest, tearing free with a violence that was also, somehow, a gentleness -- the gentleness of something so powerful that even its violent motions were measured, controlled, the way a mountain's earthquake is controlled, not by intention but by scale.

The roots became legs. Massive, pillar-like, each one the girth of a village well, they planted themselves on the forest floor with a weight that shook the earth and sent tremors radiating outward through the soil, tremors that the other trees felt and responded to -- a ripple of awareness passing through the mycelium network, a message, an alarm, an announcement: *one of us is awake*.

"Hrooom..."

The sound emerged from the creature's chest like the note of a bell cast in stone. It was a bass so low that it was felt rather than heard, a vibration that entered the body through the ribcage and resonated in the marrow of the bones. It was the voice of wood. The voice of stone. The voice of deep geological time compressed into a single syllable that meant something -- not a word, not yet, but the raw material from which words would eventually be constructed, the way the earth provides the raw material from which mountains are eventually built.

"Hrooom... hmmm... *baruuuuu*..."

The creature rose to its full height.

Five meters. Perhaps more. The scale was difficult to judge because there was nothing to judge it against -- nothing in Cepot's experience, nothing in Dawala's experience, nothing in the collective experience of any Tuyul who had ever lived provided a reference point for a living tree that stood on two legs and looked down at the world with eyes the color of the earth. Its crown was a mane of leaves and moss, a wild, tangled aureole of green and gold and the pale, silvery grey of old-man's-beard lichen that hung from its branches like the hair of a sage who had been meditating for a millennium. Its face was old. Not old in the way that Eyang Guru was old -- the oldness of a human life, measured in decades, bounded by birth and death. This was old in the way that mountains are old, in the way that riverbeds are old, in the way that the volcanic stone beneath the forest floor is old. It was a face carved not by years but by centuries of rain and sun and wind and the slow, patient work of mosses and lichens and the tiny creatures that lived in the crevices of its bark, each one contributing its own small modification to a surface that was less a face than a landscape -- a terrain of ridges and valleys and plateaus, mapped by lichen the way continents are mapped by vegetation, inhabited by insects the way islands are inhabited by birds.

Around them, the forest stirred.

Not just the creature before them. The forest itself. Other trees -- smaller ones, different species -- were beginning to move. A young *rasamala*, slender and vibrant, its bark the color of cinnamon, shook its crown and took a single, tentative step, its roots pulling from the soil with the hesitant motion of a creature that had not walked in decades and was not entirely sure it remembered how. Beside it, a *waringin* -- vast, multi-trunked, ancient -- groaned and shifted, its aerial roots swaying as its main trunk rotated, slowly, like a sleeper turning in bed. In the distance, Cepot could see more movement -- the canopy trembling, trunks tilting, the green curtains of moss and lichen parting as shapes -- enormous, slow, impossible shapes -- detached themselves from the background of the forest and became foreground, became individual, became entities that had been present all along, hidden in plain sight by their sheer vegetative stillness.

Eyes opened. Dozens of them. In the trunks, in the branches, in the great boles of ancient trees that Cepot had walked past without a second glance, assuming them to be what they appeared to be -- inanimate, fixed, rooted in the most literal sense. They were not. They had been sleeping. They had been waiting. And now, woken by the sound of Tuyul laughter and the scent of Tuyul fear and the old, deep vibration of one of their own stirring to consciousness, they were opening their eyes and looking at the world with an expression that was not surprise -- surprise required a faster metabolism -- but a slow, measured assessment, the way a mountain assesses the weather.

The Sanghyang Wana were awake.

"Little... beetles..."

The creature's voice was the scraping of tectonic plates. It emerged from its mouth -- a dark, mossy hollow in the lower portion of its face -- with the slowness of sap flowing in winter, each syllable a separate geological event, preceded and followed by a silence so dense that it seemed to have weight.

It looked down at them. Its eyes -- those vast, brown, forest-pool eyes -- moved with a deliberation that made the passage of clouds seem hasty. The pupils contracted, focused, and in their depths Cepot saw something that he would remember for the rest of his life: intelligence. Not the quick, sparking intelligence of a human mind, nor the cunning, predatory intelligence of a Leak, nor the mechanical, programmatic intelligence of a Buta. This was an intelligence of a different order entirely -- vast, slow, patient, distributed, the intelligence of a system rather than an individual, the intelligence of a forest that had spent ten thousand years learning the patterns of rainfall and the behavior of soil and the complex, interlocking dependencies of ten million organisms, and had distilled all of that knowledge into a single pair of brown eyes that now regarded two small, trembling Tuyul with an expression that was curious and careful and profoundly, unsettlingly gentle.

"Not... beetles," the creature decided. Its head tilted -- a motion that took several seconds and produced a cascade of small leaves and a startled gecko from the moss of its crown. "Too... soft. Too... loud. What... are... you?"

A massive hand reached down. Cepot flinched, every muscle in his body contracting with the instinctive recoil of a small creature in the presence of something enormous. But the movement was gentle -- gentler than anything that large had a right to be. The seven twig-fingers opened like a flower blooming in time-lapse, revealing a palm that was a landscape of bark and moss and the fine, pale threads of root hairs, and they scooped Dawala up from the leaves with a tenderness that was almost painful to witness -- the tenderness of a gardener lifting a seedling from a nursery bed, of a mother lifting a newborn, of something that understood, in its deep, slow, arboreal way, that the thing it held was fragile and precious and alive.

Dawala dangled from the great hand, his legs kicking feebly, his eyes enormous, his mouth opening and closing without producing sound, like a fish lifted from water. The creature raised him to eye level -- a height of four meters -- and examined him with the patient scrutiny of an entomologist examining a new species. The brown eyes moved over Dawala's face, his hands, his torn clothes, his scratched and bleeding skin. A twig-finger, its tip crowned with tiny leaves, extended and touched Dawala's cheek with a delicacy that made Cepot's throat constrict.

"Soft," the creature observed. "Warm. Growing. Alive."

It turned its vast head toward Cepot. The motion was glacial, inevitable.

"And... you?"

Cepot stood. His legs trembled, but they held. His mind had restarted -- not fully, not smoothly, but in the way that a flooded engine restarts, coughing and sputtering and producing uneven power, but *running*, and that was enough.

"We... we didn't mean to disturb you," he said. His voice came out higher than he intended. He cleared his throat. "We are -- *Aksama*! We're sorry! We are -- please don't --"

"Please, Mr. Tree," Dawala squeaked from his elevated position. "Don't squash us!"

The creature blinked. It took a long time. The eyelids -- thick, bark-armored, fringed with tiny ferns -- descended and rose with the speed of a continent drifting.

"Squash?" The word rolled around in the wooden mouth like a boulder in a river, abraded and shaped by the current of an unfamiliar tongue. "The... Sanghyang Wana... do... not... squash. We... grow. We... stand. We... remember."

The creature lowered Dawala back to the ground, but kept a protective hand near him, the twig-fingers curving around his small body like the walls of a living cradle.

"Not... beetles," it said again, as though confirming an earlier hypothesis. "You... are... the... little... ones. The... ones... who... walk... fast. The... ones... with... no... roots."

"Tuyul," Dawala whispered, steadying himself on a twig-finger as thick as his arm. "We are Tuyul. From Padalarang."

"Pa-da-la-rang..." The creature tasted the name, each syllable a separate act of mastication, turned over in the wooden mouth and examined from every angle before being accepted. "I... know... that... hill. I... knew... the... grandmother... of... the... banyan... that... grows... there. She... was... young... when... the... first... men... came... down... from... the... mountains. She... told... me... they... walked... upright... like... us... but... faster. Much... faster."

Cepot stared. The banyan in Padalarang was ancient -- the oldest tree in the village, the one they gathered under for the *musyawarah*, the one whose roots covered an area the size of three houses. It was old beyond reckoning. And this creature was claiming to have known its *grandmother*.

The creature turned its gaze to Cepot. "And... you... little... loud... one?"

Something moved in Cepot's chest. Not courage -- or rather, not the kind of courage that arrives with trumpets and a straight spine and a clear voice. This was a different kind -- the courage of the desperate, the cornered, the small creature that realizes it has nothing left to lose and therefore nothing left to fear, and from that emptiness, from that absolute zero of hope, extracts a spark of defiance that is brighter and hotter than anything produced by confidence.

"Cepot," he said. He reached for his knife, then stopped. He looked at the knife -- his short Tuyul knife, good for cutting rope and peeling fruit and, in extremis, for the quick, desperate slashes that had kept him alive on the burning islets of Selat Sunda. He looked at the creature -- five meters of living wood, ancient as the mountains, gentle as rainfall. He sheathed the knife. The gesture cost him something, but it earned him more.

"*Aing* is Cepot. And we are friends of the trees. We fight the Shadow King."

The air changed.

It was not a metaphor. The air literally changed -- the temperature dropped, the moisture thickened, and the subsonic vibration that had been building in the earth since the creature's awakening deepened into a frequency that Cepot felt in his teeth. The creature's expression -- if an expression composed of bark and moss and centuries of weathering could be said to change -- darkened. The brown of its eyes deepened to the color of bog water, of old iron, of the earth after a fire has passed through it. The moss on its shoulders bristled, each strand stiffening, rising, the way the hair on a dog's back rises in the presence of a threat. A shadow seemed to pass over the sun -- not a cloud, not the canopy, but a shadow that originated in the creature itself, a dimming of the light that surrounded it, as though the air near its body had become denser, more opaque, heavy with an emotion that the human vocabulary had a word for but that the arboreal vocabulary expressed differently, expressed as root-pressure and bark-tension and the slow, tectonic grinding of anger that has had centuries to build.

"The... Shadow... King..." The words came out heavy, each one a stone dropped from a height. They were not spoken with haste or heat. They were spoken with the slow, measured deliberation of a sentencing judge, each syllable weighted with evidence accumulated over millennia. "The... burner. The... hewer. The... enemy... of... the... green."

"His servants took us," Cepot said, finding his voice, finding that the voice was stronger now, steadied by the creature's anger, because anger -- even this vast, slow, geological anger -- was better than the indifferent patience of a forest that did not care. This creature cared. This creature was furious. And fury, however slow, was an ally. "They brought us here. They are cutting the forest on the slopes of Merapi. We saw the smoke."

"Smoke," the creature groaned. The sound was tectonic -- a shifting of strata, a grinding of plates. "Yes... I... smell... it. The... young... ones... cry... out. Their... voices... come... through... the... roots. The... iron... bites. The... fire... burns. My... children... are... falling."

The twig-fingers of the protective hand curled tighter around Dawala, not threateningly but instinctively, the way a parent's hand tightens on a child's when danger is mentioned.

"I... am... Mbah Jangkung," it said. "The... Long... Father. I... am... the... shepherd... of... the... trees. The... keeper... of... the... old... ways. The... last... of... the... first... planting. And... you... little... roots... you... bring... news... of... axe... and... fire."

As the last syllable left the wooden mouth and settled into the soil like a seed, the forest responded.

It began with the small things. A *kodok pohon* -- a tree frog, bright as an emerald, no larger than the tip of Dawala's thumb -- emerged from a curled leaf near Mbah Jangkung's ankle and sat upon the bark, its throat pulsing with a rapid, liquid song that rose into the canopy like a signal flare of sound. A second frog answered from somewhere above. Then a third. Then a dozen, their voices layering into a chorus so precise, so intricately timed, that it sounded less like the spontaneous music of amphibians and more like the tuning of an orchestra -- an ensemble of living instruments calibrating themselves to a pitch that the forest had not heard in a very long time: the pitch of awakening.

Then the insects came.

Not in swarms -- not with the mindless, buzzing aggression of disturbed hives -- but in processions. Beetles emerged from the leaf litter in lines, their carapaces gleaming with metallic greens and blues and the deep, oily purple of volcanic obsidian, and they marched in formation toward Mbah Jangkung's roots, circling them in concentric rings, their antennae waving in patterns that might have been salutation, or recognition, or the chemical equivalent of weeping. Moths unfolded themselves from the bark of neighboring trees -- pale, enormous, their wings the texture of old silk, patterned with eye-spots that stared at the world with a counterfeit intelligence -- and drifted toward the ancient spirit in slow, spiraling orbits, as though drawn by a gravity that was not physical but spiritual, the pull of something so old and so fundamental that every small life in the forest recognized it as the center of their world.

A column of army ants, which had been crossing the clearing floor on its own inscrutable errand, paused. The lead ants stopped, their mandibles opening and closing in what appeared to be confusion, and then the entire column -- ten thousand individuals, a single organism distributed across ten thousand bodies -- turned, as one, and flowed toward Mbah Jangkung's nearest root, parting around it like a river around a stone, and continuing on their way, but slowly now, and with a deference that Cepot had never seen an ant display toward anything.

The birds came next.

A pair of *julang mas* -- golden-throated hornbills -- descended through the canopy in a spiral, their enormous casqued beaks catching the filtered light and blazing gold, their wingbeats producing the deep, rhythmic *whump-whump-whump* that Cepot had heard earlier. They landed on a branch near Mbah Jangkung's crown and sat there, side by side, their ancient, reptilian eyes fixed on the great spirit with the steady regard of sentinels who have been waiting for their lord to wake and are satisfied, at last, to see him standing. A flock of *perkici* -- rainbow lorikeets -- erupted from a *damar* canopy in a burst of green and scarlet and electric blue, circling Mbah Jangkung's crown in a chattering, chromatic vortex before settling in the lichen of his shoulders, where they preened and murmured and occasionally glanced down at Cepot and Dawala with the bright, evaluative curiosity of creatures who have opinions about everything and are not shy about expressing them.

From the understory, larger animals appeared. A *kancil* -- a mouse deer, the smallest deer in the world, no taller than Cepot's knee, with legs like pencils and eyes like drops of black resin -- stepped out from behind a fern and stood at the edge of the clearing, its tiny hooves barely dimpling the moss, its delicate nostrils flaring as it sampled the air around Mbah Jangkung. A civet, its grey fur banded with dark stripes, emerged from a hollow log and sat upright, its sharp face lifted, its whiskers twitching. A pangolin -- armored, ancient, improbable -- trundled from beneath a root and curled itself into a partial ball at the base of Mbah Jangkung's foot, as though the great spirit's presence were a guarantee of safety, a perimeter within which no predator would dare to hunt.

And from the canopy above -- from the high, sun-touched branches where the epiphytes bloomed and the air was thinner and brighter -- a shadow descended. It was a *lutung budeng*, a Javan langur, its fur the deep, lustrous black of volcanic glass, its face framed by a halo of silver-grey that gave it the aspect of a sage who has spent decades in contemplation and has emerged with conclusions that it does not intend to share. It landed on Mbah Jangkung's forearm with the weightless precision of a creature that has calculated the physics of every branch it has ever touched, and it sat there, grooming itself with meticulous care, and occasionally pausing to regard the two Tuyul below with an expression that combined scholarly interest with a faintly patronizing amusement, as though it had been studying the behavior of ground-dwelling bipeds for some time and had developed theories that were not entirely flattering.

"*Gust*," Dawala breathed, turning in a slow circle to take in the gathering of creatures. "*Karuhun*... they're all coming. It's like... it's like Nini's garden when she waters it at dawn. Everything comes."

"Except Nini's garden doesn't have a pangolin," Cepot muttered, eyeing the armored creature at Mbah Jangkung's foot with a wariness born of having once been bitten by a considerably smaller animal. "Or a monkey that looks like it's grading my performance."

"They... come... because... they... remember," Mbah Jangkung said, observing the gathering of creatures around his roots with the slow, measured satisfaction of a patriarch whose household, long scattered, has begun to reassemble. "The... small... ones... carry... the... forest's... memory... in... their... blood. When... the... old... wood... wakes... they... feel... it. The... way... you... feel... your... heart... beating. They... do... not... choose... to... come. They... come... because... they... are... part... of... this... body. My... body. The... forest's... body. We... are... one... flesh."

He lowered himself, slowly, into a seated position -- a motion that took nearly a minute to complete and that produced, in the earth beneath, a tremor so deep and so sustained that Cepot felt it in his molars. The great spirit settled his root-legs beneath him, his trunk-body creaking and groaning like the hull of a ship at anchor, and the creatures that had gathered around him adjusted their positions accordingly -- the beetles circling wider, the moths drifting higher, the pangolin rolling a few inches to the left -- with the practiced, unconscious coordination of organisms that have been sharing space for millennia.

Above them, three *elang Jawa* -- Javan hawk-eagles -- had begun to circle, their broad wings catching the thermals that rose from the canopy's sun-warmed surface. They turned in wide, unhurried arcs, their shadow-crosses drifting across the forest floor like the hands of a clock so vast that its face was the sky itself. They did not call out. They did not need to. Their presence was announcement enough -- the raptors of the high canopy, the keepers of the aerial kingdom, had come to bear witness to the waking of the deep wood.

"You... asked... who... I... am," Mbah Jangkung said, and his voice, now that he was seated, seemed to rise from the earth itself rather than from his wooden mouth -- a sound that was less voice than vibration, less vibration than the slow, rhythmic pulse of the planet's own heartbeat, transmitted upward through layers of stone and soil and root until it emerged into the air as language. "I... will... tell... you. But... you... must... be... patient. My... story... is... long. Longer... than... your... lives. Longer... than... the... lives... of... your... grandchildren's... grandchildren. I... have... been... here... since... the... soil... was... new."

Cepot sat down. There was nothing else to do. The forest had decided to tell a story, and the forest's stories did not arrive on the timescale of a Tuyul's attention span.

"In... the... beginning," Mbah Jangkung said, "there... was... only... stone. Hot... stone. The... mountains... were... rivers... of... fire... and... the... air... was... poison... and... nothing... green... existed. The... *Pertiwi*... the... Earth... Mother... was... barren. Her... skin... was... cracked... and... burning. She... wept... but... her... tears... were... steam."

The tree frog on his ankle sang a single, liquid note. The langur on his forearm paused its grooming and tilted its head, listening.

"Then... the... rain... came. The... first... rain. Not... water... as... you... know... it... but... something... thicker... richer... alive. It... fell... from... a... sky... that... was... not... yet... blue... and... where... it... touched... the... stone... the... stone... softened. Where... it... pooled... in... the... cracks... the... cracks... filled... with... mud. And... in... the... mud... the... first... seed... waited."

He touched the earth beside him with one vast twig-finger, and where he touched it, the moss seemed to brighten, to deepen in color, as though responding to a memory that was carried not in the mind but in the cellulose, in the very fiber of his being.

"I... was... that... seed," Mbah Jangkung said. "Or... my... ancestor. It... is... difficult... to... tell. Trees... do... not... distinguish... between... self... and... ancestor... the... way... you... fast... ones... do. My... roots... are... the... roots... of... the... first... planting. My... rings... contain... the... memory... of... every... year... since... the... stone... learned... to... be... soil. I... am... old... little... beetles. I... am... very... old. And... I... have... stood... here... watching... and... waiting... and... growing... for... so... long... that... the... mountains... around... me... have... risen... and... fallen... and... risen... again... and... I... have... not... moved."

"Until now," Cepot said quietly.

The brown eyes, vast as forest pools, turned toward him. In their depths, rings within rings of amber and green and the dark, fertile brown of humus shifted and contracted. The langur on Mbah Jangkung's forearm stopped grooming. The frogs fell silent. Even the moths, those pale, spiraling satellites, seemed to slow in their orbits.

"Until... now," Mbah Jangkung agreed. And in those two words, spoken with the slow, tectonic finality of a geological event, Cepot heard the weight of a decision that had been building for centuries -- the decision of the most patient creature in the world to abandon patience, and act.

## D. The Memory of the Forest

They sat at the base of Mbah Jangkung's trunk -- or rather, at the base of what had been his trunk and was now his legs, his lower body, the root-structure that anchored him to the earth even as he stood above it. The other Sanghyang Wana that had stirred in the vicinity had settled back into a watchful stillness, their eyes half-open, their attention focused on the conversation between the ancient shepherd and his tiny, damaged visitors. Dawala sat in the curve of a root-knee, his legs dangling, his earlier terror transformed into a wide-eyed wonder that made him look, for the first time in days, like the child he still was. Cepot leaned against the bark of Mbah Jangkung's calf, his arms crossed, his face set in the expression of a person who is listening very hard while pretending to be casual.

"Empu Gandring," Cepot said. "The Smith. He is building an army. He is feeding the forest to his furnaces. We saw the smoke from the Selat Sunda. Grey smoke, thick, like a wound in the sky."

Mbah Jangkung stiffened. The moss on his shoulders bristled again, and a cascade of tiny insects -- beetles, mites, spiders that had made their homes in the lichen of his bark -- fell like living rain, displaced by the sudden tension of their host.

"Gandring..." The name was a curse in the wooden mouth, a sound of such deep, slow loathing that it seemed to darken the air around it. "He... was... once... a... friend."

Cepot blinked. "A friend?"

"In... the... beginning." The words emerged with the pace of glacial melt -- each syllable a droplet of meaning, falling into the pool of silence, sending out ripples that took a long time to reach the shore. "When... the... first... smiths... came... to... the... mountains... they... asked... the... trees... for... wood. And... the... trees... gave. Because... that... is... the... way. The... pact. The... balance. You... take... a... branch... you... plant... a... seed. You... fell... a... trunk... you... tend... a... sapling. This... was... the... law... before... there... were... laws."

Mbah Jangkung's great hand moved as he spoke -- not gesticulating, not in the quick, emphatic way of human speech, but slowly, illustratively, the twig-fingers miming the actions he described. A hand reaching up to take a branch. A hand pressing down into the soil to plant. The gestures were so slow, so deliberate, that they acquired a ceremonial quality, as though the creature was not merely recounting history but performing it, re-enacting a liturgy so ancient that it predated the concept of ritual itself.

"Gandring... knew... the... songs. The... old... songs. The... songs... you... sing... to... wood... before... you... cut... it. So... the... wood... knows... it... is... taken... with... respect. So... the... spirit... within... can... withdraw... to... the... roots... and... survive. He... sang... beautifully."

The creature's eyes, those vast brown pools, seemed to cloud. Not with physical obstruction -- with memory. With the particular, devastating sadness of remembering someone who was once worthy of love and is no longer.

"When... did... he... stop... singing?" Cepot asked.

"When... the... whisper... came."

"The whisper?"

"From... the... deep... stone. From... the... fire... beneath... the... mountain. The... voice... of... the... one... who... lives... in... the... dark... forge... at... the... root... of... Krakatau. The... Raja Iblis. The... Shadow... King." Mbah Jangkung's voice deepened -- if such a depth was possible -- to a register that was less sound than vibration, less vibration than the memory of an earthquake. "He... whispered... to... Gandring's... pride. He... said... *Why do you ask permission of the wood? The wood is yours. The mountain is yours. The fire is yours. You are the maker. They are the material.* And... Gandring... listened."

The silence that followed was not empty. It was full -- full of the weight of consequence, of the long chain of events that links a single moment of temptation to centuries of destruction. Cepot thought of the smoke on the horizon. He thought of the slopes of Merapi, which he had seen from the sea -- slopes that should have been green, terraced with rice and crowned with forest, but that were instead grey and scarred, the trees felled, the soil stripped, the earth exposed like a wound that would not heal.

"Now... he... only... takes," Mbah Jangkung said. "He... does... not... sing. He... does... not... plant. He... feeds... the... forest... to... his... furnaces... and... from... the... furnaces... come... iron... and... from... the... iron... come... swords... and... from... the... swords... comes... death. The... cycle... of... destruction. Take... burn... forge... kill. Take... more. Burn... more. The... green... retreats. The... soil... dies. The... rivers... run... with... ash."

Dawala, who had been listening with the quiet intensity of a creature whose natural habitat was being described, spoke. His voice was small but steady. "What about the other trees? The ones being cut? Can't they... fight?"

Mbah Jangkung turned his great head toward Dawala. The motion took three full seconds, and in those seconds, the expression in the ancient eyes shifted -- from anger to something softer, something that might have been tenderness, or might have been the arboreal equivalent of recognizing a kindred spirit.

"The... young... ones... try," he said. "The... young... *rasamala*... whose... brothers... and... sisters... are... being... felled... on... the... slopes... of... Merapi. They... are... angry. They... thrash. They... drop... branches... on... the... cutters. But... anger... without... wisdom... is... just... wind. It... blows... and... passes. The... axe... remains."

He shifted his weight, and the earth groaned beneath him. His roots -- his feet -- sank slightly into the humus, drawing sustenance, drawing calm.

"The... *jati*... the... teak... are... the... warriors. Strong. Dense. Slow... to... anger... but... terrible... when... roused. A... teak... in... full... fury... can... break... stone. Can... crush... iron. But... the... teak... are... few... now. So... many... have... been... taken. Their... wood... is... prized... by... the... smith. It... burns... hot. It... burns... long."

"And the *waringin*?" Cepot asked. "The banyan?"

"The... *waringin*... are... the... eldest. The... wisest. They... do... not... fight. They... think. They... remember. They... hold... the... councils. A... *waringin*... can... remember... a... thousand... years... of... rainfall. Can... recall... the... shape... of... a... river... before... it... changed... its... course. Can... tell... you... how... the... soil... tasted... when... the... first... *padi*... was... planted... in... these... hills."

"How long have you been here?" Dawala asked. It was not a question he had planned to ask. It emerged from a place of genuine, uncomplicated curiosity -- the curiosity of a gardener who has encountered a plant of impossible antiquity and wants, simply, to understand.

Mbah Jangkung considered the question. The considering took a while. Cepot watched the creature's face -- the bark-ridges shifting, the lichen-mapped forehead creasing, the great brown eyes moving with the slow lateral tracking of a creature that was searching its memory the way a human might search a library -- not randomly, but systematically, moving through shelves of recollection that were organized by some principle that was not chronological in the human sense but was based on some other taxonomy entirely, some arboreal filing system that sorted events not by when they happened but by how they felt, by the quality of light, by the taste of the rain, by the mineral composition of the soil.

"I... remember... when... Krakatau... was... green," Mbah Jangkung said at last. "Before... the... first... eruption. Before... the... fire... came... up... from... below. It... was... a... mountain... like... any... other. Trees... grew... on... its... slopes. Birds... nested... in... its... crags. The... water... that... ran... from... its... springs... was... clear... and... cold... and... tasted... of... basalt... and... nothing... else."

Cepot did the arithmetic. The first eruption of Krakatau -- the one that the oldest legends spoke of, the one that had split the mountain and opened the gate to the fire below -- was thousands of years ago. Before the Hindu kingdoms. Before the Sriwijaya. Before the concept of "before" had acquired its present meaning.

"The... Shadow... King... was... just... a... whisper... then," Mbah Jangkung continued. "A... murmur... in... the... deep... stone. A... disturbance... in... the... magma. We... felt... it... through... the... roots. The... oldest... *waringin*... said... *Something is waking beneath the mountain.* And... we... waited. Because... waiting... is... what... we... do. We... are... patient. Patience... is... our... weapon. Our... roots... break... stone... but... they... do... it... slowly. Over... centuries. Over... millennia. The... stone... does... not... notice... until... it... is... too... late."

He paused. In the silence, the forest breathed around them -- the collective inhalation and exhalation of ten thousand trees, a rhythm so slow that it was perceptible only to those who had stopped moving long enough to listen.

"But... patience... can... be... a... weakness," Mbah Jangkung said. "If... the... threat... moves... faster... than... the... patience. If... the... axe... falls... before... the... root... can... grow. We... were... too... slow. The... Shadow... King... grew. The... whisper... became... a... voice. The... voice... became... a... roar. And... now... the... roar... is... an... army. And... the... army... has... axes."

Mbah Jangkung fell silent for a long while. The silence was not empty but thick, layered, a compost of memory and sorrow through which old shapes moved like roots through dark soil. When he spoke again, his voice had changed -- dropped below the register of speech into something closer to the subsonic hymn of the mycelium itself, the frequency at which the forest told its stories to the earth.

"The... first... *empu*... who... came... to... the... *Wanantara*... was... not... Gandring," he said. "He... was... called... Empu... Ramadi. A... small... man. Brown... as... the... *kayu manis*... bark. He... came... from... the... lowlands... with... nothing... but... a... stone... hammer... and... a... song... his... mother... had... taught... him. The... song... was... older... than... his... people. Older... than... the... language... he... spoke... it... in. It... was... a... song... of... asking."

The twig-fingers of Mbah Jangkung's right hand moved, slowly, tracing a shape in the air that might have been a figure kneeling.

"He... came... to... the... edge... of... the... *Wanantara*... and... he... sang. He... sang... for... three... days. Three... days... on... his... knees... at... the... border... where... the... grassland... met... the... trees. He... did... not... enter. He... did... not... presume. He... sang... and... he... waited... and... on... the... third... day... a... *jati*... warrior... came... to... the... edge... and... listened. And... the... *jati*... heard... the... song... and... understood... that... here... was... a... creature... who... knew... the... difference... between... asking... and... taking."

"What did he want?" Dawala asked, his voice hushed.

"Wood," Mbah Jangkung said. "A... single... branch. He... needed... charcoal... to... smelt... the... copper... he... had... found... in... the... river. He... wanted... to... make... a... bell... for... his... village. A... bell... to... call... his... people... to... gather. Not... a... weapon. Not... a... tool... of... power. A... bell. A... voice."

The ancient spirit's eyes glistened -- or seemed to glisten, though the moisture might have been sap, or dew, or the slow accumulation of centuries of condensation in the deep hollows of his bark-lids.

"The... *jati*... gave... him... a... branch. A... fallen... branch... already... dry... already... returning... to... the... soil. It... cost... the... tree... nothing. But... Empu... Ramadi... did... not... take... it... as... though... it... cost... nothing. He... took... it... as... though... it... cost... the... world. He... knelt. He... pressed... his... forehead... to... the... bark... of... the... *jati*... and... he... said... *Aksama*... forgive... me... for... what... I... take... from... you. I... will... return... what... I... can. And... he... planted... three... seeds. One... for... the... branch... he... took. One... for... the... tree... that... gave. One... for... the... forest... that... watched."

"The pact," Cepot said.

"The... pact," Mbah Jangkung confirmed. "The... *Wanantara*... Covenant. Take... one... give... three. Use... the... dead... wood... before... the... living. Sing... before... you... cut. Plant... after... you... take. And... never... never... fell... a... tree... that... bears... the... mark... of... the... *Sanghyang*. The... old... trees. The... mother... trees. The... ones... who... carry... the... memory... of... the... forest... in... their... heartwood."

He gestured with one vast arm toward the surrounding forest, and the motion was heavy with the weight of a curator indicating the remnants of a collection that was once magnificent and is now diminished beyond recovery.

"For... generations... the... *empu*... kept... the... pact. Empu... Ramadi's... son... kept... it. His... son's... son... kept... it. The... forest... gave... and... the... smiths... returned. The... copper... bells... rang... in... the... valleys... and... the... trees... heard... the... sound... and... were... glad... because... the... bells... were... made... with... respect... and... the... sound... they... made... was... the... sound... of... the... song... that... Ramadi... had... sung... cast... in... metal... and... given... back... to... the... wind."

He paused again, and in the pause, the forest seemed to lean closer -- the surrounding Sanghyang Wana tilting their great crowns inward, as though they too were listening to a story they already knew but needed to hear again, the way the bereaved need to speak the names of their dead.

"Then... Gandring... came. Not... the... first... to... break... the... pact... but... the... worst. The... one... who... did... not... merely... forget... the... songs... but... chose... to... silence... them. He... came... with... iron... tools... where... Ramadi... had... come... with... stone. He... came... with... demands... where... Ramadi... had... come... with... a... song. And... behind... him... the... whisper... of... the... mountain... said... *Take what you need. The trees are fuel. The forest is a larder. You are the apex. They are the undergrowth.*"

"And the trees he took," Dawala said, very quietly. "Do you remember them? By name?"

The question landed in the space between them like a leaf falling onto still water. Mbah Jangkung's eyes widened -- not with surprise but with something deeper, something that might have been gratitude, the gratitude of a mourner who has been asked to speak the names of the beloved dead by someone who understands that naming is a form of resurrection, that to speak a name is to pull a thread of the lost thing back into the fabric of the present.

"I... remember... them... all," Mbah Jangkung said, and his voice cracked -- not with the quick, sharp crack of breaking wood, but with the slow, grinding fracture of a fault line shifting, a sound that was geological in its depth and devastating in its simplicity. "Every... one. Every... name. Every... ring."

He raised his great hand and pointed to the east, toward the distant grey wound of Merapi's deforested slopes.

"There... stood... *Nyi Karang Kancana*. The... Golden... Cliff... Mother. A... *rasamala*... so... tall... that... the... eagles... nested... in... her... crown... and... the... clouds... caught... in... her... upper... branches... like... wool... in... a... thorn... bush. She... was... eight... hundred... years... old. She... had... survived... four... eruptions. She... had... sheltered... ten... thousand... species... in... her... bark... and... her... branches... and... the... cathedral... of... her... roots. Gandring's... cutters... took... her... in... a... single... morning. She... screamed... through... the... mycelium... for... three... days... before... she... fell. The... sound... of... her... falling... shook... the... earth... for... a... league... in... every... direction. Where... she... stood... there... is... now... a... furnace."

Dawala's hand tightened on the bark of Mbah Jangkung's leg. His knuckles went white.

"And... beside... her... *Ki Gede Batu Hideung*. The... Great... Father... of... the... Black... Stone. A... *jati*... warrior. The... oldest... teak... on... the... eastern... slope. His... heartwood... was... so... dense... that... even... the... iron... axes... dulled... against... it. They... had... to... use... fire. They... burned... him... standing. They... did... not... even... have... the... decency... to... fell... him... first."

The subsonic vibration in the earth deepened, and the smaller Sanghyang Wana around the clearing shifted -- a ripple of anguish passing through the assembly like a wave through water, communicated not through sound but through the root-network, through the shared nervous system of the forest.

"And... *Nini Rambut Perak*," Mbah Jangkung continued, and now his voice was barely audible, a tremor in the stone, a whisper in the bedrock. "The... Silver... Hair... Grandmother. An... ancient... *waringin*... older... than... myself... by... a... century. She... held... the... memory... of... the... first... eruption... of... Merapi... in... her... heartwood. When... they... cut... her... down... a... thousand... years... of... rainfall... records... were... lost. A... thousand... years... of... knowing... which... rivers... flooded... and... which... hillsides... slipped... and... which... soils... remembered... the... taste... of... ash. Gone. In... a... morning. Fed... to... the... furnace... like... kindling."

"*Karuhun*," Dawala whispered, and the word was not an invocation but a lament. "*Karuhun*... I'm sorry. I'm so sorry."

Cepot said nothing. There was nothing to say. He sat with his back against the bark of the oldest living thing he had ever encountered and listened to it grieve, and the grief was so vast and so slow and so deep that it made his own sorrows -- the loss of Padalarang, the separation from Nini Anteh, the fear of the dark road ahead -- seem not small, exactly, but brief. His sorrows would pass. This grief had been accumulating for centuries, ring by ring, the way a tree records drought and fire and the slow, invisible poison of a changing atmosphere in the density and color of its annual growth.

Cepot looked at the ancient face and saw something he recognized. He had seen it in Raden Wijaya's eyes, in the dark moments before battle. He had seen it in Eyang Guru's face, in the stillness that preceded decision. He had seen it in his own reflection, in the moments when the jokes ran out and the fear was too large for wit to contain.

Grief. The grief of a guardian who has watched the thing he was guarding be destroyed, slowly, piece by piece, while he stood by and waited for the right moment to act, and the right moment never came, and now the thing he was guarding was half-gone and the axe was still falling.

"But you're awake now," Dawala said.

Mbah Jangkung looked down at the small Tuyul sitting in the curve of his root-knee. Dawala looked up at the ancient face. And between them, something passed -- a recognition, a connection, the meeting of two forms of gentleness across a gulf of scale and time and species so vast that it should have been unbridgeable, and yet was bridged, simply, by the fact that both of them cared about growing things.

"Yes," Mbah Jangkung said. "I... am... awake. And... the... *wana*... must... wake... too."

Cepot watched Dawala in this moment and felt something he could not name -- a swelling in his chest that was not pride, exactly, though pride was part of it. It was the feeling of watching someone you love become more fully themselves, of seeing the shape that was always latent in the clay emerge under the pressure of circumstance. Dawala's gentleness, which the world had treated as weakness, which Cepot himself had sometimes treated as weakness, was not weak. It was a frequency -- a wavelength of attention and care that vibrated at the same rate as the forest itself, and in this place, among these creatures, that frequency was not a liability but a language. Dawala spoke tree. He had always spoken tree. He just hadn't known it until now.

"You... understand... *bayu*," Mbah Jangkung said to Dawala, and it was not a question. "The... life... force. The... breath... of... the... green. You... feel... it."

"I feel the roots," Dawala said, pressing his palm against the bark of Mbah Jangkung's leg. "They're warm. Like they're alive. Like they have a heartbeat."

"They... do. Every... tree... has... a... heartbeat. Slow. One... beat... per... day. But... steady. Steady... as... the... turning... of... the... earth." Mbah Jangkung placed a twig-finger on the top of Dawala's head, the way a grandfather might rest his hand on a child's crown. "You... listen... well... little... root. Most... of... the... fast... ones... do... not... listen. They... are... too... busy... making... noise."

He turned his gaze to Cepot. The eyes were not unkind, but they were knowing.

"And... you... loud... one. You... make... noise... to... keep... the... dark... away. Yes?"

Cepot's mouth opened. Closed. Opened again. He had been seen. Not in the superficial way that people usually saw him -- the wit, the sharpness, the performing monkey of Padalarang who danced and joked so that no one would notice the fear beneath the performance. He had been seen in the way that only very old, very slow, very patient things can see -- the way that stone sees, the way that water sees, the way that time itself sees, stripping away the surface layers of personality and behavior and habit to reveal the substrate beneath: a frightened boy who loved his cousin and missed his grandmother and was trying, with every joke and every clever word, to hold together a self that the world was determined to break.

"*Aing* makes noise because *Aing* has things to say," Cepot replied. But his voice was quiet, and the usual edge was gone.

Mbah Jangkung made a sound that might have been a laugh. It was slow, deep, resonant -- the sound of wood creaking in a warm wind, of a trunk settling more deeply into its roots, of a creature whose sense of humor operated on a geological timescale producing a single, brief, devastatingly affectionate expression of amusement.

"Hroom... hm... hm... yes. Things... to... say. Many... things. Fast... things. Perhaps... some... day... you... will... learn... to... say... them... slowly."

## E. The Road to the Sabha Wana

"Come... little... roots," Mbah Jangkung said. "Climb... up. My... shoulders... are... broad. We... go... to... the... *Sabha Wana*. We... go... to... the... Moot."

"The Moot?" Dawala asked, blinking.

"The... gathering," Mbah Jangkung said. "The... waking. The... council... of... the... old... wood. If... the... furnace... burns... then... the... river... must... rise. If... the... axe... falls... then... the... root... must... answer."

He lowered his arm like a ramp -- a vast, sloping highway of bark and moss and lichen that descended from his shoulder to the ground at a gentle incline, the twig-fingers at its terminus curling inward to form a handhold, a railing, a gesture of invitation so immense in scale and so delicate in execution that it made Cepot's eyes sting with an emotion he would later deny having felt.

Cepot looked at Dawala. Dawala looked at Cepot. And for the first time in days -- since the Leak had seized them, since the night flight on the spectral tigers, since the dawn drop into the impossible forest -- they smiled. Not the brittle, defensive smile of people performing courage. A real smile. The smile of two Tuyul from Padalarang who had been invited to ride on the shoulders of a mountain and had decided, after careful consideration, that the world was stranger and more wonderful than they had been led to believe.

"After you," Cepot said, gesturing to the arm-ramp with exaggerated courtesy.

"No, after *you*," Dawala said. "You're the one who's always saying the leader goes first."

"*Aing* has never said that. *Aing* has always said the leader goes second, so there's someone to cushion the fall."

"Hrooom," Mbah Jangkung rumbled. "The... fast... ones... argue... even... about... the... order... of... climbing."

They climbed. The bark beneath their hands and feet was rough and warm, offering excellent grip, and the moss that grew in the crevices was soft as velvet, cushioning their knees where they pressed against the wood. Cepot went first -- despite his protests -- pulling himself up the gentle slope of the arm with the nimble, sure-footed confidence of a Tuyul who had spent his childhood climbing the mango trees of Padalarang. Dawala followed, slower but steadier, his hands finding holds that Cepot had missed, his feet finding purchase on surfaces that seemed, to Cepot's eyes, to offer none. It occurred to Cepot, watching his cousin climb, that Dawala moved through the vegetable world the way a fish moves through water -- with an instinctive, unthinking grace that was the product not of skill but of kinship, of belonging to the medium through which he moved.

They reached the shoulders.

The shoulders of Mbah Jangkung were a landscape. Not a smooth, simple surface, but a terrain -- a miniature ecosystem complete with its own topography, its own vegetation, its own fauna. The wood here was broad and relatively flat, forming a platform large enough for both Tuyul to sit comfortably, and it was covered with a living carpet of moss and lichen and tiny ferns and the trailing, star-flowered tendrils of a parasitic plant whose name Dawala did not know but whose small white blossoms filled the air with a scent that was part jasmine, part rain, part the indefinable fragrance of great height. Beetles moved through the moss in unhurried processions. A tiny tree frog, no larger than Cepot's thumbnail, bright green with golden eyes, regarded them from the shelter of a lichen tuft with an expression of miniature outrage at the disruption of its habitat.

"Hold... on," the spirit said.

Mbah Jangkung rose to his full height, and the world changed.

The ground dropped away. The understory -- that twilight world of fern and moss and filtered light where they had been walking for hours -- fell below them, receding with a speed that was not fast in absolute terms but that *felt* fast because it was happening to them, because they were rising, lifting, ascending through layers of forest that they had not known existed. The trunk-space of the middle canopy opened around them -- a world of horizontal branches and hanging gardens and the interlocking architecture of a thousand trees whose crowns had grown together over centuries to form a continuous platform of leaves and wood and air, a world above the world, a second forest floating above the first.

And then they broke through the canopy.

Cepot gasped.

The upper canopy of the Wanantara was a sea of green. It stretched in every direction, unbroken, rolling in gentle swells that followed the contours of the land beneath -- rising over hills, dipping into valleys, rippling in the breeze with a motion that was so fluid, so wavelike, that for a disorienting moment, Cepot could not tell whether he was looking at a forest or an ocean. The surface of this green sea was not uniform but textured -- darker where the *damar* grew in dense stands, lighter where the *rasamala* lifted their pale crowns above the general level, interrupted by the vast, spreading umbrellas of the *waringin* whose canopies were so broad that they created islands of shade visible from above. Flowers were scattered across this green expanse like offerings on a ceremonial cloth -- the red of wild hibiscus, the white of frangipani, the purple-blue of an orchid species so rare that it had no name in any language but the forest's own.

Birds moved through the air above the canopy in flocks and pairs and singles -- hornbills with their ponderous, dignified wingbeats; parakeets in screaming, green-and-red torrents; eagles circling on thermals with the patience of creatures that have all the time in the world and all the sky in which to spend it. A cloud of golden pollen drifted through a shaft of sunlight, each grain catching the light and burning like a microscopic star, so that the air itself seemed to glitter with a fine, diffuse gold that settled on Cepot's skin and hair and eyelashes and made him feel, for a moment, gilded -- touched by a radiance that was not divine but was, perhaps, the forest's way of saying *welcome to the roof of the world*.

Dawala was crying. Not with sadness -- with beauty. The tears ran down his dirty, scratched face, cutting clean tracks through the grime, and he did not wipe them away because his hands were occupied with gripping the moss of Mbah Jangkung's shoulder, and because he did not want to close his eyes for even the duration of a blink. He was seeing the forest -- his forest, all forests, the Ur-forest from which all forests descended -- from a perspective that no Tuyul had ever seen it, and the sight was so beautiful that it bypassed his mind entirely and entered his heart without mediation, raw and unprocessed and overwhelming.

"*Giri*," Dawala whispered. "The *giri*. The mountains."

Cepot turned his head to follow Dawala's gaze, and the beauty drained from the world like water from a broken vessel.

To the east, on the horizon, Gunung Merapi stood against the sky. The great volcano was a dark, perfect cone -- or it should have been perfect, but it was not. Its symmetry was marred by the plume of smoke that rose from its crater, not the white steam of a natural eruption but a grey-black column of ash and industrial filth that climbed straight up into the stratosphere, spreading at altitude into a flat, anvil-shaped cloud that blocked the sun and cast a shadow across the eastern landscape for leagues. The smoke was not coming from the mountain's natural vents. It was coming from lower on the slopes, from the ring of furnaces and forges that Empu Gandring had built -- the furnaces that were fed by the forest, that consumed the ancient trees of the lower slopes and converted them into charcoal and ash and the dark, cursed iron from which the weapons of the Shadow King's army were forged.

Even from this distance, Cepot could see the scars. The eastern slopes of Merapi, which should have been green -- which had been green, which the oldest maps showed as green, crowned with the same ancient forest that surrounded them now -- were grey. Stripped. Clear-cut. The earth exposed, raw and bleeding, the topsoil washing away in the rains to choke the rivers below with mud and ash. The deforestation was visible from the canopy as a wound in the landscape -- a spreading circle of grey death eating into the green body of the forest, advancing tree by tree, acre by acre, with the remorseless efficiency of a disease that knows no cure.

"Smoke," Mbah Jangkung rumbled. The word was heavy with the weight of witnessing -- not the quick, reactive horror of a creature seeing destruction for the first time, but the deep, accumulated grief of a guardian who has watched the destruction advance for years, who has felt each tree fall through the root-network like a nerve being severed, who has heard the screams of the young rasamala carried through the mycelium as chemical signals of distress -- warnings that traveled at the speed of fungal growth and arrived too late to help, always too late. "My... children... are... burning."

He began to walk.

The stride of a Sanghyang Wana was not like the stride of a human or a Tuyul or any creature that walked on flesh-and-bone legs. It was a stride of roots -- of structures designed not for locomotion but for anchoring, repurposed for motion with the slow, deliberate adaptation of a body that had spent centuries standing still and was now, with an effort of will that was botanical rather than muscular, choosing to move. Each step was an event. Each footfall was a minor earthquake. The vast root-feet lifted from the soil -- tearing free of the mycelium connections that had formed during even their brief period of rest, snapping fungal threads and uprooting small plants and displacing the soil in circular craters that filled immediately with water seeping up from below -- and descended again with a force that compressed the earth and sent shockwaves through the understory, shaking loose showers of leaves and startled birds and the occasional monkey, which swung away screaming through the canopy, outraged by the disruption of its arboreal commute.

The stride ate miles.

Cepot clung to the moss of Mbah Jangkung's shoulder and watched the forest flow past below them like a green river. Individual trees -- trees that were themselves enormous, that would have towered over the largest building in Padalarang -- passed at the height of Mbah Jangkung's waist, their crowns brushing against his bark with a rustling, whispering contact that might have been the arboreal equivalent of a greeting or might have been merely the physical consequence of a very large object moving through a very dense forest. Birds scattered before them in colorful explosions of alarm. A family of gibbons, hanging from a branch directly in Mbah Jangkung's path, regarded the approaching colossus with wide, golden eyes and then, with the nonchalant acrobatics of creatures for whom gravity was a suggestion rather than a law, swung to a safe distance and watched the procession pass with expressions of anthropoid fascination.

To the west, the forest descended in green terraces toward a river valley that Cepot could see as a silver thread winding through the canopy -- a river whose course had been carved over millennia by water that had first fallen as rain on the slopes of mountains that were themselves older than the concept of naming, and that now ran through channels so deeply embedded in the landscape that the trees on either bank had grown their roots across the water, forming bridges of living wood through which the river passed like a thought through a mind, continuous, unbroken, connecting upstream to downstream, highland to lowland, the volcanic springs of the summits to the brackish estuaries of the coast. Mist rose from the river in pale columns that caught the light and burned white against the green, and where the mist touched the canopy it beaded into droplets that rolled down the leaves and fell back to the water, completing a cycle of evaporation and condensation so elegant, so self-sustaining, that it needed no engine but the sun and no plumbing but the forest itself.

To the north, the land rose toward a ridge crowned with trees so ancient that their trunks had thickened beyond all proportion, becoming wider than they were tall, their bark split and fissured into plates of grey wood that looked less like trees and more like the foundations of ruined temples -- as though the forest, in its deepest antiquity, had tried to build something permanent and had succeeded, producing monuments of living wood that would outlast any stone that human hands could lay. These were the mother trees of the northern ridge, the *karuhun* of the canopy, and even from this distance Cepot could see the slow, contemplative movement of their highest branches as they turned their leaves toward the sun with the measured deliberation of worshippers turning their faces toward a source of grace.

"Look," Dawala said, pointing downward.

Below them, in a clearing barely visible through the canopy's dense weave, a stream crossed a bed of pale volcanic stone, and beside the stream, a cluster of fruit trees grew in an abundance so concentrated that it seemed deliberate -- as though some ancient gardener had planted this small orchard in the midst of the wild forest for the benefit of travelers who would not arrive for centuries. There were *durian* trees, their thorned fruit hanging from the branches like the maces of a fairy-tale army. There were *rambutan*, their red, hairy skins bright as lanterns against the green. There were *manggis* -- mangosteen -- their dark purple shells promising the white, sweet flesh within that was, in Nini Anteh's considered opinion, the finest fruit that the *Pertiwi* had ever produced.

Mbah Jangkung stopped. The cessation of motion was as sudden and total as the cessation of an earthquake -- one moment the ground was moving, the next it was not, and the silence that followed was filled with the slow, settling creaks of a vast body coming to rest.

"Eat," he said. The word was simple, unadorned, the shortest utterance Cepot had yet heard from the ancient spirit. He lowered his hand to the level of the clearing, the twig-fingers forming a ramp, and the two Tuyul scrambled down to the stream bank with the eager, undignified haste of creatures whose bellies had been making increasingly strident demands.

The *rambutan* were ripe -- so ripe that their red skins had begun to split, revealing the translucent, pearl-like flesh within. Cepot tore one open with his teeth and the juice ran down his chin, sweet and slightly acidic, and the taste of it -- the taste of *fruit*, of something grown and ripened and given freely by the earth -- was so overwhelming after the raw ferns and sour *salak* of the morning that he had to stop chewing and press the heels of his hands against his eyes, because the sweetness had done something to the barrier he had built between himself and the unbearable fact of his own vulnerability, and for a moment, just a moment, the barrier wavered.

"*Aing* is not crying because of fruit," he announced to no one in particular, his voice thick. "This is an allergic reaction. *Aing* is allergic to being rescued by trees."

"Of course, *Dika*," Dawala said, peeling a mangosteen with the reverent care of a priest opening a reliquary. He split the purple shell and the white segments lay within like a crown of pale jewels. He ate them one by one, slowly, closing his eyes between bites, and on his face was an expression that was part hunger satisfied and part something holier -- the expression of a creature receiving communion with the earth that had grown it.

Mbah Jangkung watched them eat from above, his great brown eyes half-closed, his expression -- insofar as bark and lichen and centuries of weathering could compose an expression -- one of a deep, slow, arboreal contentment. A twig-finger reached into the canopy of a nearby *durian* and plucked a fruit the size of Dawala's torso, lowering it to the ground beside the stream with the precision of a crane operated by the most patient crane-operator in history.

"Careful," he rumbled. "The... spiny... one... bites."

Cepot regarded the durian -- massive, thorned, smelling of heaven and sulfur in equal measure -- and grinned. It was the first real grin he had produced since the Leak, wide and unguarded and slightly manic, the grin of a Tuyul who has discovered that even in the darkest forest, even in the middle of a war that he did not start and cannot finish, the earth still produces *durian*, and as long as the earth produces durian, the world is not entirely lost.

"*Aing* has been fighting monsters," he said to Dawala, cracking the durian's thorned husk against a stone. "But *Aing* draws the line at being defeated by a fruit."

They ate until their bellies were tight, and Mbah Jangkung waited with the patience of something that had been waiting since before the concept of waiting was invented. Dawala found a wild honeycomb in the hollow of a dead stump near the stream -- the comb built by *tawon madu*, the forest honey bees, whose honey was darker and richer and more complex than any honey produced by the domesticated hives of the lowlands, carrying in its viscous amber the concentrated nectar of a thousand forest flowers, each one contributing its own note to a flavor so layered, so dense with botanical information, that eating it was less a gustatory experience than an education. Dawala scooped the honey with a leaf and they shared it, licking their fingers, and the sweetness entered their blood and steadied their hearts and gave them, for the first time in days, something that was not quite hope but was perhaps the soil from which hope might, given time, begin to grow.

They climbed back onto Mbah Jangkung's shoulders, sticky-fingered and full, and the great spirit resumed his walk.

Other Sanghyang Wana joined them on the march.

They emerged from the forest on either side -- slowly, ploddingly, with the careful, deliberate motion of beings for whom haste was a foreign concept. A young *rasamala* spirit, slender and vibrant, its bark the color of cinnamon, fell into step beside Mbah Jangkung with the eager, slightly clumsy energy of a youth trying to match the stride of an elder. Its eyes were bright, angry -- the eyes of a young tree whose generation was being felled. An ancient *waringin* spirit, vast and many-trunked, moved with a ponderous dignity that made Mbah Jangkung look hurried by comparison, its aerial root-limbs swaying like the arms of a dancer performing a ritual so slow that each gesture took minutes to complete. A teak warrior -- thick, dense, dark-barked, its grain-patterned face set in an expression of stone-like determination -- marched in silence, each footfall driving its root-feet deep into the earth, as though it intended not merely to walk upon the ground but to conquer it.

More came. And more. From every direction, from depths of the forest that Cepot had not known existed, the Sanghyang Wana emerged, shaking off the green weight of centuries of sleep, pulling their roots from the soil with the tearing, elemental sound of something fundamental separating -- the sound of the forest remaking itself, reorganizing its priorities, shifting from a mode of patience to a mode of purpose.

As the column grew, the young rasamala spirit that had first joined their march turned its bright, angry eyes toward Mbah Jangkung's shoulder, where Cepot and Dawala sat. It regarded them with an expression that combined curiosity with skepticism -- the skepticism of a young creature that has not yet learned that small things can carry large significance.

"These... are... the... ones?" it said, its voice faster than Mbah Jangkung's, each word separated by seconds rather than the Long Father's continental pauses. "These... little... soft... things? They... bring... the... news... of... the... furnace?"

"They bring more than news," Mbah Jangkung said. "They... bring... proof... that... the... fast... world... and... the... slow... world... can... still... speak... to... each... other. That... the... pact... is... not... entirely... forgotten."

The young rasamala considered this, its cinnamon bark flushing with a deeper shade -- the arboreal equivalent of a blush or a concession. It dipped its crown in a motion that might have been a bow, and resumed its march.

Behind them, a cluster of *damar* spirits had begun to weep -- not with sorrow, though sorrow was part of it, but with the automatic, chemical response of resinous trees to the stress of motion and the proximity of grief. Their amber tears caught the sunlight as they marched, each drop burning gold before it fell, and the trail they left behind them was a pathway of hardened resin that glittered on the forest floor like a road paved with solidified light.

A *meranti* spirit, one of the tallest Cepot had yet seen, emerged from a stand of its kin on the western ridge and strode toward the column with steps so long that each one covered the distance of a village road. Its crown breached the canopy by three meters, and as it walked, it trailed a wake of displaced air that bent the upper branches of its neighbors and sent flocks of startled starlings wheeling into the sky. It fell into step behind Mbah Jangkung with a deference that was visible in the slight inclination of its crown, the careful shortening of its stride, the way it placed its root-feet precisely in the footprints that the Long Father had left -- an act of respect so ancient and so instinctive that it might have been the first ritual ever performed by a living thing: to walk in the footsteps of the elder.

The march through the canopy took hours. The sun -- visible, here above the tree-line, as a fierce yellow disc in a sky hazed with the distant ash of Merapi's furnaces -- tracked its arc from east to zenith, and in its light, the forest below was a tapestry of a thousand greens, each shade representing a different species, a different age, a different relationship to the soil and the water and the light that sustained them all. Cepot, who had never thought much about trees -- who had climbed them, eaten their fruit, used their shade, and never once considered them as beings with their own inner lives -- found himself seeing the forest differently. Not as a collection of individual organisms but as a single, vast, interconnected entity -- a superorganism, a mind distributed across a million roots and a billion leaves, thinking its slow, green thoughts and arriving, finally, at a conclusion.

The conclusion was anger.

They arrived at the Sabha Wana as the sun began its descent.

The clearing was not a clearing in the ordinary sense. No one had cleared it. No axe, no fire, no human intention had created this space. It was a natural amphitheater -- a broad, shallow bowl in the forest floor, perhaps three hundred meters across, formed by the confluence of three ridges that descended from a low hill and created, at their junction, a depression that was open to the sky. The canopy pulled back here, as though the trees that grew on the ridges had agreed, by some ancient arboricultural pact, to lean away from the center rather than toward it, creating a circle of open sky above the bowl -- a disc of blue (or today, of grey, hazed with the distant ash) that let the sunlight pour down into the clearing in a warm, golden flood that was, after the perpetual twilight of the understory, almost overwhelming.

And the clearing was full.

Sanghyang Wana filled the Sabha Wana the way an audience fills a theater. They stood on the slopes of the bowl, on the ridges, among the trees at the edges of the clearing, their vast, slow bodies arranged in circles and clusters and irregular groupings that followed some organizational principle that Cepot could not decode. There were hundreds of them. Perhaps more -- the edges of the gathering faded into the forest, and it was impossible to tell where the Sanghyang Wana ended and the ordinary trees began, because some of them were standing so still that they had become, temporarily, indistinguishable from the trees they had once been.

They were of every species and every age. Young *rasamala* spirits stood in angry clusters, their cinnamon bark flushed with the red of agitation, their eyes bright and hot, their branch-arms gesticulating with a speed that was, for a Sanghyang Wana, practically frantic -- which is to say, they moved their arms once every ten seconds rather than once every minute. Ancient *waringin* elders occupied the center of the clearing, their vast, multi-trunked bodies spreading like islands of shade and wisdom, their aerial root-limbs hanging in still curtains, their eyes half-closed in the meditative state that was, for them, the equivalent of deep listening. Teak warriors stood in a ring around the perimeter, their dense, dark bodies forming a wall of living wood that was both audience and defense, their grain-patterned faces set in expressions that combined patience with menace in a ratio that Cepot found deeply unsettling.

There were *damar* spirits, weeping their amber resin, the tears catching the sunlight and glowing like drops of liquid fire. There were *mahogany* spirits, tall and straight, their bark the color of dried blood, their eyes the color of the earth after a volcano has enriched it. There were *meranti* spirits, some of the tallest in the gathering, their crowns reaching so high that they seemed to touch the circle of sky overhead. There were *bamboo* spirits -- not trees, strictly, but grasses, slender and flexible and numerous, standing in whispering groups at the edges of the assembly, their movements quicker and more fluid than those of their wooden cousins, their voices -- for they murmured among themselves constantly, a rustling, clicking, sliding sound that was the arboreal equivalent of gossip -- producing a continuous underscore to the deeper, slower voices of the trees.

And there were smaller spirits too. Fern-spirits, no taller than Cepot, their fronds curling and uncurling with a nervous energy that mirrored his own. Moss-spirits, flat and low, covering the ground in living carpets that observed the proceedings from below. Fungal spirits, pale and ghostly, luminescent in the shadows at the edge of the clearing, their cap-shaped heads swaying on slender stalks as they listened.

Mbah Jangkung strode into the Sabha Wana, and the assembly parted before him like the forest parting before a river. The gathered spirits turned their vast, slow eyes toward the shepherd and his passengers, and Cepot felt the weight of that collective gaze settle on him like the weight of the forest itself -- immense, patient, measuring.

"We... are... here," Mbah Jangkung announced. His voice, already deep, was amplified by the acoustics of the natural amphitheater, rolling across the gathering like thunder. "The... roots... have... spoken. The... young... are... falling. The... smoke... rises. The... iron... bites."

He paused. The silence was absolute -- not the silence of absence but the silence of attention, of a thousand ancient minds focusing simultaneously on a single point.

"And... these... little... roots..." He lifted his hand, raising Cepot and Dawala into view of the entire assembly. "These... little... ones... have... come... from... the... world... of... the... fast... ones... to... tell... us... what... we... already... know. That... the... time... for... patience... is... ending."

A murmur passed through the assembly. Not a human murmur -- not the quick, vocal rustling of a crowd. This was a deep, subsonic vibration that traveled through the ground as much as through the air, a tectonic whisper that Cepot felt in the soles of his feet and the base of his spine. The Sanghyang Wana were talking to each other, communicating through channels that bypassed the air entirely -- through the mycelium network, through root-contact, through the chemical signals that trees exchange when they are stressed or threatened or deciding something important.

A young rasamala stepped forward. Its movements were quick -- angry-quick, impatient-quick, the quick of a being whose normal timescale has been compressed by fury.

"Then... we... march!" it said. Its voice was higher than Mbah Jangkung's, faster, each word separated by seconds rather than minutes. "We... march... on... the... furnaces! We... tear... out... the... iron! We... crush... the... cutters!"

A rumble of agreement from other young spirits. Branch-arms waved. Root-feet stamped, sending tremors through the clearing.

But the waringin elders did not move. Their eyes remained half-closed. Their aerial roots swayed gently, contemplatively. And from the largest of them -- a spirit so vast, so ancient, so heavily burdened with lichen and moss and the accumulated weight of centuries that it looked less like a tree and more like a hill -- a single word emerged.

"Wait."

The word fell into the gathering like a stone into a still pond. The ripples spread outward, silencing the young rasamala, stilling the stamping root-feet, bringing the assembly back to the deep, slow rhythm that was the natural tempo of arboreal deliberation.

"This... is... the... Sabha... Wana," the ancient waringin said. Its voice was the voice of deep time, of geological patience, of a mind that had been old when the oldest rasamala in the gathering was a seed. "We... do... not... decide... quickly. We... decide... *well*. The... roots... must... speak. The... leaves... must... listen. The... wind... must... be... consulted."

Mbah Jangkung lowered Cepot and Dawala to the ground, setting them gently on the moss of the clearing floor. They stood there, tiny among the gathered giants, two small figures in a forest of living monuments, and Cepot felt, for the first time in his life, the full, overwhelming reality of his own smallness -- not the smallness of being a Tuyul, which he had always known and mostly accepted, but the smallness of being a brief, fast, noisy thing in a world of slow, vast, patient things that had been here before him and would be here after him and whose deliberations, however slow, would shake the earth.

"They're going to debate," Cepot whispered to Dawala. "They're going to debate whether to go to war."

"How long will it take?" Dawala whispered back.

Cepot looked at Mbah Jangkung, who had settled into a standing position at the edge of the inner circle, his root-feet sinking into the soil, his branch-arms folded, his eyes focused on the waringin elder with the patient attentiveness of a shepherd who knows that the flock must make its own decisions but reserves the right to guide.

"I think," Cepot said, "it might take a while."

"How long is a while?"

Cepot watched the assembly. The waringin elder was speaking again -- slowly, so slowly that each word seemed to take a year to form. The young rasamala were listening, fidgeting, their branch-arms moving with the restless energy of beings who wanted to act and were being asked to think. The teak warriors stood in their ring, impassive, waiting. The bamboo whispered. The ferns curled and uncurled. The moss observed.

"Days," Cepot said. "Maybe longer. Forest time is not our time."

Dawala sat down in the moss. He crossed his legs, placed his hands on his knees, and closed his eyes. His breathing slowed. The tension in his shoulders -- the tension that had been there since the Leak seized them, since the night flight, since the dawn fall -- eased, degree by degree, until his posture was less that of a frightened refugee and more that of a Tuyul who has found, in the most unlikely of places, a frequency he recognizes.

"Then we wait," Dawala said. "We listen. We learn their pace."

Cepot stared at his cousin. Then he sat down beside him. It was, he reflected, the hardest thing he had ever done -- harder than climbing the arm of a living mountain, harder than facing the eyes of an ancient forest spirit, harder than admitting that he was afraid. Sitting still. Being quiet. Letting the forest speak at its own speed.

But he did it.

And around them, vast and slow and patient and angry and ancient and alive, the Sabha Wana deliberated. The voices of the Sanghyang Wana rose and fell like the breathing of the earth -- slow, deep, fundamental, a conversation that had been waiting to happen for centuries and that would not be rushed by the urgency of creatures who measured time in heartbeats rather than the growth-rings of wood.

The debate would take time. The forest's patience was deep.

But so was the forest's fury.

And when the decision came -- when the last root had spoken and the last leaf had listened and the last whisper of wind had been consulted -- the answer would shake the mountains.

Even Cepot, who was not patient by nature or by inclination or by any measure that could be applied to a Tuyul from Padalarang, understood this. The Sabha Wana was not merely a council. It was a watershed. It was the moment at which the oldest, slowest, most patient force in Nusantara turned its attention from the long game of centuries to the immediate, pressing, terrible game of war. And when a force that patient decides to move, the ground itself takes notice.

Somewhere to the east, beyond the green horizon, the furnaces of Merapi belched their poison into the sky.

Somewhere to the south, two small figures climbed a mountain of fire, carrying a weight that was destroying one of them.

Somewhere to the north, a king without a crown rode toward his destiny.

And here, in the heart of the ancient forest, in the living cathedral of the Wanantara, two Tuyul from Padalarang sat among the giants and listened to the trees decide the fate of the world.
