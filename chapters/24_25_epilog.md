# Bab 24: Jalan yang Terpisah

## (Separate Paths)

---

The Selat Sunda burned.

Not with the natural fire of Krakatau, though the great volcano smoldered on the southern horizon, its dark plume rising into the stratosphere like the pillar of smoke that marks a funeral pyre built for a world. The strait burned with war -- with the orange blossoms of Buta Raksasa torches reflected in the black water, with the red glow of signal fires on a dozen volcanic islets, with the flash of steel and the brighter flash of sakti released in desperation by warriors who had moved beyond strategy into the pure, incandescent mathematics of survival.

On the northernmost island of the chain -- a jagged tooth of volcanic basalt rising from the strait like the broken jaw of a drowned giant -- the five remaining companions fought.

Raden Wijaya killed the first Buta Raksasa with a stroke so clean that the creature seemed not to realize it was dead. It took three steps forward on its massive legs, its tusked mouth still contorted in its war-scream, its stone club raised for a blow that would never fall -- and then its knees folded, and it collapsed like a temple whose foundations have been removed, and the ground shook, and Raden Wijaya was already moving, already turning, his keris in one hand and his sword in the other, both blades dark with blood that steamed in the volcanic air.

He was not the same man who had trained in the mist at Dieng.

That prince -- the exiled heir, the reluctant ranger, the man who carried his kingdom's name like a wound rather than a banner -- had died somewhere on the road between the caves of Gunung Sewu and this burning strait. What had taken his place was something older and simpler and infinitely more dangerous: a king who had found his war. Every movement was decision. Every strike was command. He did not fight with the flowing artistry of the silat master who had once demonstrated forms for a small Tuyul in the highland mist -- he fought with the devastating economy of a man who understood that each second wasted was a second stolen from two small figures climbing a mountain of fire, and he would not waste a single one.

"Dewi -- left flank! They are circling through the lava field!"

His voice carried above the chaos with an authority that was not volume but certainty -- the voice of a man whose orders were not requests but facts about the immediate future, spoken aloud merely as a courtesy. Dewi Anjani heard him and moved, and the Bidadari warrior-princess was a thing of terrible, luminous beauty as she ran -- her enchanted bow singing its high, silver note, each arrow finding the gap between a Buta Raksasa's crude iron plates with the precision of a needle finding silk. She fired as she ran. She fired without seeming to aim. Each shaft blazed with the Bidadari light that the dark things feared, and where they struck, the Buta screamed and fell, and the light lingered in their wounds like phosphorus in dark water.

Together, Raden Wijaya and Dewi Anjani were not merely effective. They were catastrophic. He drove into the Buta formations in a *Garudabihwa* -- the eagle formation, straight, deep, without hesitation -- and she moved around him in the spiraling arcs of a *Merak simper*, the peacock's covering fire, her arrows creating a perimeter of light through which no Raksasa could pass without burning. They had fought together now for weeks, through ambushes and running battles and desperate stands on islands that had no names, and in that fighting they had developed a language that needed no words -- a syntax of movement, a grammar of violence, where a shift of his shoulder told her *cover my right* and a tilt of her head told him *three approaching from the ridge* and the conversation flowed without pause, without error, without the clumsy intervention of speech.

The Buta Raksasa were enormous -- three meters tall, some of them, their grey-green skin thick as boiled leather, their tusked faces twisted with a malice that was not personal but structural, built into them by the Shadow King's ancient craft the way the pamor patterns were built into a keris. They fought with stone clubs and crude iron axes and the raw, mindless ferocity of creatures bred for destruction, and there were many of them -- fifty, perhaps sixty, pouring from the boats that had brought them across the strait's narrow channels, their war-drums pounding a rhythm that was less music than heartbeat, the heartbeat of an army that did not need courage because it had never learned fear.

Against them: five.

Tumenggung Batu stood at the center of the defensive line -- if a line of five could be called a line -- and he did not move. The Kurcaci chieftain had planted his iron-shod boots on a flat slab of volcanic basalt as though he intended to grow roots, and his war hammer described great sweeping arcs before him, each swing ending with a sound that Kian Santang, had he been there to hear it, would have recognized as the sound the Jembatan Batu had made when the Naga's weight first tested it: the deep, structural percussion of stone meeting something that should not have tried to be harder than stone. Each swing connected. Each connection ended an argument. The Buta Raksasa came at him in pairs and trios, and they broke against him the way waves break against a cliff -- with tremendous force and no lasting effect -- and he stood, and he swung, and they fell, and the basalt beneath his feet cracked from the impacts but Tumenggung Batu did not crack, because the Kurcaci are of the mountain, and the mountain does not yield.

"Is that all?" he roared, as a Buta three times his height staggered backward with its jaw shattered. "My grandmother hit harder! And she was blind in one eye!"

But his arms were heavy. His breath came in gasps that rattled in his iron breastplate. They had been fighting for hours -- or was it days? Time had become unreliable on these burning islands, measured not in hours but in waves of attack and the brief, gasping intervals between them. His hammer was slower than it had been. A fraction. A heartbeat's delay between the backswing and the strike. To an untrained eye, nothing. To a Kurcaci who had forged his first blade at the age of twelve and understood metal and momentum the way poets understand metaphor -- everything.

He would not say it. He would die before admitting weakness. But Raden Wijaya saw it, because a king sees what his warriors will not say, and the prince adjusted -- shifted the line, put himself between the thickest press of Buta and the Kurcaci chieftain, absorbed two blows on his sword that would have found the old dwarf's helm, and said nothing about it, because a king also knows when silence is the greater kindness.

And on the flanks -- darting, dodging, appearing and vanishing like fireflies in a storm -- the two Tuyul.

---

Cepot had always been clever. Cleverness was his armor, his weapon, his identity -- the sharp-tongued wit of Padalarang, the Tuyul who could talk his way out of a tiger's mouth and into its good graces. But the cleverness that served him now was not the cleverness of words. It was the cleverness of a mind that had been forced, by weeks of violence and terror and the loss of people it loved, to apply its considerable gifts to the problem of killing things larger than itself before they killed it first.

He had studied the terrain before the Buta arrived -- scrambling across the island's volcanic slopes in the last hour of daylight, cataloguing every feature with the obsessive attention of a Tuyul who knew that the landscape itself was the most powerful weapon available to someone who stood chest-high to his enemies. He found crevices where a Buta's massive foot would catch and snap. He found ledges where loose pumice could be triggered to slide. He found a narrow pass between two lava formations where the rock pinched tight enough that no creature wider than a Kurcaci could pass, and he had lured three Buta into it with a stream of insults so creative and so anatomically specific that even Tumenggung Batu had raised an eyebrow, and when the creatures wedged themselves in the gap, snarling and thrashing, Cepot had simply climbed the lava wall and dropped loose boulders on their heads until the snarling stopped.

"*Aksama bae nya, teu sopan,*" Cepot muttered as the last Buta crumbled -- *Pardon me, how rude* -- the basa loma of Padalarang delivered with the deadpan courtesy of a Tuyul whose earthy wisdom expressed itself most naturally in the language of home, even on a volcanic battlefield at the edge of the world.

He fought with his short knife when he had to -- with the quick, vicious precision of a mongoose fighting a cobra, all speed and angles and the absolute refusal to be where the enemy expected him to be. A slash across a hamstring. A blade driven into the soft flesh behind a knee. He did not fight heroically. Heroes fought fair, and Cepot had never seen the appeal of fairness when unfairness kept you breathing. He fought like what he was: a small, brilliant, terrified person who had decided that survival was a creative problem and was solving it with every tool available, including several that the silat masters would have considered beneath their dignity.

Dawala fought beside him, and the change in the younger Tuyul was the kind of transformation that the wayang tales assign to divine intervention but that the actual world produces through the simpler, more brutal mechanism of necessity.

The Dawala who had left Padalarang -- round-faced, sweet-natured, perpetually eating, frightened of the dark and of loud noises and of the idea that the world contained things less kind than his cousin Cepot's sharpest joke -- was gone. Not replaced, exactly. Still present, still visible in the wide brown eyes and the trembling lower lip and the way his voice cracked when the Buta screamed. But layered over, covered, armored -- by something that had been there all along, buried beneath the softness, waiting for a reason to emerge. He fought with a wooden staff that Tumenggung Batu had ironshod and weighted, and he fought with a ferocity that was shocking in its purity -- not rage, not bloodlust, nothing so dramatic. Just the absolute, unreasoning determination of a creature protecting the things it loves.

When a Buta broke through the line and lunged for Cepot, Dawala was there. He did not think about it. His body simply moved -- interposing itself between the stone club and his cousin with the mindless, instantaneous courage of a mother stepping in front of a child -- and his staff cracked against the Buta's wrist with a force that snapped the iron binding and sent the club spinning into the dark. The Buta howled. Dawala hit it again -- in the knee, in the jaw, in the throat -- a flurry of strikes that had no art and no elegance and no restraint, driven by the engine of pure love, and when the Buta fell, Dawala stood over it, panting, his staff raised, his round face terrible in the torchlight, and Cepot looked at him and saw something he had never seen before in his gentle, hungry, guileless cousin.

A warrior.

"Dawala," Cepot breathed.

"I'm fine," Dawala said. His voice was shaking. His hands were shaking. Everything was shaking. But he was standing, and the Buta was not.

"I didn't say --"

"I *said* I'm fine." The younger Tuyul's jaw set in a line that Cepot had never seen on that face before -- a line of iron beneath the softness, a vein of ore in gentle stone. "Stay behind me."

Cepot, who had never in his life stayed behind anyone, found that he had nothing to say.

---

The battle turned -- not through any single decisive blow but through the accumulated weight of an hour's fighting, the slow arithmetic of attrition working in their favor for once. The Buta Raksasa were not intelligent. They attacked in waves, without coordination, each creature fighting for itself, driven by the Shadow King's distant will but not guided by it -- a blunt instrument wielded by a hand too far away to aim with precision. And against them stood five beings who fought as one, whose coordination was not commanded but organic, grown from weeks of shared danger and trust and the peculiar intimacy of people who have saved each other's lives often enough to stop keeping count.

Raden Wijaya cut down three more in the final push -- moving through the Buta line like a monsoon wind through standing rice, his blade finding the seams in their crude armor with an instinct that was less training than memory, as though his ancestors' battles flowed through his blood and guided his hand. Dewi Anjani's arrows drove the survivors back toward the water's edge, their Bidadari light burning like small suns in the dark flesh of the retreating giants. Tumenggung Batu held the center until the last of the Buta broke and fled, and then he held it for three minutes more, because a Kurcaci does not cede ground on the basis of appearances, and because his legs had locked and he needed time to convince them to move again.

The island fell silent. The war-drums stopped. The torches of the retreating Buta Raksasa bobbed across the black water of the strait like a procession of funeral lanterns, growing smaller, growing dimmer, until they were only orange sparks against the immensity of the night sea.

Five warriors stood on volcanic stone and breathed.

---

"We cannot stay."

Raden Wijaya spoke first, because that was what he did now -- spoke first, decided first, moved first. The others had not agreed to this. There had been no vote, no coronation, no formal transfer of authority. It had simply happened, the way a river happens -- following the gradient of the land, flowing toward the lowest point, obeying a logic that predated intention. Raden Wijaya commanded because command was where he belonged, and the others followed because following him felt, for the first time in this terrible journey, like walking on solid ground.

They sat in a hollow of hardened lava, the five of them, their backs against the warm stone, their weapons across their knees. The strait stretched south before them, and on the horizon, Krakatau rose -- a dark cone against the deeper dark of the sky, its summit crowned with the dull red glow of its eternal fire, its plume of smoke blotting out the stars. Somewhere on those slopes, Kian Santang and Bagus were climbing.

"The diversion worked," Dewi Anjani said. Her voice was calm, measured, but beneath the composure, exhaustion moved like deep water beneath ice. Her quiver was nearly empty. Her luminous skin was dimmer than it had been -- not extinguished but reduced, a lamp turned low, conserving what remained. "We drew the main Buta force north. The southern approaches to Krakatau should be clear -- or clearer than they were."

"For now," Raden Wijaya said. "Empu Gandring will not be fooled for long. When his scouts report that the keris-bearer is not among us, he will redirect. We bought Kian Santang hours. Perhaps a day. Not more."

He stared south, toward the mountain, and his face in the volcanic glow was the face of a man performing a calculation whose variables were lives.

"We need to draw them further. Not just from Krakatau -- from the whole southern strait. We need to become a threat so great that the Shadow King cannot afford to ignore us."

He paused. The words gathered behind his eyes like clouds before a storm.

"We need an army."

Tumenggung Batu grunted. "Armies do not grow on trees, prince. Even on this cursed island."

"No," Raden Wijaya said. "They grow in kingdoms. Kingdoms that have been waiting -- afraid, leaderless, divided -- for someone to give them a reason to unite."

He reached for the keris at his belt. Not the Keris Murka -- that blade was far away now, climbing toward fire on the back of a small Tuyul. This was the other keris. The one he had carried since Dieng, the one Prabu Siliwangi had returned to him in the council chamber with words that had struck him harder than any blow: *This is yours by blood. It has been waiting.*

The Keris Nagasasra.

He drew it from its sheath, and the blade caught the red light of Krakatau's distant glow, and the pamor patterns -- serpentine, coiling, alive -- seemed to move in the firelight, flowing like water through the metal, like the dragon-serpent for which the blade was named. It was ancient. It was beautiful. It was the ancestral weapon of the Mataram throne, carried by kings for twelve generations before the Shadow King's treachery had driven the last true heir into exile. Its weight in his hand was the weight of history -- not a burden but a foundation, the bedrock upon which everything that came next would be built.

Raden Wijaya stood.

He held the Keris Nagasasra before him -- point upward, the way the old kings held their blades when swearing oaths, when claiming thrones, when speaking words that would outlast the speakers and become part of the story of the world.

"I am Raden Wijaya," he said. His voice carried the krama inggil of a coronation vow -- the highest register of Javanese, the language of sacred oaths and royal proclamations, the register in which words become binding upon the cosmos itself. "*Kula Raden Wijaya, putranipun Prabu Hayam Wuruk, ahli waris dhampar Mataram.*"

*I am Raden Wijaya, son of Prabu Hayam Wuruk, heir to the throne of Mataram.*

His voice did not thunder. It did not echo. It carried the quiet, unshakeable certainty of a man who has stopped running from his own name.

"I claim my birthright. Not for glory. Not for empire." He turned the blade, and the pamor caught the light, and the serpent patterns blazed. "For the defense of all Nusantara -- from the highlands of Sunda to the spice groves of Maluku, from the coasts of Sulawesi to the sacred valleys of Papua, from the forests of Kalimantan to the shores of the outermost islands. All Nusantara. All her peoples. United in dharma against the darkness."

The words hung in the volcanic air like the last note of a gamelan, resonating in the stone and the dark and the space between heartbeats.

Dewi Anjani knelt. The Bidadari moved with the fluid grace that made all her movements seem ceremonial, lowering herself to one knee on the volcanic stone, her bow held horizontally before her in the Bidadari salute. Her ancient eyes, those eyes that had seen the rise and fall of kingdoms, regarded Raden Wijaya with an expression that combined reverence and assessment in equal measure -- the expression of an immortal who has outlived many kings and knows which ones deserve the kneeling.

"The Bidadari recognize the bloodline of Mataram," she said. "Rise or fall, we stand with you."

Tumenggung Batu lowered himself -- slowly, with visible effort, each inch of descent an apparent violation of some deep Kurcaci principle. His iron armor groaned. His knees cracked like splitting stone. His broad face contorted with an expression that might have been reverence or might have been the physical discomfort of a creature built for standing and forced into a posture his body considered structurally unsound.

"I kneel to no man," he growled. He paused. The volcanic light played across his craggy features. "But I will make an exception. Once. Do not expect it again."

Cepot watched all of this with an expression that balanced irreverence and genuine emotion on a knife's edge -- the expression of a Tuyul who understood the weight of what was happening and could not quite suppress the impulse to comment on it.

"About time," he said. His voice was light, but his eyes were not. "I was beginning to think I'd have to draw you a diagram."

He did not kneel. He bowed -- the Sundanese bow of respect, hand to heart, head inclined -- and it was, from Cepot, the equivalent of prostration.

Dawala looked at the kneeling Bidadari, the kneeling Kurcaci, the bowing Cepot, and then at Raden Wijaya, holding the ancestral blade of a lost kingdom against a sky of fire and smoke, and his round face went through the rapid succession of expressions that meant his mind was processing something his heart had already understood.

"Does this mean we get a castle?" he asked.

Raden Wijaya's mouth twitched. Despite everything -- the blood on his sword, the dead on the island, the war that stretched ahead like a road with no visible end -- the corner of his mouth lifted, just once, just briefly, in acknowledgment of the fact that even in the darkest hours, the Tuyul could not be anything other than exactly, irreducibly, hopelessly themselves.

"Perhaps," he said. "If we survive to claim one."

"I want a room with a view," Dawala said. "And a kitchen."

"You always want a kitchen," Cepot muttered.

"Kitchens are important."

Raden Wijaya sheathed the Keris Nagasasra. The moment was over. The oath was sworn. The king had claimed his name. And now -- as with all claims, as with all oaths, as with all words spoken in the dark with fire on the horizon -- the real work began.

---

Later, in the hours between the battle's end and the march's beginning, the two Tuyul cousins sat together on a shelf of cooled lava, their legs dangling over the edge, the black water of the strait lapping at the rocks below. Behind them, Raden Wijaya and Dewi Anjani were planning the northern march, their voices low and urgent, the map of the coastline spread between them on the stone. Tumenggung Batu had fallen asleep sitting upright against a boulder, his hammer across his knees, his head tilted back, snoring with the deep, percussive regularity of a forge bellows.

Cepot's knife was in his hands, turning slowly, its edge catching the red light. His face, in repose, was different from the face he showed the world -- younger, softer, stripped of the armor of wit. He looked, for once, like what he was: a young Tuyul, far from home, tired beyond the ability of tiredness to measure, carrying a grief he had never spoken aloud because speaking it would make it real.

Dawala sat beside him, close enough that their shoulders touched. The younger Tuyul had cleaned his staff and rewrapped the iron binding with strips of cloth torn from his own shirt. His hands, which had shaken after the battle, were steady now. His round face was bruised where a Buta's backhand had caught him -- a purple bloom across his left cheek that would darken by morning -- but his eyes were clear, and there was something in them that had not been there before Gunung Sewu, before the Naga, before the bridge.

Dawala spoke first.

"Do you think Kian will be okay?"

The question was quiet, offered to the night and the water and the distant red glow of Krakatau without expectation of an answer. It was the question that lived beneath all their other questions -- beneath the strategy and the logistics and the grim arithmetic of war. It was the question that kept Dawala awake at night and that Cepot answered, each time, with a different joke, because jokes were how Cepot told the truth when the truth was too heavy for plain speech.

But tonight, Cepot did not joke.

He turned the knife in his hands, watching the light play along its edge, and when he spoke, his voice carried none of its usual sharpness. It was soft. It was honest. It was the voice of the boy beneath the wit, the heart beneath the humor.

"He has Bagus," Cepot said.

"Yes."

"That is the most dangerous weapon in Nusantara."

Dawala frowned. "Bagus? Bagus is a gardener."

"Bagus is someone who loves Kian Santang and will not let him quit." Cepot sheathed the knife. He looked at his cousin -- at the bruise and the steady hands and the new iron in the gentle eyes -- and something in his expression shifted, a tectonic movement beneath the surface, the kind of shift that changes the landscape permanently. "That is more dangerous than any keris. More dangerous than sakti, or Bidadari magic, or a Kurcaci war hammer. The Shadow King has armies. He has the Leak. He has a mountain of fire and the most terrible blade ever forged. But he does not have someone who will sit beside him when he falls and say, *Get up. I believe in you. Get up.*"

Dawala was quiet for a moment. The water lapped at the rocks. The stars turned overhead.

"We were like that once," Dawala said. "Just two Tuyul from Padalarang. Eating klepon and stealing rambutan from Nini Anteh's tree."

"We are still like that," Cepot said. "Underneath all this." He gestured vaguely at the volcanic island, the black strait, the dying fires of the battle, the entire improbable apocalypse of their present circumstances. "Underneath everything. I am still me. You are still you. We are still from Padalarang."

"But we're different."

"Yes."

"Better?"

Cepot considered this with the seriousness he usually reserved for questions of food preparation and the comparative merits of sambal varieties. "Older," he said finally. "Older is not always better. But sometimes --" He paused. Looked at the mountain on the horizon. Looked at his cousin. "Sometimes it is what is needed."

Dawala leaned against him, shoulder to shoulder, the way they had leaned against each other as children on the veranda of their grandmother's rumah panggung, watching the fireflies in the sawah at dusk. And for a moment, the war and the fire and the vast indifferent machinery of fate seemed very far away, and they were just two Tuyul, sitting in the dark, being brave in the only way that mattered -- together.

---

Dawn broke over the strait, grey and heavy, the sun a smeared disc behind the volcanic haze. Raden Wijaya stood at the island's northern point, facing the coast of Jawa -- a dark line on the horizon, barely visible through the smoke, the green highlands rising behind it in layered ridges like the steps of a giant's staircase ascending toward the interior.

Mataram was there. Somewhere beyond those ridges, beyond the forests and the rivers and the villages that still bore the names his ancestors had given them -- the throne that had been empty since his father's fall, the kingdom that had scattered like seeds in a storm, waiting for the hand that would gather them again.

He turned to face his companions. Four warriors, battered and bloodied and burning with something that exhaustion could not extinguish. A Bidadari who had lived for millennia and chose to fight beside mortals. A Kurcaci who had lost his mountain and would not lose another thing. Two Tuyul who had been children when this journey began and were children no longer.

"We go north," Raden Wijaya said. "To Mataram. My people need their king." He paused, and his voice gathered strength, drawing from the deep well of certainty that had opened within him somewhere on these burning islands, somewhere between the first battle and the last, somewhere in the alchemy of blood and fire and the desperate, irrevocable decision to stop fleeing and start leading. "And the free kingdoms of Nusantara need to unite. Not under one keris. Not under one throne. Under one *purpose*."

He looked south, toward Krakatau. The mountain's plume rose into the stratosphere, dark against dark, and beneath it, invisible, impossibly small, impossibly brave, two Tuyul were climbing.

"While Kian Santang carries the blade to the fire, we will give him time. We will raise an army. We will remind the kingdoms of Nusantara that they are not alone, that they have never been alone, that the strength of these islands has always been *gotong royong* -- mutual aid, shared labor, the work that can only be done together."

He drew a breath. Released it. Began to walk.

"The war for Nusantara begins today."

Dewi Anjani fell into step beside him. Tumenggung Batu shouldered his hammer. Cepot and Dawala exchanged a glance -- the old glance, the Padalarang glance, the look that said *here we go again* -- and followed.

Five figures. Marching north. Toward a kingdom, and a war, and the uncertain, terrifying possibility that they might actually win.

---

And on the slopes of Krakatau, night had fallen.

The mountain was not silent. Mountains of fire are never silent -- they groan and mutter and exhale, the volcanic equivalent of a sleeper's troubled breathing, a constant reminder that the stillness of stone is an illusion and that beneath the surface, always, the fire moves. Kian Santang heard the mountain's voice in every register -- the low, subsonic rumble that vibrated in his bones, the hiss of gas escaping from cracks in the basalt, the distant, irregular percussion of rockfalls on the upper slopes, and beneath it all, deeper than sound, the vast tidal pulse of magma moving through channels carved by millennia of eruptions.

They huddled in a hollow of hardened lava -- a small, scooped depression in the mountainside where an ancient flow had cooled around a pocket of gas, leaving a shallow cave barely large enough for two small Tuyul to sit with their knees drawn to their chests. The rock was warm against their backs. Not the gentle warmth of stone heated by the sun, but the aggressive, internal warmth of a mountain that was alive with fire, a warmth that said *I am not asleep, I am not safe, I am only patient.*

Lava flows glowed in the darkness below them -- rivers of molten rock moving with the slow, terrible deliberation of things that have no need to hurry because they cannot be stopped. Their light painted the mountainside in shades of orange and red, and the shadows they cast were long and restless, flickering with the pulse of the flows, so that the landscape seemed to breathe -- expanding and contracting, alive with the mountain's molten heartbeat.

Krakatau was Brahma's forge -- or so the ancient resi priests had taught. The Destroyer and Creator both dwelled in volcanic fire, the same flame that unmade the world also remaking it, pouring new stone from the earth's heart, building new land from the old. What Kian Santang carried toward the caldera was an act of sacred return: giving back to the fire what the fire had made, allowing the cycle to complete itself. Samsara in metal -- the blade's birth from the forge, its long terrible life of corruption, and now its return to the flames. The wheel turning. The karma closing.

The keris was unbearable.

It had been growing worse with every meter of altitude gained, every step closer to its place of making. The batik wrapping that Eyang Guru had blessed -- the last gift of the fallen sage, the last protection his wisdom could provide -- was failing. The sacred patterns, drawn in indigo and turmeric and mantra, were blackening at the edges, curling inward like leaves in a fire, as the keris's power burned through them from within. The blade's whispers had become screams -- a constant, shrieking chorus of demand and threat and seduction that battered against the walls of Kian Santang's mind like waves against a weakening sea wall.

*Turn back. Turn back. You will die here. The mountain will swallow you and no one will remember your name. Turn back and I will give you everything -- kingdoms, power, peace, anything, everything, just turn back, turn back, TURN BACK --*

Kian Santang pressed his palms against his ears, knowing it would do nothing, because the keris did not speak through the air. It spoke through the blood. Through the bone. Through the deep architecture of the soul where the self is built and where, if the foundations crack, the whole structure falls.

A hand found his.

Not gripping. Not pulling. Just -- there. Warm and rough and real, calloused from years of garden work and jungle walking, scarred across the knuckles from the sharp volcanic glass of the mountain's lower slopes. Bagus's hand. Closing around his. Holding on with the quiet, implacable strength of a friend who has decided that letting go is not among the things the universe is permitted to demand of him.

"I'm here," Bagus said. That was all. Two words. The simplest sentence in any language. And against the keris's screaming, against the mountain's groaning, against the fire and the dark and the crushing, annihilating terror of what lay ahead -- those two words were enough. Not a wall. Not a weapon. Just an anchor, dropped into the chaos, holding fast.

Kian Santang's fingers tightened around his friend's hand. The screaming did not stop. The mountain did not quiet. The fire below continued its slow, indifferent work of remaking the world. But the space between the screams grew wider -- just slightly, just enough -- and in that space, Kian Santang breathed. And held on. And did not break.

Above them both -- above the mountain and the strait and the five warriors marching north and the two Tuyul huddled in their hollow of warm stone -- the stars came out.

Not all of them. The volcanic haze and the mountain's smoke plume blotted out most of the sky, turning the heavens into a murky wash of grey and orange. But here and there, in gaps between the clouds, stars appeared -- faint, persistent, burning with the ancient light of fires so distant that the concept of distance itself bent beneath their weight. And in the eastern sky, low above the horizon of the Selat Sunda where the black water met the blacker sky, a constellation emerged from the haze.

Bintang Waluku.

The plough stars. What the distant peoples of the west called Orion, what the sailors of Nusantara had used since the first outrigger canoe to navigate the waters between the islands. Three bright stars in a diagonal line -- the belt, the blade, the balance -- hanging above the strait like a sign written in fire on the vault of heaven. The karuhun -- the ancestral spirits -- were said to watch through those stars, guiding their descendants across dark waters and through darker hours.

Raden Wijaya saw them, marching north along the Jawa coast, and he paused, and he looked up, and the starlight fell upon his face like a benediction, and he thought: *The same stars. The same sky. We are still under the same sky.*

Dewi Anjani saw them, and her ancient eyes softened, because she had watched those stars since before they had names, since the first Bidadari looked upward and understood that the light of distant fires could be a path.

Cepot saw them, and for once said nothing, because some things are beyond even his considerable powers of commentary.

Dawala saw them, and whispered: "Look, Cepot. The Waluku. Grandmother always said they were the eyes of the karuhun, watching over travelers."

And on Krakatau, in their hollow of warm stone, Bagus looked up through a gap in the smoke and saw the three bright stars of the Waluku, and he squeezed Kian Santang's hand, and said: "The plough stars are out."

Kian Santang opened his eyes. He looked up. And through the pain and the noise and the terror, through the keris's screaming and the mountain's groaning and the fire's patient, consuming roar -- he saw the stars. The same stars that shone over Padalarang. The same stars that had watched over his uncle's house, and the sawah, and the bamboo groves, and the veranda where he had sat as a child listening to stories of the old world. The same stars. Unchanged. Unchangeable. Burning with the same light they had burned with since the first dawn of Nusantara, when the islands were young and the seas were clean and the first stories were told by the first fires under the first sky.

Two groups. Two paths. One sky.

The Waluku hung above the strait, connecting them all -- the warriors marching to war and the Tuyul climbing toward fire, the king who had claimed his name and the keris-bearer who would unmake the darkness, the clever cousin and the gentle warrior and the immortal archer and the mountain dwarf who knelt for no man except once -- connecting them with the oldest light in the universe, the light that had been traveling since before the Shadow King's first whisper, before the first keris was forged, before the first kingdom rose or fell.

The same stars.

The same story.

Still being told.

# Bab 25: Menuju Krakatau

## (Toward Krakatau)

---

Morning broke over Krakatau, and it was not a morning at all.

The sun -- that ancient, reliable fire that had governed the days of Nusantara since the islands first rose from the sea -- existed somewhere above the volcanic haze, but it reached the mountain's slopes only as a dim red disc, a wound in the grey sky, casting a light that was not illumination but its absence made visible. The world was the color of fever: red and grey and the dull, throbbing orange of banked coals, a palette stripped of every shade that might suggest life or growth or the green, breathing mercy of the lowland forests they had left behind.

Kian Santang stood at the edge of their hollow and looked up.

The upper slopes of Krakatau were a landscape from beyond the world of the living. Rivers of lava carved bright channels down the mountainside, their surfaces crusting black and cracking open to reveal the molten orange beneath, endlessly, like wounds that refused to heal. Between the flows, the rock was a chaos of formations -- towers of cooled basalt, fields of loose pumice that shifted underfoot like dry sand, ridges of volcanic glass so sharp that they could slice skin at a touch. Cracks in the earth vented superheated gas in hissing columns that bent the air above them into shimmering distortions, so that the landscape seemed to waver and pulse, as though the mountain itself were breathing -- which, in a sense, it was. Breathing fire. Breathing poison. Breathing the slow, patient exhalation of a volcano that had been alive since before memory and would continue long after memory had ended.

Above it all -- above the lava and the glass and the hissing vents and the broken, hellish terrain -- the caldera was visible.

The great crater at the summit of Krakatau. A vast, jagged bowl carved into the mountaintop by millennia of eruptions, its rim a broken crown of dark stone against the darker sky. From within it, a glow rose -- not the sharp, directional light of a fire but the diffuse, ambient radiance of a heat source so vast that it illuminated the air itself, turning the smoke that poured from the caldera's depths into a column of dim gold that rose and rose until it joined the greater plume above and was lost in the stratosphere.

That was their destination. That impossible, burning mouth in the top of the world. The place where the Keris Murka had been forged. The place where it could be unmade. Brahma's forge -- the sacred fire that creates and destroys, the fire that the ancient resi taught was the breath of the divine, the furnace in which the cosmos itself was shaped and reshaped through the turning of the ages.

"How far?" Kian Santang asked. His voice was a ruin. The toxic air had stripped it raw over the night, turning his words into the cracked, papery whisper of an old man. He was twenty-five years old and sounded a hundred.

Bagus studied the slope above them with the practiced eye of a Tuyul who had spent his life reading terrain -- not this terrain, not this landscape of fire and death, but the principle was the same. Elevation. Gradient. Distance. The variables were different; the mathematics were eternal.

"Half a day," Bagus said. "If the lava flows don't shift. If we find a path through the vent field." He paused. "If we keep moving."

The *if* in his voice was not uncertainty. It was recognition -- the honest, unflinching acknowledgment of a man who understood that the greatest danger on this mountain was not the lava or the gas or the treacherous footing but the possibility that his friend, his keris-bearer, his Kian Santang, might stop. Might sit down on the warm stone and close his eyes and let the keris's voice fill his head and never stand again.

Kian Santang met Bagus's eyes. Saw the fear there -- not fear of the mountain, not fear of dying, but fear of watching the person he loved most in the world be consumed by inches, devoured from the inside by a blade that fed on hope the way fire fed on wood.

"I can walk," Kian Santang said. It was a promise. It was a mantra. It was the only answer he had to offer.

They began to climb.

---

The ascent was brutal in a way that went beyond the physical, though the physical was bad enough.

The slopes were steep -- not the gentle gradient of the lower mountain, where centuries of erosion had softened the angles and thin soil supported stubborn grasses, but the raw, unweathered steepness of terrain that was still being made, still being shaped by forces that regarded human presence as an irrelevance. The footing was treacherous. Loose pumice -- grey, porous, lighter than it looked -- shifted and slid beneath their feet with every step, creating small avalanches that cascaded down the slope behind them with a dry, hissing sound that mimicked the keris's whisper. Where the pumice ended, volcanic glass began -- obsidian and its sharper, more vicious relatives, a terrain of edges and points that sliced through the wrappings on their feet and left trails of blood on the dark stone.

They had tied cloths over their faces -- strips torn from their clothing, knotted behind their heads, covering their mouths and noses against the sulfurous gas that rose from the vents. The cloths were inadequate. The air that reached their lungs was hot and thin and tasted of metal and rotten eggs, and with each breath, Kian Santang felt the burning deepen -- not the sharp pain of a wound but the slow, insidious burning of tissue being damaged incrementally, steadily, with each inhalation.

Bagus climbed ahead of him. The loyal Tuyul had taken the lead without discussion, without negotiation -- a decision made by his body rather than his mind, the instinctive positioning of a guardian between the danger and the guarded. He tested each foothold before Kian Santang stepped on it. He found the paths between the lava flows -- the narrow, cooling corridors where the rock was merely hot rather than molten. He carried both their packs, having taken Kian Santang's from his shoulders at some point during the night with the quiet, unarguable efficiency of a friend who has decided that the distribution of weight is no longer a matter for debate.

Kian Santang climbed. His body was a catalogue of failures. His wounded shoulder -- the old wound, the Leak's wound from the Ciremai pass, the cold that the Bidadari healers had driven back but never fully defeated -- ached with a phantom chill that was maddening in this volcanic heat, a pocket of ice in a furnace, as though the Leak's malice had left a seed in his flesh that the keris's proximity was now calling to bloom. His hands were cut. His feet bled. His lungs labored against the thin, poisoned air, each breath a negotiation between the body's need for oxygen and the air's reluctance to provide it.

And the keris.

The Keris Murka was no longer whispering. It was no longer screaming. It had passed beyond both into a state that Kian Santang could only describe as *presence* -- an overwhelming, all-encompassing awareness that filled his mind the way the heat filled the air, leaving no space for thought, for memory, for the small, domestic comforts of selfhood that distinguish a person from a vessel. The blade was maya made metal -- illusion given form, the grandest of all deceptions forged into seven sinuous curves of pamor steel. Every vision it had shown him, every promise, every threat -- all of it maya, the veil that the ancient resi taught obscured the true nature of reality. And now, climbing toward the fire that would burn the veil away, Kian Santang understood: the keris was not merely a weapon. It was a test. A mirror held up to the soul, showing each bearer what they most desired and what they most feared, and asking: *which will you choose?*

The blade burned through its wrapping. The pamor patterns -- those sinuous, serpentine folds of metal that the empu smiths read like scripture -- glowed through the charring batik with a light that was not firelight but something older and colder, the light of a malice so ancient that it had become elemental, woven into the blade's metallurgy the way iron is woven into blood.

He stumbled.

Bagus caught him. Of course Bagus caught him. A hand under his arm, steady and sure, lifting him back to his feet with the practiced ease of a man who had been doing this all night, all week, all his life.

He stumbled again. Bagus caught him again.

And again.

And again.

Kian Santang fell, and Bagus lifted. Kian Santang stumbled, and Bagus steadied. It was not heroism. It was not courage. It was something more fundamental than either -- the simple, irreducible stubbornness of love, which does not calculate the odds or measure the cost but merely continues, step by step, hand under arm, shoulder beneath shoulder, up the mountain and into the fire and through the dark.

"Rest," Bagus said, when they had climbed for what felt like hours but might have been less. He guided Kian Santang to a shelf of rock between two cooling lava flows and pressed a water skin to his lips. The water was warm -- everything was warm on this mountain -- but it cut through the sulfurous taste in Kian Santang's mouth and reached his stomach with a shock of relief so intense that it was almost painful.

"Rest," Bagus said again, and Kian Santang sat, and closed his eyes, and for a moment the mountain's groaning faded and the keris's presence receded to a dull, heavy pressure behind his eyes, and he was just a tired Tuyul sitting on a rock, breathing, being alive, which was enough. For the moment, it was enough.

---

The Keris Murka made its final play in the last hour before the caldera.

They had climbed past the vent field -- a nightmarish maze of hissing cracks and billowing gas that Bagus navigated with the grim, methodical patience of a man defusing a bomb, testing each step, reading the patterns of gas release, finding the safe corridors between the vents. They had crossed a field of cooling lava where the crust was thick enough to hold their weight but thin enough that they could feel the molten rock beneath it, a sensation like walking on the skin of a drum filled with fire. They had climbed a final, steep pitch of bare stone where the footholds were carved by ancient erosion into shallow, inadequate shelves barely wide enough for a Tuyul's foot.

And then Kian Santang stopped.

He did not stumble. He did not fall. He simply stopped, mid-stride, as though the string that had been pulling him upward had been cut. His eyes were open but they saw nothing on the mountain -- no stone, no fire, no Bagus standing two paces ahead with his hand extended. His eyes saw something else. Something the keris was showing him, painted on the inside of his eyelids, projected onto the screen of his consciousness with the vivid, heartbreaking clarity of a wayang performance lit by the brightest blencong that ever burned.

He saw Lembur Padalarang.

Not the Padalarang he had left -- the village that had been attacked, its houses burning, its people fleeing. Not the Padalarang of his nightmares, stained with the keris's visions of fire and ruin. This was Padalarang as it should have been. Padalarang as it was in the golden hours of his memory, burnished and perfected by the keris's art until it gleamed like a jewel in the setting of his longing.

The sawah were golden. The rice heavy on the stalk, bending in the warm wind, rippling in slow, hypnotic waves across the terraces that climbed the hillside in broad, shining steps. The water in the paddies reflected a sky so blue that it hurt to look at, and in those reflections the white clouds drifted like offerings of cotton set upon a mirror. The air smelled of melati and warm earth and the green, living fragrance of rice at harvest time, and somewhere -- close, impossibly close -- the bamboo angklung were playing, their notes cascading like water over stones, like rain on a roof, like the laughter of children who have never known fear.

He saw the lembur. The brown and gold houses with their steep thatched roofs and their carved verandas, the common yard where the children played, the banyan tree where the elders sat. He saw the cooking fires and the women's laughter and the way the evening light turned the whole village to amber. He saw the path that wound down through the sawah to the river, and the river itself, silver and clean, tasting of mineral and moss.

And he saw Uncle Kian.

Not the Uncle Kian of the final days -- the haunted, hollow-eyed old Tuyul who clutched the keris to his chest and whispered to it in the dark. This was the Uncle Kian of before. Of always. The uncle who had taught him the first forms of pencak silat in the yard behind the house, whose laugh was as rare and as precious as rain in the dry season, whose hands -- strong, brown, steady -- had held Kian Santang's when he was small and the world was large and the only darkness was the friendly darkness of a highland night full of fireflies and the sound of frogs in the sawah.

Uncle Kian stood on the veranda and smiled, and the smile was real -- not the remembered ghost of a smile but a living, breathing, *present* smile, the smile of a man who had never found a cursed blade in a cave beneath the mountains, who had never been consumed by its whispers, who had lived out his years in peace and contentment and the gentle rhythms of the harvest.

*Keep me,* the keris breathed.

The voice was not the shrieking, commanding voice of the past weeks. It was soft. It was tender. It was the voice of a mother comforting a child, the voice of a friend offering shelter from the rain. It was the most dangerous voice Kian Santang had ever heard, because it did not command. It asked.

*Keep me, and all of this can be real. I can make the world you want. Not empire. Not dominion. I know you do not want those things. I know you, Kian Santang. I know the shape of your heart. You want home. You want peace. You want the people you have lost to be alive again, sitting on the veranda in the evening light, telling stories you have heard a thousand times. Keep me, and I will make it so. I will bend the world until it becomes the world you dream of. Just... keep me.*

The pamor patterns glowed through the ruined wrapping -- not with the cold light of malice but with a warm, golden radiance that mimicked the evening light of Padalarang, the lamplight on the veranda, the blencong flame that lit the wayang screen. Inviting. Comforting. Home.

Maya. All of it maya -- the great illusion, the cosmic veil. The keris was maya's instrument, weaving dreams so beautiful that the dreamer forgot they were dreaming, forgot that the veil was not the world but its shadow, forgot that truth lay only in what was real and imperfect and present.

Kian Santang's hand moved to the keris.

His fingers closed around the hilt.

The metal was warm. Not burning -- warm. The warmth of sunbaked teak, of a hand held in yours, of rice fresh from the steamer. The warmth of everything gentle and good and lost, concentrated into a blade's width of cursed metal, offered freely, without condition, without cost.

Without cost.

He almost believed it. He stood on the slopes of Krakatau with fire below and fire above and the ruins of Padalarang's golden ghost shimmering before his eyes, and his hand on the keris, and the warmth flowing through him, and for one terrible, perfect, annihilating moment, he believed it. He believed that the keris could give him what he wanted. He believed that the price was nothing more than the keeping, the carrying, the simple act of not letting go.

He almost did not hear Bagus.

---

Bagus saw Kian Santang stop. He saw the keris-bearer's eyes go distant and soft, the way they went when the blade was showing him things, painting visions on the inside of his skull. He had seen the look before -- on the road, in the caves, on the long nights when Kian Santang sat staring at nothing while his hand crept toward the keris with the slow, involuntary movement of a man reaching for water in a desert.

But this was different. This was worse. Because the expression on Kian Santang's face was not pain. It was not fear. It was not the tortured grimace of a mind under siege. It was *peace*. It was longing. It was the face of a man who has been walking through a wasteland and has suddenly found an oasis, and who is too tired and too thirsty to ask whether the water is real.

Bagus did not know what Kian Santang saw. He could not see the golden sawah or the smiling uncle or the Padalarang that the keris had built from memory and desire. He saw only his friend, standing on a mountainside of black glass and orange fire, with his hand on a cursed blade and his face lit with a tenderness that had no place in this landscape of death.

He knew the look. He had seen it on Uncle Kian's face, in the dark house at the edge of Padalarang, in the years when the old Tuyul had sat rocking in the shadows with the keris pressed to his heart, whispering *milik-ku, milik-ku,* lost in the blade's beautiful, destroying dream.

Bagus stepped forward. He did not grab the keris. He did not grab Kian Santang. He did not shout or shake or command.

He held up his hand.

Just that. His right hand, raised to the level of Kian Santang's eyes, palm open, fingers spread. A hand. A human hand -- or a Tuyul hand, which was the same thing, near enough. It was not a beautiful hand. It was calloused from years of digging in the earth and chopping wood and foraging in the forest. It was scarred across the knuckles where the volcanic glass had cut it on the climb. It was dirty -- volcanic ash ground into the creases of the palm, dried blood beneath the fingernails, the general filth of a body that had been climbing a mountain for two days without rest or water or hope. It trembled. Not dramatically -- not the theatrical shaking of a hero in the stories -- but with the fine, involuntary vibration of a muscle pushed past its limits, a body running on fumes and faith and nothing else.

"That's not real, Kian."

Bagus's voice was quiet. Not because he intended it to be quiet -- because his throat was raw and his lungs were damaged and the best his body could manage was a hoarse, cracked whisper that should have been inaudible against the mountain's groaning and the keris's singing and the constant, infernal percussion of a volcano going about its ancient business of remaking the world.

But Kian Santang heard it. In the golden dream, in the warm embrace of the keris's vision, in the perfect, peaceful lie that fit the shape of his longing like water fits a cup -- he heard it. A crack in the illusion. A stone in the stream.

"Whatever it's showing you -- it's not real."

Bagus kept his hand up. His trembling, ruined, beautiful hand. Five fingers, spread wide, offered to the air and the fire and the friend who stood at the edge of the abyss.

"This is real." He looked at his own hand as though seeing it for the first time -- the calluses and the scars and the dirt and the blood. "I'm real. The mountain is real. What's ahead is real."

He lowered his hand. He met Kian Santang's eyes -- those river-stone eyes, brown and amber, that he had known since they were both small enough to ride in a single wheelbarrow through the sawah paths of Padalarang.

"The keris only knows how to lie."

Six words. The simplest words. Words that a gardener's son from Padalarang could speak because a gardener's son from Padalarang understood, in the marrow of his bones, the difference between a thing that grows in real soil and a thing that is painted on a screen. The keris could show you a sawah of golden rice, but it could not give you the feel of mud between your toes. It could show you a smiling uncle, but it could not give you the smell of kretek smoke on a veranda in the evening. It could show you home, but it could not give you the one thing that made home real -- the imperfect, complicated, stubborn, breathing presence of the people who lived there.

The keris only knows how to lie.

Kian Santang blinked.

The vision fractured. Not all at once -- in stages, like a wayang screen coming apart in a storm. First the edges went: the distant mountains dissolving, the sky losing its blue, the clouds fading to smoke. Then the middle: the sawah browning, the water draining, the houses losing their warmth and their color. And last -- last and hardest and most painful to watch go -- Uncle Kian. The smile holding. The eyes holding. The hand raised in greeting, or farewell, or the gesture that means *come home, come home, come home.* And then gone. All of it. Gone.

The real world crashed in -- the black stone, the orange fire, the poisoned air, the mountain's groaning, the heat, the pain, the terrible, naked truth of where they were and what they had to do.

The keris hissed. A sound of pure frustration, of millennia of manipulation meeting the one thing it could not corrupt: a friendship spoken in plain words, offered without art, without strategy, without anything except the blunt, unbreakable honesty of a heart that had never learned to lie.

Kian Santang's hand opened. He released the hilt. The keris fell back against his chest, inert, furious, denied.

He looked at Bagus. At his friend's honest, exhausted, completely loyal face. At the eyes that held no vision, no dream, no lie -- only the calm, steady, unshakeable presence of a person who was exactly where he intended to be, doing exactly what he intended to do, and would not be moved by any force in Nusantara or beyond it.

"You're right," Kian Santang said.

His voice was barely a whisper. The words cost him something -- not strength, not breath, but the last remnant of the beautiful dream, the last shred of the golden lie, released into the volcanic air like smoke from an extinguished lamp.

"Let's go."

They went.

---

The caldera's edge came suddenly.

They had been climbing through a field of fumaroles -- cracks in the earth from which jets of superheated gas burst with a sound like the hissing of a thousand serpents -- when the ground leveled, and the slope ended, and they stood at the rim of the world's mouth.

The caldera of Krakatau was a wound in the earth that made all other wounds seem like scratches. A vast, jagged bowl, perhaps a kilometer across, its walls dropping steeply to a floor that was not stone but *fire* -- a lake of magma that churned and bubbled and erupted in slow, ponderous fountains of molten rock, each fountain rising ten meters, twenty meters, before falling back in cascades of liquid orange that raised curtains of steam and gas and heat. The light from the magma was so intense that looking directly at it was like looking at the sun through closed eyelids -- a searing, penetrating brightness that found its way past every defense and painted the world in a single, overwhelming shade of incandescent orange.

The heat was a physical wall. It struck them at the rim like a blow, pressing against their faces, their chests, their outstretched hands, with a force that was not wind but pure thermal energy -- the exhaled breath of the earth's interior, rising from the magma lake in waves of superheated air that distorted everything above it into a rippling, wavering hallucination. The air shimmered. The rim shimmered. The sky itself shimmered, as though reality were a wayang screen being shaken by an unseen hand.

Somewhere in that fire, the Keris Murka had been forged. Somewhere in that churning, incandescent chaos, an empu of impossible skill and absolute malice had poured the Shadow King's will into metal and given it a shape and a name and a hunger that had endured for centuries. Somewhere in that fire, the blade could be unmade. This was moksha -- liberation -- for the keris: the release of its tortured atma from the prison of pamor steel, the dissolution of its cursed existence back into the elemental fire from which it came. And it was moksha, too, for Nusantara: the liberation of a world held captive by a single blade's malice, the breaking of chains forged in this very caldera a thousand years before.

The keris knew it.

Kian Santang felt the blade's reaction before he understood it -- a convulsion, a spasm, a surge of emotion so violent that it passed through the wrapping and the bamboo case and the cloth of his shirt and struck his chest like a fist. The keris was *pulling* -- straining toward the fire with a force that was almost physical, as though the magma were a magnet and the blade were iron. But the pulling was not desire. It was not eagerness. It was the exact opposite: it was the frantic, mindless thrashing of a creature being dragged toward the edge of a cliff, the desperate, futile resistance of a thing that knows what is coming and cannot stop it.

The keris was afraid.

For the first time -- the first time in centuries, perhaps the first time since its forging -- the Keris Murka was afraid. Its whispers had gone silent. Its visions had ceased. Its malice, that vast, patient, architectural malice that had corrupted kings and ruined kingdoms and brought an entire archipelago to the edge of darkness, had contracted to a single, primal point: the fear of ending. The fear of the fire that had given it life and would now take it back.

Kian Santang stood at the caldera's rim and felt the keris's terror, and it was, in a way he had not expected, the loneliest thing he had ever felt. Because even now -- even knowing what the blade was, even knowing the ruin it had caused, even knowing that its destruction was the only hope for the world he loved -- he could feel its fear, and it was the fear of a living thing facing its death, and some part of him, some small, stubborn, irreducibly compassionate part that the keris had never managed to corrupt, felt pity.

He did not let the pity stop him. Pity was a river, and he stood on its bank, and he let it flow past.

He turned from the caldera and looked out.

The view from the rim was the view of the world. In every direction, the earth spread out beneath them -- not as a map but as a living, breathing, heartbreakingly beautiful reality that the smoke and the fire could not quite erase. To the east, the Selat Sunda glittered in the morning haze, its waters catching the dim red sun in a thousand shifting points of light, like the sequins on a dancer's selendang. Beyond the strait, the coast of Jawa rose from the sea in a wall of green that climbed from the mangrove shores through the lowland forests to the misty highlands, and beyond those highlands, invisible but present in Kian Santang's heart like a compass bearing that no corruption could alter -- the Pasundan plateau. The land of light. The land of water and rice and stone. Lembur Padalarang, where the sawah climbed the hillsides in broad, shining terraces, and the melati grew along the river, and the angklung played at harvest time.

To the west, the coast of Sumatra -- a distant, blue-grey presence on the horizon, its mountains lost in cloud, its forests a dark smudge between sea and sky. Between the two great islands, the strait itself -- the narrow, volcanic channel that separated Jawa from Sumatra, that the first sailors had crossed in their outrigger canoes, that the kingdoms had fought over and traded across and lived beside for millennia.

The whole of Nusantara. Spread out before two small Tuyul standing at the edge of the world's fire.

---

Kian Santang looked at the fire. He looked at the keris. He looked at Bagus.

He drew a breath. The air was poison and heat and the exhalation of the earth's molten interior, and it burned his throat, and he drew it anyway, because he needed it for what came next.

He spoke.

Not to the keris. Not to the Shadow King, whose distant malice he could feel pressing against the edges of the world like a hand pressing against a door. Not to fate, that impersonal, infuriating force that had reached into a peaceful lembur and plucked out the most unlikely of champions and set him on a road of fire. He spoke to the land. To Nusantara. To the islands and the seas and the people and the rice paddies and the mountains and the rivers and the forests and the temples and the stories -- to everything that the keris would consume if he failed, everything that would be lost if the darkness won, everything that was worth walking into fire to protect.

"I am Kian Santang of Lembur Padalarang. Of the Tuyul folk of Pasundan."

His voice was small. It was cracked and hoarse and barely audible above the roaring of the caldera and the groaning of the mountain. It was the voice of a young man who was afraid and exhausted and hurt in more ways than he could count, standing on the lip of a volcano, holding a blade that wanted to eat the world.

"I am small. I am afraid."

The words hung in the heated air. They did not echo. They were swallowed by the mountain's voice almost before they were spoken, consumed by the vast indifference of geological forces that had been at work since before language, before thought, before the first living thing crawled from the first warm sea.

"But I carry a thing that must be unmade. And I will see it done."

He lifted his face to the sky -- the churning, smoke-filled sky through which no sun or star was visible -- and he spoke the words that the resi at the lingga had taught, the oldest invocation, the final prayer:

"*Sang Hyang Widhi, nu Maha Agung -- paparin ingwang kakuatan kanggo ngalaksanakeun dharma ieu. Mugi seuneu suci ieu ngabebaskeun Nusantara tina maya sareng kagelap.*"

*Sang Hyang Widhi, the Supreme -- grant me the strength to fulfill this dharma. May this sacred fire liberate Nusantara from illusion and darkness.*

The keris trembled against his chest. Not with its own will this time, but with the mountain's trembling, the deep vibration of magma moving through channels beneath the caldera floor, the tectonic pulse that was this mountain's heartbeat and would, if the stories were true, be the keris's death.

Bagus stepped forward. He stood beside Kian Santang at the caldera's rim, two small figures against the immensity of fire and stone and sky, and his face -- that round, honest, utterly dependable face -- was calm. Not the false calm of a man hiding his fear. The real calm of a man who has made his decision and is at peace with it, who has weighed the cost and accepted it, who has looked at the fire and the mountain and the blade and the friend beside him and has concluded, with the simple arithmetic of the heart, that this is where he belongs.

"And I am Bagus," he said. "Son of a gardener. And I stand with him."

Kian Santang turned to Bagus. For a moment -- one suspended, infinite moment at the rim of fire -- the formality that had never existed between them flickered into being, the weight of the occasion demanding register where friendship had always used none. "*Juragan Bagus,*" Kian Santang began, the basa lemes of respect -- but his voice broke, and the formal register shattered against the truth of what they were to each other, and what came next was pure basa loma, the intimate language of two boys from Padalarang, protocol dissolved in love: "*Nembah, priya. Andika nu panghadena.*" -- *Thank you, friend. You are the best of us.*

Simple words. Honest words. Against all the Shadow King's ancient malice, against the keris's centuries of whispered corruption, against the fire and the darkness and the terrible weight of a story that had been running for a thousand years and would not end until someone small and stubborn carried a blade into a mountain and let it burn -- two small voices, speaking truth.

They stood together at the caldera's edge. The fire roared below them. The mountain groaned beneath their feet. The keris trembled and hissed and fell, at last, into something that was almost silence -- the silence of a thing that has exhausted its arguments and knows, with the cold clarity of the condemned, that the end is near.

Above them, the smoke plume rose into the stratosphere, and somewhere above the smoke, the sky was blue, and somewhere above the sky, the stars were burning, and the Bintang Waluku hung in its eternal place, watching over travelers, as it had watched since the first dawn.

Below them, the fire waited.

And Kian Santang took Bagus's hand, and they stood at the mouth of the world, and they did not look away.
