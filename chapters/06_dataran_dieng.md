# Bab 6: Dataran Dieng
## (The Dieng Plateau)

---

He dreamed of water.

Not the gentle water of the Pasundan sawah, patient and still in its terraced mirrors, but a different water altogether — dark, mineral-heavy, warm as blood, rising from cracks in the earth with the slow insistence of something that had been waiting beneath the stone for ten thousand years. The water was golden-green and thick with sulfur, and it held him the way the earth holds a seed — completely, without light, with only the deep tectonic pulse of the world's interior beating against his skin like the heart of a vast and sleeping animal.

There was no pain. There had been pain — a cold that had entered through his left shoulder and spread inward like roots of black ice threading through his blood, reaching for his heart. But the warm water had met the cold and dissolved it, the way dawn dissolves the dark: not by force, but by patience.

Someone was chanting. The voice came from above the water, muffled and musical — a *Kinanti*, the pupuh of longing and patient endurance, its syllables placed with the precision of a jeweler setting stones. The *Kinanti* was the song of those who wait and do not break — of lovers separated by mountains, of seeds waiting beneath winter soil for the return of rain. Each verse wove through the warm water and through his body and through the cold threads of shadow in his blood, and wherever it touched, the shadow retreated, and wherever the shadow retreated, something green and living grew in its place.

He rose.

---

Light. Morning light filtered through stone, through carved openings in walls older than memory. It fell in slanted columns across a room of dark volcanic rock, illuminating carved reliefs of lotus blossoms and naga serpents along the base of the walls. Above the doorway, the stern face of *kala* gazed down — the time-devourer, guardian of thresholds between the sacred and the profane. The air was warm — a dry, mineral warmth that rose from the stone itself, as though the floor were the skin of something alive and breathing far below. The scent of *kemenyan* hung thick and sweet and sacred — the old offering-scent, the smell of temples and the space between the human world and whatever lay beyond it.

Kian Santang lay on a low teak bed carved with lotus patterns, covered in *tenun* cloth of extraordinary quality — Sundanese weaving patterned in deep indigo and gold. His left shoulder was bandaged with herbs, and beneath the cotton he felt the cool weight of a *batik* poultice, its cloth patterned with symbols he could not see but somehow felt, as though the batik itself were a prayer written in wax and dye upon his wound. Beside the bed, a small bronze *lingga-yoni* stood upon a stone pedestal — the sacred union of Siwa and Parvati, heaven and earth — and at its base, fresh offerings had been laid: frangipani petals, rice grains, and a curl of kemenyan smoke rising from a clay burner.

Somewhere beyond the stone walls, a single *saron* played — each bronze note ringing clear and sustained, as though the sound did not decay but simply chose, at its own pace, to become silence.

He turned his head. Bagus was asleep in a chair beside the bed — a chair built for human stature, so the small Tuyul sat with his feet dangling, his round face slack with exhaustion, his thick brown hands resting palms-upward on his knees, as though even in sleep he held something precious he refused to release. He had not changed his clothes. The same traveling shirt, stained with mud and sweat and darker things, hung on his broad frame.

Kian Santang looked at his friend and felt gratitude expand in his chest like warmth — the quiet, devastating knowledge that this good, loyal Tuyul had followed him into darkness and sat here for days, refusing to leave.

"He has not left your side."

The voice came from the far corner, where the kemenyan smoke was thickest. Eyang Guru Batara sat on a low stone bench, his *kretek* glowing amber between two fingers, its smoke braiding with the incense. His weathered face held an expression Kian Santang had rarely seen on it: relief.

"Three days you have slept," Eyang Guru said. "The shadow was deep. The blade that struck you was forged in Krakatau's fire and quenched in the spirit realm. It was meant to drag your soul into the shadow world where the Leak dwell. A few more hours, and not even this place could have called you back."

He exhaled smoke, and in its curling shape, for just a moment, Kian Santang thought he saw the outline of a garuda, wings spread, before it dissolved.

"But Prabu Siliwangi's arts are deeper still. He labored through the night — chanting the old *mantra* of the Tri Murti, calling upon Wisnu the Preserver to hold back the shadow, drawing the darkness out through your blood. The herbs, the sacred *tirta* water from Telaga Warna blessed by the ancient *pandita* rites, the batik poultice inscribed with the fourteen syllables of protection. You are strong, boy. The shadow reached for your heart, and your heart would not yield."

Kian Santang's throat was dry; what came out was a croak. Eyang Guru pressed a clay cup to his lips — warm *jamu* of ginger, palm sugar, and something floral — and his body, which had been clenched against a cold it no longer felt, began to loosen.

"Where — "

"Dieng. The Dataran Dieng. You are safe, Kian Santang. You are in the last sanctuary."

---

On the second day, he walked.

Bagus hovered beside him with the tender ferocity of a mother hen — steadying his elbow, producing rice porridge from nowhere, murmuring half-encouragement, half-scolding ("You are walking too fast, Kang, you only just woke up, here eat this, the Bidadari cook put extra ginger in it, very good for the blood — ").

But even Bagus fell silent when they stepped outside.

The Dataran Dieng revealed itself in layers, each more astonishing than the last. The candi from which they emerged was a small stone temple carved with ancient Hindu geometries — stepped pyramids, kala-heads above the doorways, stone figures in niches worn soft by centuries of volcanic rain but still luminous with a dignity that time could not erode. The temple was warm to the touch, its stones holding the geothermal heat. Kian Santang pressed his palm to the carved wall and felt the faintest tremor — the mountain breathing. In a niche above his hand, a stone *Ganesa* — the elephant-headed god of wisdom and remover of obstacles — sat cross-legged, his trunk curved gently to one side, his expression serene despite the centuries of rain that had softened his features. Someone had placed a fresh garland of marigolds around his neck that very morning.

Dozens of candi dotted the plateau, some tall and proud with spires intact, others reduced to foundations, but all tended — swept clean, carvings oiled, courtyards planted with frangipani and marigold. In the courtyard of the largest temple, a *homa* fire — the sacred ritual flame — burned in a stone basin, its smoke carrying offerings of rice and flowers upward to the realm of the Dewata. The plateau stretched before him, a high volcanic basin at two thousand meters, the air cold and thin and electric with something beyond altitude — a *presence*, the concentrated *bayu* of a *kabuyutan*, a place the ancestors had marked as sacred beyond the reach of mortal dispute. Mist moved through the temples in slow procession, curling around spires, pooling in courtyards, drifting between pillars in shapes almost recognizable — a hand, a face, the trailing edge of a garment — before dissolving.

And the lakes. To the west lay Telaga Warna, and Kian Santang understood why the old manuscripts had labored so inadequately to describe it. The surface shifted color — not gradually, but in sudden transformations, as though the lake were a living thing cycling through moods. Blue-green to gold. Gold to turquoise. Turquoise to a deep, impossible violet. The colors were sulfur deposits from volcanic vents below — but standing there, watching it shimmer, Kian Santang did not believe in sulfur. He believed in something that had chosen to be beautiful because beauty, in this place, was a form of prayer.

Hot springs bubbled from dark stone. Fumaroles hissed at the edges, their yellow-crusted mouths breathing the hot mineral breath of the depths. The ground was warm underfoot, the earth reminding you with every step that you walked upon a living thing.

"It is a place between worlds," Eyang Guru told him. "The volcanic fire connects to the realm below — the *Bhurloka*, the underworld of spirits, ancestors, the forces that shaped the world before the first kingdom. The cold mountain air touches the realm above — the *Swarloka*, the divine, Sang Hyang Widhi, the Bidadari who walk in light. And between them, the *Bhuvaloka* — the middle world where mortals dwell. Dieng sits where these three realms of the *Triloka* overlap. That is why the ancients built here. That is why the wise ones return when the world grows dark." He paused, drawing on his kretek, and added more quietly: "I spent many years in Bali, among the *balian* priests of the mountain temples. They taught me that certain places are *tenget* — charged with spiritual power so great that the boundaries between worlds grow thin. Dieng is the most tenget place remaining in all Nusantara."

Scholars pored over lontar manuscripts in the candi libraries. Warriors practiced pencak silat in stone courtyards, breath visible in the cold air, bare feet warm on heated stone. Healers tended medicinal gardens in the volcanic soil — turmeric with leaves broad as banana fronds, ginger roots thick as forearms.

But they were not the most remarkable inhabitants of the plateau.

---

He saw the Bidadari for the first time on the third morning.

He was walking the ridge above Telaga Warna — alone, Bagus having been lured to the kitchens by a Bidadari cook who could do things with *jamu* and *kunyit* that defied mortal understanding — when the mist parted, and she was there.

She stood at the ridge's edge, looking over the lake, and the first thing he noticed was the light. It came from her skin — a faint golden luminescence, as though she were lit from within by a flame that produced no heat and cast no shadow. Her skin was the brown of polished teak, a deep warmth that the inner light turned to bronze, and it seemed to Kian Santang that she was not standing *in* the light but that the light was standing in *her*.

She was tall, and she held herself with a stillness that was not the absence of movement but its perfection — the way a balanced keris rests on its stand, complete and requiring nothing. Her black hair fell past her waist, the mist touching it with silver. Her eyes — when she turned — were the color of Telaga Warna: shifting, deep, and old. Impossibly, terrifyingly old. Eyes that had watched centuries pass the way he watched seasons.

She wore *boeh siang* — daylight cloth — so fine it moved in windless air, dyed in colors that shifted — indigo to violet, violet to rose-gold. The silk was embroidered with the *jayanti* victory pattern, motifs that might have been script or constellations. And beneath the silk, Kian Santang caught the faintest echo of something else in her bearing — the disciplined grace of the *legong* sacral movements, the sacred temple dances of Bali, each gesture a prayer encoded in the body's memory. She carried the knowledge of both Sundanese highlands and Balinese temple traditions, two streams of ancient wisdom flowing together in her veins.

"You are the one who carries it," she said.

Her voice was a *Dangdanggula* — the noblest of the pupuh forms, the song of grandeur and beauty, each syllable a note soaring upward like the smoke of sacred incense. The sound moved through Kian Santang's chest and left him feeling both profoundly seen and profoundly small.

"I — yes," he said, and his voice sounded like a frog with pretensions.

She almost smiled. The expression transformed her ancient seriousness into something unexpectedly warm, and he saw that she was not only luminous and terrifying but also, in some way he could not articulate, kind.

'*Rahayu, nu pinercaya ngagaduhan keris*,' she said — the formal Sundanese greeting spoken in the highest register, the *basa luhur* reserved for matters of sacred weight. (*Peace be upon the one entrusted with the keris.*) "I am Dewi Anjani. And you are smaller than I expected."

She turned back to the lake, and the mist closed around her, and Kian Santang stood with his mouth half open and the distinct impression that he had been weighed on scales he could not see and found — perhaps — not entirely wanting.

He would learn that Dewi Anjani was a warrior-princess of the Bidadari, one of the last celestial fighters. The bow she carried — enchanted bamboo carved with script that glowed in moonlight — had sent arrows into the hearts of creatures he preferred not to imagine. She was fierce and proud and ancient, and she regarded the Keris Murka with a wary respect that told him more about the blade's power than any of Eyang Guru's warnings. '*Kami émut kana carita Jaka Tarub,*' she had said once to Eyang Guru, her voice carrying the weight of the oldest memory — '*tukang moro anu nyokot selendang Bidadari sarta ngurung siya di dunya manusa. Kami henteu poho ka nu maok jangjang kami.*' (*We remember the tale of Jaka Tarub — the hunter who stole a Bidadari's selendang and trapped her in the mortal world. We do not forget those who steal our wings.*)

The tale was a warning — not against Kian Santang, but against the keris. For the Keris Murka, like Jaka Tarub, sought to bind what should fly free, to trap the celestial in chains of mortal will. Dewi Anjani knew this in her bones, in the ancient memory of her people, and her vigilance was the vigilance of one who had seen such binding before.

But that first encounter — the mist parting, the light, the eyes that held centuries — stayed with him like a song heard once in a dream.

---

That afternoon, Eyang Guru said, "There is someone who has been asking for you."

He led Kian Santang to a small temple half-hidden by wild frangipani, its white blossoms trembling in cold air, their sweetness so dense it was almost visible. Inside, a figure sat at a low desk, hunched over *lontar* manuscripts, scratching with a metal stylus in the careful hand of a practiced scribe.

Uncle Kian looked up, and his face cracked into a smile so wholly itself — so free of the shadows and obsessive guardedness that had clouded him for decades — that Kian Santang barely recognized him.

And then he did, and the recognition broke something open in his chest.

The old Tuyul had aged rapidly, as though the years the Keris Murka had held at bay were collecting their debt. His hair had thinned to wisps. His skin had paled to something papery, veins showing blue like rivers seen from a great height. He was smaller than Kian Santang remembered — or perhaps the keris had given him a presence that inflated him beyond his true dimensions, the way a flame enlarges the shadow of the hand that holds it.

But his eyes were clear. Clear and warm and present in a way Kian Santang had not seen since childhood, since before the wrapped thing in batik cloth had drawn a veil across his uncle's gaze. Freed from the keris, they were the eyes of a man who had set down a weight so heavy that release was itself a kind of joy.

"Kian," the old Tuyul said, and opened his arms, and Kian Santang crossed the room and knelt and was held.

The oil lamp flickered. Frangipani scent drifted through the doorway. Outside, the soft bronze voice of a saron wound through the cold air.

"I am sorry," Uncle Kian said. "I kept it too long. I told myself I was protecting it, but the truth is it was keeping me. I could feel it pulling at my will like a current beneath still water. And I passed it to you, and the passing was the most selfish thing I have ever done, and the most necessary."

Kian Santang looked at that dear, diminished face and said, '*Mulah dianggo beurat dosa eta, Eyang*' (*Do not carry that guilt, Eyang*). "You gave me truth when you could have given silence."

He did not entirely believe this. But Uncle Kian heard it, and his eyes filled and spilled, and he laughed — a small, wet, startled laugh, as though joy were a language he was relearning.

"Come," Uncle Kian said, turning to his desk. "Let me show you."

The lontar leaves were spread in careful rows, inscribed with old Sundanese script. Uncle Kian touched them with the same tenderness he had once directed at the keris — but transformed, redirected from power to memory, and the difference was the difference between hunger and love.

"I am writing it all down. The caves of Gunung Sewu. The underground river, black as ink. The chamber where the blade lay waiting. *Catatan Perjalanan Seorang Tuyul* — Travel Notes of a Tuyul." He said the title with shy pride, then paused, his hand on the last half-inscribed leaf. "I will not finish it, Kian. My hand grows tired. But the story goes on — through you. When you come back, you will take this manuscript and finish it. Your chapters and mine, bound together."

Kian Santang took his uncle's hand — light as a bird's wing, warm as geothermal stone — and promised.

---

The audience with Prabu Siliwangi took place that evening, in the largest candi — transformed into a hall of council, its walls lined with carved reliefs of the Bharatayudha, Hanoman leaping across the sea, and scenes uniquely Nusantara — the forging of the first keris, the descent of the Bidadari, the binding of the Buta. Oil lamps burned in niches. Geothermal warmth rose through the floor. At the four cardinal points of the hall, stone *lingga-yoni* marked the sacred boundaries — Siwa's presence anchoring the space, transforming a chamber of stone into a threshold between the mortal and the divine.

Prabu Siliwangi sat on a *singgasana* carved from volcanic stone, softened only by a folded batik cloth of the *semen* pattern — shoots and tendrils, growth and continuity. He was tall, his black hair silver-streaked, his beard close-trimmed. He wore white — plain fine cotton — with a royal *selendang* of indigo batik draped over one shoulder, the *parang* pattern of diagonal blades.

His eyes arrested Kian Santang. Dark, nearly black, they held warmth and sadness in perfect balance — the warmth immediate and generous, the sadness older and deeper, set behind like a mountain behind a garden. The sadness of a man who had foreseen the darkness, left his kingdom, traded his throne for a sanctuary. He was warrior, healer, scholar, and king without a throne — and the absence had not diminished his kingship but distilled it, the way fire distills ore into metal.

'*Rahayu, Kian Santang ti Padalarang,*' he said — the Sundanese greeting spoken in the measured cadence of *basa luhur*, the high register of the royal court. (*Peace be upon you, Kian Santang of Padalarang.*) "You have been wounded by the darkest weapon of the enemy, and you have survived. Warriors twice your size and ten times your age would not have withstood it. Be at ease."

Kian Santang bowed, hands pressed together in the *sembah* gesture — palms joined before his face, the ancient mark of reverence that predated every kingdom. '*Nembah, Kangjeng Prabu*,' he said. (*I pay reverence, Your Majesty.*)

"I did not summon you to receive thanks." The sadness moved forward in his eyes. "The keris you carry is no longer a secret. The Leak that pursued you were only the first. There will be others, and worse."

He rose and descended the steps with the fluid precision of a pencak silat master — a man whose body still remembered the warrior's *dharma*, even as his spirit had embraced the scholar's.

"I have sent word across Nusantara — to Mataram, Sriwijaya, the Kurcaci of Gunung Sewu, the Bidadari sanctuaries. A council will be held in three days. The fate of the Keris Murka — and all Nusantara — will be decided."

He placed a hand on Kian Santang's uninjured shoulder.

"You have carried it far, young one. Farther than anyone had the right to ask. But the road ahead is not yours alone to walk. The burden is yours, but the decision belongs to us all."

---

Evening fell over Dieng the way it falls over sacred places — slowly, reverently, as though the light were reluctant to leave.

Kian Santang came, by no path he had planned, to the shore of Telaga Warna. The lake at sunset had turned to liquid gold — a literal, luminous amber, as though the sulfur minerals had caught the last rays and held them, amplified them, offered them back to the sky.

At the lake's edge, two figures stood.

Raden Wijaya — the ranger who had saved them on Gunung Ciremai — his weathered face softened in the golden light, the lines of vigilance eased into something younger, almost vulnerable. Without armor or weapons, in only a simple shirt and sarong, he looked like what he was beneath: a man, mortal and uncertain, standing at the edge of a beautiful thing.

Beside him, Dyah Pitaloka. Kian Santang knew her before he knew her name.

She was Prabu Siliwangi's daughter, with her father's eyes — warmth and sorrow — but in her the warmth was fiercer, the sorrow not resignation but resolve. Her long black hair hung loose, the golden light catching in it like a river of dark water shot through with gold. Her beauty was not the luminous otherworldliness of the Bidadari, but human, grounded — dark intelligent eyes, a mouth that had learned laughter and gravity, skin the warm brown of the highland earth.

She was reaching for him. Her hand extended toward his, which hung at his side, close but not touching. Those few inches of golden air held everything unspoken — his exile and her birthright, his wandering and her belonging, the impossible distance between a ranger without a kingdom and a princess of Pasundan. He did not take her hand. She did not withdraw. She waited with a patience that was not submission but strength, her eyes lit with a love so steady that Kian Santang, watching from a candi doorway, felt he was intruding on something sacred.

The lake shimmered. A *jalak suren* called once from the frangipani trees and fell silent.

Raden Wijaya turned his head. He looked at her hand. He did not take it. But his fingers moved — a fraction, a breath — toward hers. The space between them narrowed, and held, and the golden light held them both.

Kian Santang turned away with a tightness in his throat. The coming darkness would not merely threaten kingdoms and keris-bearers. It would threaten this — the small, fragile space between two hands reaching for each other. The private tenderness that no war considers and no victory restores. The cost of the shadow was not only the grand and epic. It was the narrowing of the space in which love could exist.

He felt the weight of every life the keris touched. And it was heavier than the blade itself.

---

Night.

The mist descended fully, the candi floating in vapor like ruins of a dream. Kian Santang stood on the temple steps, a borrowed *selendang* around his shoulders, and looked into the darkness.

He could feel the earth. Beneath his bare feet, through heated stone, the deep volcanic pulse beat on — slow, immense, indifferent. Krakatau was part of that fire. The caldera where the Keris Murka was forged. The heat warming his feet was the same heat that had powered the forges of Raja Iblis. Fire was fire. It could heal or destroy, warm a sanctuary or forge a weapon. The choice lay not in the fire but in the hands that directed it.

Somewhere far to the west, across the Selat Sunda, Krakatau smoked in the night. The Keris Murka lay in his room, wrapped in old batik, silent and hungry, and even through the stone walls, he felt its pull — a thin cold thread connecting blade to caldera, taut as a gamelan string, vibrating at a frequency too low for ears but not for blood.

Three days until the council.

Three days of peace.

He breathed cold mountain air — sulfur and kemenyan and frangipani and stone — and closed his eyes, and prayed. Not with words. With the wordless prayer of a body standing on warm stone in the dark, asking the silence for the one thing silence cannot give: certainty.

*Sang Hyang Widhi,* he prayed, *Demi Dewata Nawa Sanga — the nine gods of the cardinal directions — I am small. I am afraid. I do not know the road, and the road does not know me. But I am here. I carry what I carry. Show me what to do when the time comes. Show me, and I will not look away.*

The mist curled.

The fumaroles breathed.

The earth pulsed on, slow and deep, beneath his feet.

And somewhere in the candi behind him, wrapped in old cloth in a dark room, the Keris Murka waited — patient as hunger, old as fire — dreaming its dark dreams of the mountain where it was made.
