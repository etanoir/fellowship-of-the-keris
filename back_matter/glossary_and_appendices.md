# AUTHOR'S NOTE

Dear reader,

The book you hold in your hands is a work of fantasy. It is also a work of love.

*Persekutuan Keris* was born from two deep and abiding passions: a lifelong admiration for the mythic storytelling of J.R.R. Tolkien, whose *The Lord of the Rings: The Fellowship of the Ring* demonstrated that fantasy literature could achieve the depth, beauty, and moral seriousness of the greatest works in any genre; and an equally deep love for the cultural heritage of Nusantara -- the Indonesian archipelago -- whose history, mythology, arts, and peoples constitute one of the richest and most astonishing tapestries of human civilization on earth.

This novel is, at its heart, an adaptation. Readers familiar with Tolkien's masterwork will recognize the bones of the story: a small, unassuming person from a peaceful homeland is entrusted with a powerful and corrupting artifact, and must carry it, with a company of diverse companions, across a dangerous landscape to destroy it at the place of its making. That structure is Tolkien's gift to the world, and I have borrowed it with gratitude and humility.

But the flesh upon those bones -- the skin and breath and beating heart -- is Nusantara's.

The kingdoms that appear in this novel are drawn from real Indonesian history. Pasundan, Mataram, Majapahit, Sriwijaya, Kediri, Kutai, Kalingga, Tarumanegara -- these were actual kingdoms and empires that rose and flourished across the Indonesian archipelago over more than a thousand years, from the early centuries of the Common Era through the colonial period. Their histories are extraordinary, and they deserve to be far better known outside Indonesia than they are.

However, I must be clear: **their portrayal in this novel is fictional and fantastical**. I have taken enormous liberties with history, geography, and chronology. Kingdoms that existed centuries apart coexist here side by side. Historical figures have been reimagined as fantasy characters. Real places have been imbued with magical properties they do not, to the best of my knowledge, actually possess -- though anyone who has stood at the rim of Ngarai Sianok at dawn or watched the mist roll across the Dieng Plateau may reasonably disagree.

This deliberate anachronism is not carelessness; it is a feature of the fantasy genre, which allows us to compress, rearrange, and reimagine history in service of mythic truth. Tolkien himself drew from Anglo-Saxon, Norse, Celtic, and Finnish traditions spanning many centuries and blended them into a single coherent mythology. I have attempted to do the same with the traditions of Nusantara, not because Indonesian culture needs to be filtered through a Western framework to be valid -- it emphatically does not -- but because the framework of epic fantasy provides a vessel capacious enough to hold, and to celebrate, the sheer scale of what Nusantara has produced.

A word about antagonists. In this novel, certain kingdoms and peoples serve as agents or allies of the Shadow King. I want to state plainly that no disrespect is intended to any cultural or ethnic group in Indonesia. The antagonist kingdoms and peoples are treated, I hope, with complexity and dignity. Villainy in this story is not a property of any culture but a consequence of choice -- of the decision to pursue domination over cooperation, fear over fellowship. The Buta are monstrous, yes, but they are monsters of metaphor, not of ethnicity. Every people in Nusantara, in this novel as in life, is capable of both darkness and light.

The mythological creatures that populate these pages -- the Tuyul, the Bidadari, the Kurcaci, the Leak, the Buta, the Genderuwo, the Naga -- are drawn from Indonesian folklore and mythology. I have adapted them with creative license, reshaping them to serve the needs of this particular story while attempting to honor their cultural origins. The Tuyul of this novel are not the mischievous coin-stealing spirits of Javanese ghost stories; they are a people, small and earthy and good, reimagined in the tradition of Tolkien's hobbits. The Bidadari are not merely the celestial nymphs of classical Javanese dance-drama; they are an ancient, luminous people whose beauty carries the weight of deep time. In every case, the adaptation is meant as tribute, not appropriation -- an acknowledgment that Indonesian mythology contains figures and archetypes every bit as powerful and resonant as those of any tradition on earth.

I am aware that I write as one person, from one perspective, about a culture of staggering diversity -- more than 17,000 islands, more than 700 living languages, hundreds of distinct ethnic groups, each with its own traditions, art forms, and ways of understanding the world. I cannot claim to represent all of Nusantara. No single book could. What I can do is open a door and invite readers to step through it, into a world that is at once fantastical and grounded in something real and living and magnificent.

This novel celebrates the incredible diversity and richness of Indonesian culture. It celebrates the *gotong royong* -- the communal spirit of mutual aid -- that has sustained the peoples of the archipelago for millennia. It celebrates the artistry of the *empu* keris-smith, the music of the gamelan and the angklung, the grace of pencak silat, the wisdom encoded in wayang kulit, the beauty of batik, the warmth of shared rice eaten from banana leaves on a tikar mat. It celebrates the volcanoes and the rice terraces and the coral seas and the rainforests and the canyon walls and the people -- above all, the people -- who have built something remarkable upon these scattered islands at the edge of the world.

This book is dedicated to the Sundanese people -- *urang Sunda* -- whose highlands inspired the Tuyul homeland of this story, whose warmth and humor and deep connection to the land gave Kian Santang and Bagus and Cepot and Dawala their hearts. And it is dedicated, with equal love and gratitude, to all the peoples of Nusantara -- Javanese, Balinese, Minangkabau, Malay, Bugis, Dayak, Batak, Papuan, Acehnese, Madurese, Sasak, and the hundreds of others whose names could fill a book of their own. Your cultures are a treasure beyond reckoning. This novel is my small, imperfect attempt to say so.

*Nembah. Terima kasih.*

With deepest respect,
The Author

# MAP OF NUSANTARA

## A Description of the Fantasy Map Appearing at the Front of This Volume

---

The map is rendered in the style of an ancient *lontar* palm-leaf manuscript, its edges darkened with age, its lines drawn in sepia ink with fine cross-hatching for mountain ranges and stippled shading for ocean depths. The cartouche in the upper right corner reads, in ornate Javanese script with a Roman transliteration below: **PETA NUSANTARA -- Tanah-Tanah Persekutuan Keris** (Map of Nusantara -- The Lands of the Fellowship of the Keris). A compass rose in the lower left, styled as a Majapahit *surya* sun-disc, marks the cardinal directions.

### The Archipelago

The map depicts the Indonesian archipelago from the northern tip of Sumatra to the eastern reaches of Papua, with the full breadth of Nusantara's island chains visible. The islands are shown in warm ochre and green, surrounded by seas rendered in deepening shades of blue-grey, with stylized wave patterns in the deeper waters. The equator is marked as a fine dashed line crossing northern Sumatra. Sea routes are drawn in dotted blue ink, with tiny illustrations of *pinisi* sailing ships and *jukung* outrigger canoes along the major lanes.

### The Island of Jawa (Java) -- Center of the Map

Java dominates the lower center of the map, oriented roughly east-west, its volcanic spine rendered as a chain of peaked mountains with wispy ink-trails representing smoke and cloud.

**Western Java -- The Pasundan Highlands (Tuyul Homeland)**

The western third of Java is labeled **TANAH PASUNDAN** in large script. The Pasundan Highlands are depicted as a lush, elevated plateau of terraced rice fields and forested volcanic slopes, colored in rich greens. Key locations marked:

- **Kampung Padalarang** -- A small cluster of *rumah panggung* houses nestled in the highland terraces south of a wide valley. Marked with a tiny illustration of a bamboo gate. This is Kian Santang's home village. (Based on the real town of Padalarang near Bandung, West Java.)

- **Gunung Tangkuban Perahu** -- A volcano with a distinctively flattened, inverted-boat profile at the northern edge of the Pasundan Highlands. Marked with the annotation: *"The Overturned Boat."* (Real volcano north of Bandung.)

- **Gunung Ciremai** -- A tall, symmetrical volcanic cone on the eastern edge of Pasundan territory, drawn with a crumbled watchtower at its summit. Labeled: *Menara Pengawas Ciremai* (Ciremai Watchtower) -- a ruined sentinel post. (Real volcano, the highest peak in West Java.)

- **Anyer** -- A harbor town on the far northwestern coast of Java, on the shore of the Sunda Strait. Drawn with a small pier, several *pinisi* ships at anchor, and a lighthouse. Labeled: *Pelabuhan Terakhir* (The Last Harbor). This is the final port before the crossing to Krakatau. (Real coastal town in Banten province.)

**Central Java -- The Volcanic Heartland**

The central portion of Java is labeled **TANAH MATARAM** and is dominated by volcanic peaks and the ancient temple complexes.

- **Dataran Dieng / Dieng Plateau** -- A high, misty plateau depicted with bubbling fumaroles, sulfurous lakes (including Telaga Warna, drawn in shifting colors), and a complex of ancient *candi* temples. Labeled: *Sanggar Kebijaksanaan* (Sanctuary of Wisdom). The Bidadari temple complex is shown as a cluster of ornate spired structures amid the mist. (Real plateau and temple complex in Central Java, elevation ~2,000m.)

- **Gunung Merapi** -- A towering, actively smoking volcano southeast of Dieng, its peak wreathed in dark cloud and drawn with an ominous red glow. At its summit, a fortress of dark stone is depicted. Labeled: *Benteng Empu Gandring* (Empu Gandring's Fortress). (Real active volcano near Yogyakarta.)

- **Ratu Boko** -- Ancient stone ruins on an elevated plateau south of the temples of Prambanan, drawn as crumbled walls, gateways, and ceremonial pools. Labeled: *Reruntuhan Ratu Boko -- Medan Pertempuran* (Ratu Boko Ruins -- The Battlefield). (Real archaeological site near Yogyakarta.)

- **Rawa Pening** -- A broad marshland depicted in murky greens and browns, with dead trees rising from standing water and a faint mist hanging over the surface. Labeled: *Rawa Berhantu* (The Haunted Marsh). Small figures of spectral shapes are hinted at in the mist. (Real lake/marshland in Central Java.)

- **Candi Barabudur** -- The great Buddhist temple is shown in profile, its nine-tiered pyramid rising from the Kedu Plain, tiny stupas crowning its terraces. Marked as an ancient landmark. (Real UNESCO World Heritage site.)

**Eastern Java -- Kingdoms of Kediri and Majapahit**

The eastern portion of Java shows two great kingdoms:

- **Kediri** -- A kingdom depicted with rolling grasslands, horse paddocks, and a walled city. Labeled: *Kerajaan Kediri -- Tanah Pasukan Berkuda* (Kingdom of Kediri -- Land of the Horse-Lords). Horsemen are illustrated galloping across open fields. (Real historical kingdom in East Java, 11th-13th century.)

- **Majapahit** -- A grand walled capital city shown with towered gates, a vast *pendopo* throne hall, and military barracks. Labeled: *Kekaisaran Majapahit* (The Majapahit Empire). The Majapahit *surya* sun emblem is drawn above the city. (Real historical empire, 13th-16th century, based near modern Mojokerto.)

**Southern Java -- The Coast and the Caves**

The southern coast of Java is drawn as a dramatic line of sea cliffs, black sand beaches, and crashing Indian Ocean waves.

- **Gunung Sewu** -- An extensive karst landscape of thousands of conical limestone hills, depicted with cave entrances marked by Kurcaci forge-symbols. Labeled: *Gua Sewu -- Kerajaan Bawah Tanah Kurcaci* (The Caves of a Thousand -- Underground Realm of the Kurcaci). The underground realm is shown in a cutaway illustration beneath the hills, revealing tunnels, forge-chambers, the great stone bridge (*Jembatan Batu*), and, in the deepest level, the lair of the Naga Basukih, drawn as a coiled serpent of fire. (Real karst landscape in southern Central/East Java.)

### The Sunda Strait and Krakatau

Between Java and Sumatra, the **Selat Sunda** (Sunda Strait) is drawn as a narrow, turbulent channel of dark water, with strong current lines and whirlpool symbols.

- **Krakatau** -- The volcanic island is depicted at the center of the Strait, far larger and more menacing than its real-world counterpart. It is drawn as a massive volcanic cone rising from black water, its caldera glowing with molten red light, dark smoke billowing from the summit into a sky stained crimson and black. At its peak, a throne of obsidian is faintly visible. Labeled: *Krakatau -- Singgasana Raja Iblis* (Krakatau -- Throne of the Shadow King). The Mordor of Nusantara. A ring of dead coral and blackened water surrounds the island. (Based on the real Krakatau volcano in the Sunda Strait.)

### The Island of Sumatra -- Western Edge of the Map

Sumatra occupies the left portion of the map, oriented northwest to southeast, its great mountain spine prominent.

- **Bukit Barisan** -- The vast mountain range running the length of Sumatra, drawn as a continuous wall of peaks with dense tropical forest on both flanks. Labeled: *Bukit Barisan -- Punggung Dunia* (Bukit Barisan -- The Spine of the World). (Real mountain range, ~1,700km long.)

- **Ngarai Sianok** -- A deep canyon cut into the Bukit Barisan near the highland town of Bukittinggi. Drawn with sheer, layered cliff walls, waterfalls cascading from multiple levels, and a mysterious mist filling the gorge floor. Labeled: *Ngarai Sianok -- Lembah Terlarang Nyai Roro Kidul* (Sianok Canyon -- The Enchanted Valley of the Queen of the Southern Sea). (Real canyon in West Sumatra.)

- **Sriwijaya** -- A great maritime kingdom depicted along the southeastern coast of Sumatra, centered on the Musi River delta. Drawn with a harbor filled with ocean-going vessels, a river palace, and a lighthouse. Labeled: *Kekaisaran Maritim Sriwijaya* (The Maritime Empire of Sriwijaya). (Real historical empire, 7th-13th century, based near modern Palembang.)

### Kalimantan (Borneo) -- The Great Island of the North

The massive island of Kalimantan fills the upper right quadrant of the map, its interior rendered in deep greens representing the ancient rainforests, with river systems drawn as sinuous blue lines threading through the jungle canopy.

- **Kutai** -- The ancient kingdom is marked on the eastern coast along the Mahakam River, depicted with *yupa* stone pillars and a riverside settlement. Labeled: *Kerajaan Kutai -- Tanah Dayak* (Kingdom of Kutai -- Land of the Dayak). Dayak longhouses are illustrated along the river banks, and the surrounding forest is marked with symbols for ironwood and camphor trees. (Real historical kingdom, one of the oldest in Indonesian history, 4th century CE onward.)

- **Dayak Territories** -- The interior of Kalimantan is labeled *Tanah Dayak* in broad script, with illustrations of river-going craft, longhouses elevated on massive ironwood pillars, and warriors bearing mandau swords and kliau shields decorated with hornbill motifs. The headwaters of the great rivers -- Mahakam, Barito, Kapuas -- are marked as sources of gold, diamonds, and sacred forest medicines.

### Sulawesi -- The Seafaring Lands

To the east of Kalimantan, the distinctive orchid-shaped island of Sulawesi is depicted with its four long peninsulas extending into the surrounding seas.

- **Bugis Lands** -- The southwestern peninsula is labeled *Tanah Bugis -- Pelaut Agung* (Land of the Bugis -- The Great Seafarers). The coastline is dotted with shipyards where *pinisi* vessels are shown under construction, their distinctive two-masted profiles rising from the beach. Sea routes radiate outward from the Bugis ports to every corner of the archipelago. (The Bugis and Makassar peoples of South Sulawesi are among the greatest seafarers in history.)

- **Tana Toraja** -- The mountainous interior of the southwestern peninsula is labeled *Tana Toraja -- Tanah Leluhur* (Toraja Land -- Land of the Ancestors), depicted with the distinctive *tongkonan* houses with their soaring saddleback roofs, carved with geometric patterns. Cliff-face burial sites are illustrated in the limestone walls. (Real highland region known for its elaborate funeral traditions and ancestor veneration.)

### Bali -- Island of the Gods

Southeast of Java, the small but richly detailed island of Bali is depicted with terraced rice fields cascading down volcanic slopes, temple complexes crowned with layered *meru* towers, and a central volcanic peak.

- **Pura Besakih** -- The great mother temple is shown on the slopes of Gunung Agung, drawn as a vast complex of courtyards, split gates (*candi bentar*), and multi-tiered pagoda towers. Labeled: *Pura Besakih -- Pura Agung Nusantara* (Besakih Temple -- The Great Temple of Nusantara). This is the spiritual center from which the Bidadari draw cultural connection, and the place where Eyang Guru Batara studied the ancient rites in his youth. (Real temple complex, the holiest in Balinese Hinduism.)

- **Bidadari Cultural Sites** -- Smaller temples and sacred springs are marked across Bali, connected by dotted paths to the Dieng sanctuary on Java, suggesting the ancient spiritual network that links the Bidadari sanctuaries across the archipelago. Sacred dance platforms for *legong* and *barong* performances are illustrated near the temple compounds.

### Maluku -- The Spice Islands

Far to the east, the scattered islands of Maluku are depicted as a chain of small, lush volcanic islands surrounded by deep blue seas.

- **Kepulauan Rempah** -- The islands are labeled *Kepulauan Rempah -- Pulau Rempah-Rempah* (The Spice Islands). Tiny illustrations show clove trees, nutmeg groves, and trading vessels loading precious cargo. Labeled with a notation: *"Source of the world's most precious spices -- clove, nutmeg, mace -- traded across the seas of Nusantara since time beyond reckoning."* Eastern trade routes are drawn connecting Maluku to Sulawesi, Java, and the wider archipelago.

### Papua -- The Far Eastern Lands

At the far eastern edge of the map, the western portion of the great island of Papua is depicted, its interior marked with dense mountain ranges and unbroken rainforest.

- **Tanah Papua** -- Labeled *Tanah Papua -- Ujung Timur Nusantara* (Land of Papua -- The Eastern Edge of Nusantara). The coastline shows villages of stilt houses over water, and the interior mountains are marked with warriors wearing elaborate bird-of-paradise plumes and *koteka*, carrying bows of palm wood. Illustrated with birds-of-paradise in flight above the forest canopy. A notation reads: *"The far eastern territories, home to peoples whose traditions reach back to the oldest human settlements in the archipelago."*

### Seas and Waterways

- **Laut Jawa** (Java Sea) -- The broad sea between Java's northern coast and Kalimantan, rendered in medium blue with trade-route dotted lines and *pinisi* ship illustrations.
- **Samudra Hindia** (Indian Ocean) -- The deep southern ocean, rendered in dark blue-grey with large wave patterns, labeled along Java's southern coast.
- **Selat Sunda** (Sunda Strait) -- The narrow passage between Java and Sumatra, heavily marked with current and danger symbols.
- **Laut Banda** (Banda Sea) -- The deep eastern sea between Sulawesi and Maluku, rendered in dark blue, with spice-trade route lines connecting the islands.
- **Selat Makassar** (Makassar Strait) -- The wide strait between Kalimantan and Sulawesi, marked with Bugis trade routes.

### The Journey Route

A **dotted red line** traces the path of the Pasukan Sembilan across the map:

1. **Kampung Padalarang** (departure) -- north through the Pasundan Highlands
2. East across the highlands, past **Gunung Ciremai** (watchtower encounter)
3. East to the **Dieng Plateau** (the Great Council)
4. South from Dieng through the volcanic highlands
5. Through **Rawa Pening** (the haunted marshlands)
6. Past **Ratu Boko** (the battlefield)
7. South into **Gunung Sewu** (the underground passage)
8. Emergence on the southern coast of Java
9. West along the coast to **Ngarai Sianok** (crossing to Sumatra via enchanted paths)
10. West and south toward **Anyer** (the last harbor)
11. The dotted line extends across the **Sunda Strait** toward **Krakatau**, ending in a question mark wreathed in flame -- *the journey continues*.

A small legend at the bottom of the map reads:

> *"Sembilan sahabat. Satu tujuan. Satu keris yang harus dimusnahkan."*
> (Nine companions. One purpose. One keris that must be destroyed.)

# CHARACTER LIST

## The Pasukan Sembilan (The Nine Companions / The Fellowship of the Keris)

---

### 1. Prabu Kian Santang
**Bearer of the Keris Murka**

A young Tuyul nobleman of Kampung Padalarang in the Pasundan Highlands. Twenty-five years old -- barely more than a youth by Tuyul reckoning, as his people commonly live to a hundred and fifty. He stands four feet tall, lean where most Tuyul are round, angular where they are soft, with warm mahogany-brown skin, river-stone brown eyes flecked with amber, and thick, perpetually unruly black hair. A reader of old *lontar* manuscripts and a self-taught practitioner of Cimande pencak silat, Kian Santang was always the Tuyul who did not quite fit -- too curious, too restless, too aware of the world beyond the highland terraces. When the Keris Murka passes to him from his Uncle Kian, he accepts the burden of carrying it to Krakatau for destruction, volunteering at the Great Council of Dieng with a word that rises from somewhere deeper than reason. He is the unlikely heart of the fellowship: small, frightened, and braver than he knows.

*Equivalent: Frodo Baggins*

---

### 2. Bagus
**The Loyal Companion**

Son of a gardener in Kampung Padalarang. Bagus is everything a Tuyul should be and Kian Santang is not: stout, broad-shouldered, round-faced, deeply rooted in the earth, and supremely content with the rhythms of planting, tending, and harvest. His hands are wide and clever with growing things. His cooking -- simple village fare, rice and fish and foraged greens prepared with quiet, selfless care -- is an act of love so consistent it becomes heroic. He follows Kian Santang not because he understands the full scope of the quest, but because his friend is going somewhere dangerous, and Bagus made a promise, and a promise made is a promise kept. His deepest gift is presence: the wordless intelligence to know when to speak and when to simply be there, offering warm water and warm rice and the steadying weight of absolute loyalty.

*Equivalent: Samwise Gamgee*

---

### 3. Cepot
**The Clever One**

A young Tuyul of Padalarang, named after the beloved *wayang golek* character of Sundanese puppet theater -- Cepot the trickster, the truth-teller, the one who punctures pomposity with laughter. Like his namesake, this Cepot talks without ceasing. He fills every silence with stories, every story with commentary, every commentary with jokes that are terrible and necessary and sometimes, despite everything, genuinely funny. He is clever in the way of survivors -- quick-witted, resourceful, able to find a way out of trouble almost as fast as he finds a way into it. His humor is armor, his laughter a weapon against despair. When Cepot falls silent, the fellowship knows the grief has cut deeper than words can reach.

*Equivalent: Meriadoc "Merry" Brandybuck*

---

### 4. Dawala
**The Young Heart**

The youngest of the four Tuyul companions, named after another character from the Sundanese *wayang golek* tradition -- Dawala, Cepot's companion, the earnest and sometimes bewildered sidekick. He is cheerful, open-hearted, easily frightened, and possessed of a courage that surprises no one more than himself. He follows Cepot into the fellowship with the unquestioning loyalty of a younger brother, and discovers along the way that the qualities he thought were weaknesses -- his tenderness, his capacity for wonder, his inability to harden himself against the world's beauty -- are in fact strengths of a kind that the quest desperately needs.

*Equivalent: Peregrin "Pippin" Took*

---

### 5. Eyang Guru Batara
**The Ancient Sage**

A wandering *empu* -- spiritual master, keris-smith, scholar, healer, and keeper of knowledge so old it predates the kingdoms. No one knows Eyang Guru's true age; he deflects questions on the subject with the practiced ease of someone who has been doing so for centuries. His white hair falls to his shoulders, his white beard to his waist. He carries a staff of gnarled *waringin* (banyan) wood and wears robes of pale grey that are perpetually stained with the dust of long roads. He is the architect of the fellowship's journey, the mapper of its route, the forger of protective *jimat* amulets for each companion. His knowledge is vast, his patience deep, his anger -- when roused -- terrible. He falls in the caves of Gunung Sewu, locked in combat with the Naga Basukih upon the great stone bridge, and his final word -- *Pergi* (Go) -- drives the fellowship onward into the light. In his youth, Eyang Guru studied the ancient rites at Pura Besakih on Bali, where he mastered the homa fire rituals and the deepest Sanskrit mantras of the Hindu-Buddhist tradition. His Balinese connection gives him a spiritual authority that bridges the Javanese and Balinese worlds, and his invocations carry the cadences of both the Dieng sanctuaries and the Besakih temples.

*Equivalent: Gandalf*

---

### 6. Raden Wijaya
**The Exiled Prince**

Heir to the fallen throne of Mataram, the great Javanese kingdom. Raden Wijaya has lived for years as a ranger -- a wanderer of the wild places, a guardian of roads and borders, known by many names and trusted fully by few. He is tall, lean, dark-skinned, with the weathered face of a man long accustomed to sleeping with one ear tuned to danger. He is a master of Aliran Mataram, the silat school of the old Javanese kings, and his movements are water given purpose -- fluid, precise, and devastating. He carries his ancestral keris and the weight of a kingdom that no longer exists. Behind the ranger's hard exterior lies a man of profound honor and quiet grief, who carries his burden the only way he knows: well. His love for Dyah Pitaloka, daughter of Prabu Siliwangi, is the unspoken thread that ties him to hope.

*Equivalent: Aragorn*

---

### 7. Dewi Anjani
**The Bidadari Warrior**

A Bidadari warrior-princess of the Dieng sanctuary -- one of the ageless celestial beings who have watched over Nusantara since before the first kingdom raised its first stone. She appears as a woman of unearthly beauty: pale gold skin that holds a faint luminance, deep amber eyes that do not blink often enough, long dark hair that falls past her waist like a curtain of silk. She wears silver armor beneath her *selendang* and carries an enchanted bow of pale wood. But her beauty is the beauty of a mountain at dawn -- vast, inhuman, and wearing a human shape as a courtesy. She is connected to the fabric of the world in ways that mortal beings are not; she hears the hum of creation itself. In battle, she is swift and silent and lethal. In council, her words carry the weight of centuries. Her name echoes the Lutung Kasarung legend of the Sundanese tradition, in which a celestial being descends to the mortal world to guide and protect the worthy. Like the divine princess of that tale, Dewi Anjani moves between the heavenly and earthly realms, carrying the wisdom of the Tri Loka. Her Balinese cultural connections run deep -- she has studied the sacred *legong* dance at the temples of Bali, and her movements in battle echo the precise, luminous grace of Balinese court dance elevated to lethal purpose.

*Equivalent: Legolas*

**A Note on This Adaptation:** In Tolkien's *The Fellowship of the Ring*, Legolas is a male Elf. In this Nusantara adaptation, the Elven equivalent -- the Bidadari -- are drawn from Indonesian mythology, where they are inherently celestial women: divine nymphs, apsaras, daughters of the heavens. Making the fellowship's archer-warrior a woman is not a departure from Tolkien for the sake of departure; it is a natural consequence of faithfully adapting the source mythology. The Bidadari *are* female in the tradition from which they come. At the same time, the author is pleased that this cultural fidelity results in at least one woman among the nine companions of the *Pasukan Sembilan* -- a small correction to a fellowship that, in its original form, contained none. Tolkien gave us the bones; Nusantara gave us Dewi Anjani.

---

### 8. Tumenggung Batu
**The Kurcaci Chieftain**

Chieftain of the Kurcaci of Gunung Sewu, master smith, and a warrior built from the same materials as the mountains he lives in. He is shorter even than the Tuyul but far broader -- a dense arrangement of muscle and bone, dark river-clay skin, an iron-grey beard braided with metal rings. He carries a war hammer of dark mountain-iron, its head the size of a coconut, and wears chainmail over leather. He arrived at the Great Council of Dieng with a personal guard of twelve Kurcaci warriors whose iron-shod boots rang like anvils on the stone paths. He knows stone the way the Tuyul know soil and the Bidadari know starlight -- not as knowledge learned but as knowledge born. In the caves of Gunung Sewu, his home territory, he navigates by the feel of the rock beneath his feet, by intuitions that flow from a thousand years of ancestral connection to the deep earth. Though his seat of power lies in the limestone caves of southern Java, Tumenggung Batu carries the heritage of the Dayak peoples of Kalimantan in his bloodline. His mother's clan were river Dayak of the Mahakam, smiths of a different tradition who worked iron drawn from the deep jungle rivers of Borneo. From them he inherited the knowledge of ironwood and river-iron, and the Dayak belief that metal carries the memory of the earth from which it was drawn. His war hammer bears carvings in both the Kurcaci cave-script and the spiral motifs of Dayak metalwork, and around his neck he wears a necklace of boar tusks and hornbill-ivory in the Dayak fashion -- a bridge between two great smithing traditions of the archipelago.

*Equivalent: Gimli*

---

### 9. Gajahmada
**The Fallen General**

General of the Majapahit Empire, the mightiest military power in Nusantara. Gajahmada is tall, broad-chested, handsome, and commanding -- a career soldier tempered by sharp, restless intelligence, a man who believes with every fiber of his being that he knows how to save the world. He wears the crimson *dodot* of Majapahit court dress, a golden *kuluk* headdress, and an exquisite keris. He carries himself like a king rather than a general, and this ambiguity -- this blurring of service and sovereignty -- is both his greatest strength and his fatal flaw. He is brave, passionate, and genuinely committed to the defeat of the Shadow King. But the Keris Murka senses his hunger for power, and whispers to him, and in the end, the whisper proves stronger than his honor.

*Equivalent: Boromir*

---

## Other Major Characters

---

### Prabu Siliwangi
**The Wise King of Pasundan in Exile**

The last great king of Pasundan, now residing at the Dieng Plateau sanctuary. Silver-haired, upright, with the unhurried grace of one who has worn authority so long it has become indistinguishable from his posture. His face is refined by age rather than weathered by it -- each line an inscription, each crease a record of grief endured and wisdom gained. He is a healer of surpassing skill, working with turmeric and galangal and sacred water, his lips murmuring prayers in old Sundanese. He convenes the Great Council (*sabha*) at Dieng to determine the fate of the Keris Murka, presiding not from a throne but from a tikar mat at the same level as every other delegate, because in *sabha*, all voices carry equal weight.

*Equivalent: Elrond*

---

### Ki Sunda
**The Village Elder**

An elder of Kampung Padalarang, a man of quiet authority and patient service. Ki Sunda wears the traditional *iket* headcloth of a Pasundan elder with the dignity of one who has earned his standing through decades of devotion to his community. He is a keeper of village customs and a voice of measured wisdom in the communal gatherings of the Tuyul. The honorific "Ki" marks him as a respected elder and spiritual figure in Sundanese tradition.

---

### Uncle Kian
**The Traveler Who Found the Keris**

An elderly Tuyul of Kampung Padalarang, uncle and guardian of Kian Santang. Once a rare thing among his people -- a Tuyul who traveled beyond the highlands, who adventured in the wider world, who returned with *lontar* manuscripts and stories and a restlessness that marked him as permanently, gently eccentric. It was Uncle Kian who found the Keris Murka in the limestone labyrinth of Gunung Sewu, not knowing what it was, carrying it home like a curious trinket. The keris extended his life unnaturally but drained him slowly, and in passing it to Kian Santang, he freed himself and burdened his nephew with the weight of the world.

*Equivalent: Bilbo Baggins*

---

### Nyai Roro Kidul
**The Queen of the Southern Sea**

One of the most powerful spiritual beings in Nusantara -- the Queen of the Southern Sea, whose domain encompasses the Indian Ocean along Java's southern coast and the enchanted depths of Ngarai Sianok. Her beauty is elemental: the beauty of deep water and high weather, calm on the surface, fathomless beneath. Her skin holds the luminous brown of sunlight seen through a wave's crest. Her hair moves with its own slow, tidal rhythm. She wears a *kebaya* of sea-green silk and *batik tulis* patterned by the sea itself. Her eyes are the dark, saturated green of the deep southern ocean. She offers the fellowship rest, counsel, and the gift of seeing -- showing Kian Santang visions in the still pool of the canyon. She tests each companion and finds them worthy, or finds them wanting, with the impartial judgment of the sea.

*Equivalent: Galadriel*

---

### Dyah Pitaloka
**The Sundanese Princess**

Daughter of Prabu Siliwangi, a Sundanese princess of grace and quiet strength. Her love for Raden Wijaya is the unspoken thread of hope that connects the exiled prince to a future beyond wandering. She remains at Dieng during the fellowship's journey, a presence felt in absence -- in the memories that sustain Raden Wijaya through the darkest passages, in the promise of a world worth saving.

*Equivalent: Arwen*

---

### Raja Iblis
**The Shadow King**

The ancient demon-lord, the great enemy, the darkness older than memory. He came from beyond the world, drawn by the *bayu* of Nusantara, desiring not to nurture but to possess, to bend every living thing to his will. He established his throne of obsidian in the molten caldera of Krakatau, where the earth bleeds fire into the sea. He forged the Keris Murka, pouring his malice into the blade -- seven *pamor* patterns coiled into the metal, each one a chain meant to bind the will of kings. He forged nine lesser keris and gave them to nine great rulers, creating the Leak. He was thrown down by the alliance of the free peoples, his body shattered, his spirit retreating into Krakatau as a thing of smoke and rage and endless hunger. He waits. He watches. He whispers through the blade.

*Equivalent: Sauron*

---

### Empu Gandring
**The Corrupted Keris-Smith**

Once a master *empu*, a smith of the sacred art, Empu Gandring was seduced by the promise of supreme craftsmanship and absolute knowledge of metal and spirit. Now he dwells in a fortress of dark stone on the summit of Gunung Merapi, surrounded by his armies of Buta and his forges of corrupted iron, a servant of the Shadow King who believes himself a partner. He breeds the Buta Raksasa -- the giant elite demon warriors -- in the volcanic fires of Merapi. His betrayal of the sacred *empu* tradition is among the novel's deepest tragedies: the corruption not merely of a person but of an art.

*Equivalent: Saruman*

---

## Peoples of Nusantara

---

### Tuyul
Small, earth-connected folk of the Pasundan Highlands. The tallest barely reach the chest of a grown Javanese man, but they are broad-shouldered, brown-skinned, and solid as the earth they tend. Their feet are wide and tough from walking barefoot through the warm mud of the *sawah*. Their eyes are large and dark and quick to laugh. They are farmers first and always, deeply connected to the land, to the rhythms of planting and harvest, to Dewi Sri and the gift of rice. They commonly live to a hundred and fifty. They keep to themselves, want nothing beyond the next harvest, and view the restless ambitions of the larger peoples with bemused tolerance. Their homeland is a paradise of terraced rice fields, bamboo groves, and raised wooden houses, and they defend it with a fierce, quiet tenacity that surprises everyone, including themselves.

*Equivalent: Hobbits*

---

### Bidadari
Celestial immortal beings of beauty and wisdom, descended from the divine light of Sang Hyang Widhi. They are the longest-lived of all the peoples -- nearly immortal, luminous, connected to the fabric of the world in ways that mortal beings cannot fully comprehend. They dwell in mountain sanctuaries such as the Dieng Plateau, guardians of ancient knowledge and the spiritual arts. Their memory stretches back to the first dawn. They are keepers of lore, healers of wounds both physical and spiritual, and, when roused to battle, warriors of terrifying grace. Their skin holds a faint luminance, their eyes see beyond the visible spectrum, and they hear the sub-frequencies of stone, water, and wind.

*Equivalent: Elves*

---

### Kurcaci
Mountain-dwelling craftsmen and miners, born from the volcanic stone itself -- from the deepest fires of the earth, where pressure and heat forge diamond from carbon and iron from ore. They are shorter than the Tuyul but far broader, enormously muscled, with skin the color of river clay and beards long and elaborately braided. They are smiths and miners, builders and delvers, proud and stubborn and as enduring as the stone that birthed them. They carved the first tunnels through Gunung Sewu. They forged the first iron tools, the first bronze gongs, the first blades. They know stone the way other peoples know air -- instinctively, completely, with devotion.

*Equivalent: Dwarves*

---

### Buta
Ogre-demons, monstrous children of spite and shadow, bred in the volcanic wastes by the Shadow King and his servant Empu Gandring. They are large, brutish, and violent, creatures of darkness who fear the full light of day. They pour from the volcanic highlands in hordes to serve the will of Raja Iblis. In Indonesian mythology, Buta are fearsome ogre-like beings of great size and malevolent disposition.

*Equivalent: Orcs*

---

### Buta Raksasa
Giant elite demon warriors, bred by Empu Gandring in the volcanic forges of Gunung Merapi. Larger, stronger, and more disciplined than ordinary Buta, they are capable of enduring sunlight and carrying out sustained military campaigns. They are the shock troops of the Shadow King's armies.

*Equivalent: Uruk-hai*

---

### Genderuwo
Cunning dark creatures that inhabit the deep places of the earth -- the lower tunnels of Gunung Sewu, the volcanic caves, the lightless spaces where even the Kurcaci do not willingly venture. They are smaller than Buta but more numerous and more devious, attacking in swarms, setting traps and ambushes. In Indonesian folklore, Genderuwo are large, hairy, malevolent spirits; here they are reimagined as a race of subterranean predators.

*Equivalent: Goblins*

---

### Leak
The nine corrupted kings -- once great rulers of Nusantara who accepted shadow-keris from Raja Iblis and were transformed into shape-shifting terrors of the night, neither living nor dead, bound forever to the Shadow King's will. They ride Harimau Siluman -- spectral tiger-mounts -- and hunt the bearer of the Keris Murka with relentless, undying malice. In Balinese mythology, Leak are shape-shifting witches and dark spirits of fearsome power; here they are reimagined as the nine ring-bound wraith-kings of the Shadow King.

*Equivalent: Nazgul / Ringwraiths*

# GLOSSARY OF INDONESIAN TERMS

*A comprehensive guide to the Indonesian, Sundanese, and Javanese terms used in this novel. Pronunciations are simplified phonetic approximations for English-speaking readers. Stress generally falls on the penultimate (second-to-last) syllable.*

---

## Titles and Honorifics

**Adipati** (ah-dee-PAH-tee) -- A title of nobility roughly equivalent to duke or regent; a ruler of a province or sub-kingdom within a larger empire.

**Balapati** (bah-lah-PAH-tee) -- An Old Sundanese (Sunda Kuna) title meaning army general or field commander. Attested in pre-Islamic manuscripts. Used in the novel for military leaders who command forces in the field. See also *Hulujurit*.

**Bayangkara** (bah-yahng-KAH-rah) -- Royal guard; a title for elite warriors serving as the personal security force of a king or *tohaan*. From Old Sundanese court vocabulary. Bayangkara serve as the protectors of the kadaton and the person of the ruler.

**Dalang** (DAH-lahng) -- The master puppeteer of *wayang kulit* or *wayang golek* shadow puppet theater. The dalang narrates, voices all characters, directs the gamelan orchestra, and controls the puppets, serving as storyteller, philosopher, comedian, and spiritual guide in a single performance that may last all night. Used metaphorically in this novel: the narrator of the prologue identifies as a dalang, and Prabu Siliwangi serves as the dalang of the Great Council.

**Dewi** (DEH-wee) -- An honorific title meaning "goddess" or "divine lady." Used before the names of celestial or noble women. *Dewi Anjani*, *Dewi Sri*.

**Dukun** (DOO-koon) -- A traditional healer, spiritual practitioner, or shaman in Indonesian society. May practice herbal medicine, spiritual counseling, divination, or darker arts. Mentioned in connection with the misunderstood reputation of the Tuyul.

**Dwija** (DWEE-jah) -- Literally "twice-born"; an Old Sundanese and Sanskrit title for a senior priest who has undergone a second spiritual initiation. Dwija serve as the highest religious authorities in the candi temples, conducting homa fire rituals and interpreting sacred texts.

**Eyang** (EH-yahng) -- A Javanese/Sundanese honorific meaning "grandfather" or "venerable elder." A term of deep respect for the aged and wise. *Eyang Guru* -- "Venerable Teacher."

**Empu** (EM-poo) -- A master keris-smith; one who has achieved the highest level of spiritual and technical mastery in the sacred art of keris-making. The title carries immense respect, as the empu is considered not merely a metalworker but a spiritual practitioner who imbues each blade with a soul. *Empu Gandring* is a corrupted empu.

**Haji** (HAH-jee) -- In the pre-Islamic Sundanese context of this novel, *haji* is a royal title meaning "king" or "ruler," attested in Old Sundanese and Old Javanese inscriptions (e.g., the Tugu inscription). This is entirely unrelated to the later Islamic meaning of one who has performed the pilgrimage to Mecca. In Old Sundanese usage, *haji* designates sovereign authority.

**Hulujurit** (hoo-loo-JOO-rit) -- An Old Sundanese (Sunda Kuna) title meaning war commander; literally "head of the warriors." Attested in the *Sanghyang Siksa Kandang Karesian* and other pre-Islamic manuscripts. The primary military leadership title in Sundanese kingdoms, used in this novel in place of the modern Indonesian *panglima*.

**Kang** (kahng) -- A Sundanese/Javanese informal honorific used between male peers or for a slightly older male acquaintance, roughly equivalent to "brother" or "bro." Used by Bagus when addressing Kian Santang.

**Ki** (kee) -- A Sundanese/Javanese honorific for a respected elder or spiritual figure, typically a man of standing in the community who has earned authority through wisdom and service. *Ki Sunda* -- the village elder of Kampung Padalarang.

**Nini** (NEE-nee) -- An honorific for an elderly woman; grandmother. *Nini Anteh* -- the eldest grandmother of Kampung Padalarang.

**Nyai** (NYAH-ee) -- An honorific title for a respected or powerful woman, particularly one of spiritual authority. *Nyai Roro Kidul* -- the Queen of the Southern Sea.

**Pak** (pahk) -- A common Indonesian honorific for an older or respected man; short for *Bapak* (father). Used as a general term of address for village elders and men of standing.

**Prabu** (PRAH-boo) -- A Sundanese royal title meaning "king" or "sovereign." Carries connotations of wisdom and righteous rule. *Prabu Siliwangi*, *Prabu Kian Santang*.

**Raden** (RAH-den) -- A Javanese title of high nobility, used for princes and the highest-ranking aristocrats. *Raden Wijaya*.

**Sang** (sahng) -- A high literary honorific placed before a name or title to denote supreme reverence. *Sang Hyang Widhi* (the Supreme Being), *Sang Dalang* (the Great Puppeteer).

**Séwaka** (SEH-wah-kah) -- An Old Sundanese term for a retainer, courtier, or servant in the royal household. A *séwaka* served the *tohaan* directly, attending to court duties and carrying out the ruler's commands. Attested in Old Sundanese manuscripts as a formal court role.

**Tohaan** (TOH-hahn) -- An Old Sundanese (Sunda Kuna) title meaning supreme ruler or lord, used for the highest authority figures. Attested in the *Sanghyang Siksa Kandang Karesian* and *Carita Parahyangan*. Carries connotations of both temporal and spiritual sovereignty.

**Tumenggung** (too-meng-GOONG) -- A high-ranking military and administrative title in Javanese and Sundanese kingdoms, roughly equivalent to a commander or lord-general. *Tumenggung Batu*.

**Yuwaraja** (yoo-wah-RAH-jah) -- Crown prince or heir to the throne. From Sanskrit *yuvaraja*. Used in Old Sundanese court hierarchy for the designated successor to the *tohaan* or *haji*.

---

## Sunda Kuna Pronouns and Greetings

*Old Sundanese (Sunda Kuna) personal pronouns and greetings used in the Sundanese dialogue of this novel, drawn from pre-Islamic manuscripts including the Sanghyang Siksa Kandang Karesian and Bujangga Manik.*

**Aksama** (ahk-SAH-mah) -- Forgiveness; a formal request for pardon. From Sanskrit *kshama*. Used in Old Sundanese as the equivalent of the modern Sundanese *hampura* ("forgive me"). Characters say "Aksama" when seeking pardon or acknowledging a transgression.

**Andika** (ahn-DEE-kah) -- You (formal, respectful). The Old Sundanese second-person pronoun used when addressing superiors, elders, or persons of high status. Replaces the modern Sundanese *anjeun* in this novel's pre-Islamic dialogue.

**Awignam astu** (ah-WEEG-nahm AHS-too) -- "May there be no obstacles." A sacred invocation from the Hindu-Buddhist tradition, used as a blessing before undertakings, ceremonies, and journeys. Functions as a formal greeting and blessing in the Old Sundanese context. See also *Om Awighnam Astu*.

**Dika** (DEE-kah) -- You (casual, familiar). The Old Sundanese second-person pronoun used among peers, friends, and close companions. Replaces the modern Sundanese *maneh* in casual Sundanese dialogue.

**Ingwang** (ING-wahng) -- I, me (formal). The Old Sundanese first-person pronoun, well-attested in manuscripts such as the *Sanghyang Siksa Kandang Karesian* and *Bujangga Manik*. Replaces the modern Sundanese *abdi*, which derives from the Arabic *abd* ("servant") and entered Sundanese via the Islamic Mataram court's *abdi dalem* system -- entirely inappropriate for the pre-Islamic setting of this novel.

**Mulah** (MOO-lah) -- A prohibition marker in Old Sundanese; equivalent to "do not" or "must not." Replaces the modern Sundanese *ulah* and *tong*. Used in commands and warnings: *"Mulah ragrag!"* ("Do not fall!").

**Nembah** (NEM-bah) -- A formal bow or gesture of deep respect; used as a greeting and expression of gratitude in Old Sundanese. Functions as the Sunda Kuna equivalent of both the modern *hatur nuhun* ("thank you") and *punten* ("excuse me"), conveying reverence through physical and verbal acknowledgment.

**Priya** (PREE-yah) -- Friend, companion, beloved. From Sanskrit/Old Sundanese. Used in place of the modern Indonesian *sobat*. Carries connotations of close companionship and mutual regard.

**Rahayu** (rah-HAH-yoo) -- A blessing-greeting meaning "may you be well" or "peace be upon you." The standard Old Sundanese greeting, used in place of the modern *sampurasun* and *kumaha damang*. Rahayu carries the warmth of a blessing rather than a mere salutation.

**Siya** (SEE-yah) -- He, she, they (third person). The Old Sundanese third-person pronoun, replacing the modern *manehna*. Not to be confused with *sia*, the disrespectful second-person pronoun of *basa kasar*.

---

## Geographic Terms

**Aranya** (ah-RAHN-yah) -- Remote forest; deep wilderness far from habitation. From Sanskrit *aranya*. Used in the novel for the most isolated stretches of forest, where even the paths of woodcutters and hunters do not reach.

**Bukit** (BOO-kit) -- Hill or mountain range. *Bukit Barisan* -- the great mountain range of Sumatra, literally "Row of Hills."

**Candi** (CHAHN-dee) -- An ancient stone temple, typically Hindu or Buddhist, built during the classical period of Indonesian history. The temples of Dieng, Barabudur, and Prambanan are all *candi*. Used throughout to describe the Bidadari sanctuary at Dieng.

**Danau** (DAH-now) -- Lake. Used for larger bodies of water. In the Old Sundanese (Sunda Kuna) register of this novel, lakes are more often referred to as *talaga* (sacred lake) or *ranu*. See *Talaga*.

**Dataran** (dah-TAH-rahn) -- Plateau or flatland. *Dataran Dieng* -- the Dieng Plateau.

**Giri** (GEE-ree) -- Mountain (Old Sundanese/Sanskrit). Used in poetic and formal Sundanese contexts in place of the common *gunung*. From Sanskrit *giri*. See also *Wukir*.

**Gunung** (GOO-noong) -- Mountain or volcano. *Gunung Merapi*, *Gunung Sewu*, *Gunung Ciremai*, *Gunung Krakatau*, *Gunung Tangkuban Perahu*.

**Jaladri** (jah-LAH-dree) -- Sea or ocean in Old Sundanese/Sanskrit poetic usage. Literally "water-holder." Used in formal and poetic passages in place of the common *laut*. See also *Sagara*.

**Laut** (LAH-oot) -- Sea or ocean. *Laut Jawa* (Java Sea), *Laut Banda* (Banda Sea). In poetic or Sunda Kuna contexts, replaced by *sagara* or *jaladri*.

**Lwah** (LWAH) -- River in Old Sundanese. Attested in Sunda Kuna manuscripts. Used in the novel in place of the common Indonesian *sungai* when characters speak in the archaic register. See also *Nadi*.

**Mandala** (mahn-DAH-lah) -- A sacred precinct or circle; a defined spiritual territory. From Sanskrit. In Old Sundanese usage, a mandala is a consecrated space -- a temple complex, a meditation ground, or a region under the spiritual authority of a resi or pandita. The Dieng sanctuary is described as a mandala of ancient power.

**Nadi** (NAH-dee) -- River (Sanskrit/Old Sundanese). Used in formal and poetic passages as an alternative to *lwah* or the common Indonesian *sungai*.

**Nagara** (nah-GAH-rah) -- Kingdom, realm, or city-state. From Sanskrit *nagara*. Used in Old Sundanese manuscripts to denote a polity or sovereign territory. Related to the Majapahit epic *Nagarakretagama* ("Guidebook for the Realm's Order").

**Ngarai** (NGAH-rye) -- Canyon or gorge. *Ngarai Sianok* -- the great canyon of West Sumatra. The "ng" is a single nasal sound, as in "sing."

**Pertiwi** (per-TEE-wee) -- Earth, the ground, the land itself. From Sanskrit *prithvi*. Used in Old Sundanese poetic contexts to refer to the earth as a living, sacred entity. *Ibu Pertiwi* -- Mother Earth.

**Pulau** (POO-low) -- Island. The Indonesian archipelago comprises over 17,000 *pulau*.

**Rawa** (RAH-wah) -- Marsh, swamp, or wetland. *Rawa Pening* -- the haunted marshlands of Central Java.

**Sagara** (sah-GAH-rah) -- Ocean or sea (Sanskrit/Old Sundanese). The poetic and formal term used in Sunda Kuna contexts in place of the common *laut*. See also *Jaladri*.

**Samudra** (sah-MOO-drah) -- Ocean (from Sanskrit). *Samudra Hindia* -- the Indian Ocean.

**Selat** (SEH-laht) -- Strait, a narrow passage of water between two landmasses. *Selat Sunda* -- the Sunda Strait between Java and Sumatra.

**Sungai** (SOONG-eye) -- River. *Sungai Citarum* -- the Citarum River of West Java. In the Sunda Kuna register of this novel, rivers are often called *lwah* or *nadi*.

**Talaga** (tah-LAH-gah) -- A sacred lake or pool in Old Sundanese. Used in the novel as the Sunda Kuna equivalent of *danau* (lake), carrying connotations of spiritual significance. *Talaga Warna* -- the Color-Shifting Lake of Dieng.

**Tanah** (TAH-nah) -- Land, earth, soil. *Tanah Sunda* -- the Land of Sunda. Carries deep connotations of homeland, rootedness, and belonging.

**Wana** (WAH-nah) -- Forest (Sanskrit/Old Sundanese). The standard Old Sundanese term for forest, used in place of the common *hutan*. See also *Aranya*, *Wanadri*, *Wanantara*.

**Wanadri** (wah-NAH-dree) -- Mountain forest; the forested slopes of volcanic highlands. A compound of *wana* (forest) and *adri* (mountain). Used in Old Sundanese descriptive passages.

**Wanantara** (wah-nahn-TAH-rah) -- Deep wilderness; the vast uncharted forest beyond settled lands. An Old Sundanese compound suggesting the unknown interior of the great forests.

**Wukir** (WOO-kir) -- Mountain (Old Javanese/Sundanese). An alternative poetic term for mountain, used alongside *giri*. From Old Javanese.

## The Keris and Its Elements

**Dapur** (DAH-poor) -- The specific form or model of a keris blade, defined by the number of *luk* (curves), the shape of the blade's base, and other structural features. Hundreds of named *dapur* exist, each with its own spiritual significance.

**Keris** (KEH-ris) -- The asymmetrical dagger of Indonesia and the Malay world, one of the most culturally significant objects in Southeast Asian civilization. A keris may be straight or sinuously curved (*luk*), and its blade is forged from layers of iron and nickel-bearing meteorite steel, creating distinctive wavy patterns (*pamor*) in the metal. Each keris is believed to possess a spiritual essence -- a *tangguh* -- and may bring fortune or ruin to its bearer. The *Keris Murka* (Keris of Wrath) is the novel's central artifact: a blade forged by Raja Iblis to dominate all the peoples of Nusantara.

**Luk** (look) -- The sinuous curves of a keris blade. The number of luk (always odd) determines the blade's classification and spiritual character. A blade with many luk is considered more powerful and more dangerous.

**Pamor** (PAH-mor) -- The distinctive wavy pattern visible on a keris blade, created by the folding and forging of different metals. The pamor is not merely decorative; it is believed to carry the spiritual essence of the blade. The Keris Murka contains seven pamor patterns, each one a chain meant to bind the will of kings.

**Tangguh** (TAHNG-gooh) -- The spiritual character or age classification of a keris; its essential nature, written in the folds of its metal. A keris's tangguh determines whether it is compatible with its bearer and what fortune or misfortune it may bring.

**Warangka** (wah-RAHNG-kah) -- The wooden sheath of a keris, typically carved from a single piece of hardwood and often decorated with fine metalwork. The warangka serves both as protective housing and as a spiritual complement to the blade.

---

## Arts, Performance, and Music

**Angklung** (AHNG-kloong) -- A traditional Sundanese musical instrument made of bamboo tubes attached to a bamboo frame; played by shaking so that the tubes strike against each other, producing a resonant tone. Each angklung produces a single note; when played together in ensemble, interlocking their notes in rapid succession, the sound is extraordinary -- like rain falling on a forest canopy, like water running over stones. Recognized by UNESCO as a Masterpiece of Intangible Cultural Heritage.

**Blencong** (BLEN-chong) -- The oil lamp used to illuminate a *wayang kulit* shadow puppet performance, hung above the screen so that the puppets cast sharp shadows upon the white cloth. In this novel, the blencong serves as a narrative frame -- the flickering flame that casts the story's shadows.

**Bonang** (BOH-nahng) -- A set of small bronze gongs laid horizontally in rows on a wooden frame, part of the Javanese gamelan ensemble. Played with padded sticks, producing bright, ringing bell-tones.

**Gamelan** (GAH-meh-lahn) -- The traditional ensemble music of Java and Bali, consisting primarily of bronze metallophones, xylophones, drums, gongs, and sometimes flute and bowed string instruments. Gamelan music is characterized by its layered, cyclical structure and its shimmering, interlocking textures. In this novel, gamelan accompanies ceremonies, councils, and moments of spiritual significance.

**Jaipongan** (jye-PONG-ahn) -- A Sundanese social dance characterized by fluid shoulder movements, quick and playful footwork, and joyful expressiveness. Developed from traditional Sundanese performing arts, jaipongan is energetic, communal, and celebratory. Danced at the Seren Taun harvest festival in Kampung Padalarang.

**Kendang** (KEN-dahng) -- A double-headed drum played with the hands, the rhythmic backbone of both Javanese and Sundanese gamelan ensembles. The kendang player sets the tempo and signals transitions to the other musicians.

**Pantun** (PAHN-toon) -- A form of traditional Malay/Indonesian poetry, consisting of four-line stanzas with an ABAB rhyme scheme. The first two lines present a nature image; the second two deliver the message. Pantun are recited at gatherings, ceremonies, and in courtship.

**Pelog** (PEH-log) -- One of the two main scale systems used in Javanese gamelan music (the other being *slendro*). Pelog is a seven-note scale with uneven intervals, producing a sound that is measured, contemplative, and richly resonant.

**Rebab** (reh-BAHB) -- A two-stringed bowed instrument used in gamelan ensembles, providing a sustained melodic line above the metallophones. The rebab's sound is thin, clear, and hauntingly beautiful.

**Tembang** (TEM-bahng) -- A Javanese or Sundanese sung poem, a form of classical vocal music with strict metrical rules governing the number of syllables per line and the vowel sounds that end each line. Tembang are repositories of philosophy, history, and moral instruction. In this novel, specific tembang forms are used throughout -- see the **Pupuh and Poetic Forms** section below for detailed entries on the named pupuh forms (*Sinom*, *Kinanti*, *Asmarandana*, *Dangdanggula*, *Durma*, *Mijil*, *Pangkur*) that appear in the narrative.

**Wayang golek** (WAH-yahng GOH-lek) -- Three-dimensional wooden rod-puppet theater, the characteristic art form of the Sundanese people of West Java. The puppets are carved and painted wooden figures operated by rods, performing stories from the Ramayana, Mahabharata, and local legends. Cepot and Dawala are named after beloved wayang golek characters.

**Wayang kulit** (WAH-yahng KOO-lit) -- Shadow puppet theater, one of the supreme art forms of Indonesia. Flat leather puppets, intricately carved and painted, are manipulated by the *dalang* between a *blencong* oil lamp and a white screen, casting shadows that tell stories from the great epics. A complete wayang kulit performance may last from dusk to dawn.

---

## Pencak Silat (Martial Arts)

**Aliran** (ah-LEE-rahn) -- A school or style of pencak silat. Different *aliran* emphasize different techniques, philosophies, and regional traditions. *Aliran Mataram* -- the silat school of the old Javanese kings, practiced by Raden Wijaya. *Cimande* -- the Sundanese style practiced by Kian Santang.

**Elakan** (eh-LAH-kahn) -- Evasive movements in pencak silat; the art of avoiding an opponent's attack through slipping, ducking, swaying, or stepping. Raden Wijaya teaches Kian Santang the elakan suited to his small stature.

**Golok** (GOH-lok) -- A large, heavy chopping knife or machete used as both a tool and a weapon throughout Indonesia. In silat, the golok is a common training and combat weapon.

**Kuda-kuda** (KOO-dah KOO-dah) -- The foundational stances of pencak silat, literally "horse stance" (from the position's resemblance to sitting on horseback). *Kuda-kuda rendah* -- the deep horse stance, sinking low with thighs parallel to the ground.

**Langkah** (LAHNG-kah) -- Footwork; the stepping patterns of pencak silat. Proper langkah is the foundation of all silat movement.

**Pencak silat** (PEN-chahk SEE-laht) -- The traditional martial art of Indonesia and the Malay world. Pencak silat encompasses a vast array of styles, techniques, and philosophies, from the practical combat methods of village self-defense to the high spiritual arts of the royal courts. It combines strikes, kicks, throws, joint locks, and weapon techniques with flowing, dance-like movement. In this novel, silat is both combat art and spiritual practice.

**Sapuan** (sah-POO-ahn) -- A sweeping technique in pencak silat; a low kick designed to take an opponent's legs out from under them.

**Sikap pasang** (SEE-kahp PAH-sahng) -- The fighting ready position in pencak silat; hands open and turning, palms tracing slow circles, body balanced and prepared to move in any direction.

**Sakti** (SAHK-tee) -- Supernatural power, spiritual potency; the inner force cultivated through meditation, ascetic discipline, and spiritual mastery. From Sanskrit *shakti*. In this novel, *sakti* replaces the modern Indonesian *tenaga dalam*, as the Old Sundanese and Sanskrit-derived form appropriate to the pre-Islamic setting. A warrior or sage of great sakti can perform extraordinary feats. See also *Bayu*.

**Tenaga dalam** (teh-NAH-gah DAH-lahm) -- *See Sakti, Bayu.* The modern Indonesian term for inner power or internal energy. In this novel, the Old Sundanese and Sanskrit-derived terms *sakti* (supernatural power) and *bayu* (life force, spiritual energy) are used instead, as befitting the pre-Islamic 8th-9th century setting.

## Food and Drink

**Bubur** (BOO-boor) -- Rice porridge; a common breakfast dish in Indonesia, served with various toppings such as chicken, peanuts, fried shallots, and sambal.

**Dendeng** (DEN-deng) -- Dried meat jerky, typically beef or buffalo, seasoned and fried until crisp. A specialty of Minangkabau (West Sumatran) cuisine.

**Gula aren** (GOO-lah AH-ren) -- Palm sugar, harvested from the sap of the aren (sugar palm) tree. Rich, caramel-flavored, and used extensively in Indonesian desserts and drinks.

**Jamu** (JAH-moo) -- Traditional Indonesian herbal medicine, typically a drink made from fresh-ground roots, leaves, bark, and spices. Jamu recipes are passed down through generations and are believed to promote health, beauty, and vitality.

**Kangkung** (KAHNG-koong) -- Water spinach, a common leafy green vegetable in Indonesian cooking, often stir-fried with garlic, chili, and shrimp paste. Bagus forages wild kangkung along stream banks during the journey.

**Kolak** (KOH-lahk) -- A sweet dessert of coconut milk simmered with palm sugar, banana, and sweet potato until thick as liquid gold. Served warm. Nini Anteh's kolak at the Seren Taun festival is described as "transcendent."

**Lalapan** (lah-LAH-pahn) -- A platter of fresh raw vegetables served as accompaniment to a meal: cucumber, basil, long beans, cabbage leaves, meant to be eaten with the hands and dipped in sambal. A staple of Sundanese dining.

**Nasi timbel** (NAH-see TIM-bel) -- Fragrant rice steamed in a banana leaf wrapping, a signature dish of Sundanese cuisine. The banana leaf imparts a subtle green sweetness to the rice.

**Rempeyek** (rem-PEH-yek) -- A crispy Indonesian cracker made from a batter of rice flour and spices, studded with peanuts, anchovies, or spinach leaves and deep-fried. A common accompaniment to rice meals.

**Rendang** (REN-dahng) -- A rich, slow-cooked meat dish in a coconut and spice sauce, originating from the Minangkabau of West Sumatra. Widely regarded as one of the world's most delicious dishes. In this novel, rendang simmers in clay pots at the Seren Taun festival.

**Sambal** (SAHM-bahl) -- A chili-based condiment or sauce, essential to Indonesian cuisine. Hundreds of varieties exist, ranging from mild to extraordinarily fiery. Described in the novel as "so fiery it made the eyes water and the spirit sing."

**Sayur asem** (SAH-yoor AH-sem) -- Tamarind vegetable soup, a Sundanese specialty, sour and savory and deeply restorative. Typically contains corn, chayote, long beans, peanuts, and young jackfruit in a tamarind broth.

**Sangu** (SAH-ngoo) -- Cooked rice (Old Sundanese/Javanese). The attested pre-Islamic term for prepared rice, used in this novel in place of more specific modern dish names. Sangu is the foundation of every meal in Nusantara, served on banana leaves with whatever accompaniments the land provides.

**Tuak** (TOO-ahk) -- Palm wine; an alcoholic beverage made from the fermented sap of the aren (sugar palm), lontar, or coconut palm. Tuak is the traditional drink of communal gatherings, celebrations, and traveler's rest across Nusantara, predating the arrival of tea, coffee, or other imported beverages. In this novel, tuak replaces anachronistic references to tea or coffee.

**Wedang** (WEH-dahng) -- A warm herbal drink; any heated beverage made from ginger, herbs, spices, or other botanical ingredients simmered in water with palm sugar. Wedang is the period-appropriate term for warm drinks in this novel, replacing anachronistic tea (*teh*) and coffee (*kopi*), which arrived in Nusantara centuries after the novel's 8th-9th century setting.

**Wedang jahe** (WEH-dahng JAH-heh) -- Warm ginger drink, made by simmering fresh ginger root with palm sugar and water. Comforting, warming, and commonly served during the cool evening hours or in the mountains.

## Hindu-Buddhist and Animist Spiritual Terms

**Atma** (AHT-mah) -- The individual soul or true self in Hindu philosophy; the eternal, unchanging essence within every living being. From Sanskrit *atman*, rendered in the Old Sundanese form *atma* as attested in Sunda Kuna manuscripts. In this novel, Eyang Guru teaches that the atma of each companion is their truest weapon against the corruption of the Keris Murka, for the blade can dominate the will but cannot touch the atma.

**Bayu** (BAH-yoo) -- Life force, breath, wind, spiritual energy. From Sanskrit *vayu* (wind, the wind god). In the Old Sundanese context of this novel, *bayu* is the vital energy that flows through every living thing, every island, every stone and drop of water in Nusantara. Replaces the modern Indonesian *prana* (also from Sanskrit but via modern usage) and *tenaga dalam* (modern Indonesian). Bayu is both the breath of life within a person and the spiritual energy of the land itself. A warrior of great *bayu* possesses inner power; a land drained of *bayu* is spiritually dead.

**Brahma** (BRAH-mah) -- The Creator god in the Hindu Tri Murti; the divine force of creation and origination. In Nusantara's Hindu-Buddhist tradition, Brahma is the source from which all things emerge. Invoked in creation stories told at the Dieng sanctuary and in the cosmological reliefs of the ancient candi.

**Dharma** (DAHR-mah) -- Cosmic law, righteousness, moral duty; the principle of order that sustains the universe. From Sanskrit. In this novel, dharma is the guiding principle of the fellowship's quest -- the recognition that the Keris Murka must be destroyed not because it is expedient, but because it is right. Characters speak of following their dharma as the path that cannot be refused.

**Homa** (HOH-mah) -- A sacred fire ritual of Hindu-Buddhist origin, in which offerings are made into a consecrated fire as prayers and mantras are chanted. Eyang Guru Batara performs homa rituals to consecrate the fellowship's jimat amulets and to purify sacred spaces before the Great Council. The fire is understood as Agni, the divine intermediary who carries offerings to the gods.

**Kabuyutan** (kah-boo-YOO-tahn) -- A sacred site in Sundanese tradition; a place of ancestral spiritual power, often a hilltop, spring, or ancient grove where the boundary between the visible and invisible worlds is thin. The kabuyutan are tended by spiritual guardians and protected by customary law. Kampung Padalarang's kabuyutan is an ancient waringin tree at the village edge.

**Karma** (KAHR-mah) -- The principle of cause and effect in Hindu-Buddhist philosophy; the understanding that every action generates consequences that shape one's future existence. From Sanskrit. In this novel, the karma of the Shadow King's forging of the Keris Murka ensures that the blade will ultimately be the instrument of his own undoing.

**Karuhun** (kah-ROO-hoon) -- Ancestors; the departed forebears in Sundanese tradition, whose spirits remain connected to the living and whose wisdom guides the community. Distinct from the more general Indonesian term *leluhur*, karuhun carries specifically Sundanese connotations of ancestral guardianship and spiritual continuity. The Tuyul honor their karuhun with sesajen offerings at the kabuyutan.

**Lingga-yoni** (LING-gah YOH-nee) -- A combined stone sculpture representing the union of Shiva (lingga, the vertical pillar) and the feminine creative principle (yoni, the horizontal base). Found in Hindu temples throughout Nusantara as a symbol of cosmic creation, fertility, and the balance of masculine and feminine forces. Lingga-yoni stones stand in the inner sanctums of the Dieng candi.

**Maya** (MAH-yah) -- Illusion; the veil of appearances that conceals ultimate reality. From Sanskrit. In Hindu-Buddhist philosophy, the material world is maya -- real in its effects but not ultimately real. In this novel, the power of the Keris Murka operates through maya, creating illusions of power and glory to seduce its bearer.

**Moksha** (MOHK-shah) -- Liberation; the release of the atman from the cycle of samsara, the ultimate goal of spiritual practice in Hindu philosophy. From Sanskrit. Eyang Guru speaks of the destruction of the Keris Murka as an act of moksha for all of Nusantara -- liberation from the chains that the Shadow King has forged.

**Om Awighnam Astu** (ohm ah-WEEG-nahm AHS-too) -- A sacred invocation from the Hindu-Buddhist tradition of Nusantara, meaning "May there be no obstacles." Spoken at the beginning of ceremonies, councils, and sacred undertakings to invoke divine protection and clear the path of impediments. Used by Prabu Siliwangi to open the Great Council at Dieng.

**Om Swasti Astu** (ohm SWAHS-tee AHS-too) -- A sacred greeting and invocation from the Hindu-Buddhist tradition of Nusantara, meaning "May all be well" or "May blessings be upon you." Used as a blessing, a greeting of peace, and an opening prayer. Replaces common greetings in formal and sacred contexts throughout the novel.

**Pura** (POO-rah) -- A Hindu temple in the Balinese and Nusantara tradition, distinguished by its layered roofs (*meru*), split gates (*candi bentar*), and walled courtyards. Unlike the solid stone candi of Javanese tradition, the pura is an open-air complex of shrines, altars, and pavilions. The layered roof of a small pura is visible from the road in the opening chapters, marking the spiritual landscape of pre-Islamic Nusantara.

**Samsara** (sahm-SAH-rah) -- The cycle of birth, death, and rebirth in Hindu-Buddhist philosophy; the wheel of existence from which moksha offers liberation. From Sanskrit. The corrupted kings who became the Leak are trapped in a terrible samsara -- neither fully alive nor fully dead, cycling endlessly through servitude to the Shadow King.

**Shiva** (SHEE-wah) -- The Destroyer and Transformer in the Hindu Tri Murti; the divine force of destruction, dissolution, and regeneration. In Nusantara's Hindu-Buddhist tradition, Shiva is widely revered as the supreme deity, particularly in the form of Shiva Mahadeva. The lingga-yoni stones in the Dieng temples are consecrated to Shiva. The destruction of the Keris Murka is understood as an act of Shiva -- the necessary destruction that makes renewal possible.

**Tri Loka** (tree LOH-kah) -- The three worlds of Hindu-Buddhist cosmology: *Bhur Loka* (the earthly realm of mortals), *Bhuvar Loka* (the middle realm of spirits and ancestors), and *Svar Loka* (the heavenly realm of the gods). The Bidadari move between all three worlds; mortals are confined to Bhur Loka but may glimpse the other realms through meditation, sacred ceremony, or the thin places where the worlds overlap.

**Tri Murti** (tree MOOR-tee) -- The Hindu trinity of Brahma (Creator), Vishnu (Preserver), and Shiva (Destroyer/Transformer). The three aspects of the divine that govern the cycle of creation, preservation, and dissolution. References to the Tri Murti pervade the spiritual landscape of this novel, from the candi reliefs at Dieng to the philosophical teachings of Eyang Guru.

**Vishnu** (WISH-noo) -- The Preserver in the Hindu Tri Murti; the divine force that sustains and protects the world between its creation and dissolution. In Nusantara's tradition, Vishnu is the guardian of cosmic order, often depicted riding the Garuda. The fellowship's quest to preserve Nusantara from the Shadow King is understood as service to Vishnu's dharma of preservation.

## Spiritual and Religious Terms (General)

**Jimat** (JEE-maht) -- An amulet or talisman, typically a small inscribed object of metal, stone, or cloth, believed to offer spiritual protection to its bearer. Eyang Guru Batara forges bronze jimat for each member of the fellowship, inscribing them with Sanskrit mantras tailored to each bearer's spiritual needs.

**Kemenyan** (keh-MEN-yahn) -- Benzoin incense resin, burned in ceremonies, councils, and spiritual rituals throughout Indonesia. Its fragrance -- resin and earth and something faintly sweet, like memory distilled to smoke -- permeates the Great Council scene at Dieng.

**Leluhur** (leh-LOO-hoor) -- Ancestors; the departed forebears whose spirits are honored and whose wisdom guides the living. Reverence for *leluhur* is a deep and pervasive element of Indonesian spiritual life.

**Mantra** (MAHN-trah) -- A sacred utterance, prayer, or incantation, often in Sanskrit or Old Javanese, used in spiritual practice, meditation, and the consecration of sacred objects. Eyang Guru inscribes mantras on the fellowship's jimat.

**Puji Dewata** (POO-jee deh-WAH-tah) -- An expression of praise and gratitude to the gods (*dewata*); literally "praise to the divine ones." Used as an exclamation of thankfulness and relief throughout the novel, reflecting the Hindu-Buddhist spiritual context of pre-Islamic Nusantara.

**Ruwatan** (roo-WAH-tahn) -- A Javanese purification ceremony performed to cleanse a person, place, or object of spiritual impurity or misfortune. Often involves wayang kulit performance, offerings, and prayer.

**Sang Hyang Widhi** (sahng HYANG WEE-dee) -- The Supreme Being in Balinese Hinduism; the divine breath behind all breathing, the creator who shaped the islands of Nusantara from fire and water. Invoked in the novel's cosmology as the ultimate source of creation.

**Semedi** (seh-MEH-dee) -- Deep meditation; a state of intense spiritual concentration practiced by warriors, sages, and rulers to gain insight, strength, or connection with the divine.

**Sesajen** (seh-SAH-jen) -- Ritual offerings made to spirits, ancestors, or deities, typically consisting of flowers, incense, rice, fruit, and other symbolic items arranged on a banana leaf or woven tray.

**Tapa** (TAH-pah) -- Ascetic practice; spiritual discipline involving fasting, isolation, meditation, or physical hardship, undertaken to gain spiritual power or insight. Related to the Sanskrit *tapas*.

**Tempat keramat** (TEM-paht keh-RAH-maht) -- A sacred or spiritually powerful place -- a mountain summit, an ancient tree, a cave, a spring -- where the boundary between the visible and invisible worlds is believed to be thin.

## Architecture and Structures

**Beranda** (beh-RAHN-dah) -- A veranda or porch, an essential feature of Indonesian domestic architecture. In the novel, every Tuyul *rumah panggung* has an east-facing beranda for greeting the sunrise.

**Joglo** (JOG-loh) -- The traditional Javanese roof style characterized by its dramatic, steeply pitched four-tiered form, rising to a central peak. The *pendopo* at Dieng features a joglo roof "rising to a final peak that vanished into the low cloud like a mountain aspiring to become sky."

**Pendopo** (pen-DOH-poh) -- An open-sided pavilion, typically with a *joglo* roof supported by carved pillars, serving as the public reception and ceremonial hall of a Javanese palace or institution. The Great Council of Dieng takes place in the grand pendopo atop the plateau.

**Rumah gadang** (ROO-mah GAH-dahng) -- The traditional communal house of the Minangkabau people of West Sumatra, distinguished by its dramatic upswept roof, said to resemble buffalo horns. A symbol of Minangkabau matrilineal culture.

**Rumah panggung** (ROO-mah PAHNG-goong) -- A raised wooden house built on stilts or pillars, common throughout the Indonesian archipelago. The floor is elevated to keep out damp and allow air circulation. Tuyul homes in Kampung Padalarang are *rumah panggung* of teak, with woven bamboo walls, steep thatched roofs, and east-facing verandas.

**Singgasana** (sing-gah-SAH-nah) -- A throne; the elevated seat of a king in the formal courts of Mataram, Sriwijaya, and other kingdoms. Notably absent from the Great Council, where Prabu Siliwangi sits on a tikar mat at the same level as every other delegate.

## Clothing and Textiles

**Baju** (BAH-joo) -- A shirt or blouse; general term for an upper garment. Kian Santang wears a "clean white baju" to the Great Council.

**Batik** (BAH-tik) -- A textile art form of Indonesia in which patterns are created by applying wax to cloth and dyeing it, the waxed areas resisting the dye. Batik patterns carry deep symbolic meaning. *Batik tulis* (hand-drawn batik) is the highest form of the art. Nyai Roro Kidul's kain is batik tulis "patterned by the sea itself." The *megamendung* pattern of Pasundan -- clouds layered upon clouds in gradations of blue -- symbolizes patience and wisdom.

**Dodot** (DOH-doht) -- A large formal cloth wrapped around the body in elaborate folds, worn by Javanese royalty and high nobility. Gajahmada wears a dodot of crimson silk.

**Iket** (EE-ket) -- A Sundanese headcloth, a square of cloth folded and tied around the head in a distinctive style. A traditional pre-Islamic headcovering worn by men across the Pasundan highlands. Prabu Siliwangi wears "the simple iket of a Pasundan nobleman." Ki Sunda wears an iket with the dignity of a village elder.

**Kain** (kah-EEN) -- A lower garment; a length of cloth wrapped around the waist and extending to the ankles. The specific wrapping style varies by region and occasion.

**Kebaya** (keh-BAH-yah) -- A traditional fitted blouse-dress worn by women throughout Indonesia, typically of sheer fabric, fastened in front with brooches (*kerongsang*). Nyai Roro Kidul wears a kebaya of sea-green silk.

**Kerongsang** (keh-RONG-sahng) -- Ornamental brooches used to fasten the front of a *kebaya*. Nyai Roro Kidul's kerongsang are gold, wrought in the shapes of seahorses and nautilus shells.

**Kuluk** (KOO-look) -- A formal headdress worn by Javanese royalty and high officials, typically a tall, structured headpiece of cloth and metalwork. Gajahmada wears a golden kuluk.

**Sarong / Sarung** (SAH-roong) -- A large tube of cloth worn wrapped around the lower body, common across Indonesia and Southeast Asia. Kian Santang wears a sarong of dark blue cotton "knotted at the waist in the Sundanese fashion."

**Selendang** (seh-LEN-dahng) -- A long, wide scarf or shawl draped over one shoulder or across the body. Dewi Anjani wears a selendang over her silver armor.

## Nature and Flora

**Bakau** (BAH-cow) -- Mangrove; the coastal trees that grow in salt water, their tangled root systems creating vital coastal ecosystems. Found along the northern coast of Java.

**Beringin / Waringin** (beh-RING-in / wah-RING-in) -- The banyan tree (*Ficus benjamina*), sacred in Indonesian culture, associated with spirits, wisdom, and the connection between the visible and invisible worlds. Eyang Guru Batara's staff is carved from *waringin* wood. A great banyan tree stands at one end of the village common in Kampung Padalarang.

**Durian** (DOO-ree-ahn) -- The large, spiky, pungent tropical fruit renowned for its powerful smell and rich, custard-like flesh. Beloved and controversial in equal measure.

**Kelapa** (keh-LAH-pah) -- Coconut; the ubiquitous tropical palm whose fruit, water, milk, oil, and fiber are used in virtually every aspect of Indonesian life.

**Melati** (meh-LAH-tee) -- Jasmine; the national flower of Indonesia, a symbol of purity, grace, and simplicity. Its perfume drifts through the villages of Tanah Sunda "in the early mornings before the mist has fully lifted."

**Meranti** (meh-RAHN-tee) -- A genus of tropical hardwood trees (Dipterocarpaceae), the great trees of the Indonesian rainforest. Meranti wood is prized for construction and furniture.

**Pandanus** (pahn-DAH-noos) -- A tropical plant with long, spiny leaves used for weaving mats, baskets, and roof thatch. Pandanus mats are laid for the Seren Taun feast.

**Rambutan** (rahm-BOO-tahn) -- A tropical fruit with a hairy red shell and sweet, translucent white flesh. Grown in Tuyul gardens.

**Salak** (SAH-lahk) -- Snake fruit; a tropical fruit with a brown, scaly skin (resembling snakeskin) and sweet-tart, crisp flesh. Native to Indonesia.

**Teak / Jati** (JAH-tee) -- The tropical hardwood tree prized for its strength, durability, and beauty. Teak is the primary building material for Tuyul *rumah panggung* and for the pillars of the grand pendopo at Dieng.

## Mythology and Creatures

**Buta** (BOO-tah) -- In Indonesian mythology, fearsome ogre-like beings of great size and malevolent disposition. In this novel, the Buta are the monstrous servants of Raja Iblis, equivalent to Tolkien's Orcs.

**Dewi Sri** (DEH-wee SREE) -- The goddess of rice and fertility in Javanese and Sundanese mythology, revered by farmers as the giver of abundance. The Tuyul give thanks to Dewi Sri at every harvest.

**Garuda** (gah-ROO-dah) -- The great mythical eagle-bird, mount of the god Vishnu in Hindu mythology, and the national symbol of Indonesia. A creature of immense power and majesty.

**Genderuwo** (gen-deh-ROO-woh) -- In Javanese folklore, a large, dark, hairy spirit of malevolent intent. In this novel, the Genderuwo are cunning subterranean creatures inhabiting the deep tunnels of Gunung Sewu.

**Harimau Siluman** (hah-REE-mow see-LOO-mahn) -- Spectral tiger-spirits; phantom tigers that serve as mounts for the Leak. In Indonesian folklore, the *harimau siluman* is a shape-shifting tiger spirit. They are "creatures of open darkness, not the enclosed dark of stone."

**Leak** (LEH-ahk) -- In Balinese mythology, shape-shifting witches and dark spirits of fearsome power. In this novel, the Leak are the nine corrupted kings, transformed by shadow-keris into wraith-beings bound to Raja Iblis's will.

**Naga** (NAH-gah) -- A serpent-dragon of immense size and power, drawn from Hindu-Buddhist and Indonesian mythology. *Naga Basukih* -- the great fire-serpent dwelling in the deepest caverns of Gunung Sewu, a creature for whom solid stone is as insubstantial as water.

**Pohon Keramat** (POH-hon keh-RAH-maht) -- A sacred tree; an ancient tree believed to be inhabited by spirits or to serve as a gateway between worlds. Often a *beringin* (banyan) tree, marked with offerings at its roots.

**Raja Iblis** (RAH-jah IB-lees) -- Literally "King of Devils" or "Demon King." The Shadow King, the ancient enemy, the great darkness of this novel. Drawn from the Javanese tradition of demon-kings in wayang and the broader Nusantara concept of malevolent spiritual overlords.

## Social Concepts and Customs

**Gotong royong** (GOH-tong ROH-yong) -- The Indonesian principle of communal mutual aid; the tradition of working together for the benefit of the community. Gotong royong is not merely a practice but a deeply held value -- the belief that shared labor and shared responsibility are the foundations of a good society. The Tuyul practice gotong royong in their communal planting and harvesting.

**Kampung** (KAHM-poong) -- A village or neighborhood; the basic unit of Indonesian community life. *Kampung Padalarang* is Kian Santang's home village. In the Sunda Kuna dialogue of this novel, characters use the Old Sundanese term *lembur* for village, which is attested in pre-Islamic manuscripts and remains in use in modern Sundanese. See *Lembur*.

**Lembur** (LEM-boor) -- Village (Old Sundanese/Sunda Kuna). The attested pre-Islamic term for a village or settlement, used in Sundanese dialogue throughout this novel in place of the Indonesian *kampung*. Lembur carries connotations of rootedness, home ground, and communal belonging that go beyond mere geography.

**Musyawarah** (moo-shah-WAH-rah) -- *See Sabha.* The modern Indonesian term for communal deliberation and consensus-building, derived from Arabic. In this novel's pre-Islamic setting, the Old Sundanese/Sanskrit term *sabha* is used instead.

**Sabha** (SAH-bah) -- Assembly, council, or deliberation (Old Sundanese/Sanskrit). The formal gathering of leaders, elders, and representatives to discuss matters of communal importance. From Sanskrit *sabha* (assembly hall). In sabha, all voices carry equal weight; the process values collective wisdom over individual authority. The Great Council at Dieng is conducted as a sabha: "The farmer spoke as freely as the prince. The question was not who held power, but what wisdom the gathering could produce." Replaces the modern Indonesian *musyawarah* (from Arabic) in this pre-Islamic setting.

**Sawah** (SAH-wah) -- A wet rice paddy; the flooded terraces in which rice is cultivated. The sawah is the foundation of Indonesian agricultural civilization and the heart of Tuyul life.

**Seren Taun** (SEH-ren TAH-oon) -- The Sundanese annual harvest festival, a day of thanksgiving, feasting, music, dance, and communal celebration marking the end of the rice harvest. The opening chapter's festival in Kampung Padalarang is Seren Taun.

**Sumpah Palapa** (SOOM-pah PAH-lah-pah) -- The legendary oath sworn by the historical Gajahmada, prime minister of the Majapahit Empire, vowing not to taste *palapa* (spices/luxury) until all the islands of Nusantara were united under Majapahit rule. In this novel, the Sumpah Palapa resonates as a symbol of Gajahmada's ambition -- both noble and dangerous.

**Tikar** (TEE-kahr) -- A woven mat, typically of pandanus or bamboo, used for sitting, sleeping, and dining throughout Indonesia. The Tuyul elders sit on tikar mats to drink *wedang* and trade stories. Tikar mats are laid for the *sabha* at Dieng.

## Maritime and Transport

**Jukung** (JOO-koong) -- A traditional outrigger canoe, used for fishing and coastal transport throughout the Indonesian archipelago. Illustrated on the novel's map in the coastal waters.

**Pinisi** (pee-NEE-see) -- A traditional Indonesian two-masted sailing ship, originally built by the Bugis and Makassar peoples of South Sulawesi. The pinisi is one of the great sailing vessels of maritime Southeast Asia, capable of long ocean voyages. Shown in the harbor of Anyer and along the map's sea routes.

## Sunda Kuna Warrior and Material Terms

**Adiwira** (ah-dee-WEE-rah) -- Great hero; supreme warrior. A compound of *adi* (supreme, foremost) and *wira* (hero). Used as both a title and a descriptor for the mightiest champions. Replaces the modern Indonesian *pahlawan* in this novel's pre-Islamic register.

**Ayu** (AH-yoo) -- Beautiful, lovely (Old Sundanese/Sanskrit). The standard Old Sundanese term for feminine beauty, replacing the modern *cantik*. Carries connotations of grace and refinement.

**Kancana** (kahn-CHAH-nah) -- Gold (Old Sundanese/Sanskrit). The formal and poetic term for gold, used in place of the common *emas*. From Sanskrit *kancana*. *Kancana* conveys the sacredness and spiritual significance of gold in Hindu-Buddhist culture.

**Lalita** (lah-LEE-tah) -- Graceful, charming, elegant (Sanskrit/Old Sundanese). A more nuanced term for beauty, emphasizing grace and charm rather than mere physical attractiveness.

**Manik** (MAH-nik) -- Gem, jewel, precious stone (Old Sundanese/Sanskrit). Used alongside *ratna* as the Sunda Kuna vocabulary for precious stones. From Sanskrit *mani*. Found in the Old Sundanese text *Bujangga Manik*, where it forms part of the protagonist's name.

**Ratna** (RAHT-nah) -- Gem, jewel (Sanskrit). The formal Sanskrit-derived term for precious stones, used in place of the common *permata*. In Hindu-Buddhist tradition, ratna are among the sacred offerings.

**Wira** (WEE-rah) -- Hero, warrior, brave one (Old Sundanese/Sanskrit). The standard Old Sundanese term for a hero or courageous fighter. From Sanskrit *vira*. Used in the novel as both a noun ("a wira of Pasundan") and an adjective ("wira in battle"). See also *Adiwira*.

**Yoda** (YOH-dah) -- Soldier, warrior (Old Sundanese). An attested Old Sundanese term for a common soldier or fighting man, used in place of the modern *prajurit*. Distinct from *wira* (hero) or *hulujurit* (commander), *yoda* denotes the rank-and-file warrior who forms the backbone of an army.

## Battle Formations of Nusantara

*Classical Nusantara military formations drawn from Old Javanese and Old Sundanese military treatises, used in the combat scenes of this novel.*

**Babah buhaya** (BAH-bah boo-HAH-yah) -- Crocodile jaw formation. An enveloping pincer maneuver in which two flanking forces close around an enemy like the jaws of a crocodile, trapping them between converging lines. Used for ambush and encirclement.

**Gagak sangkur** (GAH-gak SAHNG-koor) -- Crow formation. A loose, opportunistic formation in which warriors spread out like a flock of crows, attacking from multiple angles simultaneously and withdrawing before the enemy can mass against them. Suited to irregular terrain and guerrilla warfare.

**Garudabihwa** (gah-roo-dah-BIH-wah) -- Eagle formation. A broad-winged battle formation modeled on the spread wings of the Garuda. The center holds firm while the flanks sweep outward and forward to enfold the enemy. The most prestigious and aggressive of the classical formations, associated with royal armies.

**Kidang sumeka** (kee-DAHNG soo-MEH-kah) -- Deer formation. A swift, mobile formation emphasizing speed and evasion, designed for rapid strikes and retreats. Warriors in kidang sumeka formation move with the fleetness and agility of deer, striking and withdrawing before the enemy can respond.

**Lemah mrewasa** (LEH-mah mreh-WAH-sah) -- Swift field formation. A rapid-deployment battle array designed for open terrain, emphasizing speed of maneuver and the ability to shift formations quickly in response to changing battlefield conditions.

**Makarabihwa** (mah-kah-rah-BIH-wah) -- Crown or sea-creature formation. Named after the *makara*, the mythical sea creature of Hindu-Buddhist iconography. A formation anchored by a strong center ("crown") with flexible, sweeping flanks that can curl inward like the tail of a makara.

**Merak simper** (MEH-rahk SIM-per) -- Peacock formation. A display formation that conceals strength behind beauty -- warriors arranged in a fan-shaped array that appears ceremonial but can snap into combat readiness with devastating speed. Named for the peacock's ability to transform from beauty to aggression in an instant.

**Singhabihwa** (sing-hah-BIH-wah) -- Tiger formation. A compact, powerful battle formation modeled on the crouching tiger -- concentrated force held in reserve, then unleashed in a single devastating charge. Emphasizes shock and raw power over maneuver.

## Kawih (Old Sundanese Song Forms)

*Classical Old Sundanese song classifications used in this novel, distinct from the Javanese pupuh forms. Kawih are the indigenous Sundanese musical tradition, predating the adoption of Javanese pupuh meters.*

**Kawih bwatuha** (KAH-wih bwah-TOO-hah) -- Ancient songs; the oldest stratum of Sundanese vocal music, believed to predate the kingdoms. Kawih bwatuha carry the melodies and words of the deep past, songs of creation, of the first ancestors, of the shaping of the land. They are sung at the most sacred ceremonies and at moments when connection to the ancient past is needed.

**Kawih lalanguan** (KAH-wih lah-lah-NGOO-ahn) -- Pleasure songs, entertainment songs. Lighter compositions meant for enjoyment, feasting, and communal celebration. Kawih lalanguan are sung at harvest festivals, weddings, and gatherings, filling the air with joy and warmth.

**Kawih sasambatan** (KAH-wih sah-sahm-BAH-tahn) -- Lament songs, songs of sorrow. Mournful compositions sung in times of grief, loss, and mourning. The kawih sasambatan give voice to the pain that words alone cannot express, carrying sorrow on melody so that it might be shared and, in sharing, eased.

**Kawih sisindiran** (KAH-wih see-sin-DEE-rahn) -- Allegorical songs, songs of hidden meaning. Compositions that use metaphor, wordplay, and indirection to convey messages that cannot be spoken plainly -- political commentary, social critique, romantic overtures, and philosophical puzzles wrapped in the guise of simple melody.

## Batik and Textile Motifs

*Traditional textile patterns referenced in this novel, drawn from the batik and weaving traditions of the Pasundan Highlands and broader Nusantara.*

**Boeh siang** (BOH-eh SEE-ahng) -- Daylight cloth; a sacred textile woven for ceremonial and funerary purposes. *Boeh siang* is white or pale cloth associated with purity, the transition between worlds, and sacred ritual. In Old Sundanese tradition, boeh siang accompanies the dead and marks the boundary between the visible and invisible realms.

**Hujan riris** (HOO-jahn REE-ris) -- Drizzle pattern; a fine, delicate batik motif of tiny diagonal lines suggesting gentle rain falling across the cloth. Evokes the mountain climate of the Pasundan Highlands, where a light drizzle (*riris*) drifts through the terraced valleys.

**Jayanti** (jah-YAHN-tee) -- Victory pattern; a batik motif of interlocking geometric forms symbolizing triumph and divine blessing. From Sanskrit *jayanti* (victorious). Worn by warriors before battle and by rulers on ceremonial occasions.

**Kembang muncang** (KEM-bahng MOON-chahng) -- Candlenut flower motif; a batik pattern depicting the small white flowers of the candlenut tree (*kemiri*), a plant sacred in Sundanese agricultural tradition. Worn in daily life and at harvest celebrations.

**Poleng rengganis** (POH-leng reng-GAH-nis) -- The checkered pattern of Rengganis; a distinctive black-and-white or multi-colored checked cloth associated with the legendary Sundanese princess Rengganis. Poleng patterns represent the duality of existence -- light and dark, sacred and profane, order and chaos held in balance.

**Surat awi** (SOO-raht AH-wee) -- Bamboo-script pattern; a batik motif of fine parallel lines and geometric forms inspired by the appearance of inscriptions carved into bamboo strips (*prasasti awi*). Evokes the literary tradition of the Pasundan Highlands.

## Other Terms

**Elang** (EH-lahng) -- Eagle; specifically, the Javan hawk-eagle, a majestic bird of prey. An elang circles the peaks above Dieng during the Great Council, its cry barely audible in the grey sky.

**Jembatan Batu** (jem-BAH-tahn BAH-too) -- Stone bridge. The great natural stone bridge spanning the chasm in the caves of Gunung Sewu, where Eyang Guru Batara makes his final stand against the Naga Basukih.

**Kecamatan** (keh-chah-MAH-tahn) -- An administrative sub-district; a unit of local government in Indonesia. Used humorously by Cepot to describe the size of the Naga Basukih: "a fire-snake the size of a kecamatan."

**Kretek** (KREH-tek) -- The distinctive Indonesian clove cigarette, named for the crackling sound the cloves make when they burn. Note: while the clove trade is ancient in Nusantara (cloves originate from Maluku), the kretek cigarette is a modern invention. In this novel's 8th-9th century setting, cloves appear as a trade spice and incense ingredient rather than as a smoking product.

**Lontar** (LOHN-tahr) -- Palm-leaf manuscripts; the traditional writing medium of Indonesia before the introduction of paper. Dried leaves of the *lontar* (palmyra) palm are cut into strips and inscribed with a stylus, the incisions darkened with soot. Kian Santang's passion for reading lontar manuscripts sets him apart from other Tuyul and connects him to the wider world.

**Nusantara** (noo-SAHN-tah-rah) -- The Indonesian archipelago; literally "the islands between" (from Old Javanese *nusa* "island" + *antara* "between/other"). The term encompasses all the islands of Indonesia and, historically, the broader Malay world. In this novel, Nusantara is the name of the fantasy world itself.

**Pergi** (PER-gee) -- Go. The single word spoken by Eyang Guru Batara as he falls into the chasm of Gunung Sewu, his final command to the fellowship.

## Sunda Kuna Grammar Notes

*A guide to the Old Sundanese (Sunda Kuna) grammatical patterns that appear in the Sundanese dialogue and narration of this novel. These patterns are drawn from attested Old Sundanese manuscripts, principally the Sanghyang Siksa Kandang Karesian, Bujangga Manik, Carita Parahyangan, and the Amanat Galunggung. They differ from modern Sundanese in systematic ways that reflect the older structure of the language.*

### VS (Verb-Subject) Word Order

Old Sundanese frequently places the verb before the subject, particularly in formal declarations, prayers, and dramatic pronouncements. Modern Sundanese generally follows SV (subject-verb) order. In this novel, VS order appears in approximately 30-40% of Sundanese phrases, concentrated in the most formal and solemn passages.

*Example:* "Mawa boga kami" (literally "Carries provisions we") rather than the modern "Kami mawa boga" ("We carry provisions").

### The -in- Infix (Passive/Completed Action)

The *-in-* infix, inserted after the first consonant of a verb root, marks passive voice or completed action. This is one of the most distinctive features of Old Sundanese grammar, shared with Old Javanese and other Austronesian languages.

| Form | Root | Meaning |
|------|------|---------|
| **ginawé** | gawé (to make) | was made, was crafted |
| **tinangtu** | tangtu (to determine) | was determined, was decided |
| **pinareng** | pareng (to permit) | was permitted, was granted |
| **sinembah** | sembah (to honor) | was honored, was revered |
| **dinarmaan** | darma (to gift) | was gifted, was bestowed |
| **tinulis** | tulis (to write) | was written, was inscribed |

### The -um- Infix (Active/Ongoing Action)

The *-um-* infix, inserted after the first consonant of a verb root, marks active voice or ongoing action. It suggests action in progress, habitual action, or a state of becoming.

| Form | Root | Meaning |
|------|------|---------|
| **gumanti** | ganti (to change) | changing, replacing |
| **sumebar** | sebar (to scatter) | spreading, scattering |
| **cumeluk** | celuk (to call) | calling out, crying out |
| **tumuwuh** | tuwuh (to grow) | growing, flourishing |
| **lumaku** | laku (to walk) | walking, traveling |
| **lumampah** | lampah (to journey) | journeying, proceeding |

### The ma Particle (Clause Marker)

The particle *ma* serves as a clause marker in Old Sundanese, often appearing between elements of a sentence to link clauses or emphasize what follows. It has no direct equivalent in modern Sundanese or English.

*Examples:*
- "Aya ma nu..." -- "There are those who..." (the *ma* adds emphasis and formality)
- "Lamun ma..." -- "If indeed..." (the *ma* strengthens the conditional)

### The -keun Suffix (Causative)

The *-keun* suffix creates causative verbs -- verbs that mean "to cause (something) to happen" or "to make (something) be done." This suffix survives in modern Sundanese but appears more frequently in Old Sundanese texts.

*Examples:*
- **warahkeun** -- to teach, to convey knowledge (from *warah*, teaching)
- **tuliskeun** -- to cause to be written, to have inscribed
- **hayukeun** -- to enliven, to make vibrant
- **jayakeun** -- to make victorious

### Mulah (Prohibition)

The word *mulah* functions as the Old Sundanese prohibition marker, equivalent to the modern Sundanese *ulah* or *tong* ("do not," "must not"). It carries a stronger, more absolute quality than its modern successors.

*Example:* "Mulah ragrag!" -- "Do not fall!" / "You must not fall!"

# PUPUH AND POETIC FORMS

*The classical sung poetry of Java and Sunda, integral to the musical and spiritual fabric of this novel.*

---

In the oral and literary traditions of Java and Sunda, the **pupuh** is a form of sung poetry governed by strict metrical rules: each pupuh form prescribes the number of lines per stanza, the number of syllables per line, and the vowel sound that must end each line. But beyond their formal structure, the pupuh forms carry distinct emotional registers -- each is associated with a specific mood, occasion, and musical character. A skilled dalang, poet, or singer chooses the appropriate pupuh not merely for its sound but for its feeling, matching the form to the emotional truth of the moment.

In this novel, specific pupuh forms replace generic "tembang" references throughout, each chosen to reflect the emotional landscape of the scene in which it appears.

---

### Sinom
**(SEE-nohm)**

**Emotional register:** Wisdom, teaching, reflection, maturity
**Musical character:** Gentle and measured, with a flowing melodic line that suggests the unhurried pace of instruction and contemplation. Sinom carries the quality of morning light -- clear, warm, gradually illuminating.
**Used in the novel:** Teaching scenes between Eyang Guru and the fellowship; Prabu Siliwangi's counsel to Kian Santang; moments of reflection and the transmission of wisdom from elder to youth.

---

### Kinanti
**(kee-NAHN-tee)**

**Emotional register:** Hope, longing, anticipation, the ache of journeying
**Musical character:** Gently rising and falling, with a melodic contour that suggests movement toward something desired but not yet reached. Kinanti is the sound of a traveler who can see the mountain but has not yet climbed it -- hopeful, yearning, sustained by faith.
**Used in the novel:** Journey scenes; moments of hope amid hardship; the fellowship's passages through unknown territory; Raden Wijaya's unspoken longing for Dyah Pitaloka and the restoration of his kingdom.

---

### Asmarandana
**(ahs-mah-rahn-DAH-nah)**

**Emotional register:** Love, beauty, romance, tenderness
**Musical character:** Melodic and sweet, with the quality of a love letter set to music. Asmarandana is the most intimate of the pupuh forms -- its sound is the sound of longing fulfilled, of beauty recognized and cherished.
**Used in the novel:** Scenes of beauty and tenderness; the Bidadari sanctuary at Dieng; moments of connection between Raden Wijaya and Dyah Pitaloka; the luminous landscapes of Nusantara rendered in their full beauty.

---

### Dangdanggula
**(dahng-dahng-GOO-lah)**

**Emotional register:** Grandeur, majesty, great occasions, coronation and triumph
**Musical character:** Expansive, noble, and stately, with a broad melodic range that suggests open spaces and high vistas. Dangdanggula is the pupuh of kings and great events -- its sound fills the air the way sunlight fills a valley.
**Used in the novel:** The Great Council at Dieng; moments of revelation and grandeur; the description of great kingdoms and their courts; scenes where the full scope and majesty of Nusantara is invoked.

---

### Durma
**(DOOR-mah)**

**Emotional register:** Battle, conflict, war, fierce determination
**Musical character:** Driving and martial, with a rhythmic intensity that suggests the clash of weapons and the surge of battle. Durma is aggressive, forceful, and relentless -- the pupuh of the warrior in the heat of combat.
**Used in the novel:** Battle scenes; the attack at Ratu Boko; combat in the caves of Gunung Sewu; Eyang Guru's confrontation with the Naga Basukih; any scene where violence and conflict dominate.

---

### Mijil
**(MEE-jil)**

**Emotional register:** Grief, loss, farewell, sorrow
**Musical character:** Slow, plaintive, and deeply sorrowful, with descending melodic phrases that seem to drain away like water seeping into earth. Mijil is the sound of tears that have been wept past the point of weeping -- grief that has become quiet, profound, and enduring.
**Used in the novel:** The fall of Eyang Guru Batara; the fellowship's mourning; scenes of loss and farewell; the weight of sacrifice recognized and honored.

---

### Pangkur
**(PAHNG-koor)**

**Emotional register:** Determination, resolve, oath-taking, setting forth
**Musical character:** Strong, forward-moving, and purposeful, with a driving rhythm that suggests marching feet and clenched fists. Pangkur is the pupuh of the vow -- the moment when doubt is set aside and the decision to act is made irrevocable.
**Used in the novel:** The fellowship's oath at Dieng; moments of determination and resolve; Kian Santang's decision to bear the Keris Murka to Krakatau; Gajahmada's Sumpah Palapa.

---

### Related Poetic Forms

**Kidung** (KEE-doong) -- An Old Javanese lyric poem, less formally structured than the pupuh but carrying deep emotional resonance. Kidung are sung in Middle Javanese and often deal with themes of love, nature, and spiritual longing. In this novel, kidung are sung by the Bidadari in their mountain sanctuaries -- songs of deep time, of memory stretching back before the first kingdoms, rendered in a language older than the courts that later formalized it.

**Kakawin** (kah-KAH-win) -- A formal poetic form derived from Sanskrit metrical patterns (*vrtta*), written in Old Javanese (Kawi). Kakawin are the highest literary form of classical Javanese civilization, composed in praise of kings, gods, and great events. The *Nagarakretagama*, the great kakawin of Majapahit, is the most famous example. In this novel, kakawin verses are inscribed on the stone reliefs of Dieng and chanted at the most solemn ceremonial moments.

# LANGUAGE REGISTERS OF NUSANTARA

*The speech levels and social registers that shape dialogue throughout this novel.*

---

The languages of Nusantara are not monolithic -- they are layered, stratified, and exquisitely sensitive to context. Both Sundanese (basa Sunda) and Javanese (basa Jawa) employ elaborate systems of speech registers that encode social relationships directly into the structure of language itself. The words one uses to say "you," "eat," "go," or "speak" change depending on who is speaking, who is being addressed, and the formality of the occasion. To speak in the wrong register is not merely a grammatical error; it is a social transgression -- an act of disrespect or presumption that carries real consequences.

**Important note on historical register:** The Sundanese dialogue in this novel uses **Sunda Kuna** (Old Sundanese) vocabulary and grammar, drawn from pre-Islamic manuscripts of the 8th-15th centuries such as the *Sanghyang Siksa Kandang Karesian*, *Bujangga Manik*, *Carita Parahyangan*, and the *Amanat Galunggung*. This means that pronouns, greetings, and key vocabulary differ from modern Sundanese. For example, the first-person formal pronoun is *ingwang* (not the modern *abdi*, which derives from Arabic), the standard greeting is *rahayu* (not *sampurasun*), and village is *lembur* (not *kampung*). The four-tier register system described below still applies, but the specific words within each tier reflect the archaic forms appropriate to the novel's 8th-9th century setting. See the **Sunda Kuna Grammar Notes** section and the **Sunda Kuna Pronouns and Greetings** glossary section for detailed reference.

In this novel, these speech registers are used to deepen character, establish relationships, and mark shifts in emotional intensity. The register in which a character speaks tells the reader as much about the scene as the words themselves.

---

## Basa Sunda: The Four Tiers of Sundanese Speech

Sundanese employs four principal speech registers, ranging from the roughest and most informal to the most elevated and sacred:

### Basa Kasar (Rough Speech)
**(BAH-sah KAH-sahr)**

The lowest and most unrefined register, used to express anger, contempt, or raw emotion. Basa kasar is the language of the battlefield, of curses and threats, of creatures who have no regard for social niceties. In this novel, the Buta speak in basa kasar -- their Sundanese, when they use it at all, is crude, broken, and deliberately offensive. Characters who normally speak in higher registers may drop into basa kasar in moments of extreme anger or distress, and the shift is always shocking.

*Example from the novel:* A Buta snarling *"Sia! Geura ka dieu!"* ("You! Get over here!") -- using *sia*, the most disrespectful form of "you."

### Basa Loma (Informal Speech)
**(BAH-sah LOH-mah)**

The everyday register used among peers, friends, and family members of equal status. Basa loma is warm, familiar, and relaxed -- the language of village life, shared meals, and comfortable companionship. The four Tuyul companions -- Kian Santang, Bagus, Cepot, and Dawala -- speak to one another in basa loma throughout the novel, and the warmth of their friendship is carried in the register itself.

*Example from the novel:* Cepot saying *"Hayu kami indit, lur!"* ("Come on, let's go, friend!") -- using *kami* (we/us, Old Sundanese) and *lur* (friend/sibling, casual).

### Basa Lemes (Refined Speech)
**(BAH-sah LEH-mes)**

The polite, refined register used when addressing elders, superiors, or persons of higher social standing. Basa lemes expresses respect, deference, and propriety. Characters use basa lemes when speaking to Eyang Guru, to Prabu Siliwangi, or to any figure of authority. The shift from basa loma to basa lemes when a young Tuyul addresses an elder is immediate and instinctive -- a mark of upbringing and character.

*Example from the novel:* Kian Santang addressing Prabu Siliwangi: *"Nembah, Kangjeng Prabu. Ingwang bade ngalaksanakeun tugas ieu."* ("I bow in gratitude, Your Majesty. I will carry out this duty.") -- using *ingwang* (I, Old Sundanese formal), *nembah* (respectful bow/thanks, Old Sundanese), *Kangjeng* (exalted), and formal verb forms.

### Basa Luhur (Highest Speech)
**(BAH-sah LOO-hoor)**

The most elevated register, reserved for addressing or speaking about royalty, the divine, and the sacred. Basa luhur is the language of the Bidadari, of sacred ceremonies, of royal decrees, and of prayers. When Dewi Anjani speaks, her Sundanese carries the cadence of basa luhur -- luminous, precise, and weighted with centuries of usage. Basa luhur is also the register in which the ancient mantras and sacred invocations are rendered.

*Example from the novel:* Dewi Anjani in council: *"Sang Hyang Widhi parantos maparin pituduh. Urang kedah tumut kana dharma."* ("The Supreme Being has given guidance. We must follow the dharma.") -- using *parantos* (has, highest form), *maparin* (to give, exalted), and *kedah* (must, formal).

---

## Basa Jawa: The Three Tiers of Javanese Speech

Javanese employs three principal speech registers, each with its own complete vocabulary:

### Ngoko (Informal Javanese)
**(NGOH-koh)**

The base register of Javanese, used among close friends, family of equal age, and in situations where formality is unnecessary or inappropriate. Ngoko is also the language of the battlefield -- in combat, there is no time for politeness. Raden Wijaya speaks ngoko with fellow warriors, and the shift to ngoko in council signifies either deep familiarity or deliberate provocation.

*Example from the novel:* Raden Wijaya on the battlefield: *"Ayo, aja mundur! Perang iki durung rampung!"* ("Come on, don't retreat! This battle isn't finished!") -- using *aja* (don't, informal), *iki* (this, ngoko).

### Krama (Formal Javanese)
**(KRAH-mah)**

The middle register, used in formal situations, council speech, diplomacy, and when addressing persons of higher status. Krama replaces virtually every common word with a distinct formal equivalent -- the Javanese speaker must essentially know two complete vocabularies. At the Great Council of Dieng, the Javanese delegates speak in krama, and the formality of the register lends weight and gravity to their deliberations.

*Example from the novel:* A Javanese delegate at the Council: *"Kula badhe ngaturaken pamrayogi, Kangjeng Prabu."* ("I would like to offer a suggestion, Your Majesty.") -- using *kula* (I, formal), *badhe* (will, krama), *ngaturaken* (to offer, formal).

### Krama Inggil (Highest Javanese)
**(KRAH-mah ING-gil)**

The most elevated register, reserved for addressing royalty, speaking of the divine, and the most solemn ceremonial occasions. Krama inggil is the language of royal decrees, sacred ceremonies, and the speech of the highest-ranking nobles. In this novel, Gajahmada's formal pronouncements and the sacred ceremonies at the candi temples are rendered in krama inggil.

*Example from the novel:* A royal decree: *"Sang Nata Agung dhawuh, bilih Keris Murka kedah dipunsirnakaken."* ("The Great King commands that the Keris Murka must be destroyed.") -- using *Sang Nata Agung* (the Great King, highest), *dhawuh* (to command, krama inggil), *dipunsirnakaken* (to be destroyed, highest formal).

---

# LEGENDS AND FOLKLORE OF NUSANTARA

*The eleven traditional legends woven into the narrative of Persekutuan Keris.*

---

The stories of Nusantara are as numerous as its islands. The following legends, drawn from the oral and literary traditions of Java, Sunda, Bali, and the wider archipelago, have been woven into the fabric of this novel -- not as retellings, but as living echoes. Characters encounter these legends as part of their world's history, as warnings, as parallels to their own journeys, and as the deep cultural memory that gives Nusantara its soul.

---

### 1. Sangkuriang and Tangkuban Perahu
**Origin:** Sundanese (West Java)
**Referenced in:** Chapter 1

The legend of Sangkuriang tells of a young man who, through a tragic chain of events, unknowingly falls in love with his own mother, Dayang Sumbi. When Dayang Sumbi discovers the truth and sets him an impossible task -- to build a dam across the Citarum River and a great boat before dawn -- Sangkuriang nearly succeeds through supernatural power. Dayang Sumbi tricks him by creating the illusion of dawn, and Sangkuriang, enraged, kicks the unfinished boat upside down, creating the volcano Tangkuban Perahu ("The Overturned Boat"). The novel opens in the shadow of this volcano, and the legend serves as a reminder that unchecked passion and the refusal to accept truth can reshape the very landscape.

---

### 2. Timun Mas (The Golden Cucumber)
**Origin:** Javanese (Central Java)
**Referenced in:** Chapter 4

A childless widow receives a golden cucumber seed from a giant (Buta Ijo), promising to surrender the child that grows from it when the child turns seventeen. The daughter, Timun Mas, is given magical items by a hermit -- cucumber seeds, needles, salt, and shrimp paste -- which she uses to escape the pursuing giant, each item transforming into obstacles (a cucumber field, a bamboo forest, a sea, a bog of mud). The legend resonates in the novel as a parallel to escape and pursuit, and as a reminder that the smallest, most ordinary things -- seeds, salt, cleverness -- can overcome monstrous power.

---

### 3. Lutung Kasarung
**Origin:** Sundanese (West Java)
**Referenced in:** Chapters 5-6

A celestial being takes the form of a black lutung (leaf monkey) and descends to the mortal world to assist Purbasari, a princess unjustly exiled by her jealous elder sister Purbararang. The lutung guides, protects, and ultimately reveals his divine nature, and Purbasari is restored to her rightful place. In this novel, the legend is echoed through Dewi Anjani, whose celestial nature is concealed beneath a mortal guise, and whose guidance of the fellowship mirrors the lutung's guardianship of the wronged princess. The connection between divine intervention and humble disguise runs through both tales.

---

### 4. Jaka Tarub and the Bidadari
**Origin:** Javanese (Central Java)
**Referenced in:** Chapters 6-7

A young man named Jaka Tarub discovers celestial nymphs (Bidadari) bathing in a forest pool and steals the *selendang* (celestial shawl) of one, Nawang Wulan, preventing her from returning to heaven. She becomes his wife, but when she discovers the hidden shawl, she reclaims it and ascends back to the heavens, leaving behind their daughter. The legend informs the novel's portrayal of the Bidadari as beings caught between worlds -- celestial yet drawn to the mortal realm, luminous yet capable of loss. The *selendang* as a symbol of identity and freedom recurs throughout.

---

### 5. Bawang Merah Bawang Putih (Red Onion, White Garlic)
**Origin:** Javanese/Malay
**Referenced in:** Chapter 9

Two stepsisters -- Bawang Putih (kind, gentle, hardworking) and Bawang Merah (cruel, lazy, deceitful) -- represent the eternal contrast between virtue and vice. Through trials and supernatural encounters, Bawang Putih is rewarded for her goodness while Bawang Merah is punished for her cruelty. In the novel, this legend serves as a parallel to the moral choices facing the fellowship -- the contrast between those who serve others and those who serve themselves, between the fellowship's selfless purpose and the Shadow King's selfish domination.

---

### 6. The Legend of Ratu Boko
**Origin:** Javanese (Central Java)
**Referenced in:** Chapter 8

The ruins of Ratu Boko, perched on a plateau south of Prambanan, are associated with legends of a giant king (Prabu Boko) and his daughter, and with battles between the forces of light and darkness. The historical ruins -- stone gateways, ceremonial pools, crumbled walls -- carry an atmosphere of fallen grandeur and ancient conflict. In the novel, the fellowship passes through these ruins, and the location becomes the site of a great battle, the legend of ancient conflict merging with the fellowship's present struggle.

---

### 7. Ajisaka and the Origin of Javanese Script
**Origin:** Javanese (Central Java)
**Referenced in:** Chapter 10

Ajisaka is the legendary figure who brought civilization, writing, and the Javanese script to Java. The legend tells of his two faithful servants, Dora and Sembada, whose mutual loyalty leads to mutual destruction -- each killing the other because neither will yield his master's command. From their tragedy, Ajisaka creates the Javanese alphabet, each syllable a memorial to the cost of blind obedience. In the novel, the legend is invoked as a meditation on loyalty, communication, and the danger of commands followed without understanding -- themes that resonate deeply with the fellowship's journey.

---

### 8. Calon Arang
**Origin:** Balinese/Javanese
**Referenced in:** Chapter 11

Calon Arang is the legendary widow-witch of Girah, a powerful practitioner of dark magic who unleashes plague and death upon a kingdom that has spurned her daughter. She is ultimately defeated by the sage Empu Bharadah through spiritual combat. The legend represents the dangers of sorcery turned to vengeance and the corruption of spiritual knowledge. In the novel, Calon Arang serves as a dark parallel to Empu Gandring -- both figures who turned sacred knowledge to destructive ends, both consumed by the very power they sought to wield.

---

### 9. Nyi Roro Kidul (The Queen of the Southern Sea)
**Origin:** Javanese/Sundanese
**Referenced in:** Chapters 14-16 (deepened)

The legend of Nyi Roro Kidul is among the most powerful and pervasive in Javanese and Sundanese tradition. In the deepened version used in this novel, the Queen of the Southern Sea is identified with the legend of Dewi Kadita -- a Sundanese princess cursed with a disfiguring skin disease who throws herself into the Indian Ocean and is transformed into the immortal queen of the sea realm. Her domain encompasses the entire southern coast of Java and extends into the enchanted depths of Ngarai Sianok. The novel honors the traditional prohibitions associated with her realm: the taboo against wearing green near the southern sea (green being her sacred color), the danger of swimming at the southern beaches, and the requirement that visitors to her domain show proper respect through offerings and humility. Her sea domain operates by its own rules, and those who enter it must abide by them.

---

### 10. Malin Kundang
**Origin:** Minangkabau (West Sumatra)
**Referenced in:** Chapters 21-23

Malin Kundang is the legendary ungrateful son -- a poor boy who goes to sea, makes his fortune, and returns as a wealthy merchant, only to deny and humiliate his aged mother when she comes to greet him at the harbor. His mother's curse turns him and his ship to stone, a monument to the consequences of betrayal and ingratitude. In the novel, the legend serves as a parallel to the theme of betrayal -- of those who turn their backs on their origins, their bonds, and their obligations in pursuit of power. The legend resonates with Gajahmada's fall and with the broader theme of loyalty tested by ambition.

---

### 11. Si Pahit Lidah (The Bitter Tongue)
**Origin:** Malay/Sumatran
**Referenced in:** Chapters 19-20

Si Pahit Lidah is the legend of a man whose words become reality -- whatever he speaks comes true, including curses spoken in anger. This terrible gift becomes a curse in itself, as careless words unleash consequences that cannot be undone. In the novel, the legend is invoked in connection with the power of speech, oaths, and the irrevocable nature of certain words once spoken. The Sumpah Palapa, Gajahmada's great oath, carries an echo of Si Pahit Lidah -- words of power that, once released, take on a life of their own.

# KINGDOMS OF NUSANTARA

## A Guide to the Historical Kingdoms Adapted in This Novel

---

### Pasundan (The Sundanese Kingdom)

**Historical basis:** The Sundanese people have inhabited the western portion of the island of Java since before recorded history, and their territory -- often called Tatar Sunda or Tanah Pasundan -- has been home to several historical kingdoms. The most notable is the **Kingdom of Sunda** (known from inscriptions dating to the 7th century CE), which was centered in the highlands around modern-day Bogor and Bandung. The Sundanese kingdom maintained its independence for centuries, distinguished by its Hindu-Buddhist culture, its agricultural prosperity, and its resistance to Javanese expansion from the east. The legendary King **Prabu Siliwangi** (historically associated with the 15th-century king Sri Baduga Maharaja of the Sunda Kingdom) is one of the most beloved figures in Sundanese tradition -- a wise, just, and semi-mythical ruler whose name is synonymous with Sundanese cultural identity. The Sundanese highlands are renowned for their extraordinary beauty: terraced rice fields, volcanic peaks, tea plantations, and a climate of perpetual mild warmth. Sundanese culture is known for its distinctive music (angklung, degung, kacapi suling), its performing arts (wayang golek), its martial tradition (pencak silat, particularly the Cimande school), and its cuisine (noted for fresh vegetables, sambal, and nasi timbel).

**In this novel:** Pasundan is the homeland of the Tuyul -- reimagined as the Sundanese highlands, the "Shire" of Nusantara. Kampung Padalarang, Kian Santang's village, sits in the highland terraces south of modern Bandung. Prabu Siliwangi is adapted as the wise king in exile at Dieng, the convener of the Great Council, the healer who tends to Kian Santang's wound. The Pasundan Highlands represent paradise -- the green, peaceful, abundant land that the fellowship fights to protect.

---

### Mataram (The Central Javanese Kingdom)

**Historical basis:** The name Mataram refers to several historical kingdoms in Central Java. The **Mataram Kingdom** of the 8th-10th centuries (sometimes called "Ancient Mataram" or "Hindu-Buddhist Mataram") was a powerful Central Javanese state that produced the great temple complexes of Barabudur and Prambanan. Its rulers, drawn from the Sailendra and Sanjaya dynasties, were patrons of art, architecture, and religion on a monumental scale. A later **Sultanate of Mataram** (16th-18th centuries) was the last major Javanese kingdom before colonial domination. The name Mataram evokes the golden age of Javanese civilization -- the era of great temples, refined court culture, sophisticated philosophical thought, and powerful kingship.

**In this novel:** Mataram is a fallen kingdom -- its glory diminished, its throne vacant, its people scattered. Raden Wijaya, the exiled prince and heir, wanders as a ranger, keeping alive the silat traditions and the honor of his lineage. His name is borrowed from the historical **Raden Wijaya** who founded the Majapahit Empire in 1293, but in this adaptation he is reimagined as the dispossessed prince who must reclaim his heritage. Mataram represents the theme of lost greatness awaiting restoration.

---

### Majapahit (The East Javanese Empire)

**Historical basis:** The **Majapahit Empire** (1293-1527 CE) was the largest and most powerful state in Indonesian history, an empire that at its zenith under Prime Minister **Gajah Mada** (the historical spelling) claimed suzerainty over much of maritime Southeast Asia -- from Sumatra to parts of Borneo, Bali, and beyond. Based near modern Mojokerto in East Java, Majapahit was a Hindu-Buddhist thalassocracy of extraordinary sophistication, producing the epic poem *Nagarakretagama*, codifying Javanese law and custom, and projecting military and cultural power across the archipelago. Gajah Mada himself is one of the towering figures of Indonesian history: a military genius, political strategist, and nation-builder whose **Sumpah Palapa** -- his oath to unite the archipelago -- is one of the most famous pronouncements in Indonesian heritage. Majapahit is often invoked as the historical precedent for Indonesian national unity.

**In this novel:** Majapahit is the great military power of Nusantara, proud and ambitious. Gajahmada (adapted spelling) serves as its representative in the fellowship -- a general of commanding presence, genuine courage, and dangerous ambition. His fall mirrors the tragedy of Tolkien's Boromir: a good man undone by the belief that power, if wielded by the right hands, can save the world. Majapahit represents the seductive danger of empire -- the noble ideal of unity corrupted by the desire to dominate.

---

### Sriwijaya (The Sumatran Maritime Empire)

**Historical basis:** The **Sriwijaya Empire** (7th-13th centuries CE) was a powerful Buddhist maritime state based on the island of Sumatra, centered near modern Palembang on the Musi River. Sriwijaya controlled the Strait of Malacca and the Sunda Strait, dominating the sea trade routes between China, India, and the Spice Islands. It was a center of Buddhist scholarship -- the Chinese monk I Tsing studied there in the 7th century and described it as a major center of learning with over a thousand monks. Sriwijaya's power was naval and commercial rather than territorial; it controlled trade through a network of tributary ports rather than through direct conquest of inland territories. The empire's decline in the 13th century, due to competition from Javanese kingdoms and the Chola dynasty of India, ended Sumatra's dominance of regional trade.

**In this novel:** Sriwijaya is the maritime empire -- its strength is ships and sailors, its knowledge is of tides and sea routes. The Sriwijayan envoy at the Great Council is "a lean, dark-skinned man with the tattoos of a seafarer marking his forearms in patterns of wave and wind." Sriwijaya represents the outward-looking, cosmopolitan spirit of Nusantara -- the tradition of navigation, trade, and connection that bound the islands together across vast distances of open sea.

---

### Kediri (The East Javanese Kingdom)

**Historical basis:** The **Kingdom of Kediri** (1042-1222 CE) was an East Javanese Hindu kingdom that succeeded the earlier Airlangga kingdom. Kediri was a center of literary and cultural achievement, producing major works of Old Javanese literature. The kingdom was known for its military strength and its horse-breeding traditions -- the plains of East Java, with their open grasslands and volcanic soil, were well suited to raising cavalry horses. Kediri fell when Raden Wijaya (the historical figure) overthrew its last king, Ken Arok, and established the Singhasari kingdom, which would later become Majapahit.

**In this novel:** Kediri is the "Kingdom of the Horse-Lords" -- a realm of open grasslands and mounted warriors, analogous to Tolkien's Rohan. The Kediri horseman at the Great Council sits "with his legs folded awkwardly beneath him, more accustomed to a saddle than a mat." Kediri represents the martial tradition of the Javanese plains -- swift, proud, and fiercely independent.

---

### Kutai (The East Kalimantan Kingdom)

**Historical basis:** The **Kingdom of Kutai** (approximately 4th century CE onward) is considered one of the oldest Hindu kingdoms in Indonesian history, located on the eastern coast of Kalimantan (Borneo) near the Mahakam River. Kutai is known primarily from a set of inscriptions (*yupa* stone pillars) written in Sanskrit, which describe the kingdom's ruler, Mulavarman, and his generous sacrifices. These are among the earliest written records in Indonesian history. Kutai represents the deep antiquity of Indonesian civilization and its early connections with Indian cultural and religious traditions.

**In this novel:** Kutai is mentioned as one of the distant kingdoms of Nusantara, representing the far eastern extent of the archipelago's civilized world. The Keris Murka was forged "to enslave every throne from the highlands of Pasundan to the distant shores of Kutai" -- Kutai marks the farthest boundary of the Shadow King's ambition. It represents the vastness of Nusantara, the sheer scale of what is at stake. Tumenggung Batu's Dayak heritage connects his bloodline to the ancient Kutai territories.

---

### Kalingga (The Central Javanese Buddhist Kingdom)

**Historical basis:** The **Kingdom of Kalingga** (also spelled Holing in Chinese sources) was an early Buddhist kingdom in Central Java, known from Chinese records dating to the 6th-7th centuries CE. The kingdom was ruled by a queen, **Sima** (or Shima), who was renowned for her fierce justice and her devotion to Buddhist law. Kalingga was an important center of Buddhist scholarship and maintained trade and diplomatic relations with China. Its exact location is debated, but most scholars place it in the northern coastal plain of Central Java, near modern Jepara or Pekalongan.

**In this novel:** Kalingga is referenced as one of the ancient kingdoms of Nusantara's past -- a Buddhist realm of learning and justice, now long vanished, its legacy preserved in the stone reliefs of the Dieng galleries and in the lontar manuscripts that scholars study. Kalingga represents the deep history of Nusantara, the layers upon layers of civilization that preceded the novel's present moment.

---

### Tarumanegara (The West Javanese Hindu Kingdom)

**Historical basis:** The **Kingdom of Tarumanegara** (approximately 4th-7th centuries CE) was one of the earliest Hindu kingdoms in western Java, centered in the lowlands near modern-day Bogor and Bekasi. Known from a series of stone inscriptions (including the famous Tugu and Ciaruteun inscriptions), Tarumanegara was ruled by King **Purnawarman**, who left carved footprints in stone along with Sanskrit inscriptions praising his rule. The kingdom controlled the trade routes of western Java and maintained connections with India. Tarumanegara is considered a predecessor of the later Sundanese kingdoms.

**In this novel:** Tarumanegara is invoked as the deep ancestral past of the Pasundan region -- the oldest known kingdom of western Java, whose stone inscriptions are among the earliest written records of Indonesian civilization. The carved stone galleries of Dieng include reliefs depicting Tarumanegara as one of the first human kingdoms, part of the long chronicle of Nusantara's history that Prabu Siliwangi narrates to Kian Santang. Tarumanegara represents origins -- the deep roots from which the tree of Sundanese and Javanese civilization grew.
