# BAGIAN I: JALAN NU LUNGKAWING

# Bab 1: Keberangkatan di Fajar Merah

## (Departure in the Red Dawn)

---

*Wanda anyar geus dibuka.*
*Layar dikembangkeun, blencong nyebrotkeun cahaya beureum.*
*Sang Dalang metik cempala, ngetrok kotak.*

*The new act opens.*
*The screen is unfurled, the lamp casts a red glow.*
*The Dalang strikes the wooden chest.*

---

The dawn over the Selat Sunda did not break; it bled.

It rose from the east not as a herald of new light but as a wound in the skin of the world, a sullen, clotting crimson that stained the low-hanging belly of the clouds. The sun itself was a stranger here, a dim and fevered eye peering through the perpetual shroud of volcanic ash that drifted from the southern horizon, where Krakatau -- the Fire-Mountain, the Forge of Malice -- breathed its grey poison into the stratosphere.

On the northernmost islet of the volcanic chain, a jagged splinter of basalt thrusting from the churning black waters, five figures stood in the shadow of their own victory, though it tasted like defeat. The air was thick with the copper tang of spilled blood and the sulfurous reek of the Buta Raksasa corpses that littered the rocks, their grey-green flesh already beginning to liquefy in the heat, returning to the corrupted earth from which they had been shaped.

Raden Wijaya wiped the blade of the Keris Nagasasra with a piece of silk torn from a dead enemy's sash. The metal, dark and rippled like the surface of a deep river, seemed to drink the light, its *pamor* patterns pulsing with a faint, rhythmic heat. He sheathed it, the click of wood on metal sounding unnaturally loud in the sudden silence.

He turned his gaze north. The coast of Jawa was a serrated line of darkness against the red sky, a wall of mangrove and mountain that promised no welcome.

"The wind changes," Dewi Anjani whispered.

The Bidadari warrior-princess stood poised on a pinnacle of rock, her silver armor catching the dull gleam of the dawn. Her skin, usually luminous with the starlight of her people, was dimmed by exhaustion, yet her beauty remained -- terrible and remote, like the beauty of a glacier or a storm. She did not look at the dead. Her ancient eyes, the color of amber preserved in ice, scanned the horizon with a focus that pierced the haze.

"*Bayu* from the south carries the scent of shadow," she said, her voice a melody of wind chimes, speaking the High Language. "The Shadow King knows we linger. He knows the *manik* has passed his gates."

"Let him know," Tumenggung Batu grunted. The Kurcaci chieftain sat heavily on a flat stone, his iron-shod boots resting in a puddle of black ichor. He began to unbuckle his battered breastplate, his thick, calloused fingers moving with surprising delicacy. "Let him chew on the knowledge that his elite guard lies rotting on a rock in the middle of nowhere. It might give him indigestion."

He looked up at Raden Wijaya, his craggy face etched with lines of fatigue that even stone-folk could not hide. "We cannot stay here, Prince. The tide is turning. When the water rises, the Naga Laut will come to feed on the dead. And they are not particular about whether their meat is cold or warm."

Raden Wijaya nodded. "*Inggih*. We leave now."

He looked to the two smallest members of their diminished company. Cepot and Dawala sat together near the water's edge, their feet dangling over the abyss. They looked impossibly small against the vast, indifferent backdrop of the smoking sea. Cepot was cleaning his short knife, his movements mechanical, his usually sharp, clever face stripped of its mask of wit. Dawala sat with his head on his knees, his shoulders shaking with a silent rhythm that might have been weeping or might have been the shivering of shock.

They were Tuyul of the highlands, children of the green earth and the gentle rain. They belonged in the sawah, under the shade of the banana trees, listening to the gamelan play in the village square. They did not belong here, in this landscape of fire and ash, where the sea boiled and the sky bled. And yet, they were here. Because Kian Santang was here. Because Bagus was here. Because friendship, once forged, is a chain stronger than the iron of any dungeon.

Raden Wijaya walked to them. He knelt, bringing his face level with theirs.

"Cepot," he said softly. "Dawala. Can you row?"

Cepot looked up. His eyes were red-rimmed, but dry. "Can *Aing* row?" He let out a short, sharp laugh that sounded like a dry branch snapping. "*Gust!* *Aing* can row, Raden. *Aing* can swim. *Aing* can fly if you give me enough *tuak* and a good reason. Just get us off this rock!"

"The current is strong," Raden Wijaya warned. "And the strait is full of eyes. We must be silent as the shadow of a cloud."

"We are Tuyul," Dawala said, lifting his head. His round face was pale, but his jaw was set with a stubbornness that reminded the prince of the stones of Dieng. "*Karuhun* watch over us... we know how to be small. We know how to not be seen."

They boarded the *prahu* -- a narrow, low-slung craft of dark teak that they had salvaged from the wreckage of the Buta landing party. It was a vessel of war, built for speed and silence, its prow carved in the likeness of a garuda's beak. Raden Wijaya took the tiller. Tumenggung Batu and Dewi Anjani took the rear oars, their strength a mismatch of earth and air. Cepot and Dawala took the front, their small arms gripping the wood with white-knuckled determination.

They pushed off. The obsidian sand grated against the hull, a sound like teeth grinding in a fever dream, and then they were afloat. The black water took them.

---

The crossing was a journey through a nightmare made of mist and salt. The Selat Sunda was no longer a waterway; it was a graveyard. The wreckage of centuries of trade and war drifted in the currents -- shattered hulls of Sriwijayan galleys, tangles of rigging that floated like drowned hair, bloated carcasses of sea-creatures killed by the toxins venting from the sea floor.

The mist thickened as they moved north. It was not a clean, white mist of the mountains. It was a yellow, sulfurous fog that clung to the skin and burned the throat. It distorted distance and sound, so that the slap of the waves against the hull sounded like footsteps following them, and the cry of a distant gull sounded like a scream.

Raden Wijaya steered by instinct and the faint, throbbing pull of the Keris Nagasasra, which seemed to yearn for the land of its ancestors. He kept his eyes on the water, watching for the telltale V-wake of a Naga Laut or the dorsal fin of a shark-demon.

"Silence," Dewi Anjani hissed. She stopped rowing, her paddle hovering inches above the water. "*Manusa*, something moves upon the waves."

They drifted in silence. The mist swirled around them, forming shapes that reached and grasped and dissolved.

Then, out of the yellow gloom, it appeared.

A ship.

But not a ship of wood and sail. It was a ruin, a ghost-hulk drifting on the tide. Its masts were broken jagged stumps. Its sails were tattered rags of black cloth that hung limp in the still air. And on its deck, motionless as statues, stood ranks of figures wrapped in grey shrouds.

They were dead. But they were standing.

"The Ship of the Lost," Tumenggung Batu rumbled, his voice a vibration in the deck. "Cursed sailors. Bound to the Shadow King's will, denied the peace of the sea floor."

The hulk drifted past them, silent as a tomb. The shrouded figures did not move, did not turn their heads. But Raden Wijaya felt the weight of their attention, a cold, pressureless touch on the back of his neck.

"*Duh Gusti...*" Raden Wijaya whispered, invoking the Lord in the high tongue. "Grant them *Moksa*."

They passed into the shadow of the ghost-ship, and for a moment, the world was cold. Then the mist swallowed it, and they were alone again.

"The coast," Cepot whispered. "*Gust!* Look."

Ahead, the fog was thinning. The black water gave way to a tangle of roots that rose from the sea like the skeletal fingers of a submerged forest. The mangrove swamps of Java.

The *bakau* trees were ancient and twisted, their roots arching high above the water to form a labyrinth of wooden cages. The canopy was dense and dark, blocking out the red light of the dawn. The air here was heavy with the smell of rot -- decaying vegetation, stagnant mud, and the deep, rich scent of life fermenting in the heat.

"We land here," Raden Wijaya said, guiding the *prahu* toward a narrow channel between two massive root systems. "We abandon the boat. We move inland on foot, under the cover of the canopy."

The keel scraped on a submerged root. The boat lurched.

"Steady!" Tumenggung Batu growled.

Suddenly, the water around them erupted.

It was not a creature of the deep. It was the roots themselves. Or things that looked like roots. Long, sinuous tentacles of wet wood and slime lashed out from the water, seizing the gunwales of the *prahu*.

"Ambush!" Dewi Anjani cried, her bow already singing. An arrow flashed, pinning a tentacle to the trunk of a tree. The wood screamed -- a high, human shriek that froze the blood.

These were not roots. They were *Siluman Bakau* -- mangrove demons, shape-shifters that dwelt in the liminal space between land and sea.

The boat tipped. Cepot and Dawala were thrown sideways. Dawala scrambled for purchase, his hand slipping on the wet teak.

"CEPOT!"

A hand -- huge, webbed, and dripping with slime -- rose from the black water and clamped around Dawala's ankle.

"*Mulah!*" Cepot lunged, grabbing his cousin's arm. "Hold on, *Dika*!"

Raden Wijaya drew his keris. He leaped from the stern, landing on the thrashing root-demon. He slashed downward, the Nagasasra severing the limb in a spray of black sap. The creature roared and thrashed, overturning the boat completely.

They were in the water.

It was warm, thick, and blindingly dark. Raden Wijaya kicked upward, breaking the surface, gasping for air. The mist was swirling violently now, whipped by a sudden wind.

"To the roots! Climb!" he shouted.

Tumenggung Batu was already hauling himself up a mangrove trunk, his armor streaming water. Dewi Anjani flitted from root to root, firing arrows into the churning water below.

But where were the Tuyul?

"Cepot! Dawala!" Raden Wijaya scanned the dark water.

A scream echoed through the swamp.

"RADEN! HELP! *GUST!*"

It was Cepot's voice. Raden Wijaya spun. Through the mist, he saw them.

They were being dragged. Not down into the water, but *away*.

Two figures -- tall, gaunt, clad in tattered robes of grey mist -- were skimming across the surface of the water on spectral tigers. The Leak. The scouts of the Shadow King. One of them held Dawala tucked under its arm like a sack of rice. The other gripped Cepot by the scruff of his neck.

The spectral tigers moved with impossible speed, their paws barely touching the water, leaving trails of blue fire in their wake. They were heading north.

"*Sira!* Stop!" Raden Wijaya roared, using the command form. He plunged into the water, swimming with desperate, frantic strokes. "Cowards! Fight me!"

The Leak turned its hooded head. Beneath the cowl, points of red fire burned. It laughed -- a sound like stones rattling in a dry gourd.

"*The King hunts the small game,*" the Leak hissed, its voice echoing in Raden Wijaya's mind. "*While the great prize walks into the fire alone.*"

And then they were gone. Vanished into the mist and the maze of the mangroves.

Raden Wijaya reached the roots where the Leak had been. There was nothing there. Only the rippling water and the fading trail of blue fire.

He slammed his fist against the wood, the bark tearing his skin. "*Duh Gusti!* Damn you to the void!"

Dewi Anjani landed lightly beside him. Her face was pale, her eyes wide with a sorrow that spanned centuries.

"They are taken," she whispered. "Taken to the tower of the Smith. *Kala* (Time) runs short."

Tumenggung Batu pulled himself onto the root, spitting black water. He looked at the empty space where the Tuyul had been. He looked at Raden Wijaya.

"We hunt them," the dwarf growled. His voice was low, dangerous, vibrating with the promise of violence. "We do not leave our own. Not to those things."

Raden Wijaya looked north, toward the distant, smoking cone of Merapi. He thought of Mataram, burning. He thought of the *Dharma* he owed to his ancestors. And then he thought of Cepot's laugh, and Dawala's fear, and the trust they had placed in him.

He sheathed his keris.

"We hunt," Raden Wijaya said. "We run until our lungs burst. We run until *Surya* fails. And we do not stop until we find them."

The Three Hunters turned their backs on the sea and plunged into the dark heart of the swamp.

---

*Sang Dalang pauses, the cempala silent.*

*"The fellowship is broken, my friends. The king turns to the hunt, the sage remains in the fire, and the small ones are carried into the shadow. But look now -- look to the wastes of the Sunda shore, where another path begins."*

# Bab 2: Si Burik di Rawa Bakau

## (Si Burik in the Mangrove Swamps)

---

*Wanda anyar deui.*
*Dina layar, aya dua Tuyul leutik leumpang dina taneuh hideung.*
*Sisi basisir nu panas, dimana seuneu papanggih jeung cai.*

*Another act.*
*On the screen, two small Tuyul walk on black earth.*
*At the edge of a hot shore, where fire meets water.*

---

The western coast of Jawa was a desolate liturgy of ash and stone, a landscape where the earth had been flayed raw by the fires of the deep. Here, the green mercy of the archipelago ended. The ground was a mosaic of cooled basalt, fissured and sharp, the color of old charcoal and dried blood. Nothing grew here but silence.

Kian Santang sat on a ridge of razor-sharp obsidian, his knees drawn tight to his chest. The air was thin and hot, tasting of metal and rotten eggs -- the breath of the volcano that loomed behind them, its dark cone blocking out the southern stars. The Keris Murka was heavy against his back, a physical weight that seemed to increase with every step they took away from the sea. It was silent now, but its silence was not peace; it was the holding of breath before a scream.

He looked at his hands. They were trembling. Not with fear, though fear was a constant, cold companion in his belly. They trembled with a hunger he did not want to name. A hunger to touch the hilt. To feel the warmth of the gold. To hear the voice that promised him a way out of this pain.

"Eat," a voice said.

Kian Santang started. Bagus was there, crouching beside him, offering a strip of dried meat on a leaf. Bagus's face was gaunt, smeared with ash and sweat, but his eyes were the same -- steady, brown, anchored in a reality that Kian Santang felt slipping away.

"I'm not hungry," Kian Santang whispered.

"Eat," Bagus repeated, pressing the food into Kian's hand. "We have miles to walk before we find water. You need strength, *Priya*."

Kian Santang forced himself to chew. The meat was tough and tasteless, like leather, but he swallowed it. For Bagus.

"The mangroves are ahead," Bagus said, pointing north. A dark line of vegetation broke the monotony of the ash fields, a tangle of shadows rising from the grey mud. "We might find a spring there. Or at least shade."

"Or Leak," Kian Santang murmured.

"Or Leak," Bagus agreed. "But we have to move. The sun is coming up, and out here, we are just bugs on a plate."

They stood, their legs protesting, and began the descent toward the swamp.

---

The mangrove forest was a cathedral of rot.

The *bakau* trees here were ancient, their roots twisted into tortuous shapes that resembled the limbs of tortured men. They rose from the black, oily mud like the ribs of a leviathan, their branches weaving a canopy so dense that the light of day was reduced to a sickly, greenish gloom. The air hung heavy and motionless, saturated with the smell of decay -- decomposing leaves, stagnant water, and the underlying, sharp tang of volcanic sulfur.

They moved slowly, climbing over slick roots and wading through pools of sludge that sucked at their boots. Every step was a struggle. Every breath was a mouthful of steam.

"It watches us," Kian Santang whispered.

"What watches us?" Bagus asked, his hand tightening on the hilt of his gardening knife.

"The swamp. The mud. It feels... hungry."

Bagus stopped. He held up a hand. "Listen."

At first, there was only the dripping of water and the distant, subsonic rumble of the earth. But then, Kian Santang heard it.

*Pat. Squelch. Pat.*

A soft, wet sound. The sound of skin slapping against mud.

"Behind us," Bagus breathed.

They turned. The shadows of the mangroves were deep and shifting.

"There," Kian Santang pointed.

A shape detached itself from the roots of a massive banyan. It was small, roughly the size of a child, but its proportions were wrong. Its limbs were too long, too thin, jointed at odd angles. Its head was large and hairless, bobbing on a spindly neck.

It crawled toward them on all fours, moving with a fluid, spider-like grace.

"Jimat-ku..." the creature hissed.

The voice was a wet rattle, like stones shaken in a flooded jar. It was speaking *Basa Loma*, the low Sundanese of the village, but the words were twisted, chewed up and spat out by a mouth that had forgotten how to speak.

"Milik aing... milik aing... jimat kancana..." -- *Mine... mine... the golden amulet...*

"*Gust!*" Bagus whispered, retreating a step. "What is it?"

Kian Santang stared at the creature. As it crept closer into a shaft of filtered light, he saw its skin -- pale and sickly, mottled with patches of green slime and grey scales. Its eyes were huge, bulging orbs of milky white that seemed to gather the dim light and reflect it back with a hungry luminescence.

It was a ruin. A wreck of a living thing.

But it was familiar.

"Jimat-ku!" the creature shrieked, a high, thin wail of longing. It lunged.

It moved faster than anything that wasted should be able to move. It was a blur of pale limbs and snapping teeth. It hit Kian Santang in the chest, knocking him backward into the mud. Cold, clammy hands clawed at his shirt, reaching for the batik wrapping on his back.

"Give it! Give it to Si Burik! Thief! Thief!"

Kian Santang struggled, gagging on the creature's breath -- a foul stench of raw fish and old graves. He kicked out, but the creature clung to him like a leech.

"Get off him!" Bagus roared. "*Mulah!*"

Bagus threw himself onto the creature's back, wrapping his thick arm around its neck. He hauled it backward, ripping it away from Kian Santang. The creature hissed and thrashed, its limbs flailing, but Bagus held on with the desperate strength of a gardener protecting his only flower.

"Stop!" Bagus shouted, tightening his grip. "Stop or *Aing* will snap your neck!"

The creature went limp. It collapsed into the mud, wheezing, its large eyes rolling in its head.

"Aksama... aksama... ampun!" it whimpered. "Mulah paehan... mulah paehan Si Burik!" -- *Do not kill... do not kill Si Burik!*

Kian Santang scrambled to his feet, wiping the black slime from his face. He drew his short blade, his hand shaking. He looked down at the creature -- at the pathetic, shivering heap of bones and skin.

And he felt a shock of recognition that went through him like an ice pick.

"Si Burik?" Kian Santang asked, his voice trembling. "Is that... your name?"

The creature looked up. Its eyes fixed on Kian Santang's face, searching, assessing. "Burik... Burik... yes... Si Burik found the amulet... Si Burik carried the amulet... to the cave... but the amulet was stolen! Stolen by the nasty Tuyul! Stolen by Kian Perkasa!"

Kian Santang froze. The world seemed to tilt on its axis.

"Kian Perkasa," he breathed. "My uncle."

"Uncle!" Si Burik spat the word like poison. "Thief! He took it! He took my precious! My birthday present!"

"He found it in the cave," Kian Santang said, the pieces of the puzzle falling into place with a terrifying click. "You... you lost it there. And he found it."

"Lost?" Si Burik wailed, rocking back and forth. "Not lost! Hiding! Resting! And then... gone! Gone! And Si Burik has wandered... wandered in the dark... seeking... seeking..."

Kian Santang looked at Bagus. Bagus was staring at the creature with a mixture of horror and revulsion.

"He's a Tuyul," Kian Santang said quietly. "Bagus... look at him. He was like us. Once."

Bagus looked at Si Burik's hands -- webbed, clawed, but still possessing the five fingers of their kin. He looked at the shape of the ears.

"The keris did this," Bagus whispered. "*Karuhun*... it ate him."

"It kept him alive," Kian Santang said. "Beyond his time. It twisted him." He looked at the creature, and he saw his own reflection in a dark mirror. This was the end of the road. This was what waited for the bearer who did not let go.

"We should kill him," Bagus said, his voice hard. "He's dangerous. He'll betray us the moment we turn our backs."

"No!" Si Burik shrieked, cowering in the mud. "No kill! Si Burik helps! Si Burik knows the way! Knows the paths! The master wants to go to the Fire, yes? To the Big Fire?"

Kian Santang lowered his blade. "You know the way to the caldera? To the forge of the Shadow King?"

"Yes! Yes!" Si Burik nodded frantically. "Si Burik has been there! Si Burik knows the secret stairs! The dark tunnels! The Buta do not see Si Burik. Si Burik is small. Si Burik is quiet."

"He's lying," Bagus warned.

"I don't think so," Kian Santang said. He looked into the creature's pale eyes. He saw the hunger there, yes. But he also saw fear. And a desperate, pathetic loneliness. "He wants the keris. He wants to be near it. He will lead us to the fire because that is where the keris is going."

Kian Santang stepped closer. He spoke in a voice that was not entirely his own -- a voice that carried a sudden, strange authority, the command of the Ring-bearer.

"You will guide us, Si Burik. You will take us to the Black Gate. And you will not touch the *jimat*. Do you swear it?"

Si Burik trembled. He looked at the wrapped blade on Kian Santang's back. He licked his lips.

"Swear... yes... swear by the *jimat*..."

"Swear by the *jimat*," Kian Santang repeated.

"Si Burik swears!" the creature hissed, pressing his face into the mud. "Si Burik serves the master of the precious! We go... we go to the Mountain of Fire!"

Kian Santang looked at Bagus. "Let him up."

Bagus hesitated, then released his grip. Si Burik scrambled away, scuttling to a safe distance. He crouched there, rubbing his neck, grinning a toothless, broken grin.

"We go now?" Si Burik asked. "Nice master. Good master."

"We go," Kian Santang said.

He walked past the creature, his head high, though his heart was cold. He was leading them into the dark, with a monster for a guide, and the only light he had was the terrible, burning fire on his back.

---

*The Dalang strikes the chest once.*

*"The shadow grows deeper, my children. The bearer binds himself to the broken thing, and the path turns downward. But hope is a stubborn root. Now, cast your eyes to the east -- to the ancient trees of Wanantara, where the earth remembers what the axe forgets."*

# Bab 3: Sanghyang Wana di Wanantara

## (The Forest Guardians of Wanantara)

---

*Wanda anyar.*
*Gunung Merapi ngageleger di kajauhan.*
*Leuweung kolot nu tara kasaba ku manusa.*

*A new act.*
*Gunung Merapi rumbles in the distance.*
*An ancient forest where man rarely treads.*

---

The Wanantara was not merely a forest; it was a memory of the earth from before the time of men.

Here, the air was cool and green, filtered through a canopy so dense that the sun was no more than a rumor, a scattering of gold coins upon a floor of velvet moss. The trees were titans -- *rasamala* and *damar* and the sacred *waringin* -- their trunks vast pillars of grey bark that rose hundreds of feet into the gloom. They were bearded with hanging lichens that swayed in the breeze like the hair of sleeping giants, and their roots, thick as the walls of a fortress, coiled through the soil in slow, patient knots.

Cepot stumbled over a root, catching himself on a fern frond as large as a roof. His breath came in ragged gasps. His clothes were torn, his skin scratched by a thousand thorns, his face a mask of dirt and exhaustion.

"Dawala," he hissed, peering into the emerald twilight. "Dawala, if you're hiding, this isn't funny. *Aing* is not in the mood for games!"

"I'm... I'm not... hiding," a small voice quavered from behind a curtain of hanging vines. Dawala emerged, his round face pale as a moon in the daylight. He was shivering, though the air was mild. "*Gust*... I'm lost, Cepot. We're both lost. This forest... it goes on forever. It doesn't have an end."

Cepot leaned against the trunk of a massive teak tree, trying to steady his racing heart. "Every forest has an end, *Dika*. Padalarang has an end. The world has an end."

"This one doesn't," Dawala insisted, his eyes wide and darting. "Can't you feel it? *Karuhun*... the trees... they're looking at us. Not like the trees at home. These ones... they have eyes."

Cepot shivered. He felt it too -- a weight of attention that pressed against his skin. It wasn't the hostile glare of the Leak, nor the mindless hunger of the Buta. It was something else. A slow, vast, contemplative awareness. A patience that measured time in the movement of tectonic plates rather than the beating of hearts.

"It's just the wind," Cepot said, though he didn't believe it. "We need to keep moving. The Leak dropped us when the sun rose, but they'll be back when the shadows lengthen. We have to find high ground. Maybe we can see... something."

"I'm hungry," Dawala whispered, sinking onto a mossy root. "I haven't eaten since... since the boat. *Aing* could eat a raw cassava right now."

"Don't talk about food," Cepot snapped, his stomach giving a treacherous growl. "Talk about walking. *Mulah* give up now."

"I can't walk," Dawala said, burying his face in his hands. "My legs are water. I just want to go home, Cepot. I want Nini Anteh's *kolak*. I want to sit on the veranda and watch the rain."

Cepot looked down at his cousin. He felt a surge of frustration, followed instantly by a wave of protective fierce love. Dawala was soft. He was kind. He was everything the world was currently trying to kill.

"Get up," Cepot said gently, reaching out a hand. "Come on, *Priya*. Just a little further. I promise."

Dawala reached up.

And the root he was sitting on moved.

It didn't break. It didn't shift under his weight. It *flexed*. Like a muscle.

Dawala shrieked and scrambled backward, crab-walking through the leaf litter. "*Gust!* It moved! It moved! The tree moved!"

"Don't be stupid," Cepot began, but the words died in his throat.

A deep, groaning vibration shuddered through the earth. The mossy trunk Cepot was leaning against trembled. Above them, the canopy rustled with a sound like a great wind, though the air was still.

Slowly, impossibly, the tree began to change.

A fissure in the bark widened, revealing a depth of darkness that blinked. Two eyes -- large, brown, deep as forest pools -- opened in the wood. A branch creaked and bent downward, revealing itself to be a long, gnarled arm ending in seven twig-fingers. The roots shifted, pulling themselves from the soil with a tearing sound of snapping earth, transforming into massive, pillar-like legs.

"Hrooom..."

The sound was a bass note so low it vibrated in Cepot's chest. It was the voice of wood. The voice of stone.

"Little... beetles..."

The creature loomed over them, five meters tall, crowned with a mane of leaves and moss. Its face was old -- older than Eyang Guru, older than the temples of Dieng. It was a face carved by centuries of rain and sun.

"Why... do... you... disturb... the... sleep... of... the... roots?"

Cepot stood frozen, his mouth agape. His mind, usually a whirl of jokes and strategies, had ground to a complete halt. There were no jokes for this.

"We... we didn't mean to," Dawala squeaked from the ground. "*Aksama*! We're sorry! Please, Mr. Tree... don't squash us!"

The creature blinked. It took a long time.

"Squash?" The word rolled around in the wooden mouth like a boulder. "The... Sanghyang Wana... do... not... squash. We... grow. We... stand. We... remember."

A massive hand reached down. Cepot flinched, but the movement was gentle. The twig-fingers scooped Dawala up from the leaves as tenderly as a gardener lifting a seedling. The creature examined the shaking Tuyul with its deep, slow eyes.

"Not... beetles," the creature decided. "Too... soft. Too... loud. What... are... you?"

"Tuyul," Dawala whispered. "From Padalarang."

"Pa-da-la-rang..." The creature tasted the name. "I... know... that... hill. I... knew... the... grandmother... of... the... banyan... that... grows... there."

The creature turned its gaze to Cepot. "And... you?"

"Cepot," he managed to say. He sheathed his knife, realizing how ridiculous it looked against a living mountain. "*Aing* is Cepot. We... we are friends of the trees. We fight the Shadow King."

The creature's expression darkened. A shadow seemed to pass over the sun. A low growl rumbled through its trunk, shaking the leaves of its crown.

"The... Shadow... King..." The words were heavy with ancient anger. "The... burner. The... hewer. The... enemy... of... the... green."

"His servants took us," Cepot said, finding his courage. "They brought us here. They are cutting the forest on the slopes of Merapi. We saw the smoke."

"Smoke," the creature groaned. "Yes... I... smell... it. The... young... ones... cry... out. The... iron... bites."

The creature lowered Dawala back to the ground, but kept a protective hand near him.

"I... am... Mbah Jangkung," it said. "The... Long... Father. I... am... the... shepherd... of... the... trees. And... you... bring... news... of... axe... and... fire."

"Empu Gandring," Cepot said. "The Smith. He is building an army. He is feeding the forest to his furnaces."

Mbah Jangkung stiffened. The moss on his shoulders bristled.

"Gandring..." The name was a curse. "He... was... once... a... friend. He... sang... to... the... wood... before... he... cut. Now... he... only... takes."

The ancient spirit turned his gaze toward the east, where the dark cone of Merapi polluted the sky.

"The... patience... of... the... forest... is... deep," Mbah Jangkung rumbled. "But... even... roots... can... break... stone... if... they... grow... angry... enough."

He looked back at the Tuyul.

"Come... little... roots. Climb... up. My... shoulders... are... broad. We... go... to... the... *Sabha Wana*. We... go... to... the... Moot."

"The Moot?" Dawala asked, blinking.

"The... gathering," Mbah Jangkung said. "The... waking. If... the... furnace... burns... then... the... river... must... rise."

He lowered his arm like a ramp. Cepot looked at Dawala. Dawala looked at Cepot. And for the first time in days, they smiled.

They climbed onto the shoulders of the giant. Mbah Jangkung rose to his full height, lifting them into the cool air of the canopy.

"Hold... on," the spirit said.

And with a stride that ate the miles, the Guardian of Wanantara began to march, carrying the seeds of the Shadow King's destruction upon his back.

# Bab 4: Pasukan Kuda Kediri

Tegalan alang-alang di wates Kediri warnana siga emas nu geus kabeuleum ku poe.

The savanna of the Kediri borders was the color of gold tempered by an unforgiving sun. Here, the dense, emerald canopy of the Wanantara forest thinned into a vast expanse of *alang-alang* grass, a sea of silver-tipped stalks that rippled and hissed in the dry wind like a thousand escaping serpents. The air was thick with the scent of parched earth, wild jasmine, and the sharp, ozone tang of distant volcanic lightning. Above, the sky was a hard, brilliant turquoise, stripped of clouds, vibrating with the incessant drone of cicadas that sounded like the grinding of unseen gears.

Raden Wijaya moved through the tall grass with the rhythmic, effortless stride of a man who had forgotten how to tire. His batik sarong, the *parang rusak* pattern now stained with the grey ash of the strait and the dark loam of the forest, whipped against his legs. He walked not as an exile, but as a predator in his own domain, his hand never straying far from the hilt of the Keris Nagasasra.

*Am I leading them to salvation or to a grave?* The thought was a constant, heavy stone in his gut. He had lost the Tuyul. He had lost the *jimat* bearer. He had lost the wise old man who had started it all. All that remained were the Warrior and the Hunter, and a kingdom that might already be dead.

"The land is wounded," Dewi Anjani whispered, her voice carrying over the wind like a flute note. The Bidadari moved behind him, her feet barely depressing the dry soil. Her skin, usually luminous, was dimmed by the harsh sun, but her eyes were sharp as diamond. "*Bayu* is thin here, *Manusa*. I can hear the grass weeping for the water that has been turned to steam by the Shadow King's breath."

"It is more than the wind," Raden Wijaya said, stopping at the crest of a low rise. He knelt, pressing his palm to the sun-baked earth. He closed his eyes, feeling the vibrations of the world. "The ground trembles. Not with the pulse of the mountain, but with the cadence of iron on stone. *Kuda*."

Tumenggung Batu grunted, hoisting his war hammer. The Kurcaci was a moving fortress of iron and basalt, sweat streaming down his broad face into his beard. "Horses? Good. My legs are shorter than my patience right now. If they are enemies, I will take their legs. If they are friends, I will take a ride."

Across the plain, a cloud of dust rose against the blue, moving with a speed that no Buta horde could match. It was a golden-brown veil, illuminated from within by the flash of bronze and the glint of spear-tips.

"Pasukan Kuda Kediri," Raden Wijaya said, rising. "The Horse Lords of the East. I had heard they still held the plains, but I expected to find them dead or driven to the mountains."

The riders emerged from the dust like figures carved from the savanna itself. They were mounted on *kuda turangga* -- the swift, lean horses of the eastern ranges, their coats shining like polished copper, their manes braided with bells and protective amulets. The riders wore breastplates of hardened leather and bronze, their heads crowned with the *kuluk* of Kediri warriors, their faces masked by the dust of the long patrol.

At their head rode a man of imposing presence, his armor inlaid with silver filigree, a long *tombak* spear held vertically in his right hand. He led his troop in a wide, sweeping arc, a *Singhabihwa* formation that hemmed the three travelers against the rise of the hill.

"*Mulah gerak!*" the leader thundered, his voice a command that brooked no argument. "*Kula Kanjeng Tumenggung Arya Kamandanu, balapati wates Kediri. Sira, sapa wani lumaku ing lemah ingsun mawa hawa pati?*"

*Do not move! I am Kanjeng Tumenggung Arya Kamandanu, general of the Kediri borders. You there, who dares to walk upon my land carrying the scent of death?*

Raden Wijaya stood slowly, his hands held open and away from his blades. He looked up at the rider, meeting his gaze with the calm authority of a man who has looked into the eyes of dragons. He spoke in the *Krama Inggil*, the highest register of the Javanese court, the language of kings that recognized neither dust nor distance.

"*Sembah bakti, Kanjeng Tumenggung. Kula Raden Wijaya, putranipun Prabu Hayam Wuruk, ahli waris dhampar Mataram. Kula mawa wartos malapetaka saking Selat Sunda, sarta mawa harepan kanggo Nusantara.*"

*Respectful greetings, Kanjeng Tumenggung. I am Raden Wijaya, son of King Hayam Wuruk, heir to the throne of Mataram. I bring news of disaster from the Sunda Strait, and hope for Nusantara.*

The riders went silent. The horses shifted nervously, their bells chiming softly in the heat. The name *Mataram* hung in the dry air like a prayer, heavy with the weight of lost glory.

Arya Kamandanu lowered his spear, his dark eyes narrowing as he studied the travel-worn prince, the Bidadari archer, and the Kurcaci warrior. He saw the filth, the fatigue, the bloodstains on the batik. But he also saw the bearing, the way the man stood as if the earth belonged to him by right of blood.

"Mataram is a ghost, prince," the Tumenggung said, his voice softening but remaining guarded. "A kingdom of shadows and burning rice fields. We have heard that the Shadow King's Leak ride the halls of Giri Mataram, and that Prabu Jayabaya sits upon a throne of ash, his mind clouded by the whispers of Ki Surasenta."

He looked south, toward the dark plume of Merapi that dominated the horizon. The volcano was a constant sore in the sky, a reminder of the darkness that festered at the heart of the island.

"We are all that remains of the free shield. My men die daily in the alang-alang, hunted by Buta scouts and the iron-men of Empu Gandring. If *sira* seeks the throne, *sira* seeks a grave."

"I do not seek a throne," Raden Wijaya said, his voice echoing with the iron certainty of the first book's resolution. "I seek the people. I seek the alliance. If Mataram falls, Kediri is but a snack for the Naga Laut. If we do not stand together now, there will be no one left to tell our stories. *Dharma* demands it."

Arya Kamandanu studied him for a long, silent moment. The wind rustled the dry grass, whispering secrets of the dead who lay beneath the soil.

"The King of Mataram would not walk the plains as a ranger," the Tumenggung said. "But the man who stands before me has the eyes of a king. And the Bidadari light does not shine for liars."

He turned to his men, raising his spear. "*Paringana kuda!* Give them horses! We ride for Giri Mataram at dusk. If the prince wishes to face the Shadow in the Balairung, the Horse Lords will carry him to the gate. *Inggih!*"

"*Inggih!*" the riders shouted in unison, their spears clashing against their shields.

Raden Wijaya looked at the distant mountains. The road was long, and the shadow was growing, but the Horse Lords had answered. The first thread of the new alliance was being spun in the dust of the Kediri plains.

# Bab 5: Dwija Putih

## (The White Sage)

---

*Gunungan ditancepkeun dina kenca, miring ka tengah.*
*Wanda anyar geus dibuka: Wanda Manyura.*
*Cahaya blencong bodas ngagentos beureum, siga bulan di luhur leuweung.*

*The Gunungan is set at the left, tilted toward the center.*
*A new act is opened: The Act of the Peacock.*
*The lamp's white light replaces the red, like the moon above the forest.*

---

Leuweung Wanantara di wates Mataram warnana siga pérak nu geus kabeuleum ku tiis.

The Forest of Wanantara at the Mataram borders was the color of silver tempered by a preternatural chill. It was not the green, breathing heart of the Sunda highlands, nor the dusty, golden savanna of Kediri. It was a place of deep, primordial stillness, where the *wanantara* (deep wilderness) met the *wanadri* (mountain forest), and where the trees were so old that their bark had turned to grey-white stone. Moss like frozen waterfalls hung from the branches of ancient *rasamala* trees, and the air smelled of wet stone, crushed cardamom, and the faint, electric ozone of a gathering storm.

The three companions moved through the undergrowth with a silence that was more than caution -- it was reverence. Raden Wijaya tracked the footprints of the Leak scouts that had taken Cepot and Dawala, his eyes scanning the crushed ferns and the broken twigs with the focus of a man deciphering a sacred text.

*I am a hunter of shadows,* he thought, his mind a quiet pool of determination. *But the shadows here are old. They do not flee; they wait.* He felt the weight of the *Nagasasra* at his hip, a reassuring presence, but even the sacred iron felt cold in this ancient air.

Dewi Anjani moved behind him, her silver-shod feet barely touching the forest floor, her bow held horizontally before her chest. Her eyes were wide, reflecting the silver light of the canopy.

"The trees are... different here," Tumenggung Batu grunted, his breath visible in the chilled air. "They're not just watching. They're... waiting. Like a mountain waiting for the first crack of winter."

"The *Sanghyang Wana* are awake," Dewi Anjani whispered, her golden eyes widening. "The *karuhun* of the wood stir, *Manusa*. The forest prepares for a reckoning."

They stopped at the edge of a deep ravine, where a mountain stream fell in a cascade of white water into the dark below. The mist from the falls rose in shimmering veils, turning the forest into a labyrinth of grey and silver.

"There," Raden Wijaya said, his hand on the hilt of the Keris Nagasasra.

A figure stood on a flat shelf of rock across the ravine, half-hidden by the swirling mist. It was a man, tall and thin, his head crowned with hair as white as the falls themselves. He was robed in a simple shirt and sarong of undyed cotton -- a white so pure and so luminous that it seemed to absorb the dim light of the canopy and cast it back. He leaned on a staff of polished teak, its surface glowing with a faint, steady gold.

"*Duh Gusti...*" Raden Wijaya breathed, the invocation slipping from his lips unbidden. "Eyang? Eyang Guru?"

The figure turned slowly.

It was indeed the face of Eyang Guru Batara -- the same weathered skin, the same gentle lines around the eyes, the same long, white beard that Kian Santang had loved. But it was changed. The exhaustion was gone. The fragility of age was replaced by a strange, terrifying vitality, as though the old man had been rebuilt from the very *geni suci* (sacred fire) that had once threatened to consume him. His eyes were not brown, but a clear, brilliant gold, like the core of a sun that had never known darkness.

"Dwija Putih," Dewi Anjani whispered, her knees touching the forest floor in the Bidadari salute. "The White Sage. The walker of the *buana* (worlds) has returned."

Eyang Guru Batara -- the Dwija Putih -- raised his staff, and the mist around him cleared, revealed him in his full, magnificent radiance.

"*Om Swasti Astu, putranipun Mataram,*" he said, his voice a deep, resonant bell that carried over the sound of the falls. "*Rahayu ka andika, Bidadari sarta Kurcaci nu satia. Persekutuan ieu tacan pareum, sanajan dunya geus mimiti poek.*"

*May there be peace, son of Mataram. Blessings to you, faithful Bidadari and Kurcaci. This fellowship has not been extinguished, even as the world has begun to darken.*

Raden Wijaya knelt, his head bowed. "We thought *sira* had fallen, Eyang. Into the fire... beneath the Jembatan Batu."

"I did fall, Raden," the White Sage said, a faint smile playing across his lips. "I fell into the fire, and the fire did not burn me. It... warahkeun (taught) me. It showed me the deep architecture of the world, where the *bayu* flows through the stone and the sea. I was the servant of the sacred fire; now I am the fire itself, given form and purpose once more."

He stepped across the ravine, his feet walking on the air as though it were solid stone, his white robes streaming behind him like the feathers of a celestial bird.

"The two Tuyul are safe," he said, his hand touching Raden Wijaya's shoulder. "Mbah Jangkung has found them. They have reached the *Sabha Wana*. The forest is marching on Merapi, to reclaim the heartwood that Empu Gandring has stolen."

"And Kian Santang?" Tumenggung Batu asked, his voice rough with emotion. "And the keris?"

The Dwija Putih's golden eyes went distant, as though he were looking through the mountain itself to the volcanic wastes of the strait.

"The *manik* bearer climbs the Land of Fire," he said. "He is guided by a ghost of the past, a creature of shadow and memory. The path is narrow, and the darkness is thick. But he is not alone. Bagus is with him. And the *karuhun* watch through the stars of Waluku."

He looked toward the east, where the mountains of Mataram rose from the forest like the bastions of a lost world.

"But your path, Raden Wijaya, leads to Giri Mataram. The Horse Lords are ready, but the King is not yet awake. Prabu Jayabaya is under the spell of Ki Surasenta, and the Shadow King's influence has turned the throne to ash."

The Dwija Putih struck his staff against the earth, and a pulse of golden light flared outward, pushing back the shadows of the Wanantara.

"We ride for Mataram! The siege of Candi Abang is coming, and the world will see if the line of Hayam Wuruk is still made of iron or only of dreams. *Dharma* calls!"

Raden Wijaya stood, his face set in the expression of a king who had found his destiny. The Three Hunters, now led by the White Sage, turned toward the rising sun, their path clear, their hearts renewed.

# Bab 6: Balairung di Giri Mataram

---

Balairung Agung di Giri Mataram warnana siga haseup menyan nu geus kolot sarta poek.

The Great Hall of Giri Mataram was the color of ancient, stagnant incense smoke. It was a space of shadows and silence, where the grandeur of the Mataram kings had been slowly eroded by a spiritual sickness. The air was thick and heavy, smelling of stale jasmine, old batik, and the cloying, sweet scent of *menyan* (incense) that rose in thick, grey plumes from bronze censers. High above, the teak beams of the ceiling were lost in the gloom, their carvings of nagas and garudas obscured by a century of soot.

Raden Wijaya, Dewi Anjani, and Tumenggung Batu stood at the threshold of the hall, their travel-worn clothes a stark contrast to the opulence of the Mataram court. Beside them stood the Dwija Putih, his white robes a blinding, impossible light in the stagnant dark.

*This is the heart of my people,* Raden Wijaya thought, his heart heavy with grief. *Choked by smoke and whispers. Where is the laughter of the court? Where is the song of the gamelan? It is as silent as a tomb.*

At the far end of the hall, upon a throne of obsidian and gold, sat **Prabu Jayabaya**. He was a man who should have been in the prime of his strength, but he looked like a ghost of himself. His face was pale and drawn, his eyes sunken and unfocused, his crown of gold and gems slipping low upon his brow. He was draped in robes of heavy silk that seemed to weigh him down, pinning him to the throne like a specimen in a collector's case.

Crouched beside the throne was a thin, rat-like figure with a long, drooping mustache and eyes that never rested. This was **Ki Surasenta**, the King's advisor, his voice a constant, oily murmur in the Prabu's ear. He wore the black and red *dodot* of a Mataram courtier, but the colors were muddy, and the *parang* patterns on his sarong were twisted and wrong.

"Who are these... intruders?" Jayabaya's voice was a weak, papery rasp that barely carried across the hall. "Ki Surasenta... why have you allowed these beggars to enter my presence?"

Ki Surasenta leaned closer to the King, his lips curling in a snarl. "They are messengers of doom, *Gusti Prabu*. Rangers and Bidadari who seek to disturb your peace with tales of war and fire. They carry the scent of the Shadow King's strait, seeking to bring the contagion into your sacred halls."

The Dwija Putih stepped forward, his staff striking the marble floor with a sound that echoed like a thunderclap.

"*Mulah!*" the White Sage thundered, his golden eyes blazing. "The contagion is already here, Ki Surasenta. It sits beside the throne, whispering poison into the ear of a king who has forgotten how to lead."

Jayabaya flinched, his hand trembling on the arm of his throne. "I... I have not forgotten. Mataram is safe. The borders are... secure."

"The borders are burning, Prabu Jayabaya!" Raden Wijaya stepped forward, his voice the *Krama Inggil* of a true heir, ringing with an authority that made the courtiers in the shadows flinch. "*Duh Gusti*, look at me! The Horse Lords of Kediri are all that stand between your people and the Buta Raksasa. The Shadow King's armies are massing at the gates of Merapi, and *Sira* sits here in a cloud of incense, listening to a snake!"

Ki Surasenta laughed, a high, skittering sound. "A snake? I am the King's only true friend. I have kept him safe from the cold winds of the east. I have given him the peace that his ancestors could not find."

"You have given him a cage!" Tumenggung Batu roared, his hammer held tight. "You've turned a lion into a house-cat!"

The Dwija Putih raised his staff, and the golden light at its tip flared into a blinding sun. "Prabu Jayabaya! Look at me! Look into the light of the *geni suci* and remember who you are!"

Ki Surasenta shrieked and shielded his eyes, his form seeming to shrink and wither in the holy radiance. "No! My Lord, do not look! It is a trick... a Bidadari illusion!"

But Jayabaya did look. For the first time in years, the King's sunken eyes met the golden gaze of the Sage, and the fog of incense and whispers began to lift. He saw the Dwija Putih, and he saw Raden Wijaya, and in the prince's face, he saw the features of the great King Hayam Wuruk, his own kinsman.

"Wijaya?" the King whispered, his voice gaining a sudden, terrifying clarity. "Is it... truly you? The son of my brother?"

"It is, Uncle," Raden Wijaya said, his voice softening. "I have returned. Not to take your throne, but to help you defend it. To restore *Dharma* to this hall."

Jayabaya stood. It was a slow, painful movement, as though he were shedding a physical skin of lead and silk. He looked at Ki Surasenta, who was now cowering on the floor, his rat-like face twisted with fear.

"Get... out," Jayabaya said, the voice of the Lion of Mataram returning at last. "Get out of my sight, Surasenta. You have fed me lies and stolen my strength. If *Sira* is still in this hall by sunset, I will feed you to the crocodiles of the Solo river."

Ki Surasenta scrambled to his feet and vanished into the shadows, his robes fluttering behind him like the wings of a bat.

The King turned to the Dwija Putih and Raden Wijaya. He was still thin, still pale, but the light was back in his eyes, and his hand was steady as he reached for the ancestral sword of Mataram that hung on the wall behind him.

"The Horse Lords are waiting," Raden Wijaya said.

"Then we ride," Jayabaya said, his voice echoing through the hall. "The Balairung is closed. The gates of Giri Mataram are open. Let the Shadow King know that the Lion of Mataram is awake, and he is hungry for war. *Inggih!*"

The Great Hall, once a place of stagnant smoke and whispers, was suddenly filled with the sound of movement and the clash of steel. The King had returned to his senses, and the war for Mataram had begun.

# Bab 7: Pintu Gerbang Malapetaka

## (The Gate of Doom)

---

*Gunungan ditancepkeun dina tengen, miring ka kenca.*
*Wanda anyar geus dibuka: Wanda Galak.*
*Langit poek, haseup hideung ngukuntit gunung seuneu.*
*Sora gamelan nu ngaguruh, siga sora ombak nu nabrak karang.*

*The Gunungan is set at the right, tilted to the left.*
*A new act is opened: The Act of Fierceness.*
*The sky is dark, black smoke stalks the volcano.*
*The sound of gamelan thunders, like waves crashing against a reef.*

---

Tanah di hareupeun Gerbang Hideung warnana siga areng nu geus tiis sarta tulang nu geus ancur.

The land before the Black Gate was the color of cold charcoal and crushed bone. It was a desolation so absolute that it seemed less a landscape than a scar on the skin of the world. Here, on the lower slopes of the great caldera complex, nothing grew. Not the stubborn *alang-alang* grass, not the lichen that clings to volcanic rock, not even the twisted *bakau* roots that survived in the poison swamps below. There was only ash -- deep, grey, shifting ash that swallowed sound and footprint alike -- and the jagged teeth of cooled lava flows that rose like the ribs of dead giants.

Kian Santang lay flat on a ridge of razor-sharp obsidian, peering over the edge. Beside him, Bagus was a warm, trembling presence in the ash. Si Burik crouched a few paces away, sniffing the sulfurous air, his pale eyes fixed on the monolithic structure that barred their path.

The *Gerbang Hideung* -- the Black Gate.

It was not a gate of wood or iron. It was a sheer wall of obsidian, three hundred feet high, carved from the living rock of the caldera rim by the Shadow King's dark *sakti* in the First Age. It spanned the only pass that led directly into the inner sanctum of Krakatau, a narrow defile between two towering cliffs of volcanic basalt. The gate had no hinges, no handles, no seams. It was a single, seamless slab of black glass, polished to a mirror finish that reflected nothing but the smoke and the fire of the mountain above.

But it was not the gate itself that froze the blood in Kian Santang's veins. It was the army camped before it.

Thousands of fires burned in the ash fields below. The Buta Raksasa were there in numbers beyond counting -- vast regiments of grey-green flesh and black iron, their tents made of flayed skins, their war-drums beating a slow, tectonic rhythm that vibrated in the rock beneath Kian Santang's chest. Among them moved the smaller, quicker shapes of human mercenaries from the corrupted coastal kingdoms, and the towering, spectral forms of the Leak commanders on their shadow-steeds.

"We cannot pass," Bagus whispered, his voice barely audible over the wind. "*Karuhun*... there are too many eyes. Too many noses."

Kian Santang gripped the hilt of the Keris Murka. The blade was awake now, humming with a dark, eager resonance. It knew where it was. It knew its master was near. *Home,* it whispered. *Open the gate. Call to him. He will welcome you like a son.*

*Aing will not call him,* Kian Santang thought, his mind a fortress of fear and stubbornness. *Aing will throw you into his fire, and silence you forever.* But even as he thought it, his hand stroked the hilt with a lover's tenderness.

"No," Kian Santang hissed aloud, pressing his forehead against the cold stone to silence the voice. "We find another way."

Si Burik crept closer, his movements fluid and silent as a lizard's. He tugged at Kian Santang's sleeve with a long, bony finger.

"Gerbang ditutup... ditutup ku *mantra* Raja Iblis," the creature rasped. "The Gate is closed... closed by the Shadow King's spell. Only the Buta can enter. Or the... *jimat* bearer who surrenders."

"We do not surrender," Kian Santang said, looking at the creature. "Is there another way, Si Burik? You said you knew the paths. *Mulah* lie to me now."

Si Burik's eyes shifted. He looked away, toward the high, jagged peaks that formed the western wall of the caldera. A shadow passed over his face -- a look of cunning warred with a look of genuine terror.

"Aya... aya jalan séjén," he whispered. "There is... another way. A secret way. A dark way."

"Where?" Bagus asked, suspicious.

"Up... up... up the stairs," Si Burik pointed a trembling finger toward a narrow, almost invisible cleft in the rock face, high above the main pass. "The Stairs of the Scorpion. *Tangga Kalajengking*. Steep... hard... but secret. The Buta do not go there. The Leak do not fly there."

"Why?" Kian Santang asked.

Si Burik shivered. "Sieun... sieun ku *Nyi Blorong*..." -- *Afraid... afraid of Nyi Blorong...*

"Nyi Blorong?" Bagus paled. "The Serpent Queen? The Weaver of Shadows?"

"She sleeps there," Si Burik hissed. "She guards the high pass. But she sleeps... mostly. If we are quiet... if we are small... we can sneak past. Into the tunnel. Into the caldera."

Kian Santang looked at the Black Gate, impassable and swarming with enemies. He looked at the high, dark cleft in the mountains, ominous and silent. He felt the weight of the keris on his back, heavier with every hour.

"We take the stairs," Kian Santang decided.

Bagus looked at him, fear plain in his honest eyes. "Kian... Nyi Blorong is a legend. A monster from the old tales. If she is real..."

"Then we must be quieter than legends," Kian Santang said. He turned to Si Burik. "Lead us. But if you lead us into a trap, Si Burik... the keris will know."

Si Burik cringed, hiding his face in his hands. "No trap! No trap! Si Burik good... Si Burik helps the master of the *jimat*... *gollum*... *gollum*..."

The creature turned and began to scramble up the scree slope toward the cliffs, moving on all fours. Kian Santang and Bagus followed, leaving the fires of the Buta army behind, climbing into the shadows of the high peaks where something ancient and hungry waited in the dark.

# Bab 8: Benteng Candi Abang

## (The Siege of the Red Temple)

---

*Gunungan ditancepkeun dina tengah, miring ka katuhu.*
*Wanda anyar geus mitembeyan: Wanda Perang.*
*Kilat ngaburinyay di langit poek, hujan ngaragragan batu beureum.*
*Sora gamelan gancang sarta ribut, siga kuda nu keur lumpat.*

*The Gunungan is set at the center, tilted to the right.*
*A new act begins: The Act of War.*
*Lightning flashes in the dark sky, rain falls on red stone.*
*The gamelan is fast and chaotic, like horses running.*

---

Batu beureum Candi Abang baseuh ku getih sarta hujan nu teu eureun-eureun.

The red stone of Candi Abang was wet with blood and rain that never ceased. The ancient fortress, carved into the sheer face of Giri Mataram, was a marvel of the ancestors -- walls of dark red andesite blocks, fitted without mortar, rising in tiered ramparts that followed the spine of the mountain like the scales of a sleeping dragon. Tonight, those scales were battered by a storm of iron.

Below the walls, the valley was a sea of torches. Ten thousand Buta Raksasa, their grey-green skin gleaming in the rain, surged against the gates with battering rams made of iron-shod teak. Their war-cries were a chaotic roar that drowned out the thunder, a sound of pure, mindless fury.

Raden Wijaya stood on the main rampart, the rain streaming from his hair, the Keris Nagasasra in his hand. Beside him, King Jayabaya fought with the fury of a man reclaiming his soul. The Lion of Mataram was awake, his *dodot* torn, his crown lost, but his eyes blazing with the *semangat* of his ancestors.

"They are endless!" Jayabaya shouted, parrying a Buta's spear-thrust. "For every one we kill, two more rise from the mud!"

"Hold the line!" Raden Wijaya roared, his voice cutting through the storm. "We must hold until dawn! The Horse Lords are coming! *Inggih!*"

But the dawn felt a lifetime away.

On the eastern wall, Tumenggung Batu and Dewi Anjani fought back-to-back. The Kurcaci was a whirlwind of destruction, his hammer smashing Buta kneecaps and skulls with rhythmic precision. "Forty-two!" he bellowed, counting his kills. "Is that the best you have, you swamp-born uglies?"

Dewi Anjani did not count. She sang. Her bow hummed a high, deadly note, each arrow finding a throat or an eye with supernatural accuracy. But her quiver was running low, and the Bidadari light in her skin was dimming, drained by the proximity of so much darkness.

"They are breaching the postern gate!" a soldier cried out from the lower courtyard.

Raden Wijaya looked down. A massive explosion of black powder -- *obat bedil* from the Shadow King's alchemists -- had shattered the heavy timber doors. Buta poured into the inner keep, their tusks bared, their stone clubs raised.

"*Duh Gusti!*" Jayabaya cursed. "To the keep! Fall back! Defend the inner sanctum!"

They retreated, fighting step by bloody step up the stairs of the main temple. The rain turned the stone slick, and men fell screaming into the courtyard below. Raden Wijaya held the rear, his keris flashing in the lightning, buying time for the wounded King to reach safety.

They barricaded the heavy bronze doors of the sanctuary. Outside, the Buta hammered against the metal, a deafening gong-beat of doom.

"It is over," Jayabaya said, leaning against a pillar, his chest heaving. "We are trapped. The dawn will find only our bones."

Raden Wijaya walked to the narrow window that looked out over the eastern valley. The rain was easing. The sky was turning a bruised purple.

*The sun rises,* he thought. *But who will see it?*

And then he heard it.

Faintly at first, then growing louder, a sound that rose above the storm and the battle. Not thunder. Not drums.

A horn.

A single, clear note, winding through the valleys of Mataram like a river of sound. It was the horn of Kediri.

"Look!" Raden Wijaya shouted, pointing to the ridge.

On the crest of the eastern hill, the sun broke through the storm clouds. And in that shaft of golden light, they appeared.

Thousands of them.

The Horse Lords of Kediri. Their bronze armor shone like fire. Their spears were a forest of steel. At their head rode Kanjeng Tumenggung Arya Kamandanu, his white *kuluk* catching the light.

"FOR MATARAM!" the cry rolled down the mountain like an avalanche. "FOR NUSANTARA!"

The riders charged. They swept down the slope in a *Garudabihwa* formation, their horses thundering into the flank of the Buta army. The impact was catastrophic. The Buta, focused on the walls, broke under the sudden assault of heavy cavalry. Panic spread through their ranks.

"NOW!" Raden Wijaya threw open the bronze doors. "Ride out! Ride out and meet them! *Sira!* With me!"

The defenders of Candi Abang poured from the fortress, their despair turned to fury. Raden Wijaya led the charge, the Keris Nagasasra blazing with the light of the morning sun. King Jayabaya rode beside him, laughing with the pure joy of battle.

The Shadow King's army was caught between the hammer of the Horse Lords and the anvil of the fortress. They broke. They fled. They died in the mud of the valley, their torches extinguished, their malice shattered by the combined strength of Men, Bidadari, Kurcaci, and the ancient bond of *gotong royong*.

The siege was lifted. The Red Temple stood.

# Bab 9: Amarah Sanghyang Wana

## (The Wrath of the Forest Guardians)

---

*Gunungan ditancepkeun dina tengen, miring ka tengah.*
*Wanda anyar geus mitembeyan: Wanda Murka.*
*Langit beureum, seuneu ngaduruk leuweung, sora tangkal nu runtuh.*
*Sora gamelan gancang sarta beurat, siga sora batu nu digulung.*

*The Gunungan is set at the right, tilted to the center.*
*A new act begins: The Act of Wrath.*
*The sky is red, fire burns the forest, the sound of falling trees.*
*The gamelan is fast and heavy, like the sound of rolling stones.*

---

Di suku Gunung Merapi, leuweung Wanantara keur ngaduruk.

At the foot of Gunung Merapi, the Wanantara forest was burning. The ancient trees, older than the kingdoms of men, were being fed to the forges of Empu Gandring. The air was choked with soot and the screams of dying wood. Smoke hung heavy in the canopy, blotting out the sun, turning the world into a perpetual twilight of ash and ember.

Cepot sat on Mbah Jangkung's shoulder, coughing into his sleeve. Beside him, Dawala clung to a branch-limb, his eyes watering.

"Look at it," Cepot whispered, pointing to the clearing below. "They're... they're tearing them apart. *Gust!* It's like watching a butcher."

Buta Raksasa with massive axes of black iron were hacking at the trunks of giant *rasamala* trees. They worked with a brutal efficiency, felling the forest giants, stripping their branches, and dragging the logs into the gaping maw of the ironworks built into the mountainside. The forges roared with an unnatural heat, their chimneys belching poison into the sky.

Mbah Jangkung stood at the edge of the clearing, his wooden form trembling with a rage that shook the ground beneath his roots.

"This... is... not... right," the ancient spirit rumbled, his voice like rocks grinding together deep underground. "The... smith... has... forgotten... the... pact. He... takes... without... asking. He... burns... without... planting. *Duh Gusti...*"

"They're killing the forest, Mbah!" Dawala cried out, pointing to a grove of young saplings being trampled by the Buta's iron-shod boots. "Look! Even the little ones!"

A sound came from the depths of the forest behind them. A low, mournful groan that rose in pitch until it became a roar of collective fury. The *Sabha Wana* -- the assembly of the forest spirits -- had gathered. Hundreds of Sanghyang Wana, from the towering banyans to the slender teak spirits, emerged from the shadows. Their eyes glowed with green fire. Their limbs creaked with the tension of centuries of patience finally snapping.

"The... Ents... are... going... to... war," Cepot realized, his breath catching in his throat. "*Aing* never thought I'd see it."

Mbah Jangkung raised his massive arms.

"SANGHYANG WANA!" his voice boomed across the valley, silencing the axes and the forges. "AWAKE! THE ROOTS ARE DEEP! THE MEMORY IS LONG! TODAY... WE... MARCH!"

The forest erupted.

It was not a battle of strategy. It was a geological event. The Sanghyang Wana surged forward like a living landslide. Banyan roots as thick as pythons ripped through the earth, shattering the stone foundations of the ironworks. Teak limbs swatted Buta Raksasa aside like flies. Ancient oaks crushed the forges with single, ponderous blows, scattering molten iron and screaming demons.

"Break the dam!" Mbah Jangkung commanded, pointing to the reservoir above the ironworks where Empu Gandring had diverted the sacred river for his cooling pits. "Let... the... water... cleanse... the... wound!"

Cepot and Dawala watched in awe as three massive river-spirits tore down the stone dam.

The water roared. It was a wall of white fury, carrying boulders and trees and the accumulated rage of the mountain itself. It crashed into the valley, flooding the ironworks, quenching the fires in great hissing clouds of steam. The Buta panicked, scrambling up the slopes, but the water caught them, washing them away like debris in a monsoon flood.

Empu Gandring appeared on a high balcony of his dark tower, screaming curses at the forest, his face twisted with the madness of his corruption. He raised his staff to summon dark *sakti*, but a boulder thrown by a particularly angry teak spirit shattered the balcony beneath his feet. He fell back into the tower, howling in frustration as the water swirled around its base.

"The... tower... stands," Mbah Jangkung rumbled, watching the dark spire rise from the flood. "But... the... forges... are... silent. The... iron... is... cold. The... forest... reclaims... its... own."

He looked at the two small Tuyul on his shoulder.

"You... have... done... well... little... roots," he said, his anger subsiding into a deep, weary satisfaction. "Without... your... eyes... we... might... have... slept... until... the... end... of... the... world."

Cepot looked at the flooded valley, at the ruined ironworks, at the steam rising into the red sky. He looked at Dawala, who was grinning through his tears.

"We did it," Cepot whispered. "We actually did it. *Aing* can't believe it."

The forest breathed again. The smoke began to clear. And high above, on the slopes of Merapi, the white banner of the Dwija Putih could be seen moving through the trees, signaling that the war on this front was won.

# Bab 10: Gua Kalajengking

## (The Scorpion's Lair)

---

*Gunungan ditancepkeun dina kenca, miring ka katuhu.*
*Wanda anyar geus dibuka: Wanda Galau.*
*Gua poek, sora ngeclakna cai, sora napas beurat di kadeukeutan.*
*Lampu blencong pareum, ngan ukur hiji obor leutik di tengah layar.*

*The Gunungan is set at the left, tilted to the right.*
*A new act is opened: The Act of Turmoil.*

*The lamp is extinguished, only a single small torch in the center of the screen.*

---

Gua Kalajengking bauna siga kuburan kuno nu geus lila katutup.

The Scorpion's Lair smelled of ancient tombs long sealed from the world. The air was thick and stagnant, heavy with the scent of dry rot, old spider silk, and something else -- a sharp, acrid musk that made the hairs on Kian Santang's arms stand on end. The tunnel was narrow, its walls of jagged volcanic rock pressing close, glistening with a pale, phosphorescent fungus that gave off a sickly green light.

Kian Santang moved slowly, one hand on the wall to guide him, the other gripping the hilt of his short blade. Behind him, Bagus breathed in short, terrified gasps. Ahead, Si Burik scuttled on all fours, his pale eyes glowing in the dark, muttering to himself in a language of clicks and hisses.

"Up... up... nearly there... nearly through..." Si Burik whispered, pausing to look back at them with a wide, toothless grin. "Through the tunnel... into the caldera... safe... yes... safe."

"Are you sure this is the way?" Kian Santang asked, his voice echoing strangely in the confined space. "It feels... wrong. Like we are walking into a throat."

"Throat? No! No throat! Just... passage! Secret passage!" Si Burik giggled, a sound like dry leaves scraping stone. "Master carries the *jimat*... master must be careful... master must trust Si Burik... *gollum*..."

They climbed higher. The tunnel widened into a large, irregular chamber. The floor was covered in a thick layer of dust and bones -- small bones, bird bones, rat bones... and larger bones that looked suspiciously like the femurs of Buta.

"Wait," Bagus said, grabbing Kian Santang's arm. "Look at the walls."

Kian Santang raised his small torch. The walls were covered in thick, grey webs. Not normal spider webs -- these were ropes of silk as thick as a man's arm, sticky and strong, draped across the ceiling like rotting curtains.

"Webs," Kian Santang whispered. "Si Burik... what lives here?"

Si Burik had vanished into the shadows ahead.

"Si Burik!" Kian Santang called out. "Where are you?"

Silence.

Then, a sound from the darkness above. A chittering sound. A clicking of mandibles. A shifting of many legs on stone.

*Skitter-skitter-click.*

"Run!" Kian Santang shouted, pushing Bagus back toward the tunnel entrance. "It's a trap! *Mulah* look back!"

From the ceiling dropped a horror.

It was not a spider. It was a scorpion -- a *kalajengking* of nightmares, the size of a water buffalo. Its carapace was black as obsidian, shining with a wet, oily sheen. Its eight legs ended in hooked claws that scrabbled on the stone. Its tail arched over its back, tipped with a stinger the size of a spearhead, dripping with a venom that hissed when it hit the floor. And its eyes -- eight of them, clustered in two rows -- burned with a cold, malevolent intelligence.

This was the spawn of *Nyi Blorong*, the serpent-queen's pet, guardian of the high pass.

"SCREEEECH!"

The creature lunged. Kian Santang dove to the side, rolling across the bone-strewn floor. The stinger struck the stone where he had been standing, shattering rock.

"Kian!" Bagus screamed, drawing his gardening knife. He charged the creature, slashing at one of its legs. The knife sparked against the hard shell but did not penetrate.

The scorpion turned on Bagus, its massive pincers snapping. It caught him by the shoulder, lifting him into the air like a rag doll. Bagus cried out in pain as the claws dug into his flesh.

"Let him go!" Kian Santang roared. He drew the Keris Murka.

The blade flared with a dark, violet light. The scorpion shrieked and recoiled, blinded by the sudden radiance of the Shadow King's weapon. It dropped Bagus, who fell heavily to the floor, clutching his bleeding shoulder.

"Run, Bagus! Go!" Kian Santang shouted, waving the keris. "I'll hold it off! *Aing* commands you!"

"I won't leave you!" Bagus gasped, trying to stand.

"You have to! Find Eyang Guru! Find help! GO!"

Kian Santang charged the creature, thrusting the keris at its exposed underbelly. The blade sank deep into the soft flesh between the armor plates. The scorpion screamed -- a sound that tore at the ears -- and thrashed violently, its tail whipping around.

The stinger caught Kian Santang in the chest.

Pain. Cold, numbing pain spread through his body instantly. His vision blurred. His legs gave way. He fell to his knees, the keris slipping from his hand.

"Kian!" Bagus screamed again, but he was already scrambling down the tunnel, driven by the pure instinct of survival and the desperate hope of bringing aid.

The scorpion loomed over Kian Santang, its mandibles clicking. But it did not strike again. It began to spin. Thick, sticky silk poured from its spinnerets, wrapping Kian Santang in a cocoon of grey web.

The last thing Kian Santang saw before the darkness took him was Si Burik's face peering from a crevice in the wall, grinning.

"Master sleeps now..." the creature whispered. "Master sleeps... and Si Burik takes... the *jimat*..."

Kian Santang closed his eyes. The web tightened. The darkness was complete.
